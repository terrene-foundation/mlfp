Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.8Reinforcement Learning

Total time: ~90 minutes  ·  10 slides  ·  ~9 min per slide

RL is conceptually different from all prior lessons — learning from interaction rather than static data. Spend extra time on the RL loop (Slide 2) and the Bellman equations (Slide 3). The PPO-to-RLHF bridge (Slide 8) is the most important forward connection — it sets up M6.3. The five-algorithm table (Slide 6) is reference material; walk it quickly and tell them to use the textbook for details.

Slide 1~3 minTitle

Reinforcement Learning

Frame: "This is the final lesson of Module 5 and the final DL paradigm. We have learned from images (CNN), sequences (RNN), text (Transformers), and generated data (GANs). RL learns from interaction — the agent acts in an environment and learns from the consequences. This is how AlphaGo beat the world champion and how ChatGPT was aligned with human preferences."

→ "The RL loop."
Slide 2~6 minRL loop

The RL loop

Walk the diagram slowly. "The agent observes a state, chooses an action, the environment transitions to a new state and produces a reward. The agent uses this experience to improve its policy — the mapping from states to actions. This cycle repeats thousands or millions of times."

Navigation analogy: "Imagine driving in an unfamiliar city without GPS. You make turns (actions), observe streets (states), and learn from wrong turns (negative reward) and progress (positive reward). After enough exploration, you know the optimal route."
→ "The mathematical foundation: Bellman."
Slide 3~7 minBellman

Bellman equations

Walk both equations. "The value V(s) is the expected cumulative reward starting from state s. The Q-value Q(s,a) is the expected cumulative reward taking action a in state s and acting optimally afterward. The discount factor gamma says future rewards are worth less than immediate ones — a dollar today vs a dollar tomorrow."

For experts: mention the contraction mapping proof of convergence — iterating the Bellman operator converges to the fixed point Q*.
→ "DQN approximates Q* with a neural network."
Slides 4–5~12 minDQN and PPO

DQN and PPO

Walk the code and formulas. DQN: "We train a network to predict Q-values for each action. Experience replay breaks correlation. Target network stabilises training." PPO: "Instead of learning Q-values, we directly learn a policy. The clipped objective prevents the policy from changing too much in one update — that is the 'proximal' in PPO."

→ "Five algorithms for five business problems."
Slides 6–7~10 minApplications and Gym

Five algorithms and custom environments

Walk the table briefly. Then focus on the Gymnasium code: "A custom environment is a Python class with step() and reset(). step() takes an action, computes the next state and reward, and returns them. That is the entire interface. Once you have this, any RL algorithm can train on your business problem."

→ "The bridge to M6."
Slide 8~8 minRLHF bridge

PPO → RLHF → DPO

This is the key forward connection. Walk the diagram. "PPO is a policy gradient algorithm. RLHF uses PPO to align LLMs: a reward model trained on human preferences scores the LLM's outputs, and PPO fine-tunes the LLM to maximise that reward while staying close to the original policy. DPO, which we will study in M6.3, skips the reward model entirely — it learns directly from preference pairs. Simpler, cheaper, often equally effective."

→ "Mistakes."

Mistakes and recap
