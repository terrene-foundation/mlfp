Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.4 A/B Testing and Experiment Design

~45 minutes · 13 slides · ~3.5 min per slide

This lesson is where statistics becomes decision-making. Your tone should shift from "here is the math" to "here is the workflow". The pre-registration slide and the SRM slide are the ones that will save learners time and money in real projects.

Slide 1~1 minTitle

A/B Testing and Experiment Design

Open: "2.3 gave you hypothesis tests. 2.4 assembles them into industrial A/B testing — the workflow Booking.com, Grab and Shopee all run. By the end of the day you will run a full A/B test from scratch on a BOGO promotion."

→ "Why randomisation is the foundation."
Slide 2~3 minRandomisation

Why randomisation

State the fundamental problem of causal inference. "We never see both worlds for the same person. Randomisation is the trick that makes the two groups we do see be, on average, identical — so the difference we measure is the causal effect, not some confounder."

Concrete framing: "Imagine you want to test whether umbrellas cause rain. You measure umbrella owners and non-owners. Owners see more rain. Does that mean umbrellas cause rain? No — rainy places cause umbrella ownership. Randomisation prevents this kind of reverse reasoning because you assign, not observe."
→ "Before touching data — pre-register."
Slide 3~3 minPre-registration

Pre-register before you touch the data

Walk the five items. Emphasise the discipline: "Once you've seen the data you cannot fairly decide the test. Your brain has already adjusted. Commit everything upfront — in writing — before the first user is randomised."

Tell the peeking story. "The single most common way to get a false positive in industry: run until significance, then stop. If you do this, your false positive rate isn't 5% — it's 30-50%. Most 'wins' you see reported are this."

→ "Power analysis before data collection."
Slide 4~4 minPower analysis

Power analysis — sizing

Walk both formulas. Point at the pieces: "The numerator is a function of how careful you want to be — $z_\alpha$ for false positives, $z_\beta$ for false negatives. The denominator is the signal-to-noise ratio. If the effect is big relative to noise, you need fewer observations."

Work the numbers: "Baseline 8% conversion. Want to detect 1pp lift. $p(1-p) = 0.074$. $(z_{0.025} + z_{0.80})^2 \approx 7.85$. So $n = 2 \cdot 7.85 \cdot 0.074 / 0.0001 \approx 11{,}600$ — wait, per arm is half. Around 5,800 per arm. 11,600 total."

For experts: mention that this is the Normal approximation; for small $n$ or unbalanced arms, use statsmodels.stats.power.NormalIndPower.
→ "Power curves to see the scaling."
Slide 5~2 minPower curves

Power curves

Show how power grows with $n$ for two effect sizes. Key insight: "Power scales with $\delta^2$. Halve the effect, quadruple the sample. If your PM says 'we expect a 0.5pp lift', check whether they can afford the sample size. Most of the time they cannot."

→ "The first check before outcome analysis."
Slide 6~4 minSRM

SRM — the first check you run

Sell SRM as a hygiene check: "Before you look at whether the treatment worked, check whether the randomiser worked. If you asked for 50/50 and got 52/48 on 10,000 users, something is broken — bot filter, redirect bug, pipeline error. That analysis is contaminated."

Walk the chi-squared. Stress the threshold: "Use 0.0001, not 0.05. SRM is a hygiene alarm. You want very few false alarms that cause engineers to waste time debugging nothing."

Story: "At one company I worked with, SRM caught a bug where the treatment variant took 50ms longer to load. Users on slow connections timed out before reaching the randomiser. The control had 10% more users. Without SRM, we would have concluded 'treatment kills conversions' and rolled back a good feature."
→ "Now the framework for data collection."
Slide 7~4 minCollection framework

Data Collection Framework

Walk the five questions. For each, give a concrete example from the BOGO campaign:

  • Why: "Does BOGO increase conversion by 1pp? If yes, ship. If no, kill."
  • What: "Primary: conversion rate. Secondary: AOV, RPV. Guardrail: page load time."
  • Where: "Internal: event stream, order database. External: none needed."
  • How: "Real-time event logging; nightly batch for reconciliation."
  • Frequency: "Run for 14 days minimum to cover both weekday and weekend patterns."
→ "The common pitfalls."
Slide 8~3 minCommon pitfalls

Common data pitfalls

Walk the list. Silos and red tape are organisational — name them honestly. Aggregated-only is the one to watch in finance and public- sector data. Selection bias is almost inevitable; the fix is logging exposure, not just outcomes. Temporal leakage is what kills experiments during marketing pushes.

→ "The primary test."
Slide 11~3 minPrimary test

Worked: the primary test

Walk the code. z-test for proportions gives p-value. CI tells us how big the effect really is. Lift is the point estimate. Report all three.

→ "Write the recommendation."
Slide 12~3 minReporting

Reporting to the business

Read the recommendation aloud. Point at each piece: the decision (ship), the effect (1.3 pp with CI), the p-value (0.003), the business value ($240K/quarter), the guardrail check. This is the shape of every good A/B test report.

Tell them what to NEVER do: "Don't say 'we prove'. Don't quote just a p-value without a CI. Don't report the effect in percent when the stakeholder wants dollars."
