Speaker Notes — Lesson 1.6 Data Visualisation

Total time: ~55 minutes · 15 slides · ~3.5 min average

Visualisation is a design lesson as much as a code lesson. The learners are used to numbers and code; today introduces perception and taste. Show lots of good/bad examples. Run every chart live so they see the interactive Plotly output.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Data Visualisation

Open with: "A good chart answers a question faster than the best table in the world. A bad chart misleads its reader in seconds. The gap between good and bad is learnable — and today we learn it."

→ "The first rule."
Slide 2~3 minOne-question rule

One chart, one question

Spend time on this. Every chart the learners make for the rest of their career should start with the question in a single sentence. "Which district grew fastest?" — a chart. "Everything about HDB" — a disaster.

Tell them: "If you cannot state the question, you are not ready to build the chart. Go back and figure out what decision the reader is supposed to make."

→ "Now the perception layer."
Slide 3~4 minGestalt principles

Gestalt principles: how eyes group shapes

Walk each of the four panels. Proximity — close dots look grouped. Similarity — same colour looks grouped. Enclosure — a box binds tightly. Continuity — the eye follows a line as a trend.

Tell them: "This is why line charts work for time-series. The eye interpolates between points automatically. You don't have to tell the reader 'this is a trend' — they see it."

The history: "These principles are from 1920s German psychology. Nothing more recent has replaced them. Every chart guideline in every book you will read traces back to this list."

For beginners: "You don't need to memorise the names. Just remember: if you put two dots close together or colour them the same, readers will think they belong together. That's enormously useful."
→ "Now the six chart types."
Slide 4~3 minSix chart types table

Six chart types cover 95% of questions

Walk the table. For each, ask learners to imagine a concrete HDB question that fits. "How is price distributed?" — histogram. "How has price changed?" — line. "Which town is most expensive?" — horizontal bar. Build the vocabulary.

Make the 95% point explicit. "You don't need to learn 40 chart types. Six will take you through most of your career. Specialists exist for the other 5%."

→ "The scientific backing."
Slide 5~4 minCleveland-McGill ranking

🔵 Cleveland–McGill perceptual ranking

This is the single most useful chart-design insight in the lesson. Walk the ranked list. Position on a common scale (bar lengths on a common axis) is the most accurate task the eye can perform. Colour hue is the worst.

Tell them: "If you remember one thing from this lesson: when a comparison matters, use bars. If the comparison doesn't matter, use whatever you like. If you want to hide the comparison, use a pie chart."

For experts: "Cleveland and McGill ran controlled experiments in 1984 where they asked people to estimate quantities from different chart types. The ranking is empirical, not opinion. Every modern chart library's defaults (including Plotly's) were shaped by this paper."
→ "The tool."
Slide 6~3 minModelVisualizer API

ModelVisualizer API

Walk the code. Point at each argument. "Data — a Polars DataFrame. Column — which column to chart. Title — one-sentence takeaway. Returns a Plotly Figure, which has .show() for Jupyter and .write_html() for saving."

Tell them: "Every chart method follows this shape. Once you learn one, you can guess the rest. Same engine across M1 EDA and M5 model evaluation — consistent look and feel."

→ "Chart 1 — histogram."
Slide 7~3 minHistogram

Histogram — distribution

Run it live. Show the right skew — most transactions in the 350k-600k range, a long tail of expensive flats. Ask what this means for mean versus median ("mean > median for right-skewed data").

Point at the bins. "40 bins is usually a good starting number. 10 is too coarse — you'll miss multi-modal shapes. 200 is too fine — every bin looks like noise."

→ "Chart 2 — scatter."
Slide 8~3 minScatter

Scatter — relationship between two numbers

The sample-first rule. Run it once without sampling — show the grey blob that 500k points produces. Then run with sample(5000) and show the clear positive slope.

Point at the spread. "At 100 sqm, prices range from 400k to over 900k. Area alone explains maybe 40-50% of price variance. The other half is town, floor level, lease. That's what the ML models in M3 will learn."

→ "Chart 3 — bar."
Slide 9~3 minBar

Bar — ranking categories

Run it. Point at the horizontal orientation. "Town names are long — horizontal bars let them be readable. Vertical bars would require rotated labels, which are harder to read."

Emphasise the sort. "Always sort bar charts by the value you're comparing. Unsorted bars force the reader to scan back and forth — you are making them do work the chart should have done."

→ "Chart 4 — line."
Slide 10~3 minLine

Line — change over time

Run it. "This is continuity in action. Your eye follows the line and reads it as a trend — you don't consciously compute the slope, you see it."

Note: "We're using training_history for a non-ML line chart because it's a general-purpose line plotter. Plotly-native go.Scatter with mode='lines' is an alternative if you want more control."

→ "Chart 5 — heatmap."
Slide 11~3 minHeatmap

Heatmap — correlations at a glance

Run it. Walk the diagonal (each variable perfectly correlated with itself = 1). Point at the off-diagonals. "resale_price correlates strongly with price_per_sqm because they share the numerator."

Tell them: "Colour encodes magnitude. Diverging palette around zero — red for negative, blue for positive. This is the one place colour encodes quantity well — because the comparison is between two colours, not among many."

→ "Chart 6 — stacked bar."
Slide 12~3 minStacked bar

Stacked bar — composition

Run it. "Each bar is one town, split by flat type. The total height is the number of transactions; the colour segments are the mix."

Note the caveat: "Stacked bars are good for one comparison at a time — either the total across towns, or the share within a single town. Comparing 3-room share across towns is actually hard because the baseline moves. For share comparisons, use 100% stacked bars."

→ "Charts to avoid."
Slide 13~3 minAnti-patterns

Four charts to avoid

Walk each. The 3D ban is firm. Show a 3D bar chart — point at how the back bars look shorter than the front bars even when they're taller. "You cannot compare quantities through perspective."

The pie chart rule: "Five slices is the usable limit. Ten slices is unreadable. Twenty slices is hostile. Most pie charts should have been horizontal bars."

The truncated-axis rule: "Bar charts encode the value in the bar length. If your bars start at 400 instead of 0, you've lied about the lengths. Never truncate a bar-chart y-axis."

→ "And titles."
Slide 14~2 minTitles

Title = takeaway, not topic

Walk the before/after. Tell them: "The title is the first thing the reader reads. Often the only thing. Put the conclusion there — 'central districts command a 60% premium' — not the topic."

This is a habit shift they will resist at first. Keep pushing.

→ "Reflect and bridge."
Slide 15~3 minReflection

What you can now do

Walk the bullets. Pause on each.

Tease 1.7: "Today you hand-built six charts. Tomorrow you'll meet the engine that does the entire EDA for you. Point DataExplorer at any dataset, and it profiles every column, surfaces eight kinds of quality alerts, and generates a suite of diagnostic visualisations — the same charts you just built, at scale."
