Lesson 5.5 Generative Models: GANs and Diffusion

A generative model learns to produce new samples that look like the training data. The VAE in Lesson 5.1 already did this with a reconstruction loss plus KL regulariser. Today we meet two alternatives: the Generative Adversarial Network, which trains a generator and a discriminator in a minimax game, and the diffusion model, which noises data progressively and learns to reverse the process. You implement a DCGAN-style MLP generator and a WGAN-GP, debug mode collapse, and get a tour of DDPM.

The GAN Setup

A GAN has two networks playing a zero-sum game:

  • A generator $G_\theta : \mathbb{R}^{d_z} \to \mathbb{R}^{D}$ maps a latent noise vector $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ to a generated sample $G(z)$.
  • A discriminator $D_\phi : \mathbb{R}^{D} \to [0, 1]$ tries to tell real samples from generated ones. It outputs the probability that its input is real.

The minimax objective

Goodfellow's 2014 paper frames it as a two-player game:

$$\min_{G} \max_{D} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\![\log D(x)] \;+\; \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}\![\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$
$D(x)$ — prob real is real $D(G(z))$ — prob fake looks real $\min_G \max_D$ — adversary

D wants to push the first term up (recognise real) and the second up (recognise fake). G wants to push the second term down (fool D). At the optimum, if both networks are infinitely expressive and trained to convergence, $p_G = p_{\text{data}}$ — G exactly matches the true distribution.

🔵 Theory — The JS divergence connection

At the optimum of D, the GAN objective reduces to $2 \cdot \mathrm{JS}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_G) - \log 4$, where JS is the Jensen-Shannon divergence — a symmetric variant of KL. So minimising the original GAN loss with respect to G is minimising JS divergence between real and generated. Unlike KL, JS is always finite, which is (one) reason GANs can be trained on supports that don't overlap.

Training dynamics

In practice you alternate:

  1. Update D: sample a real batch and a fake batch, compute the standard binary cross-entropy loss, step D's parameters.
  2. Update G: sample new fakes, compute a loss that is high when D correctly classifies them as fake, step G.

The loss for G is often written as $\max \; \mathbb{E}_z[\log D(G(z))]$ rather than $\min \; \mathbb{E}_z[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$ — mathematically equivalent at the optimum but gives a stronger gradient when D is confident the fakes are fake (early training).

DCGAN-style Generator and Discriminator

A DCGAN (Radford et al. 2015) uses convolutions for image data. For the simple 2-D distribution we will use today, MLPs are enough. 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class Generator(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, z_dim=8, out_dim=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(z_dim, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, out_dim),
        )
    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z)

class Discriminator(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_dim=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(in_dim, 64), nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Linear(64, 64), nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Linear(64, 1),      # logit, no sigmoid
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x).squeeze(-1)

Discriminators traditionally use LeakyReLU (not plain ReLU) because dead neurons in D stall training for G. Leaky avoids this by letting a small gradient through for negative inputs.

The training loop

opt_g = torch.optim.Adam(G.parameters(), lr=2e-4, betas=(0.5, 0.999))
opt_d = torch.optim.Adam(D.parameters(), lr=2e-4, betas=(0.5, 0.999))

for epoch in range(200):
    for real in real_loader:
        bs = real.size(0)
        # ─── 1. Update D ─────────────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, z_dim, device=device)
        fake = G(z).detach()
        d_real = D(real)
        d_fake = D(fake)
        loss_d = F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(
            d_real, torch.ones_like(d_real)
        ) + F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(
            d_fake, torch.zeros_like(d_fake)
        )
        opt_d.zero_grad(); loss_d.backward(); opt_d.step()

        # ─── 2. Update G ─────────────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, z_dim, device=device)
        d_fake = D(G(z))
        # "non-saturating" loss: G wants D to say 1 on fakes
        loss_g = F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(
            d_fake, torch.ones_like(d_fake)
        )
        opt_g.zero_grad(); loss_g.backward(); opt_g.step()

The betas=(0.5, 0.999) is standard for GANs — momentum 0.5 is more stable than the default 0.9. The .detach() on the fake tensor when updating D stops gradients from flowing into G during D's update (we only want D's parameters to move).

Mode Collapse — the Main Failure Mode

GANs are notoriously unstable. The most famous failure is mode collapse: the generator finds one fake that fools the current discriminator and keeps producing it over and over. If the true distribution has eight Gaussian blobs in 2-D, mode collapse makes the generator emit only one of them (or at worst, a single point).

WGAN and the Wasserstein distance

Arjovsky et al. (2017) replaced JS divergence with the Wasserstein-1 (Earth Mover) distance. Instead of a probability, the "critic" (what we call D in Wasserstein land) outputs a real number; the loss is simply the difference of critic outputs on real and fake:

$$\min_{G} \max_{D} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\![D(x)] \;-\; \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}\![D(G(z))]$$
No sigmoid, no BCE Requires D to be 1-Lipschitz = Wasserstein-1 distance

The Wasserstein formulation only equals the Earth Mover distance if D is 1-Lipschitz — its outputs cannot change by more than 1 when its input changes by 1. The original WGAN enforced this by clipping D's weights to $[-0.01, 0.01]$ after each step (crude, limits model capacity). WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al. 2017) replaced weight clipping with a gradient penalty:

$$\mathcal{L}_D = \mathbb{E}[D(G(z))] - \mathbb{E}[D(x)] + \lambda \, \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x}}\!\bigl[(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}} D(\hat{x})\|_2 - 1)^2\bigr]$$
$\hat{x}$ — random interpolation $\lambda$ — penalty weight (usually 10) target gradient norm = 1

The penalty term pushes the gradient norm of D along a random line between real and fake toward 1 — the Lipschitz constraint. Works far better than weight clipping: it preserves model capacity, trains more stably, and almost eliminates mode collapse in practice. 

def gradient_penalty(D, real, fake, device):
    batch_size = real.size(0)
    alpha = torch.rand(batch_size, 1, device=device)
    interpolated = alpha * real + (1 - alpha) * fake
    interpolated.requires_grad_(True)
    d_inter = D(interpolated)
    grads = torch.autograd.grad(
        outputs=d_inter,
        inputs=interpolated,
        grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(d_inter),
        create_graph=True,
        retain_graph=True,
    )[0]
    gp = ((grads.norm(2, dim=1) - 1) ** 2).mean()
    return gp

Evaluating Generative Models: FID

How do you know if your generator is any good? Human evaluation is expensive and subjective. The standard automatic metric for image generation is the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID):

$$\text{FID}(r, g) \;=\; \|\mu_r - \mu_g\|_2^2 \;+\; \text{Tr}\!\bigl(\Sigma_r + \Sigma_g - 2(\Sigma_r \Sigma_g)^{1/2}\bigr)$$
$\mu_r, \Sigma_r$ — stats of real features $\mu_g, \Sigma_g$ — stats of generated Tr — matrix trace

FID passes both real and generated images through a pre-trained Inception network, extracts a pool of activations, fits Gaussians to both, and measures the Fréchet distance between them. Lower is better. FID below 20 is "you can probably ship it"; below 5 is "very close to real".

Diffusion Models in One Page

Diffusion models (DDPM, Ho et al. 2020; Stable Diffusion 2022) take a completely different approach. Instead of adversarial training, they:

  1. Forward process: progressively add Gaussian noise to a training image over $T$ steps until it is pure noise.
  2. Reverse process: train a network (usually a UNet) to predict the noise that was added at each step, so the process can be reversed.
  3. Sampling: start from pure noise, apply the reverse process $T$ times, end with a generated sample.

The training objective is simply MSE between the predicted noise and the actual noise added at each step. There is no adversary, no mode collapse, and the quality is generally higher than GANs. The cost is that sampling requires $T$ forward passes (typically 50–1000 steps), whereas a GAN samples in one forward pass. Recent distillation techniques (consistency models) bring diffusion sampling down to 1–4 steps.

🟣 Advanced — Diffusion vs GAN vs VAE decision

For high-quality image generation with large models and compute, diffusion is the current SOTA. For fast sampling, GANs are still competitive. For structured representations (where you want the latent to mean something), VAEs remain the tool of choice. For conditional generation with text (Stable Diffusion, Midjourney), diffusion plus a text encoder is the dominant stack.

Worked Example: DCGAN and WGAN-GP on Eight Gaussians

📝 Goal

Build a GAN and a WGAN-GP on a 2-D "eight Gaussians on a ring" toy distribution. Observe mode collapse in the vanilla GAN. Observe WGAN-GP recovering all eight modes. This mirrors exercise 5 of M5.

Step 1 — the target distribution. 
def make_eight_gaussians(n):
    angles = torch.linspace(0, 2 * math.pi, 9)[:-1]
    centers = torch.stack([torch.cos(angles), torch.sin(angles)], dim=1)
    idx = torch.randint(0, 8, (n,))
    points = centers[idx] + 0.05 * torch.randn(n, 2)
    return points
Step 2 — vanilla GAN training (as shown above).

Expect to see the generator collapse onto one or two of the eight modes after a few hundred steps. Plot the generated samples every 100 steps to watch it happen.

Step 3 — WGAN-GP update rule. 
for epoch in range(200):
    for real in real_loader:
        # ── Critic (D) update ──
        z = torch.randn(bs, z_dim, device=device)
        fake = G(z).detach()
        d_real = D(real)
        d_fake = D(fake)
        gp = gradient_penalty(D, real, fake, device)
        loss_d = d_fake.mean() - d_real.mean() + 10.0 * gp
        opt_d.zero_grad(); loss_d.backward(); opt_d.step()

        # ── Generator update (every n_critic steps) ──
        z = torch.randn(bs, z_dim, device=device)
        loss_g = -D(G(z)).mean()
        opt_g.zero_grad(); loss_g.backward(); opt_g.step()

The critic loss is just the difference of means plus gradient penalty. The generator loss is the negative critic output — G wants to make the critic produce large values on its fakes. Typically you take $n_{\text{critic}} = 5$ critic steps per generator step, though the 1:1 ratio works fine on this toy problem.

Step 4 — visualise the samples. 
G.eval()
with torch.no_grad():

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Forgetting .detach() on D's update

When updating D, the fake samples come from G. If you do not detach them, gradients will flow through G during D's backward pass and corrupt G's parameters. Always fake = G(z).detach() when training D.

⚠ Mistake 2: BCE on WGAN, Wasserstein on GAN

Vanilla GAN uses binary_cross_entropy_with_logits. WGAN uses plain means (no sigmoid, no BCE). Mixing them up is a rite of passage. Symptom: WGAN "loss" is meaningless numbers because you accidentally applied a sigmoid somewhere.

⚠ Mistake 3: Wrong gradient penalty target

WGAN-GP penalises the gradient norm toward 1, not toward 0. The penalty is $(\|\nabla\|_2 - 1)^2$, not $\|\nabla\|_2^2$. Targetting zero is a "one-sided" variant sometimes used in WGAN-LP but it is not the canonical WGAN-GP loss.

⚠ Mistake 4: Learning rates too high

GAN training is fragile. Standard LRs for Adam are 2e-4 for both G and D. Beta_1 = 0.5 (not 0.9). These are the Radford et al. DCGAN defaults and they are a decent starting point. Do not deviate without a good reason.

Cross-References

GAN generators are structurally very similar to VAE decoders (Lesson 5.1) — they take a latent and emit a sample. The difference is the training signal: VAEs optimise reconstruction + KL; GANs optimise an adversarial game. Diffusion models descend from the denoising autoencoder (also 5.1): the forward process is just progressive corruption and the reverse process is progressive denoising. The discriminator is a standard classifier CNN (Lesson 5.2). In Module 6, we use diffusion as a building block for text-to-image systems and revisit FID for alignment metrics.
