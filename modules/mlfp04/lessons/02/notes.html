Speaker Notes — Lesson 4.2 EM Algorithm and Gaussian Mixture Models

Total time: ~75 minutes  ·  18 slides  ·  ~4 min per slide

This is the mathematically densest lesson in the first half of M4. The pedagogical challenge: the math is intimidating but the algorithm is simple. Keep coming back to the 20-line implementation — students who can read code can understand EM through the code even if the Jensen inequality argument is beyond them today. Save the ELBO for the Advanced audience cue.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

EM Algorithm and Gaussian Mixture Models

Frame it: "Yesterday K-means gave every customer a single label. That is a convenient lie. Today we remove the lie and learn how to say '70% cluster A, 30% cluster B'. The cost is exactly one new algorithm — EM — which you will implement in 20 lines by the end of this session."

"If the name 'Expectation-Maximisation' sounds scary, ignore it — by the end it will just be two alternating steps you can write on a napkin."
→ "Hard labels lie. Here's why."
Slide 2~4 minHard labels lie

Hard labels lie

Give a concrete example: "Customer A buys twice a month and spends moderately. Is she 'high-value' or 'average'? K-means picks one. The honest answer is 'somewhere between'. GMM gives you that somewhere."

Key business point: soft assignments unlock decisions you cannot make with hard labels. "Should we send this customer a loyalty offer? Depends on how likely she is to be a loyal customer. If K-means says 'yes cluster 2' but GMM says 'only 45% cluster 2', maybe don't."

→ "Here's the picture."
Slide 3~3 minHard vs soft diagram

Hard vs soft in one picture

Walk the two panels. Same data, same boundary point. K-means commits; GMM reports. Point out that GMM also gives you the cluster shapes via the covariance matrices — the ellipses in the right panel.

→ "Let's make this rigorous. GMM has a generative story."
Slide 4~5 minGenerative story

The generative story

Walk the two steps. Emphasise that this is how the data was generated, not how we classify it. "Nature rolls a weighted die — the $\pi_k$ — to pick a cluster, then samples from that cluster's Gaussian." Reverse-engineering this story IS the clustering problem.

"This is our first example of a latent variable model. The cluster assignment $z$ is latent — we see $x$ but not $z$. Half of classical ML is latent variable models trained with EM or MCMC."
→ "And the problem is: we can't maximise the likelihood directly."
Slide 5~4 minWhy hard

Why maximising the likelihood is hard

Point at the "log of a sum" expression. "If the sum inside the log was a single term, we could take derivatives and solve. But a log of a sum has no closed-form maximum. We need a clever workaround — and that workaround is EM."

→ "Here is the loop."
Slide 6~3 minEM loop diagram

The EM loop

Walk the four boxes. Initialise → E-step → M-step → check convergence → loop back. The structure is identical to gradient descent in spirit: pick a starting point, iterate, stop when things stop improving. The difference is that each step here has a closed form.

→ "E-step first."
Slide 7~5 minE-step derivation

E-step: responsibilities via Bayes' rule

Walk the formula slowly. The numerator is "prior on cluster $k$ times likelihood of $\mathbf{x}_n$ under cluster $k$". The denominator normalises across clusters. This is exactly Bayes' rule from M2.

"If you forget the formula, remember the story: 'Given everything I know about $\mathbf{x}_n$ and the current cluster parameters, what's the probability this point came from cluster $k$?' That is $r_{nk}$."
"The responsibilities define the posterior $q(z)$ over latent variables in the ELBO decomposition. EM is block-coordinate ascent on $\mathbb{E}_{q}[\log p(x,z)] + \mathbb{H}(q)$ — the E-step fixes $\theta$ and maximises over $q$, the M-step fixes $q$ and maximises over $\theta$."
→ "Given responsibilities, the M-step is a weighted average."
Slide 8~5 minM-step

M-step: weighted means

Walk each formula. $N_k$ is "effective number of points in cluster $k$". $\mu_k$ is "weighted mean of the data, weighted by responsibility". $\Sigma_k$ is "weighted covariance around the new mean". $\pi_k$ is "fraction of effective mass". Every formula is just a weighted MLE.

→ "The magic: EM is guaranteed to improve."
Slide 9~4 minConvergence

Convergence guarantee

State the guarantee clearly: "Every EM iteration is guaranteed to not decrease the log-likelihood. This is why the algorithm converges." For the beginner audience that's enough — they should trust the guarantee and move on.

"The proof is Jensen's inequality applied to the expectation of $\log p(x, z)$ under $q(z)$. The resulting lower bound is the ELBO — evidence lower bound — which EM monotonically improves. This framework generalises to variational inference, which we use in M6 for LLM fine-tuning."

Warn about local maxima: "Like K-means, EM can get stuck. Always run 3-5 random initialisations and keep the best log-likelihood."

→ "Now let's write it in 20 lines."

Twenty lines of EM (1/2)

Walk the code. Random initialisation with identity covariances (point at this — students will get singular matrices if they try to init with zeros). Then the E-step: compute responsibilities with mvn.pdf, stack them, normalise.

→ "And the M-step plus convergence check."
Slide 11~4 minCode 2/2

Twenty lines of EM (2/2)

Walk through $N_k$, $\pi$, $\mu$, $\Sigma$. Point at the covariance computation — it is the single trickiest line. The outer product diff.T @ diff gives the unweighted scatter; the weighting by r[:, k:k+1] makes it the weighted scatter.

Convergence check: compare new log-likelihood to old, stop if the change is less than tolerance. The 1e-12 additions are there to avoid log(0) when a point has zero density under every cluster (numerical safety).

→ "How many clusters? BIC answers this."
Slide 12~4 minBIC

Picking $K$ with BIC

Walk the BIC formula. "$-2\log \hat{\mathcal{L}}$ rewards fit. $p \log N$ penalises complexity. Lower is better. Try $K = 2$ to $10$ and pick the minimum. In practice, BIC often agrees with silhouette on the right K, but disagrees when there are clusters of very different sizes."

→ "One more knob: covariance type."
Slide 13~3 minCovariance types

Covariance types: tied, diag, full

Walk the table. In business data with $D < 20$ features, full is fine. Above that, switch to diag. For very high dimensions (say image features), use tied to share a single covariance across clusters — this is essentially Linear Discriminant Analysis.

→ "And one more thing: GMMs are everywhere in modern ML."
Slide 14~4 minMixture of Experts

Mixture of Experts — where GMM meets GPT-4

Introduce MoE. "Replace the constant $\pi_k$ with a function of the input. Now different inputs pick different experts. Modern large language models — Mixtral, DeepSeek, GPT-4 — use this pattern to scale to hundreds of billions of parameters efficiently."

"MoE in transformers routes tokens through a few experts at a time, selected by a softmax over a small gating network. Training uses load-balancing losses to prevent expert collapse. It is a direct descendant of Jacobs, Jordan, Nowlan and Hinton's 1991 MoE paper."
→ "Let's run EM on real data."
Slide 15~4 minWorked example

Worked example: 3 Gaussians in 2D

Walk the code. Generate 3 ground-truth Gaussians. Run EM. Compare against sklearn. Point out that both should report very similar log-likelihoods — this is the self-consistency check that validates your implementation.

→ "The payoff: find the uncertain customers."
Slide 16~3 minUncertain points

The uncertain points are where the money is

Business framing: "In a real customer segmentation pipeline, the points with $\max_k r_{nk} < 0.6$ are the ambiguous ones. They are exactly the customers worth manual review — you cannot act on them with confidence from an automated system alone."

Drive home: this is information K-means threw away. It is the kind of decision-making value that soft clustering unlocks.

→ "Common mistakes."
Slide 17~3 minMistakes

Four rookie mistakes

Walk all four. #1 (zero-init covariance) is the single most common bug in homegrown EM implementations. #4 (full covariance in high dims) is the most common cause of poor sklearn GMM results.
