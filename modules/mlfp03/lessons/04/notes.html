Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.4 Gradient Boosting Deep Dive

Total time: ~75 minutes · 14 slides · ~5 min per slide average

This is the math-heavy highlight of Module 3. The derivation is five lines but each line needs a full minute of explanation. Expect confusion at the Taylor expansion; expect satisfaction when the split-gain formula pops out. Leave 15 minutes at the end for the live run and comparison.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Opening

Set stakes: "The derivation we are about to do won XGBoost the KDD 2016 best paper and made gradient boosting the default tool for tabular competitions. Knowing it deeply — not just knowing the API — is how you avoid being a button-pusher when the model misbehaves in production."

→ "Two philosophies of ensembling."
Slide 2~4 minBagging vs boosting

Two ensembling strategies

Walk both diagrams. "Random forest from last lesson is bagging — many high-variance trees in parallel, averaged to reduce variance. Boosting is the opposite. Sequential. Each new tree fits the residuals of the ensemble so far. The trees are weak — depth 3 to 6. The ensemble becomes strong because each addition fixes what the previous one got wrong."

Punchline: "Bagging reduces variance; boosting reduces bias. Opposite problems, opposite ensembles."

→ "The recipe in one equation."
Slide 3~3 minBoosting recipe

The additive update

Walk the equation. "Start with a constant — the mean for regression, the log-odds of the base rate for classification. At each step, fit a new tree to the gradient of the loss, scale it by $\eta$, and add it to the ensemble."

$\eta$ as bias-variance dial: "Low $\eta$ with many trees is the universal 'better generalisation' recipe. Typical production values are 0.01 to 0.1. Coupled with early stopping, you never need to tune $n\_estimators$ by hand."

→ "Now the math that makes XGBoost special."
Slide 4~5 minXGBoost objective

The regularised objective

Walk the two equations. First the raw objective: loss summed over rows plus a regulariser on the tree. Then the Taylor expansion — slow down here, this is the key move.

"Fix the ensemble's current prediction $\hat{y}^{(t-1)}$. We add a new tree $f_t$, so every prediction becomes $\hat{y}^{(t-1)} + f_t(x)$. We Taylor-expand $L$ around $\hat{y}^{(t-1)}$ in the perturbation $f_t(x)$ — first order is $g_i f_t$, second order is $\tfrac{1}{2} h_i f_t^2$. Drop the zero-order term, keep the two perturbation terms."

For beginners new to Taylor: "Taylor expansion is 'approximate a function near a point with a polynomial'. First order is a straight line tangent to the curve. Second order adds a parabola that matches the curvature. That parabola is what lets us solve the leaf weight in closed form on the next slide."
→ "Now group by leaf and solve."
Slide 5~5 minLeaf weight closed form

Solve in closed form

The algebra: "A tree assigns every row to exactly one leaf. So the sum over rows becomes a sum over leaves of a sum over rows in each leaf. Within a leaf, $f_t(x_i) = w_j$ is constant — pull it out of the inner sum and we get $G_j w_j + \tfrac{1}{2}(H_j + \lambda) w_j^2$, a simple quadratic in $w_j$."

Minimise: set the derivative to zero, solve. $w_j^* = -G_j / (H_j + \lambda)$. Substitute back into the objective to get the closed form for the optimal ensemble loss at a fixed tree structure.

For experts: "This is a Newton step per leaf. The second derivative $H_j$ is the curvature; dividing by it gives the optimal step size. That's why second-order boosting converges faster than first-order Friedman boosting."
→ "And finally the split gain — the whole point of the derivation."
Slide 6~5 minSplit gain

The split-gain formula

Frame it. "We have a closed form for the loss given a fixed tree. Now we decide: should we split this leaf or not? Compute the loss before the split (one leaf) and after the split (two leaves). Subtract. If the improvement is positive, split; otherwise, don't."

Walk the boxed formula. "Three terms. The first two are the post-split contribution of the new left and right leaves. The third is the pre-split contribution of the leaf we're splitting. Minus $\gamma$, the fixed cost of adding a leaf."

Key properties: "This formula is loss-agnostic. You plug in $g_i$ and $h_i$ from any twice-differentiable loss and the same split-gain formula works — log-loss, squared error, Huber, whatever. That's why XGBoost supports arbitrary custom losses. And the evaluation is trivially parallel across candidate splits, which is how XGBoost gets its speed."

→ "LightGBM takes speed even further with two tricks."
Slide 7~4 minLightGBM tricks

LightGBM speedups

Walk the three ideas. "Histogram splits: instead of evaluating every numeric threshold, LightGBM bins values into 256 buckets and only evaluates splits at bucket boundaries. That is $O(n + 256)$ instead of $O(n \log n)$ per feature per level — orders of magnitude faster on large datasets."

"GOSS: train on only the informative rows. Large-gradient rows are under-fit; keep all of them. Small-gradient rows are well-predicted; randomly sample 10% of them. Net: ~30% of the data per iteration, ~3× speedup."

"Leaf-wise growth: instead of growing all nodes at a depth before going deeper, LightGBM splits the leaf with the single largest gain. Unbalanced trees, but each split is locally optimal, faster convergence. Risk: overfitting — you control it with num_leaves."

→ "GOSS details if you want the intuition."
Slide 8~3 minGOSS intuition

Why sampling small-gradient rows is safe

"If a row already has a small gradient, it is close to the model's current prediction. Training on it contributes little new information. You can downsample it safely. Large-gradient rows — where the model is still wrong — you keep all of them. GOSS is the formalisation of 'focus on the hard examples'."

Up-weighting: "The sampled low-gradient rows get weight $(1-a)/b$ so that the overall gradient estimate is unbiased. Without that, GOSS would systematically under-weight the easy region."

→ "CatBoost has a totally different superpower."
Slide 9~5 minCatBoost ordered

Ordered target encoding

Motivate: "If you have a 500-category postal code feature, the standard trick is mean-target encoding — replace each postal code with the mean default rate in that postal code. Problem: you used the target to build the feature. Classic leakage."

Solution: "CatBoost imposes an artificial row ordering. For each row $i$, it computes the target encoding using only rows $j < i$. Row $i$'s own target is not in the encoding of row $i$. No leakage. And the ordering is reshuffled on every boosting iteration to avoid systematic bias toward the specific ordering."

When to pick CatBoost: "Many high-cardinality categorical features and you don't want to hand-engineer the encoding. Think Singapore postal codes, product IDs, or MCC codes. CatBoost handles them natively."

→ "Now the live comparison."
Slide 10~5 minTrain all three

Three boosters, one dataset

Walk the code. Point out that we're using AUC-PR as the eval metric — the credit-scoring dataset is 12% default rate, and that means accuracy and ROC-AUC can lie. AUC-PR (average precision) is the right imbalance metric.

Run it if you can. Expected: all three are within 0.005 AUC-PR of each other on this dataset. The differences become meaningful only at much larger scales.

→ "Early stopping — the one hyperparameter you don't tune."
Slide 11~4 minEarly stopping

Free tuning via validation

"Don't guess n_estimators. Set it to a large number — 1000 or 5000 — pass an eval_set, and set early_stopping_rounds. Training halts when the validation metric hasn't improved for that many rounds. best_iteration tells you where it stopped, and that's your optimal n_estimators."

Warn: "Your eval_set needs to be representative of production. If it is from a different distribution, early stopping will stop at the wrong place. Lesson 3.7 shows how to combine early stopping with cross-validation."

→ "A quick guide for which booster to reach for."
Slide 12~3 minWhich booster

Picking your weapon

Walk the table. Make it practical: "XGBoost is my default when I want reproducibility — the algorithm is the most stable across versions. LightGBM when I have millions of rows and need to finish training in minutes. CatBoost when the dataset has many categorical columns and I don't want to hand-code encoders."

Cheat: "Or use all three via Kailash's AutoMLEngine which we'll see in Lesson 3.7 — it ensembles the three boosters automatically."

→ "Three traps."
Slide 13~3 minTraps

Three expensive mistakes

Walk the three. Trap 1 (ROC-AUC on imbalanced) is the bridge to Lesson 3.5 — promise to explain it fully there. Trap 2 (no early stopping) is the single most common form of wasted training time in practice. Trap 3 (tuning lr and n_estimators independently) is the novice's grid-search trap.

→ "Reflection and bridge."
Slide 14~3 minReflection + bridge

What you can do now + Lesson 3.5

Run the bullets. The derivation one — "I can derive the XGBoost split gain from first principles" — is the psychological payoff. Let them sit with the fact that they now understand the math behind the most popular algorithm in production tabular ML.

Bridge: "Your credit-scoring model has an AUC-PR that sounds great, but you haven't actually looked at what it predicts. Does it send 0.5 for a real 50% risk, or is that number inflated? Does the model ever catch any actual defaults at useful precision? Tomorrow's lesson asks those questions and shows you the four techniques — SMOTE, focal loss, cost-sensitive learning, calibration — that turn a raw classifier into a production decision system."
