Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.7 CUPED and Causal Inference

Total time: ~55 minutes  ·  14 slides  ·  ~4 min per slide average

This is the most technically advanced lesson in Module 2. CUPED is straightforward once the derivation clicks, but the derivation itself requires comfort with variance algebra. Spend time on Slides 3-5 making the formula intuitive. DiD is conceptually simpler but the parallel trends assumption needs careful framing. SRM is the easiest section — use it as a breather between the two heavier topics.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

CUPED and Causal Inference

"You can run A/B tests (2.4) and build regression models (2.5-2.6). Today we combine them. CUPED uses regression to make A/B tests more powerful. DiD uses the same comparison logic to measure causal effects when you cannot randomise. And SRM tells you when your experiment is broken before you waste time analysing it."

For beginners: "CUPED is a trick that lets you detect smaller effects with the same data. DiD is a trick for measuring effects from observational data. SRM is a safety check."
→ "Why do A/B tests fail to reach significance?"
Slide 2~3 minThe problem

The problem: noisy experiments

Start with the practical pain: "You run an A/B test for two weeks. The treatment shows a +3% lift in spending. But $p = 0.12$. Your manager says 'not significant, kill the feature'. But you know the effect is real — there is just too much noise."

"Three options: run longer (expensive), get more users (not possible), or reduce the noise. CUPED does the third — for free."

→ "The formula."
Slide 3~5 minCUPED formula

CUPED: the key formula

This is the critical slide. Walk the derivation slowly:

  1. "We define an adjusted metric: $Y_{\text{adj}} = Y - \theta(X - E[X])$."
  2. "We want to choose $\theta$ to minimise variance of $Y_{\text{adj}}$."
  3. "Take the variance: $\text{Var}(Y) - 2\theta\text{Cov}(Y,X) + \theta^2\text{Var}(X)$."
  4. "This is quadratic in $\theta$ — set derivative to zero."
  5. "Optimal: $\theta^* = \text{Cov}(Y,X)/\text{Var}(X)$."
  6. "Plug back in: $\text{Var}(Y_{\text{adj}}) = \text{Var}(Y)(1 - \rho^2)$."

Walk the table: "$\rho = 0.7$ gives 51% reduction — the same as doubling your sample size."

If the algebra is overwhelming: "The punchline is this: if your pre-experiment spending correlates 0.7 with post-experiment spending, CUPED halves the variance. That is the only number you need to remember."
For experts: "Note that $\theta^*$ is exactly the OLS coefficient from regressing $Y$ on $X$. CUPED is regression adjustment. The adjusted metric is the residual."

When does CUPED work?

Walk the three requirements. Emphasise the warning about post-experiment covariates: "This is the most common mistake. If you adjust for post-experiment purchase frequency, and the treatment increased purchase frequency, you have just adjusted away the treatment effect."

Slides 7-8~5 minSRM

SRM: Sample Ratio Mismatch

"Before you analyse any A/B test, check the split. This takes 30 seconds and can save you from a completely invalid analysis."

Walk the code and the table. "5000/5000 is perfect. 4900/5100 is borderline — probably fine but worth checking. 4800/5200 is a clear SRM — something is broken."

Walk the causes on Slide 8. Bot filtering is the most common in Singapore e-commerce.

→ "What if you cannot randomise at all?"
Slides 9-12~12 minDiD

Difference-in-Differences

Slide 9: Frame the problem. "Singapore's cooling measures apply to everyone. You cannot randomise. But you can compare districts that were more affected (central, high-value) to those less affected (suburban, lower-value)."

Slide 10: Walk the diagram carefully. "Blue line is control. Green line is treated. Before the policy, both trend upward at similar rates. After the policy, the treated group changes slope — the gap between actual and counterfactual is the ATT."

Slide 11: "The parallel trends assumption is everything. If the control group was going to change slope anyway (for reasons unrelated to the policy), DiD gives you the wrong answer."

Slide 12: Walk the code. It is just four means and one subtraction.

For experts: "DiD can be implemented as a regression with group, time, and group-time interaction terms. The interaction coefficient is the ATT. This generalises to multiple periods and multiple groups."
