Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.5 Linear Regression

~60 minutes · 15 slides · ~4 min per slide

This is the lesson that converts M2 from pure statistics into applied modelling. Spend the time on slide 4-5 (the OLS derivation) — that is the only matrix-algebra derivation in MLFP, and learners either get it here or pretend to understand it forever. Do the derivation live. Let them see the gradient calculation.

Slide 1~1 minTitle

Linear Regression

Open: "Regression is the most-used statistical model in the world. It predicts continuous outcomes, and every coefficient comes with a $t$-test you already know. Today we derive it from scratch — the matrix form $(X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$ — and apply it to HDB prices."

→ "The model."
Slide 2~3 minThe model

The model

Write the equation. Walk the pieces. Point out the ceteris paribus interpretation: "Beta_j is what happens to $y$ when $x_j$ goes up by one, holding all the other predictors constant. That 'holding constant' phrase is everything — it's why multivariate regression is more powerful than plotting $y$ against one variable at a time."

Concrete: "If I look at HDB prices vs floor area, I see a strong upward trend. But is it just floor area, or is it that bigger flats happen to be in more expensive towns? Regression lets me ask: what's the effect of area, after I've already accounted for town?"
→ "The picture."
Slide 3~2 minScatter picture

The picture

Walk the scatter. Point at one residual — "this flat is above the line, so the model under-predicted its price. The residual is the vertical distance. OLS finds the line that makes the sum of squared vertical distances as small as possible."

→ "Let's do the algebra."
Slide 4~5 minOLS loss

Deriving OLS — the loss

Stack the equations into matrix form. "One equation per row of data. Stacking gives us $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ where $X$ is $n$ by $(k+1)$, the first column of ones giving us the intercept."

Write the squared-error loss as a dot product. "$(y - X\beta)^\top (y - X\beta)$ is the sum of squared residuals in matrix form. This is what we minimise."

Expand the product. "Three terms: $y^\top y$ doesn't depend on $\beta$, so it drops out of the gradient. The middle term is linear in $\beta$. The last term is quadratic."

→ "Take the gradient, set to zero."
Slide 5~5 minNormal equations

Deriving OLS — setting the gradient to zero

Take the gradient. "Using matrix calculus, the derivative of $-2\beta^\top X^\top y$ with respect to $\beta$ is $-2 X^\top y$. The derivative of $\beta^\top X^\top X \beta$ is $2 X^\top X \beta$."

Set to zero. "$X^\top X \beta = X^\top y$. These are the normal equations. They have this name because the residual vector is normal — meaning perpendicular — to the column space of $X$."

Solve. "If $X^\top X$ is invertible, multiply both sides by its inverse. That gives us the formula that is boxed on every regression textbook on the planet: $\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$."

If beginners freeze on matrix calculus: "You don't need to redo this derivation yourself. What you need to know is: one formula, four symbols. $(X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$. That's OLS. Everything else is bookkeeping."
For experts: mention that this is also the MLE under i.i.d. Normal errors — the Normal log-likelihood leads to the same normal equations. OLS and Normal MLE are the same thing.
→ "Standard errors come from the covariance of $\hat\beta$."
Slide 6~4 minSE and t-stat

Standard errors and $t$-stats

State $\text{Var}(\hat\beta) = \sigma^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}$. "The diagonal gives the variance of each coefficient. Take square roots to get standard errors."

Then the estimator of $\sigma^2$: residual sum of squares divided by $n - k - 1$. "Same Bessel's correction from 2.2. We used $k+1$ degrees of freedom estimating the coefficients, so $n - k - 1$ are free."

Finally the $t$-statistic. "$\hat\beta_j / \text{SE}(\hat\beta_j)$. Exactly the same statistic you built in 2.3 for one-sample tests. Every regression coefficient is a hypothesis test in disguise."

→ "Evaluating the fit."
Slide 7~3 minR²

$R^2$: proportion of variance explained

Walk the formula. "1 minus the fraction of variance the model did not explain. $R^2 = 1$ is perfect, $R^2 = 0$ is no better than guessing the mean."

State the trap: "$R^2$ only increases as you add predictors. Even pure noise predictors push $R^2$ up. This is why we look at adjusted $R^2$, which penalises the extra degrees of freedom you spent."

→ "And the F-stat."
Slide 8~3 minF-stat

$F$-statistic: model vs intercept-only

Walk the formula. "Ratio of explained variance per parameter to unexplained variance per residual degree of freedom. Large $F$ means the model is doing real work beyond the intercept."

Give typical numbers: "For a well-fit HDB model you'll see $F$ in the thousands and a p-value that's numerically zero. That's fine — just means the predictors definitely have explanatory power. The more interesting story is in the individual $t$-statistics."

→ "Categoricals need encoding."
Slide 9~3 minDummy encoding

Categorical variables — dummy encoding

Walk the diagram. "Four flat types become three binary columns. The dropped category — 3-room here — is the base. An observation in the base category has all zeros on the dummies. The coefficient on is_4room is the price difference between 4-room and 3-room, holding other predictors constant."

Warn about the dummy variable trap: "If you include all four dummies and an intercept, $X^\top X$ is singular — you have perfect collinearity. Always drop one category."

→ "Let's do HDB."
Slide 10~3 minHDB load

Worked: HDB load and design matrix

Walk the code. Load, filter, engineer age and log_price. Dummy-encode town. Build $X$ with the intercept column. Print the shape so they see $(n, p)$ where $p = k + 1$.

→ "Solve the normal equations."
Slide 11~3 minSolve

Worked: solve the normal equations

Show the four lines. "This is OLS. Four lines of numpy. np.linalg.solve is more stable than np.linalg.inv followed by a multiply — use solve."

Then the standard errors. Five more lines. "You now have everything you need — coefficients, standard errors, $t$-statistics. No scipy or statsmodels involved. This is OLS from scratch."

→ "Evaluate."
Slide 12~2 minR² and F

Worked: R² and F

Compute the sums of squares and read off $R^2$, adjusted $R^2$, and $F$. Give typical values: 0.82 $R^2$, $F$ in the thousands.

→ "Interpretation."
Slide 13~3 minInterpretation

Interpreting coefficients (log-price)

Walk the table. Each coefficient gets a plain-English interpretation. Emphasise the log trick: "Because we regressed on $\log(\text{price})$ rather than price itself, coefficients are approximately percentages. The 0.012 on floor area means 1.2% per square metre."

Then the ceteris paribus disclaimer. "Every one of these interpretations is conditional on the other predictors being in the model. Drop town, and the area coefficient will shift."

→ "Non-linearity within OLS."
Slide 14~3 minNon-linearity

Non-linearity without leaving OLS

Show the three tricks. Polynomial for curvature. Interaction for context-dependent effects. Log transform for skewed positive outcomes.

Close with: "Linear regression is much more powerful than 'fit a straight line'. As long as the model is linear in the coefficients, you can put anything into $x$ — powers, products, logs — and still use OLS."

→ "Rookie mistakes."
Slide 15~2 minMistakes + recap

Three rookie mistakes + recap

Walk the three. Then the skills list. Tease 2.6: "Linear regression predicts continuous outcomes. What if $y$ is binary — attrition yes/no, click yes/no, ICU mortality yes/no? Linear regression predicts outside 0-1 and is clumsy. Tomorrow: logistic regression."

→ End. 15-min break.
