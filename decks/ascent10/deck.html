The Alignment Problem in One Slide

Foundations

AI alignment means making AI systems do what humans actually want, not just what humans literally said.

Analogy: You tell a robot "make the house clean." It throws everything in the bin — the house is clean, but not what you wanted. Alignment is teaching the robot what "clean" really means to you.

Three approaches, from simplest to most powerful:

  1. Prompting — tell the model what to do (fastest, limited)
  2. Fine-tuning — train the model on your examples (persistent)
  3. Preference alignment — teach the model human values (deepest)
The cleaning robot analogy is powerful for beginners. For experts: this is the reward misspecification problem. The gap between the specified objective and the intended objective is the alignment gap.

Instruction Tuning: The SFT Recipe

Foundations

SFT teaches a model to follow instructions by training on instruction → response pairs:

{
  "instruction": "Summarise this article about HDB prices...",
  "response": "HDB resale prices rose 2.1% in Q4..."
}
  • Training loss computed only on the response (instructions are masked)
  • Data quality matters more than data quantity — 1,000 excellent examples beat 100,000 noisy ones
  • Decontamination: ensure no benchmark data in training set
Emphasise data quality. The Alpaca paper showed 52K examples were enough to make LLaMA follow instructions. Quality of examples is the bottleneck, not quantity.

Catastrophic Forgetting

Foundations

When you fine-tune a model on new data, it can forget what it previously knew:

Analogy: Teaching a chef to make sushi so intensively that they forget how to make pasta. The new skill overwrites the old one.

Mitigations:

  • LoRA: Freeze original weights — forgetting is structurally impossible
  • Data mixing: Include general-purpose data alongside domain data
  • Low learning rate: Gentle updates preserve pre-trained knowledge
  • Early stopping: Monitor validation loss on general benchmarks
This is why LoRA became dominant. By freezing the original weights and only training small adapters, catastrophic forgetting is structurally prevented. The original knowledge is always there.

  • Conversation: "You are a helpful ML tutor..."

10.2 — LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation

Foundations

The Core Idea

Instead of updating all 7 billion parameters, LoRA adds tiny trainable matrices alongside the frozen original weights.

Full Fine-Tuning

  • Update ALL parameters
  • 7B model = 28 GB of gradients
  • Need multiple A100 GPUs
  • Catastrophic forgetting risk

LoRA

  • Freeze the original weights
  • Add tiny trainable matrices
  • 0.1–1% of parameters
  • Single GPU, laptop-viable
Analogy: Full fine-tuning rewrites the entire textbook. LoRA adds sticky notes to the pages that matter.
The sticky notes analogy is excellent for beginners. For experts: LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) exploited the observation that weight updates during fine-tuning have low intrinsic dimensionality.

LoRA: The Decomposition

Theory

In a transformer, each layer has weight matrices W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}. LoRA decomposes the weight update as a low-rank product:

W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + BA
where B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}, A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}, and r \ll d

Only B and A are trained. W_0 stays completely frozen.

Walk through each symbol. W_0 is the original pre-trained weight matrix. Delta W is what we WOULD change during fine-tuning. LoRA says: don't compute Delta W directly — approximate it as the product of two small matrices B and A.

Make the numbers concrete. d=4096 is typical for a 7B model's attention layer. With rank r=16, we go from 16.7 million trainable parameters to just 130 thousand. That's a 128x reduction. This is why LoRA runs on a laptop.

Why Does Low Rank Work?

Theory

Aghajanyan et al. (2020) showed that pre-trained models have a low intrinsic dimensionality:

  • The weight updates during fine-tuning live in a low-dimensional subspace
  • Most of \Delta W's singular values are near zero
  • Rank r = 8 to r = 64 captures >95% of task-relevant information
Connection to M6 (PCA): Just as PCA finds that most variance lives in a few principal components, LoRA exploits the fact that weight updates are approximately low-rank. SVD of \Delta W reveals most singular values cluster near zero.
This connection to PCA is critical. Students who understood M6 dimensionality reduction will immediately grasp why low-rank works. The fine-tuning update matrix is high-dimensional but the "useful" part lives in a tiny subspace.

LoRA: Forward Pass

Theory

During inference, the output for input x is:

h = W_0 x + BAx = (W_0 + BA)x
At deployment: merge W_{\text{merged}} = W_0 + BA — zero inference overhead
Key insight
A is initialised with Kaiming normal; B is initialised to zero. So at the start of training, \Delta W = BA = 0 — the model begins as the original pre-trained model.
Two critical points: (1) B is initialised to zero so training starts from the pre-trained model exactly. (2) At deployment you can merge BA into W_0 — the merged model has the SAME size and speed as the original. No inference overhead at all.

Walk through the derivation step by step. The compression ratio d/2r is the key formula. Higher d (larger models) = MORE savings. Lower r = more compression but less expressiveness. This is the rank-quality tradeoff.

QLoRA: Fine-Tune on Consumer Hardware

Theory

QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) combines three innovations:

  1. 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantisation: Quantise W_0 to 4 bits — 4x memory reduction
  2. Double quantisation: Quantise the quantisation constants too
  3. Paged optimisers: Offload optimiser states to CPU when GPU memory runs low
Result: Fine-tune a 65B parameter model on a single 48GB GPU
QLoRA is what makes fine-tuning accessible to everyone. A 7B model with QLoRA fits on a 6GB GPU. A 13B model fits on a 16GB GPU. A 65B model fits on a 48GB A6000. This is the democratisation of alignment.

NF4: Why Normal Float?

Theory

Pre-trained weights follow an approximately normal distribution. NF4 is information-theoretically optimal for normally-distributed data:

  • Divide the normal distribution into 16 equal-probability bins (4 bits = 16 values)
  • Each bin boundary is at a quantile of N(0, 1)
  • More bins near zero (where most weights cluster) — fewer at the tails
  • Result: minimal information loss compared to uniform INT4
Connection to M2 (Statistics): Quantiles from M2! NF4 uses the exact same concept — dividing a distribution into equal-probability regions.
Beautiful connection to M2 statistics. NF4 is just quantile-based quantisation for normal distributions. Students who understood quantiles in M2 can see exactly why this works — it allocates more precision where the density is highest.

DoRA: Weight-Decomposed LoRA

Advanced

DoRA (Liu et al., 2024) decomposes weight updates into magnitude and direction:

W' = m \cdot \frac{W_0 + BA}{\|W_0 + BA\|_c}
where m is a learned magnitude vector and \|\cdot\|_c is column-wise norm
  • Observation: full fine-tuning changes both magnitude AND direction; LoRA mostly changes direction
  • DoRA adds a learnable magnitude component to close the gap
  • Consistently outperforms LoRA with minimal extra parameters
For advanced students: DoRA's insight is that decomposing weight changes into magnitude and direction components allows the adapter to better approximate full fine-tuning behaviour. The overhead is just one learnable vector per adapted layer.

LoRA+ and QA-LoRA

Advanced

LoRA+

  • Key insight: matrices A and B should have different learning rates
  • B (output projection) benefits from higher LR
  • A (input projection) needs lower LR for stability
  • Typical ratio: \eta_B / \eta_A = 16
  • Consistent improvement over vanilla LoRA

QA-LoRA

  • Quantisation-aware LoRA training
  • Train with quantisation noise in the loop
  • Result: merged model quantises cleanly
  • No quality loss from post-training quantisation
  • Critical for deployment to edge devices
These are refinements that matter in production. LoRA+ is a free lunch — just set different learning rates. QA-LoRA matters when you know you'll quantise the merged model for deployment.

10.3 — Preference Alignment

Foundations

Beyond Instruction Following

SFT teaches a model to follow instructions. But following instructions is not enough:

The problem: "Write a persuasive email to sell this product" — the model follows the instruction, but writes something manipulative and dishonest. It followed the instruction but violated our values.

Preference alignment teaches the model to choose better responses over worse ones — not just to produce any response.

The key distinction: SFT teaches WHAT to do. Preference alignment teaches HOW to do it well. A model can follow instructions perfectly and still be harmful, unhelpful, or dishonest. Alignment closes that gap.

RLHF: The Original Pipeline

Foundations

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) was the first alignment method (Ouyang et al., 2022 / InstructGPT):

1. Pre-train LLM → 2. SFT → 3. Train Reward Model → 4. RL (PPO)
  • Step 3: Humans rank model outputs. Train a reward model to predict human preferences.
  • Step 4: Use PPO (reinforcement learning) to optimise the LLM against the reward model.

RLHF works but is complex: two models (LLM + reward), unstable RL training, expensive human annotation.

RLHF was how ChatGPT was trained. It works, but it's complex and expensive. The reward model can be hacked, PPO training is notoriously unstable, and human preference data is expensive to collect. This motivates DPO.

The Reward Model

Theory

The reward model learns to predict human preferences using the Bradley-Terry model:

P(y_1 \succ y_2 \mid x) = \sigma(r(x, y_1) - r(x, y_2))
where \sigma is the sigmoid and r(x, y) is the learned reward

Given a prompt x with a preferred response y_w and a rejected response y_l:

\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l))\right]
Maximise the gap between preferred and rejected rewards
The Bradley-Terry model is elegant: it says "the probability that y1 is preferred over y2 is a sigmoid of their reward difference." This is the same logistic model from M4, just applied to preferences instead of classification. The loss pushes the reward of preferred responses higher and rejected responses lower.

Reward Hacking

Theory

The RLHF objective includes a KL constraint to prevent the model from deviating too far:

\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_{x,y \sim \pi}\left[r(x,y)\right] - \beta \cdot \text{KL}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}})
\pi = policy (LLM), \pi_{\text{ref}} = reference (SFT model), \beta = constraint strength
Without KL constraint: The model learns to produce gibberish that "hacks" the reward model — it scores high on the proxy reward but produces nonsensical text. This is reward hacking.
Reward hacking is the central failure mode of RLHF. The model finds patterns in the reward model that score high but don't correspond to actual quality. The KL constraint says "you can improve, but don't stray too far from the SFT model." Beta controls how tight this leash is.

DPO: Skip the Reward Model

Foundations

The Key Insight

What if we could align the model without training a reward model and without running RL?

RLHF
  1. Collect preference data
  2. Train reward model
  3. Run PPO against reward
  4. Manage two models in memory
DPO
  1. Collect preference data
  2. Train directly on preferences
  3. Simple classification-like loss
  4. Single model + frozen reference

DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) derives a closed-form solution that eliminates steps 2 and 3.

This is the most elegant result in modern ML alignment. DPO shows that you don't NEED a separate reward model or RL loop. The same result can be achieved with a simple loss function. This is one of those rare cases where a theoretical insight dramatically simplifies engineering.

DPO Derivation: Step 1

Theory

Start from the RLHF objective with KL constraint:

RLHF objective
\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_{x,y \sim \pi}\left[r(x,y)\right] - \beta \cdot \text{KL}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}})
Optimal policy (closed-form)
The solution to this KL-regularised optimisation is: \pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right)
where Z(x) = \sum_y \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp(r(x,y)/\beta) is the normalisation constant
Step 1: we know the optimal policy for any reward function under a KL constraint. This is a classic result from KL-regularised reinforcement learning. The key: the optimal policy is the reference policy scaled by the exponentiated reward. Z(x) normalises it to sum to 1.

DPO Derivation: Step 2

Theory

Rearrange to express the reward in terms of the policy:

Solve for reward
From \pi^*(y|x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp(r(x,y)/\beta), take logs: r(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)
Key observation
The reward is the log-ratio between the optimal policy and the reference policy (plus a constant that depends only on x).
Step 2 is just algebra — rearrange and take logs. The crucial insight is coming: when we substitute this into the Bradley-Terry model, the Z(x) terms will cancel because they don't depend on y.

DPO Derivation: Step 3 (The Magic)

Theory

Substitute into the Bradley-Terry preference model:

Reward difference
r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l) = \beta \left[\log \frac{\pi^*(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \log \frac{\pi^*(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right] + \cancel{\beta \log Z(x)} - \cancel{\beta \log Z(x)}
The Z(x) terms CANCEL! No intractable partition function.
THIS is the magic moment. The Z(x) terms cancel because they appear identically in both r(x,y_w) and r(x,y_l). This is what makes DPO possible — we never need to compute the intractable normalisation constant. Pause here and let students appreciate the elegance.

DPO: The Final Loss

Theory

The DPO loss function — simple, elegant, powerful:

\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta; \pi_{\text{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \left[\log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right]\right)\right]
y_w = preferred response, y_l = rejected response, \beta = deviation strength

This is a binary cross-entropy loss — the same kind of loss you used in M4 for classification!

The final DPO loss is remarkably simple. It's just a sigmoid on the difference of log-ratios. Students who did logistic regression in M4 will recognise the structure. The log-ratio pi/pi_ref is the "implicit reward" — no separate reward model needed.

DPO: Why It's Elegant

Foundations
  • No reward model: The log-ratio \beta \log(\pi/\pi_{\text{ref}}) IS the implicit reward
  • No RL loop: Standard gradient descent on a classification-like loss
  • Stable training: No policy collapse, no reward hacking
  • Simple data format: Triplets of (prompt, chosen, rejected)
  • Reference model: Keep the SFT model frozen as \pi_{\text{ref}}
DPO is the recommended alignment method for most production use cases. Start here unless you have specific reasons for RLHF.
DPO is the default recommendation in 2026. It's simpler to implement, more stable to train, and produces comparable results to RLHF for most tasks. The only cases where RLHF (PPO) still wins are when you need very fine-grained control over the reward signal.

The β Parameter

Theory

\beta controls how far the model can deviate from the reference policy:

βBehaviourUse Case
Small (0.05–0.1)Stay close to reference (conservative)Safety-critical applications
Medium (0.1–0.5)Balanced adaptationMost production use cases
Large (0.5–1.0)More freedom to change (aggressive)Creative / style transfer
Analogy: β is the length of the leash. Small β = short leash (the model stays close to the SFT behaviour). Large β = long leash (the model has more freedom to develop new patterns).
The leash analogy works well. In safety-critical applications (medical, legal), use small beta to prevent the model from drifting too far. For creative applications, larger beta allows more novel outputs.

GRPO: Group Relative Policy Optimisation

Theory

DeepSeek's approach (DeepSeekMath, 2024; DeepSeek-R1, 2025) — no critic, no reward model:

  1. Generate G responses per prompt
  2. Score each with a verifiable reward (e.g., correct math answer)
  3. Use group-relative ranking as the advantage signal
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \text{mean}(\{r_1, \ldots, r_G\})}{\text{std}(\{r_1, \ldots, r_G\})}
Normalise rewards within each group to get advantages

GRPO cuts RL compute by ~50% vs PPO by eliminating the critic network entirely.

GRPO is how DeepSeek-R1 was trained — the model that shocked the industry in January 2025. The key insight: you don't need a learned critic if you can normalise rewards within a group of responses. This is essentially REINFORCE with a group-relative baseline.

GRPO: Connection to REINFORCE

Theory

GRPO is a variant of PPO where the value baseline is replaced by group statistics:

PPO

  • Critic network V_\phi(s) as baseline
  • A_t = r_t + \gamma V(s') - V(s)
  • Two networks to train
  • Complex, memory-heavy

GRPO

  • Group mean reward as baseline
  • \hat{A}_i = (r_i - \bar{r}) / \sigma_r
  • One network to train
  • Simpler, 50% less compute

GRPO still uses PPO's clipped objective — just replaces the advantage computation.

The connection to PPO is important — students will learn PPO in the RL section. GRPO takes PPO's clipped objective but replaces the learned critic with simple group statistics. This is why it's both simpler and cheaper.

RLVR: Verifiable Rewards

Advanced

The biggest shift in 2025: from human preferences to verifiable rewards for reasoning tasks:

  • Math: Is the final answer correct? (binary reward)
  • Code: Does the code pass unit tests? (binary reward)
  • Logic: Is the proof valid? (verifiable)
The modular post-training stack (2025-2026):
1. SFT for instruction following
2. DPO/SimPO/KTO for preference alignment
3. GRPO/DAPO with verifiable rewards for reasoning

DAPO (Decoupled Clip and Dynamic Sampling) improved on GRPO by achieving +3 on AIME 2024 with 50% fewer training steps.

RLVR is the most consequential shift in post-training since DPO. For tasks where you can verify correctness (math, code), you don't need human preferences at all. Just generate many solutions, check which are correct, and use that as the reward signal. This is how reasoning models are trained.

ORPO: One-Step Alignment

Theory

ORPO (Hong et al., 2024) combines SFT and preference alignment in a single training step:

\mathcal{L}_{\text{ORPO}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{OR}}
where \mathcal{L}_{\text{OR}} = -\log \sigma\left(\log \frac{\text{odds}(y_w|x)}{\text{odds}(y_l|x)}\right) is the odds-ratio loss
  • No separate SFT stage — no distribution shift between SFT and alignment
  • No reference model — even simpler than DPO
  • Uses odds ratios instead of log-probabilities

ORPO has zero external dependencies: no reward model, no reference model, no separate SFT.

ORPO is attractive for teams that want to minimise complexity. One training run does both SFT and alignment. The tradeoff: you need the right data format (instruction + chosen + rejected) from the start. Good for greenfield projects; harder to retrofit.

SimPO: Simple Preference Optimisation

Theory

SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) uses average log probability as the implicit reward — no reference model needed:

r_{\text{SimPO}}(x, y) = \frac{1}{|y|} \log \pi_\theta(y \mid x)
Length-normalised log-probability as reward signal
  • Removes the reference model entirely (memory savings)
  • Length normalisation prevents the model from preferring shorter/longer outputs
  • Outperforms DPO by 6.4 points on AlpacaEval 2, 7.5 on Arena-Hard
SimPO's insight is that you don't even need a reference model. The model's own average log-probability is a good enough reward signal. Length normalisation is the key innovation — without it, the model would game the reward by generating very short (high log-prob) responses.

KTO: Kahneman-Tversky Optimisation

Theory

KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) requires only binary labels — no preference pairs needed:

  • Based on prospect theory: losses weigh more than equivalent gains
  • Data format: (prompt, response, good/bad) — no paired comparisons
  • Perfect for production systems where binary feedback is cheap (thumbs up/down)
Practical advantage: Collecting paired preferences is expensive ($5–15 per comparison). Binary feedback ("Was this response helpful? Yes/No") costs almost nothing at scale.
KTO is named after Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky (prospect theory, Nobel Prize). Their insight that "losses loom larger than gains" is encoded directly in the loss function. The practical advantage is enormous: you can align models using simple thumbs up/down feedback from production users.

Constitutional AI & RLAIF

Theory

Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) replaces human feedback with AI feedback:

  1. Define a "constitution" — a set of principles (e.g., "be helpful, harmless, honest")
  2. Model generates responses
  3. Model critiques its own outputs against the constitution
  4. Model revises its responses based on self-critique
  5. Train on the improved outputs (RLAIF = RL from AI Feedback)
Process Reward Models: Instead of rewarding only the final answer, reward intermediate reasoning steps. "Did the model reason correctly at step 3?" This produces more reliable reasoning chains.
Constitutional AI is how you scale alignment without hiring thousands of annotators. The model becomes its own teacher. Process reward models are the frontier — rewarding intermediate reasoning steps, not just final answers. This connects directly to how GRPO trains reasoning models.

10.4 — Reinforcement Learning Fundamentals

Foundations

What is Reinforcement Learning?

An agent interacts with an environment, takes actions, receives rewards, and learns to maximise cumulative reward.

Analogy: Teaching a dog to fetch. You don't show it labelled examples of "correct fetch" (supervised learning). You let it try things and reward good behaviour. Over time, it learns the sequence: see ball → run → pick up → return → treat!
ParadigmDataSignal
SupervisedLabelled (x, y) pairsCorrect answer for each input
UnsupervisedUnlabelled xStructure in data
ReinforcementExperience (s, a, r, s')Delayed reward signal
RL is fundamentally different from supervised learning. No labelled examples. No teacher showing the right answer. Just an agent, an environment, and a reward signal that might be delayed. The dog doesn't get a treat after each step — only after completing the whole sequence.

The MDP Framework

Theory

A Markov Decision Process (MDP) formalises RL with five components:

SymbolComponentExample (Grid World)
\mathcal{S}StatesAll grid positions
\mathcal{A}ActionsUp, Down, Left, Right
P(s' \mid s, a)Transition probabilitiesMove succeeds 80%, slips 20%
R(s, a)Reward function+1 at goal, -1 at trap
\gammaDiscount factor0.99 (value future rewards)

Policy: \pi(a \mid s) = probability of taking action a in state s

Trajectory: s_0, a_0, r_1, s_1, a_1, r_2, \ldots

Introduce each component with the grid world example. States are positions. Actions are moves. Transitions can be stochastic (the agent might slip). Rewards are sparse (only at goal/trap). The policy is what we want to learn — the optimal strategy for navigating the grid.

The Markov Property

Theory
P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t, s_{t-1}, a_{t-1}, \ldots) = P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t)
The future depends only on the present state, not the history
Analogy: In chess, the next position depends only on the current board state and the move you make — not on how you got to this position. The current state contains all the information you need.

If the Markov property doesn't hold, you need history (POMDPs) or memory (LSTMs/Transformers).

The Markov property is the key assumption. In practice, many real-world problems are partially observable (you don't see the full state), but MDPs are the foundation that everything else builds on. Mention that LLMs as RL agents violate this — they use context (history) heavily.

Value Functions

Theory

Two ways to measure "how good" a state or action is:

State-value function
V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r_{t+1} \mid s_0 = s\right]
"How much total reward can I expect starting from state s and following policy \pi?"
Action-value function
Q^\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r_{t+1} \mid s_0 = s, a_0 = a\right]
"How much total reward if I take action a in state s, then follow \pi?"

Connection: V^\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) Q^\pi(s, a)

V tells you "how good is this state?" Q tells you "how good is this action in this state?" The relationship between them is simply: V is the weighted average of Q over all actions, weighted by the policy. These two functions are the foundation of ALL value-based RL.

The Discount Factor γ

Foundations

Why discount future rewards?

  • Uncertainty: The further into the future, the less certain the reward
  • Mathematical: Without discounting, the sum might diverge to infinity
  • Behavioural: Humans discount future rewards too (a dollar today > a dollar next year)
γBehaviourAnalogy
0Only care about immediate rewardImpulsive
0.5Reward halves each stepShort-sighted
0.910-step horizonModerate planning
0.99100-step horizonLong-term strategic
1.0No discounting (episodic only)Infinite patience
The effective horizon is approximately 1/(1-gamma). Gamma=0.99 means the agent effectively plans about 100 steps ahead. Gamma=0.9 means about 10 steps. Use this to give students intuition for what gamma values mean in practice.

Bellman Expectation Equation

Theory

The value of a state can be decomposed recursively:

V^\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s'} P(s' \mid s, a)\left[R(s, a, s') + \gamma V^\pi(s')\right]
Value = expected [immediate reward + discounted future value]
Intuition
"The value of where I am now = what I expect to get this step + the discounted value of where I'll end up." This recursive structure is the foundation of all RL algorithms.
The Bellman equation is THE equation of RL. Walk through it piece by piece. Sum over actions (weighted by policy). Sum over next states (weighted by transition probability). For each: immediate reward plus discounted future value. This recursion is what allows us to compute values efficiently.

Bellman Optimality Equation

Theory

The optimal value function maximises over actions:

V^*(s) = \max_a \sum_{s'} P(s' \mid s, a)\left[R(s, a, s') + \gamma V^*(s')\right]
Replace \sum_a \pi(a|s) with \max_a — always take the best action
Q^*(s, a) = \sum_{s'} P(s' \mid s, a)\left[R(s, a, s') + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a')\right]
The optimal Q-function — the foundation of Q-learning
The difference between expectation and optimality: expectation uses the policy's action distribution, optimality always picks the best action. If we can solve the Bellman optimality equation, we have the optimal policy. The challenge: we usually don't know P(s'|s,a).

Exploration vs Exploitation

Foundations

The fundamental dilemma of RL:

Exploitation

Do what you know works best. Go to the restaurant you love.

Safe, reliable, local optimum

Exploration

Try something new. Visit a restaurant you've never been to.

Risky, potentially better, global optimum

ε-greedy: With probability ε, take a random action (explore). With probability 1-ε, take the best known action (exploit). Start with high ε (explore a lot) and gradually reduce it (exploit more).
The restaurant analogy works perfectly for beginners. Epsilon-greedy is the simplest exploration strategy and the one used in most practical RL. Start with epsilon=1.0 (pure exploration) and decay to epsilon=0.01 (mostly exploitation).

10.5 — Dynamic Programming & Model-Free Methods

Theory

When You Know the World

If we know P(s' \mid s, a) and R(s, a), we can solve the MDP exactly:

  • Policy Evaluation: Given a policy, compute its value function iteratively
  • Policy Improvement: Given a value function, improve the policy greedily
  • Policy Iteration: Alternate evaluation and improvement until convergence
  • Value Iteration: Combine both: V_{k+1}(s) = \max_a \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)[R + \gamma V_k(s')]
Limitation: Requires complete knowledge of the environment (transition probabilities). In practice, we rarely have this. This motivates model-free methods.
Dynamic programming is the foundation but rarely used directly in practice. The important concept is the evaluate-improve cycle. Students should understand WHY we need model-free methods: in the real world, we don't know the transition probabilities.

Monte Carlo Methods

Theory

Learn from complete episodes of experience:

Algorithm
1. Run a complete episode following policy \pi
2. For each state visited, compute the return G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{T-t} \gamma^k r_{t+k+1}
3. Update: V(s) \leftarrow V(s) + \alpha [G_t - V(s)]
  • Advantage: No bootstrapping — uses actual returns, unbiased
  • Limitation: Must wait for the episode to end (high variance)
  • First-visit MC: Only count the first time each state is visited
  • Every-visit MC: Count every visit to each state
Monte Carlo is conceptually the simplest model-free method. Play the whole game, look at the total score, and update your estimates. The problem: high variance (one bad episode can distort estimates) and you must wait until the episode ends (can't learn in the middle of a game).

Temporal Difference Learning

Theory

TD learns from incomplete episodes by bootstrapping:

V(s) \leftarrow V(s) + \alpha \underbrace{\left[r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)\right]}_{\text{TD error } \delta}
Update after EACH step, not after the whole episode

Monte Carlo

Uses actual return G_t

Unbiased, high variance

Must wait for episode end

TD(0)

Uses TD target r + \gamma V(s')

Biased, low variance

Updates every step

TD is the workhorse of RL. The TD error delta = r + gamma*V(s') - V(s) measures "how surprised the agent is." If delta > 0, the outcome was better than expected. If delta < 0, worse. This signal drives learning step by step, no need to wait.

SARSA: On-Policy TD Control

Theory
Q(s, a) \leftarrow Q(s, a) + \alpha \left[r + \gamma Q(s', a') - Q(s, a)\right]
SARSA: State-Action-Reward-State'-Action' — updates using the action actually taken
  • On-policy: Learns the value of the policy being followed (including exploration)
  • If following ε-greedy, SARSA learns the value of ε-greedy behaviour
  • Conservative: accounts for the fact that the agent sometimes explores (takes random actions)
SARSA is "on-policy" because it uses the action a' that the agent actually takes (which might be random due to epsilon-greedy). This makes SARSA conservative — it values states near cliffs lower because it knows the agent might accidentally fall off during exploration.

Q-Learning: Off-Policy TD Control

Theory
Q(s, a) \leftarrow Q(s, a) + \alpha \left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s, a)\right]
Q-Learning: always uses the BEST action for the update (regardless of what was actually done)
SARSA
Uses \gamma Q(s', a')
a' = action actually taken
On-policy (conservative)
Q-Learning
Uses \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a')
Always picks the best
Off-policy (optimistic)

Q-Learning converges to Q^* (optimal) under mild conditions, regardless of exploration policy.

The key difference: SARSA updates using what the agent actually did; Q-Learning updates using what the agent SHOULD have done (the best action). Q-Learning is off-policy because the update rule is independent of the exploration strategy. This is the foundation of DQN.

TD(λ): Blending MC and TD

Advanced

TD(λ) blends Monte Carlo (full return) and TD(0) (one-step) using eligibility traces:

G_t^\lambda = (1 - \lambda) \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda^{n-1} G_t^{(n)}
\lambda = 0 = TD(0), \lambda = 1 = Monte Carlo
λBehaviourBias/Variance
0Pure TD (one-step bootstrap)High bias, low variance
0.5Blend of short and long returnsBalanced
1.0Pure Monte Carlo (full return)Low bias, high variance
TD(lambda) is the general framework that unifies MC and TD. In practice, lambda=0.95 is common — it gives most of the low-variance benefit of TD while still using longer returns for accuracy. This exact idea reappears in PPO as GAE(gamma, lambda).

10.6 — Deep Reinforcement Learning

Foundations

From Tables to Neural Networks

Q-Learning uses a table to store Q(s, a) for every state-action pair. But what if there are millions of states?

Analogy: Imagine memorising the value of every possible chess position. Impossible — there are more positions than atoms in the universe. Instead, learn a function that approximates the value of any position.

Replace the Q-table with a neural network: Q(s, a; \theta)

This is the transition from tabular RL to deep RL. The Q-table works for small problems (grid worlds). For real problems (games, robotics, LLMs), you need function approximation. Neural networks are the dominant choice — hence "deep" RL.

DQN: Deep Q-Network

Theory

Mnih et al. (2015) combined Q-Learning with deep neural networks to play Atari games:

\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta^-) - Q(s, a; \theta)\right)^2\right]
\theta^- = target network (slowly updated copy)

Two innovations that made deep Q-Learning stable:

  1. Experience Replay: Store transitions (s, a, r, s') in a buffer, sample randomly (breaks correlation)
  2. Target Network: Use a separate, slowly-updated network for the target (prevents oscillation)
DQN was the breakthrough that showed RL could work at scale. Experience replay breaks the temporal correlation in sequential data (which would cause neural networks to overfit to recent experience). The target network provides a stable learning target. Together, they make deep Q-Learning converge.

Policy Gradient: Learning the Policy Directly

Theory

Instead of learning Q-values and deriving a policy, directly learn the policy \pi_\theta(a \mid s):

\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot G_t\right]
REINFORCE: increase probability of actions that led to high returns
Intuition
If an action led to a good outcome (G_t > 0), increase its probability. If bad (G_t < 0), decrease it. The gradient \nabla_\theta \log \pi tells us which direction to push the parameters.

Problem: High variance — returns G_t fluctuate wildly between episodes.

REINFORCE is the simplest policy gradient algorithm. The log trick (from M2 probability) converts the gradient of an expectation into an expectation of a gradient — making it computable via sampling. The high variance problem motivates baselines and actor-critic methods.

Variance Reduction: Baselines

Theory

Subtract a baseline b(s_t) from the return to reduce variance:

\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot (G_t - b(s_t))\right]
Any baseline that doesn't depend on a_t preserves the unbiased gradient
  • Best baseline: the value function b(s_t) = V(s_t)
  • G_t - V(s_t) = advantage A(s_t, a_t) — "how much better was this action than average?"
  • This leads to actor-critic methods
The advantage function is the key insight. Instead of asking "was this good?" (absolute return), ask "was this BETTER THAN AVERAGE?" (advantage). This dramatically reduces variance. If all actions get reward 100, subtracting the baseline 100 gives advantages near 0 — no noisy gradient updates.

Actor-Critic

Theory

Two networks working together:

Actor \pi_\theta(a \mid s)

The policy network. Decides which action to take.

Updated by: policy gradient with advantage

Critic V_\phi(s)

The value network. Evaluates how good a state is.

Updated by: TD error minimisation

A(s_t, a_t) \approx r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t)
Advantage = TD error — "how much better than expected?"

A2C (Advantage Actor-Critic) is the synchronous version. A3C adds asynchronous parallel actors.

Actor-Critic combines the best of both worlds: the actor learns the policy, the critic evaluates it. The advantage estimate uses the critic's value function as the baseline. This is the architecture that PPO builds on.

10.7 — PPO: Proximal Policy Optimisation

Foundations

The Dominant RL Algorithm

PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) is used in:

  • RLHF for ChatGPT, Claude, and most aligned LLMs
  • Robotics (OpenAI's dexterous manipulation)
  • Game playing (Dota 2, StarCraft)
  • Scientific discovery (protein folding with AlphaFold)
Why PPO? Simple to implement, stable, and works across many domains. The "Honda Civic of RL" — not the flashiest, but reliably gets you where you need to go.
PPO is the algorithm that made RL practical for production. Before PPO, RL algorithms were notoriously brittle — small hyperparameter changes would cause training to collapse. PPO's clipping mechanism provides a simple guardrail against catastrophic updates.

The Problem PPO Solves

Theory

Policy gradient methods have a critical failure mode: too-large updates destroy the policy.

The cliff problem: One large gradient step takes the policy to a region where it performs terribly. The poor performance generates bad data, which makes the next update even worse. The policy spirals into catastrophe.

TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015) solved this with a KL constraint — but required complex second-order optimisation.

PPO achieves the same stability with a simple clipped objective.

TRPO was the theoretical breakthrough — constrain the KL divergence between old and new policy. But TRPO requires computing the Fisher information matrix (second-order), which is expensive and hard to implement. PPO achieves similar results with first-order methods (standard gradient descent).

PPO: The Probability Ratio

Theory

Define the probability ratio between new and old policy:

r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t \mid s_t)}
r_t = 1 means the action probability hasn't changed
r_tMeaning
< 1New policy is less likely to take this action
= 1No change in action probability
> 1New policy is more likely to take this action

If r_t is far from 1, the policy has changed a lot — potentially dangerously.

The probability ratio is the key concept. When r_t = 1, the policy hasn't changed. When r_t = 2, the action probability has doubled. PPO's clipping mechanism limits HOW FAR r_t can deviate from 1 in a single update. This is the guardrail against catastrophic updates.

PPO: The Clipped Objective

Theory
L^{\text{CLIP}} = \mathbb{E}\left[\min\left(r_t \cdot A_t, \; \text{clip}(r_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \cdot A_t\right)\right]
Take the minimum of unclipped and clipped objective — prevents too-large updates
How clipping works
\text{clip}(r_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) restricts r_t to the range [1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon].
Typical \epsilon = 0.2, so r_t \in [0.8, 1.2] — each action's probability can change by at most 20%.
Analogy: Imagine a speed limiter on a car. You can accelerate, but the limiter prevents you from going above 120 km/h. PPO's clipping is a "speed limiter" for policy updates.
This is the core of PPO. Walk through the min carefully. When the advantage is positive (good action), r_t can increase but is capped at 1+epsilon. When the advantage is negative (bad action), r_t can decrease but is capped at 1-epsilon. The min ensures we never get an overly optimistic gradient.

PPO Clipping: Visual Intuition

Theory

Positive Advantage (A_t > 0)

"This action was good"

  • We want to increase r_t (make action more likely)
  • But clip at 1 + \epsilon — don't increase too much
  • Gradient is zero beyond the clip

Negative Advantage (A_t < 0)

"This action was bad"

  • We want to decrease r_t (make action less likely)
  • But clip at 1 - \epsilon — don't decrease too much
  • Gradient is zero beyond the clip

The result: monotonic improvement in expectation, with bounded policy change per step.

Draw this on the board if possible. The clipped region creates a "trust region" around the current policy. The policy can improve within this region, but cannot jump to a wildly different policy in a single step. This is what makes PPO stable.

GAE: Generalised Advantage Estimation

Theory

PPO uses GAE (Schulman et al., 2015) to compute advantages:

\hat{A}_t^{\text{GAE}(\gamma, \lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}
where \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t) is the TD error
λAdvantage EstimateProperty
0\delta_t (one-step TD)High bias, low variance
0.5Blend of short and long horizonsBalanced
0.95Mostly long-horizonRecommended default
1.0Monte Carlo return minus baselineLow bias, high variance
GAE is essentially TD(lambda) applied to advantage estimation. Lambda=0.95 with gamma=0.99 is the standard. This gives a good balance: mostly long-horizon returns (low bias) with just enough bootstrapping (low variance). Students saw TD(lambda) earlier — GAE is the same idea.

PPO Training Loop

Theory
  1. Collect: Run current policy \pi_{\theta_\text{old}} for T timesteps, storing (s, a, r, s')
  2. Compute: Calculate advantages using GAE(\gamma, \lambda)
  3. Optimise: For K epochs, optimise L^\text{CLIP} using minibatch SGD
  4. Update: \theta_\text{old} \leftarrow \theta, go to step 1

Typical hyperparameters:

ParameterTypical Value
\epsilon (clip range)0.1 – 0.2
\gamma (discount)0.99
\lambda (GAE)0.95
K (epochs per batch)3 – 10
Learning rate3e-4
The PPO loop is simple: collect data, compute advantages, optimise for K epochs, repeat. The key difference from standard policy gradient: we reuse the same batch of data for K epochs (efficiency), but clipping prevents overfitting to that batch. This is why PPO is so sample-efficient.

SAC: Soft Actor-Critic

Theory

SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) adds an entropy bonus to encourage exploration:

J(\pi) = \sum_t \mathbb{E}\left[r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s_t))\right]
Maximise reward AND policy entropy — "be good, but stay uncertain"
  • α (temperature): Controls exploration-exploitation balance
  • Off-policy: Uses experience replay (more sample-efficient than PPO)
  • Continuous actions: Designed for continuous action spaces (robotics)
  • Stable: Automatic temperature tuning prevents entropy collapse
SAC is PPO's main competitor. SAC is off-policy (more sample-efficient) and designed for continuous control (robotics). PPO is on-policy (simpler) and works well for both discrete and continuous actions. SAC is the default for robotics; PPO is the default for LLMs.

Combining Fine-Tuned Models

What if you have multiple fine-tuned models, each good at different things?

Analogy: You have three chefs — one excellent at Italian, one at Japanese, one at French. Model merging combines their skills into a single chef who can cook all three cuisines. No additional training needed.
  • Combine capabilities without retraining
  • Merge multiple LoRA adapters or full models
  • Result: a single model with combined strengths
Model merging has become a mainstream technique in 2025-2026. The key insight: if two models share the same base architecture, their weight differences can be combined in ways that preserve the capabilities of both. This is especially powerful with LoRA adapters.

Merging Methods

Theory
MethodHow It WorksStrength

TIES-Merging: Resolving Conflicts

Theory

When merging models, parameter sign conflicts destroy information. TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) fixes this:

  1. Trim: Remove small delta parameters (below threshold — they're noise)
  2. Elect: For each parameter, majority vote on the sign (positive or negative)
  3. Merge: Average only the parameters that agree on sign
Why sign matters: If Model A says "increase weight by +0.5" and Model B says "decrease by -0.3", naive averaging gives +0.1. But both models were making deliberate changes! TIES keeps the majority direction.
TIES is one of the most important merging innovations. The insight is that sign conflicts are the main source of quality degradation when merging. By resolving conflicts through majority voting, TIES preserves the intentional changes while averaging out noise.

DARE: Drop And Rescale

Theory

DARE (Yu et al., 2024) takes a different approach:

  1. Compute delta: \delta_k = W_k - W_{\text{base}}
  2. Randomly drop a fraction p of delta parameters (set to zero)
  3. Rescale remaining parameters by 1/(1-p)
  4. Merge the rescaled deltas
Insight: Most fine-tuning deltas are redundant. Dropping 90% and rescaling preserves the model's behaviour while reducing interference between merged models. Same dropout intuition from M7 neural networks!
DARE's insight is strikingly similar to dropout — randomly zeroing parameters during merging reduces co-adaptation between the merged models' weight changes. The rescaling ensures the expected magnitude stays the same. Students who understood dropout in M7 will see the parallel immediately.

Evolutionary Model Merging

Advanced

Akiba et al. (2024) used evolutionary algorithms to automatically optimise merge recipes:

  • Use CMA-ES (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy) to search over merge hyperparameters
  • Genome: per-layer merge weights for TIES + DARE
  • Fitness: benchmark performance on target tasks
  • Result: merged models that outperform much larger models
Key result: An evolutionary-merged model from two 7B models outperformed a single 13B model on math and Japanese language tasks. No training compute needed — just merging.
Evolutionary merging is the frontier of model merging. Instead of manually choosing merge weights, use an evolutionary algorithm to search over all possible merge configurations. The result: merged models that consistently outperform their components without any additional training.

Theory

(permissions, budget, clearance) ∩ Task Envelope

Monotonic Tightening

Theory

A child in the hierarchy can never exceed its parent's permissions:

Rule
If R is a child of T, then for all resources x:
\text{access}(R, x) \leq \text{access}(T, x)
  • Department budget = $1,000 → Team budget ≤ $1,000 → Role budget ≤ Team budget
  • Department clearance = Confidential → Team clearance ≤ Confidential
  • Compile-time validation: compile_org() rejects violations

This is the same principle as file system permissions: a subdirectory cannot have more permissions than its parent.

Monotonic tightening is intuitive — you can't give a junior data scientist more budget than the entire ML Platform team. The file system analogy works well. Bias Auditing

Theory

Three key fairness metrics (from M4 model evaluation):

Demographic Parity
P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 1)
Approval rates should be equal across groups
Equalised Odds
P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = y, A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = y, A = 1)
TPR and FPR should be equal across groups
Calibration
P(Y = 1 \mid \hat{P} = p, A = a) = p for all groups
If the model says 70% likely, it should happen 70% of the time for ALL groups
Impossibility theorem: You cannot satisfy all three simultaneously (except in trivial cases). Choose based on your application's fairness priorities.

Differential Privacy

Advanced

Prevent models from memorising individual training examples:

P[\mathcal{M}(D) \in S] \leq e^\epsilon \cdot P[\mathcal{M}(D') \in S] + \delta
\epsilon-differential privacy: adding/removing one person barely changes the output
  • DP-SGD: Add calibrated noise to gradients during training
  • ε (epsilon): Privacy budget — smaller = more private, noisier
  • Trade-off: Privacy vs utility — more noise = worse model quality
  • Composition: Privacy budget depletes with each query/epoch

Practical values: ε = 1–10. Research-grade: ε < 1. The Samsung leak would be prevented by training with DP.

Differential privacy is the mathematical gold standard for privacy in ML. DP-SGD adds noise to each gradient update so the model cannot memorise any individual example. The epsilon parameter controls the privacy-utility tradeoff. In practice, epsilon between 1 and 10 gives reasonable utility with meaningful privacy guarantees.

This eight-step lifecycle is the production deployment pattern that every ML engineer should know. Each step uses a specific Kailash engine. The loop from step 8 back to step 1 represents the continuous improvement cycle. In production, this loop runs automatically with human oversight at the governance gate (step 5).

Debugging Traces

Theory

When an agent fails in production, you need the full trace:

# Every governed agent action is traced
trace = governed.last_trace()

print(trace.steps)
# [
#   Step(action="search", input="Q4 sales",
#        cost=0.02, clearance_check="passed"),
#   Step(action="sql_query", input="SELECT ...",
#        cost=0.0, clearance_check="DENIED",
#        reason="tool not in allowed_tools"),
# ]

print(trace.total_cost)    # $0.02
print(trace.governance)    # GovernanceContext(...)
print(trace.audit_id)      # UUID for regulatory reference

Every trace has a UUID that can be referenced in regulatory audits.

Debugging traces are the operational backbone of governed AI. When something goes wrong, you can reconstruct exactly what the agent did, why it was denied, how much it cost, and who authorised it. This satisfies the EU AI Act's record-keeping requirements and makes incident response straightforward.

Synthesis: If You Followed the Math

Theory
  • LoRA works because weight updates have low intrinsic dimensionality — SVD shows most singular values are near zero
  • DPO is derived from the KL-regularised RL objective. The Z(x) terms cancel in the Bradley-Terry model.
  • PPO's clipped objective provides monotonic improvement by limiting the policy ratio to [1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]
  • GAE(\gamma, \lambda) blends TD and MC returns for advantage estimation
  • The Bellman equations decompose value recursively: value now = reward now + discounted value next
For theory-track students: these are the mathematical foundations that connect M10 to everything before it. LoRA connects to PCA (M6). DPO connects to logistic regression (M4). PPO connects to gradient descent (M4). The Bellman equations are the foundation of all value-based RL.

Synthesis: For the Experts

Advanced
  • GRPO + RLVR is the 2025-2026 frontier: verifiable rewards for reasoning, no critic needed
  • DAPO improves on GRPO with decoupled clipping and dynamic sampling
  • Evolutionary model merging produces better models than any individual fine-tune
  • HQQ quantisation requires no calibration data and is 50x faster than GPTQ
  • Process reward models (rewarding intermediate steps) are the next frontier in alignment
  • The fairness impossibility theorem means you must choose your fairness criterion, not try to satisfy all
For expert-track students: these are the cutting-edge developments that will shape the next 1-2 years of the field. GRPO/DAPO for reasoning, evolutionary merging for model combination, process reward models for verification. These students should be reading the papers directly.
