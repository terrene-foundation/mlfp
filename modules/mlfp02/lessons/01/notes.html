Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.1 Probability and Bayesian Thinking

Total time: ~45 minutes  ·  14 slides  ·  ~3 min per slide average

This is the first lesson of Module 2 and the first time many learners will touch real statistical notation. Expect anxiety around Greek letters and formulas. Your job is to make probability feel mechanical — it is just counting with weights — and Bayes' theorem an inevitable algebraic consequence. The COVID example is the hook.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Probability and Bayesian Thinking

Open by reminding the class what M1 did: explored data, computed summary statistics, drew charts. "M2 asks the harder question: given what you saw, how sure should you be? That is the language of probability." Set expectation: today we derive Bayes' theorem from scratch and use it on HDB prices.

If beginners look nervous about math: "Everything we do today is high-school algebra plus counting. No calculus. The formulas look scary but they unpack into multiplication and division."
→ "From exploration to inference."
Slide 2~2 minMotivation

From exploration to inference

Walk the contrast: M1 was descriptive, M2 is inferential. The key new concept is uncertainty. "When I compute a mean from 12 months of weather, is that mean the truth or a noisy estimate? If I got 12 more months, how different would the number be? Probability is how we quantify those questions."

→ "Probability starts with just a weight on an event."
Slide 3~3 minProbability basics

Probability is a weight on an event

Walk through the axioms without calling them axioms. $P(A)$ is a number between 0 and 1. Total over all outcomes is 1. Joint probability of two things happening; conditional probability restricts attention to a sub-world where $B$ has happened.

Emphasise the multiplication rule: $P(A, B) = P(A \mid B) P(B)$. Say it both ways. This is the only fact you need to derive Bayes.

Analogy for conditional: "Imagine a box of 100 marbles. $P(\text{red})$ is red marbles divided by 100. $P(\text{red} \mid \text{big})$ is red-and-big divided by big — you throw away the small marbles and recount inside the big ones."
→ "Bayes' theorem is what you get when you divide."
Slide 4~3 minDeriving Bayes

Deriving Bayes' theorem

Do the algebra live on the slide. "I have $P(A \mid B) P(B) = P(B \mid A) P(A)$ from the multiplication rule. Divide both sides by $P(B)$, and I get Bayes." Name the four pieces — posterior, likelihood, prior, evidence.

For experts: mention that $P(B)$ is computed by the law of total probability and in continuous Bayes becomes an integral. That integral is why MCMC exists.
→ "Let's see Bayes in action with a COVID test."
Slide 5~3 minCOVID tree

The COVID ART tree

Walk the tree from left to right. Start with the population, 1% infected branch, 99% not infected branch. At each end, branch again by test result. Point at the four leaves — joint probabilities. Make sure learners see that the four probabilities sum to 1.

"Everything we need is on this tree. Bayes' theorem is just: which leaves do you care about, and what's their share of a horizontal cross-section?"

→ "Now the punchline."
Slide 6~4 minCOVID calculation

You test positive — how worried?

Walk through the calculation step by step. $P(+)$ is the total probability of a positive test: add the two positive branches, $0.0090 + 0.0198 = 0.0288$. Then $P(T \mid +)$ is the infected-positive leaf divided by all positive leaves, $0.0090/0.0288 \approx 0.31$.

Pause here. "31 percent. Most doctors say 90. The test is 90% sensitive, but sensitivity is $P(+ \mid T)$ — the exact wrong direction. Bayes is the flip."

If beginners are shocked: "The reason is the base rate. Out of 10,000 people, only 100 are sick. The test catches 90 of them. But among the 9,900 healthy ones, 2% is 198 false positives. So when you see a positive, most of the time it's a false alarm from the huge healthy group."
→ "Probability lives inside distributions."
Slide 7~3 minDistributions

Distributions — the shapes of uncertainty

Show three curves: Normal, Beta, Poisson. Say when you reach for each one. "Normal for measurements. Beta for probabilities. Poisson for counts. Exponential for waiting times — not shown but I'll mention it. Uniform is the 'I know nothing' prior."

Don't get into PDFs and CDFs yet — that's 2.2. Today the picture is enough.

→ "Two numbers summarise any distribution: its mean and its variance."
Slide 8~3 minE[X] and variance

Expected value and variance

Expected value is "probability-weighted average". Walk a simple example — dice: $E[X] = \sum (1/6) \cdot i$ for $i = 1..6$, which is 3.5. Variance measures spread.

Then show the two equivalent forms. Walk the expansion slowly on the slide — this derivation will be invoked in 2.2 (MLE) and 2.5 (R-squared), so it is worth getting right now.

For experts: the computational form $E[X^2] - \mu^2$ is what numpy uses under the hood for one-pass variance.
→ "Integrals are hard — conjugate priors make them unnecessary."
Slide 9~3 minConjugate priors

Conjugate priors — Bayes without integrals

Explain the word: "conjugate" means the prior and posterior belong to the same family. Pick a Beta prior for a proportion, observe Binomial data, your posterior is still Beta. The update rule is elementary: $\alpha += $ successes, $\beta += $ failures.

For Normal–Normal, the update blends prior and data by precision (inverse variance). Precision is how much you "trust" each source. More data = higher precision on the likelihood side = pulls posterior toward sample mean.

→ "Let's do the HDB update."
Slide 10~4 minHDB worked example

HDB 4-room Tampines — the update

Walk the code line by line. Set the prior. Set the data. Compute precisions. Compute posterior mean as precision-weighted average. Compute posterior std as $\sqrt{1 / \text{total precision}}$. Print the three numbers.

Interpret: "The posterior mean $533K$ sits between the prior $480K$ and the sample mean $542K$. It's pulled closer to the sample because $n=30$ gives the data more precision than the prior. The posterior std $9.6K$ is smaller than either input — we're more confident."

If beginners flinch at the code: "Copy it first, understand it second. We will re-derive this update in 2.2 with MLE."
→ "What happens as n grows?"
Slide 11~2 minn grows

As $n$ grows, the data wins

Walk the curves: dashed prior, dashed likelihood, solid posterior. "With small $n$, the posterior is between the two. With large $n$, the posterior collapses onto the likelihood — the prior has almost no effect. This is why Bayesians and frequentists agree in the limit of infinite data."

→ "And that brings up the credible vs confidence question."
Slide 12~3 minCredible vs confidence

Credible vs confidence

This is the trickiest conceptual point of the day. Say it carefully. "A 95% credible interval is a probability statement about the parameter: given our prior and data, there is a 95% chance the true mean is in this range. A 95% confidence interval is a statement about the procedure: if we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the intervals we compute would contain the true mean."

Tell them: "Most people — including many senior analysts — talk about CIs as if they were credible intervals. Today you know the difference. In 2.2 we'll build the frequentist CI explicitly."

If beginners are confused: "Don't worry if it doesn't click yet. Just remember: the Bayesian one says what you want; the frequentist one says what you can prove."
→ "One last concept: sampling bias."
Slide 13~2 minFriendship paradox

The friendship paradox

Deliver as a fun fact: "Your friends, on average, have more friends than you do. It's true. Not because you're unpopular — because of how we sampled." Point at the formula. "The expected degree along a random edge is $E[X^2]/E[X]$, which is always at least $E[X]$ by Cauchy–Schwarz."

Then land the practical point: "Any time you sample through relationships — surveys recruited from existing users, customer feedback forms, social-media polls — you over-sample high-activity users. Bayesian methods don't fix this; you have to model the selection."

→ "Three common mistakes and we close."
Slide 14~3 minRecap + bridge to 2.2

Three rookie mistakes + what next

Walk the three mistakes. Then hit the recap bullets on the next slide. Tease 2.2: "Today we had a prior. Tomorrow we'll ask: what if I only have data and no prior? The answer is maximum likelihood estimation — find the parameter that makes the observed data most likely. Then we'll derive the sample variance formula you already know ($s^2 = \sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 / (n-1)$) and I promised you in 1.1."

→ End of Lesson 2.1. Take 10 minutes.
