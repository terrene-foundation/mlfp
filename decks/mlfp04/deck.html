4.1 Clustering

Discovering group structure without labels

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 1 min. Lesson title slide. "In M3, you predicted outcomes using labelled data. Now: what if there are no labels?"

K-Means: The Workhorse

Foundations
  1. Choose $k$ centroids randomly
  2. Assign each point to the nearest centroid
  3. Recompute centroids as cluster means
  4. Repeat until assignments stabilise
$$J = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{x \in C_k} \|x - \mu_k\|^2$$
K-means objective: minimise within-cluster sum of squared distances
Sensitivity: Random init can converge to local minima. k-means++ selects initial centroids spread apart.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Walk through the algorithm step by step. Draw on whiteboard if possible. Emphasise convergence is guaranteed but to a LOCAL minimum — hence k-means++.

Choosing k: The Elbow Method

Foundations
  • Plot inertia (J) vs number of clusters k
  • Look for the "elbow" where adding clusters gives diminishing returns
  • Subjective — complement with silhouette analysis
Gap statistic: compare within-cluster dispersion to a null reference distribution. More principled than visual elbow.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. The elbow method is the most common but least rigorous. Silhouette and gap statistic are better. Show all three in the exercise.

Hierarchical Clustering

Foundations

Agglomerative (bottom-up)

  1. Each point = own cluster
  2. Merge two closest clusters
  3. Repeat until one cluster remains

Linkage Methods

MethodDistanceEffect
SingleMin pairwiseChains; elongated clusters
CompleteMax pairwiseCompact, spherical
AverageMean pairwiseBalanced
Ward'sVariance increaseMin variance; most popular
Dendrogram: tree diagram showing merge history. Cut at a threshold to obtain clusters. No need to pre-specify k.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Explain each linkage method with a visual analogy. Ward's is usually the best default. Dendrograms are unique to hierarchical clustering — they let you see the full merge history and decide k after the fact.

DBSCAN & HDBSCAN

Foundations

DBSCAN

  • Core point: ≥ minPts within $\varepsilon$
  • Border point: within $\varepsilon$ of core
  • Noise point: neither
  • Finds arbitrary-shaped clusters
  • Does NOT require k

HDBSCAN

  • Hierarchical extension — auto-selects $\varepsilon$
  • Handles varying density
  • Outputs cluster membership probabilities
  • Best general-purpose density-based method
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. DBSCAN's key advantage: it finds clusters of any shape and identifies noise. HDBSCAN removes the pain of tuning epsilon. In practice, HDBSCAN is almost always preferred over DBSCAN.

Spectral Clustering

Theory
  • Construct similarity graph from data
  • Compute graph Laplacian: $L = D - W$
  • Eigendecompose $L$; use bottom-$k$ eigenvectors as features
  • Apply K-means in this new space
When to use: non-convex clusters where K-means and hierarchical fail (e.g., concentric rings, interleaving spirals).
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. This is theory-layer. The key insight: spectral clustering transforms data into a space where K-means works, even on complex geometries. The graph Laplacian captures connectivity, not distance.

Cluster Evaluation: Internal Metrics

Foundations
$$s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i),\; b(i))}$$
Silhouette score: $a(i)$ = avg intra-cluster distance, $b(i)$ = avg nearest-cluster distance. Range: $[-1, 1]$
Davies-Bouldin
$DB = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k} \max_{j \ne i} \frac{s_i + s_j}{d(c_i, c_j)}$
Lower = better. Compares cluster spread to inter-cluster distance.
Calinski-Harabasz
$CH = \frac{SS_B / (k-1)}{SS_W / (n-k)}$
Higher = better. Ratio of between- to within-cluster variance.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. The silhouette score is the most interpretable: +1 means perfectly assigned, 0 means on the boundary, -1 means misassigned. Davies-Bouldin and Calinski-Harabasz are complementary.

4.2 EM Algorithm & Gaussian Mixture Models

Soft clustering — probabilistic assignment to groups

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 1 min. "K-means gives hard assignments: each point belongs to exactly one cluster. What if a customer is 60% loyal and 40% at-risk? GMMs give you that."

Hard vs Soft Clustering

Foundations

K-Means (Hard)

  • Each point → exactly 1 cluster
  • Binary assignment: in or out
  • Cannot express uncertainty

GMM (Soft)

  • Each point → probability per cluster
  • $P(\text{cluster}_k \mid x) = 0.6, 0.3, 0.1$
  • Expresses uncertainty naturally
Soft assignments give $k$ probability features per point — richer than a single cluster label.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. This is a conceptual slide. The business value: if a customer is on the boundary, you might want to treat them differently than someone firmly in a segment.

The EM Algorithm: E-Step

Theory
E-Step: Compute Responsibilities

For each point $x_n$ and cluster $k$, compute the probability that $x_n$ belongs to cluster $k$:

$$r_{nk} = \frac{\pi_k \;\mathcal{N}(x_n \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \pi_j \;\mathcal{N}(x_n \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)}$$
$\pi_k$ = mixing coefficient (prior), $\mathcal{N}$ = Gaussian density
Intuition: "How much does cluster $k$ explain this point?" Responsibilities are normalised — they sum to 1 for each point.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. The E-step is Bayes' theorem applied per point. "Given the current parameters, what is the posterior probability that this point came from cluster k?" The denominator normalises so all responsibilities sum to 1.

The EM Algorithm: M-Step

Theory
M-Step: Update Parameters

Using responsibilities as weights, recompute cluster parameters:

$$\mu_k = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk} \, x_n}{\sum_n r_{nk}} \qquad \pi_k = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk}}{N}$$
Weighted mean (soft K-means) and updated mixing coefficients
Covariance update: $\Sigma_k = \frac{\sum_n r_{nk}(x_n - \mu_k)(x_n - \mu_k)^T}{\sum_n r_{nk}}$
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. "If K-means is hard voting, the M-step is soft voting. Every point contributes to every centroid, weighted by how much it belongs there." The key insight: EM is a generalisation of K-means where assignments are soft.

EM Convergence & Log-Likelihood

Theory
$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \log \left( \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \;\mathcal{N}(x_n \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k) \right)$$
Log-likelihood is guaranteed non-decreasing at every EM iteration
  • EM alternates E-step and M-step until $\mathcal{L}$ converges
  • Converges to a local maximum (not necessarily global)
  • Run multiple random initialisations; pick the best $\mathcal{L}$
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. The monotonic convergence proof is elegant but beyond scope. What matters: each iteration improves or maintains the fit. Like K-means, it can get stuck — hence multiple restarts.

GMM: EM Applied to Gaussians

Foundations
  • A GMM models data as a mixture of $K$ Gaussian distributions
  • Each component has its own $\mu_k$, $\Sigma_k$, and $\pi_k$
  • EM finds the parameters that best explain the data
  • Soft assignments = posterior responsibilities $r_{nk}$
EM as a general template: EM works for ANY latent variable model, not just Gaussians. It is the backbone of LDA (4.6), matrix factorisation (4.7), and the training of neural networks (4.8).
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. This is the big picture: EM is not specific to GMMs. It is a universal optimisation algorithm for models with hidden variables. We will see it again in LDA and implicitly in backpropagation.

Mixture of Experts: Modern Application

Advanced

From GMM to MoE:

  • GMM: each component is a Gaussian → soft assignment
  • MoE: each component is a neural network expert → gating network selects which expert processes which input
  • Modern LLM architectures (e.g., GPT-4 class) use Sparse MoE
Preview (M5+): The gating network is itself a small neural network that learns to route inputs. Same principle as GMM responsibilities — scaled to billions of parameters.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. Advanced layer only. The connection: GMMs assign each point to components probabilistically. MoE assigns each token to expert networks probabilistically. Same math, different scale.

4.3 Dimensionality Reduction

Feature compression — discovering latent axes

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 1 min. "Clustering compresses n data points into k groups. Dimensionality reduction compresses n features into k features. Both are automated feature engineering."

PCA Step 1: Decorrelate

Foundations
  • Rotate axes to align with directions of maximum variance
  • New axes = principal components (PCs)
  • PCs are orthogonal (uncorrelated)
$$\max_{\mathbf{w}} \; \text{Var}(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{X}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|\mathbf{w}\| = 1$$
First PC maximises projected variance; subsequent PCs are orthogonal
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Draw the 2D cloud on the whiteboard. Show how the first PC points along the longest axis. Emphasise: PCA does not discard features yet — it only rotates.

PCA Step 2: Reduce

Foundations
  • Keep top-$k$ components by variance explained
  • Drop the rest — they capture noise
$$\text{Variance explained}_k = \frac{\lambda_k}{\sum_i \lambda_i}$$
$\lambda_k$ = eigenvalue of the $k$-th component. Scree plot shows this.
Scree plot: plot variance explained vs component number. Choose $k$ where the curve flattens.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "The scree plot is to PCA what the elbow plot is to K-means. Look for the bend."

PCA via SVD

Theory
$$\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U} \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^T$$
$\mathbf{U}$ = left singular vectors, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ = singular values, $\mathbf{V}$ = right singular vectors (loadings)
  • Principal components = columns of $\mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$
  • Loadings = columns of $\mathbf{V}$ — interpret what each PC represents
  • SVD is numerically more stable than eigendecomposition
Connection to 4.7: Matrix factorisation for recommenders also uses SVD. PCA factorises $\mathbf{X}$; collaborative filtering factorises the user-item matrix $\mathbf{R}$. Same algebra, different data.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. This slide plants the seed for 4.7. "Remember this SVD equation. When we get to recommender systems, you will see R = U * V^T — the same factorisation idea."

Reconstruction Error

Theory
$$\|\mathbf{X} - \hat{\mathbf{X}}\|^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^{p} \lambda_i$$
Information lost = sum of discarded eigenvalues
Trade-off: fewer components = more compression but more information loss. Choose $k$ to retain (e.g.) 95% of total variance.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "Reconstruction error tells you exactly what you lost. In the exercise, students will plot this trade-off."

Kernel PCA

Theory
  • Standard PCA finds linear directions of max variance
  • Kernel PCA maps data to higher-dimensional space via kernel trick
  • Common kernels: RBF (Gaussian), polynomial
  • Captures nonlinear structure that PCA misses
Limitation: No explicit loadings — harder to interpret. No inverse transform. Use when visualisation or downstream accuracy matters more than interpretability.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. Kernel PCA extends PCA the same way kernel SVM extends linear SVM (M3). The kernel trick avoids computing the high-dimensional mapping explicitly.

t-SNE: Visualisation Specialist

Foundations

How it works

  • Convert distances to probabilities in high-D
  • Find low-D layout with similar probabilities
  • Minimise KL divergence between distributions

Key properties

  • Perplexity (~5-50): effective number of neighbours
  • Preserves local structure well
  • Distorts global structure
  • Non-deterministic
  • NOT for feature extraction (no inverse transform)
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "t-SNE is for LOOKING, not for feeding into downstream models. Distances between clusters in t-SNE plots are NOT meaningful — only local neighbourhood structure is preserved."

UMAP: The Modern Default

Foundations
t-SNEUMAP
SpeedSlow ($O(n^2)$)Fast ($O(n \log n)$)
Global structureDistortedBetter preserved
DeterministicNoYes (with seed)
Feature extractionNo inverseHas inverse — can be used as features
Scalability~10k points~1M+ points
Practical rule: Use UMAP for both visualisation AND as features for downstream models. Use t-SNE only when you want a second opinion on local structure.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. UMAP is the clear winner in practice. It is faster, more scalable, and preserves more global structure. It also appears in BERTopic (4.6) as a preprocessing step.

Intrinsic Dimensionality

Theory
  • How many dimensions does the data actually live in?
  • 100 features might lie on a 5-dimensional manifold
  • Methods: scree plot (variance threshold), correlation dimension, MLE estimators
Practical heuristic: Choose $k$ components that explain $\geq$ 95% cumulative variance. This gives a good estimate of intrinsic dimensionality.

4.4 Anomaly Detection

Outlier discovery — finding what doesn't belong

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 1 min. "Clustering finds groups of similar points. Anomaly detection finds points that belong to no group."

Statistical Outlier Detection

Foundations
Z-Score Method
$z = \frac{x - \bar{x}}{s}$
Outlier if $|z| > 3$ (3-sigma rule)
IQR Method
$\text{Outlier if } x < Q_1 - 1.5 \cdot IQR$ $\text{or } x > Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot IQR$
Robust to non-normal distributions
Z-score assumes normality. IQR is more robust for skewed data. Winsorisation: cap outliers at a percentile instead of removing them.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. These are from M2. Quick recap. The key limitation: they are univariate. Real anomalies are often multivariate — a point can be normal in every single feature but anomalous in the combination.

Isolation Forest

Foundations
  • Build random trees that isolate points by random splits
  • Anomalies are isolated in fewer splits (shorter path length)
  • Normal points require many splits to isolate
$$s(x, n) = 2^{-\frac{E[h(x)]}{c(n)}}$$
$h(x)$ = path length, $c(n)$ = avg path length in a BST of $n$ points. Score near 1 = anomaly.
Why it works: anomalies are few and different. Few = isolated quickly by random splits. Different = separated early in the tree.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Isolation Forest is the most practical ML anomaly detector. It is fast, handles high dimensions, and requires no assumptions about data distribution.

Local Outlier Factor (LOF)

Theory
$$\text{LOF}_k(x) = \frac{\sum_{o \in N_k(x)} \frac{\text{lrd}_k(o)}{\text{lrd}_k(x)}}{|N_k(x)|}$$
Ratio of local reachability densities. LOF $\gg 1$ = anomaly (much less dense than neighbours)
  • Density-based: compares a point's density to its neighbours'
  • Detects anomalies in varying-density data where Isolation Forest may struggle
  • More expensive: $O(n^2)$ for naive implementation
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. LOF is the density-based complement to Isolation Forest. Use LOF when the data has regions of very different density.

Score Blending: Combining Detectors

Foundations
  • No single detector is best everywhere
  • Normalise scores to [0, 1] across detectors
  • Combine: simple average, weighted average, or max-vote
  • Blended scores are more robust than any individual method
Pattern: Z-score catches distributional outliers. Isolation Forest catches multivariate anomalies. LOF catches density anomalies. Together: comprehensive coverage.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "This is ensemble thinking applied to anomaly detection. The same principle from M3's ensemble methods, but for unsupervised outlier detection."

4.5 Market Basket Analysis

Co-occurrence pattern discovery — finding what appears together

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 1 min. "Clustering finds groups of similar POINTS. Association rules find groups of items that appear TOGETHER in transactions."

Association Rules: The Metrics

Foundations

Support: $\;\text{supp}(X) = \frac{\text{count}(X)}{\text{total transactions}}$

Confidence: $\;\text{conf}(X \to Y) = \frac{\text{supp}(X \cup Y)}{\text{supp}(X)}$

Lift: $\;\text{lift}(X \to Y) = \frac{\text{conf}(X \to Y)}{\text{supp}(Y)}$

Lift > 1 = positive association. Lift = 1 = independent. Lift < 1 = negative association.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Walk through each metric. Support = how common. Confidence = if you bought X, what is the probability you also bought Y. Lift = is the co-occurrence more than chance?

Apriori vs FP-Growth

Foundations

StrategyGenerate candidates, prune by min supportCompress into FP-tree, mine directly
PassesMultiple passes over dataTwo passes (build tree, mine)
SpeedSlow for large itemsetsMuch faster — no candidate generation
MemoryMany candidates in memoryCompact tree representation
In practice: FP-Growth is preferred for large datasets. Apriori is pedagogically simpler.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. Apriori is the classic algorithm; FP-Growth is the practical one. Students should understand both but use FP-Growth in production.

From Rules to Features

Theory

Association rules are not a dead end. They connect forward in two ways:

  1. Rules as features: "bought_bread_AND_butter" becomes a binary feature for a supervised model (M3)
  2. Co-occurrence → embeddings: "finding patterns in co-occurrence data" is exactly what collaborative filtering does with latent factors (4.7)
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. This is the design-level connection. Association rules discover explicit co-occurrence patterns. Collaborative filtering discovers latent patterns. Neural embeddings generalise both.

4.6 NLP — Text to Topics

Foundations

TF-IDF: Derivation

Theory
$$\text{tfidf}(t, d) = \text{tf}(t, d) \times \log\frac{N}{\text{df}(t)}$$
$\text{tf}$ = term frequency in document $d$, $\text{df}$ = number of documents containing $t$, $N$ = total documents

Why it works

  • "the" appears everywhere → low IDF → low weight
  • "kailash" appears in few docs → high IDF → high weight

BM25 extension

  • Adds term frequency saturation
  • Normalises by document length
  • Standard in search engines
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Walk through the derivation. "Common words get low weight because they appear in many documents. Rare, topic-specific words get high weight."

LDA: Latent Dirichlet Allocation

Theory
$$P(\text{word} \mid \text{doc}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} P(\text{word} \mid \text{topic}_k) \cdot P(\text{topic}_k \mid \text{doc})$$
Each document = mixture of topics. Each topic = distribution over words.
  • Generative model: assumes documents were produced by sampling topics, then words
  • Inference: reverse the process to discover topics
  • Output: per-document topic proportions — these are features
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. "LDA is like a probabilistic clustering of documents. Each document belongs to multiple topics with probabilities — exactly like GMM's soft assignments."

NMF & BERTopic

Foundations

NMF

  • Factor TF-IDF matrix: $V \approx WH$
  • $W$ = document-topic, $H$ = topic-word
  • Non-negative constraint → additive, interpretable parts

BERTopic

  • Transformer embeddings + UMAP + HDBSCAN + c-TF-IDF
  • Captures semantic meaning, not just word counts
  • State of the art for topic modelling
Method pipeline: BERTopic uses UMAP (4.3) for dimensionality reduction and HDBSCAN (4.1) for clustering. Everything connects.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. NMF is matrix factorisation applied to text — same idea as PCA and collaborative filtering. BERTopic combines everything from this module into one pipeline.

Topic Coherence: Evaluating Quality

Theory
$$\text{NPMI}(w_i, w_j) = \frac{\log \frac{P(w_i, w_j)}{P(w_i) \cdot P(w_j)}}{-\log P(w_i, w_j)}$$
Normalised Pointwise Mutual Information. Range: $[-1, 1]$. Higher = more coherent topic.
  • NPMI: do topic words co-occur more than chance? (external corpus)
  • UMass: do topic words co-occur in the training corpus?
  • Complement with human evaluation: do topic labels make sense?
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "NPMI is the silhouette score of topic modelling — it tells you whether the top words in a topic actually belong together."

Matrix Factorisation: The Core Idea

Theory
$$\min_{U,V} \sum_{(u,i) \in \text{observed}} (r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^T \mathbf{v}_i)^2 + \lambda(\|\mathbf{u}_u\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2)$$
Factorise $R \approx UV^T$. $U$ = user embeddings, $V$ = item embeddings. $\lambda$ = regularisation.
  • Each user = a vector of $k$ latent preferences
  • Each item = a vector of $k$ latent attributes
  • Rating = dot product of user and item vectors
Connection to PCA (4.3): PCA factorises $X = U\Sigma V^T$. Collaborative filtering factorises $R = UV^T$. Same algebra, different data.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. This is the key mathematical slide. "We saw SVD in PCA. Here we apply the same idea to a user-item matrix. The latent vectors are LEARNED by minimising prediction error."

ALS: Alternating Least Squares

Theory
Alternating optimisation
  1. Fix $V$, solve for $U$: $\;\mathbf{U} = (\mathbf{V}^T\mathbf{V} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{R}^T$
  2. Fix $U$, solve for $V$: $\;\mathbf{V} = (\mathbf{U}^T\mathbf{U} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{U}^T \mathbf{R}$
  3. Repeat until convergence
Why ALS? The joint problem $\min_{U,V}$ is non-convex. But fixing one and solving the other is a least-squares problem (convex, closed-form). Alternating makes progress each step.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. ALS is to matrix factorisation what EM is to GMMs — alternate between two steps, each of which is easy, until convergence.

Forward Pass

Foundations
$$\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b} \qquad \mathbf{a} = f(\mathbf{z})$$
$\mathbf{W}$ = weight matrix, $\mathbf{b}$ = bias vector, $f$ = activation function, $\mathbf{a}$ = activations (output of this layer)
Example: one hidden layer

Input $\mathbf{x}$ → $\mathbf{z}_1 = \mathbf{W}_1\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1$ → $\mathbf{a}_1 = f(\mathbf{z}_1)$ → $\mathbf{z}_2 = \mathbf{W}_2\mathbf{a}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2$ → $\hat{y} = \mathbf{z}_2$

MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. "The forward pass is just matrix multiplication, addition, and a nonlinear function. That is all a neural network does — over and over, layer by layer."

Linear Regression as a Neural Network

Foundations
  • Zero hidden layers: $\hat{y} = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} + b$ — this IS linear regression (M3)
  • Add one hidden layer with activation → model learns nonlinear features
  • Add more hidden layers → model "writes its own parametric function"
Universal approximation theorem: A network with 2+ hidden layers and nonlinear activations can represent ANY continuous function (given enough neurons).
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. "Linear regression is a neural network with no hidden layers. Everything you learned in M3 is a special case. Adding hidden layers adds automatic feature engineering."

Loss Functions & Gradient Descent

Foundations
$$\text{SSE} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 \qquad \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} = -x \cdot (y - \hat{y})$$
Gradient Descent Update
$$w_{\text{new}} = w_{\text{old}} - \eta \cdot \frac{\partial J}{\partial w}$$

$\eta$ = learning rate. Iterate until loss converges.

Intuition: The gradient points "uphill." Subtracting it moves "downhill" towards lower loss.
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. Walk through a concrete example with HDB price data: start with random weight, compute error, compute gradient, update weight. Show the error decreasing. "This is the engine of all deep learning."

Backpropagation: Chain Rule Through Layers

Theory
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_2} \cdot \frac{\partial a_2}{\partial z_2} \cdot \frac{\partial z_2}{\partial a_1} \cdot \frac{\partial a_1}{\partial z_1} \cdot \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial w_1}$$
Chain rule: propagate error gradients backward through each layer
  • Forward pass: compute predictions
  • Compute loss: predicted vs actual
  • Backward pass: compute gradient of loss w.r.t. each weight
  • Update: adjust all weights in the direction that reduces loss
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 3 min. "Backpropagation is just the chain rule from calculus, applied layer by layer. Each layer passes its local gradient backward. The product gives the total gradient."

Dropout & Batch Normalisation

Foundations

Dropout

  • Randomly zero out neurons during training
  • Rate: typically 0.1–0.5
  • Prevents co-adaptation
  • Off during inference

Batch Normalisation

$y = \gamma \frac{x - \mu_B}{\sqrt{\sigma_B^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta$
  • Normalise layer inputs to $\mu=0$, $\sigma=1$
  • Stabilises training, enables higher LR
  • Layer norm for transformers (M5)
MLFP Module 4 — Terrene Open Academy
Timing: 2 min. Dropout is like training an ensemble of sub-networks. Batch norm shifts and scales activations to keep gradients healthy.

Learning Rate Schedules & Early Stopping

Foundations

LR Schedules

  • Step decay: halve LR every N epochs
  • Cosine annealing: smooth decay to 0
  • Warmup + cosine: increase then decay
  • One-cycle: increase then decrease
  • ReduceLROnPlateau: reduce when val loss stalls

Gradient Clipping & Early Stopping

  • Gradient clipping: cap gradient magnitude to prevent explosion
  • Early stopping: stop when validation loss increases for $p$ consecutive epochs (patience)
  • Both prevent wasted compute and overfitting
