Lesson 2.5 Linear Regression

Linear regression is the most widely used statistical model in the world, and the first predictive model you will build. This lesson derives OLS from first principles — squared-error loss, normal equations, the closed- form $\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$ — and connects regression to the $t$-tests, $R^2$, and $F$-statistics you already know. Regression coefficients are sample statistics, and the inference tools from 2.3 apply directly.

The model

Linear regression models a continuous outcome $y$ as a linear combination of predictors $x_1, \ldots, x_k$ plus an error term:

$$y_i \;=\; \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{ik} + \varepsilon_i$$

The intercept $\beta_0$ is the expected $y$ when all predictors are zero. Each slope $\beta_j$ is the expected change in $y$ for a one-unit increase in $x_j$ holding all other predictors constant — the "ceteris paribus" interpretation. The errors $\varepsilon_i$ are the unobserved residuals that capture everything the linear combination does not explain.

Deriving OLS

Stack the $n$ observations into vector/matrix form. Let $y$ be the $n$-vector of outcomes, $X$ the $n \times (k+1)$ design matrix (first column all ones for the intercept), $\beta$ the $(k+1)$-vector of coefficients, and $\varepsilon$ the $n$-vector of errors:

$$y \;=\; X\beta + \varepsilon$$

We want to pick $\beta$ to minimise the sum of squared residuals:

$$S(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2 \;=\; (y - X\beta)^\top (y - X\beta)$$
$S(\beta)$ — the loss vector form makes differentiation clean

Expand the loss:

$$S(\beta) \;=\; y^\top y - 2\beta^\top X^\top y + \beta^\top X^\top X \beta$$

Take the gradient with respect to $\beta$ and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial \beta} \;=\; -2 X^\top y + 2 X^\top X \beta \;=\; 0$$

Solving for $\beta$ gives the normal equations:

$$X^\top X \beta \;=\; X^\top y$$

If $X^\top X$ is invertible (which requires the columns of $X$ to be linearly independent — no perfect multicollinearity), we can solve:

$$\boxed{\;\hat\beta \;=\; (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\;}$$
$(X^\top X)^{-1}$ — normal matrix inverse, $(k+1)\times(k+1)$ $X^\top y$ — score vector, $(k+1) \times 1$
🔵 Theory: why squared error?

Squared-error loss is not arbitrary. If the errors $\varepsilon_i$ are i.i.d. Normal with mean 0 and variance $\sigma^2$, the MLE of $\beta$ (from Lesson 2.2) is exactly the OLS estimator. Writing out the log-likelihood of a Normal model and differentiating gives back the normal equations. So OLS is not just "intuitive" — it is MLE under the Normal assumption.

Fitted values and residuals

The fitted values are $\hat y = X\hat\beta$. The residuals are $e = y - \hat y$. The residual vector is always orthogonal to the column space of $X$ (a consequence of the normal equations: $X^\top e = 0$), which is why OLS is sometimes described as "projecting $y$ onto the column space of $X$".

Standard errors and the $t$-statistic

Under the Normal-error assumption, $\hat\beta$ itself is Normally distributed around the true $\beta$ with covariance matrix:

$$\text{Var}(\hat\beta) \;=\; \sigma^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}$$

We estimate $\sigma^2$ from the residuals:

$$\hat\sigma^2 \;=\; \frac{e^\top e}{n - k - 1}$$

Note the $n - k - 1$ divisor — one degree of freedom is consumed by each of the $k + 1$ coefficients, leaving $n - k - 1$. This is the general form of Bessel's correction. The standard error of coefficient $\beta_j$ is the square root of the $j$-th diagonal entry of $\hat\sigma^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}$.

The $t$-statistic for testing $H_0: \beta_j = 0$ is:

$$t_j \;=\; \frac{\hat\beta_j}{\text{SE}(\hat\beta_j)}$$
same $t$-statistic as Lesson 2.3 distributed $t_{n-k-1}$ under $H_0$

$R^2$ and the $F$-statistic

$R^2$ is the proportion of variance in $y$ explained by the model. Define:

  • $\text{SS}_{\text{tot}} = \sum_i (y_i - \bar y)^2$ — total sum of squares (spread of $y$).
  • $\text{SS}_{\text{res}} = \sum_i (y_i - \hat y_i)^2$ — residual sum of squares (what the model did not explain).
  • $\text{SS}_{\text{reg}} = \sum_i (\hat y_i - \bar y)^2$ — regression sum of squares (what the model did explain).

These satisfy $\text{SS}_{\text{tot}} = \text{SS}_{\text{reg}} + \text{SS}_{\text{res}}$ (because the residuals are orthogonal to the fitted values).

$$R^2 \;=\; 1 - \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{res}}}{\text{SS}_{\text{tot}}} \;=\; \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{reg}}}{\text{SS}_{\text{tot}}}$$
$R^2 = 1$ — perfect fit $R^2 = 0$ — no better than the mean

$R^2$ only goes up when you add predictors — even useless ones. The adjusted $R^2$ penalises model complexity:

$$R^2_{\text{adj}} \;=\; 1 - (1 - R^2) \cdot \frac{n - 1}{n - k - 1}$$

The $F$-statistic

The $F$-test compares your full model to the intercept-only model (just the mean of $y$). It asks: "does the model do better than guessing $\bar y$ for everyone?"

$$F \;=\; \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{reg}} / k}{\text{SS}_{\text{res}} / (n - k - 1)} \;=\; \frac{R^2 / k}{(1 - R^2) / (n - k - 1)}$$
numerator — variance explained per parameter denominator — unexplained variance per residual df

Under $H_0$ (all slopes are zero), $F$ follows an $F_{k, n-k-1}$ distribution. Large $F$ → small p-value → reject the intercept-only model in favour of yours.

Categorical variables and dummy encoding

Linear regression takes numbers. A categorical variable like flat_type ∈ {3-room, 4-room, 5-room, executive} must be encoded as numbers. The standard method is dummy encoding: pick one category as the base (often the most common) and create a binary column for each other category.

The coefficient on is_4room is the expected difference in the outcome between a 4-room flat and a 3-room flat (the base), holding all other predictors constant. You must always identify the base category when interpreting dummy coefficients.

Non-linearity: polynomials and interactions

"Linear" in linear regression means linear in the coefficients — not linear in the predictors. You can add polynomial and interaction terms and still use OLS:

  • Polynomial terms: add $x^2$ (or $x^3$) to capture curvature. HDB floor area has diminishing returns — the second square metre is more valuable than the hundredth.
  • Interaction terms: add $x_1 \cdot x_2$ to capture effects that depend on each other. A 30-year-old flat in Bishan may not behave like a 30-year-old flat in Yishun.
  • Log transform: take $\log y$ when $y$ is strictly positive and right-skewed. Coefficients then have a multiplicative interpretation: a one-unit change in $x$ multiplies $y$ by $\exp(\hat\beta)$.

Worked Example: HDB Price Model

📝 Goal

Build a model for Singapore HDB 4-room resale prices using floor area, age of the flat, and town as predictors. Interpret coefficients, test significance, evaluate fit, and add polynomial features.

Step 1 — Load and prepare. 
import polars as pl
import numpy as np
from shared import MLFPDataLoader

df = (
    MLFPDataLoader().load("mlfp02", "hdb_resale.parquet")
    .filter(pl.col("flat_type") == "4 ROOM")
    .filter(pl.col("town").is_in(["TAMPINES", "BISHAN", "YISHUN", "JURONG WEST"]))
    .with_columns(
        age=(pl.col("year") - pl.col("lease_commence_date")),
        log_price=pl.col("resale_price").log(),
    )
)
print(df.shape)
Step 2 — Build the design matrix with dummy encoding. 
df_encoded = df.to_dummies(["town"], drop_first=True)
y = df_encoded["log_price"].to_numpy()
X_cols = ["floor_area_sqm", "age", "town_BISHAN", "town_TAMPINES", "town_YISHUN"]
X = df_encoded.select(X_cols).to_numpy()
X = np.column_stack([np.ones(len(X)), X])   # intercept
print(X.shape)
Step 3 — Solve the normal equations. 
XtX = X.T @ X
Xty = X.T @ y
beta_hat = np.linalg.solve(XtX, Xty)

names = ["intercept"] + X_cols
for name, b in zip(names, beta_hat):
    print(f"{name:>22s}: {b:+.4f}")

Typical output: the intercept is the baseline log-price; the coefficient on floor_area_sqm is how log-price changes per additional square metre; the town dummies are the premia over Jurong West (the dropped category).

Step 4 — Standard errors and $t$-statistics. 
n, p = X.shape
y_hat = X @ beta_hat
e = y - y_hat
sigma2 = (e @ e) / (n - p)
cov_beta = sigma2 * np.linalg.inv(XtX)
se = np.sqrt(np.diag(cov_beta))
t_stats = beta_hat / se

from scipy import stats as scipy_stats
p_vals = 2 * (1 - scipy_stats.t.cdf(np.abs(t_stats), df=n - p))

for name, b, s, t, pv in zip(names, beta_hat, se, t_stats, p_vals):
    print(f"{name:>22s}  b={b:+.4f}  se={s:.4f}  t={t:+6.2f}  p={pv:.3g}")
Step 5 — $R^2$, adjusted $R^2$, and $F$-statistic. 
ss_tot = ((y - y.mean())**2).sum()
ss_res = (e**2).sum()
ss_reg = ss_tot - ss_res
r2     = 1 - ss_res / ss_tot
r2_adj = 1 - (1 - r2) * (n - 1) / (n - p)
k      = p - 1
F      = (ss_reg / k) / (ss_res / (n - p))
p_F    = 1 - scipy_stats.f.cdf(F, k, n - p)

print(f"R²       = {r2:.4f}")
print(f"R²_adj   = {r2_adj:.4f}")
print(f"F        = {F:.1f} on ({k}, {n - p}) df, p = {p_F:.3g}")
Step 6 — Add a polynomial term. 
X_poly = np.column_stack([X, df_encoded["age"].to_numpy() ** 2])
beta_poly = np.linalg.solve(X_poly.T @ X_poly, X_poly.T @ y)
e_poly = y - X_poly @ beta_poly
r2_poly = 1 - (e_poly @ e_poly) / ss_tot
print(f"R² with age²: {r2_poly:.4f}")

The squared age term captures the curvature — prices drop with age but at a decreasing rate as flats approach lease decay. Adjusted $R^2$ will usually improve (but only slightly).

Interpreting coefficients

CoefficientInterpretation
$\hat\beta_{\text{floor\_area}} = 0.012$Each additional $m^2$ increases $\log$ price by 0.012 → $\approx 1.2\%$ increase in price
$\hat\beta_{\text{age}} = -0.004$Each additional year of age decreases $\log$ price by 0.004 → $\approx 0.4\%$ decrease
$\hat\beta_{\text{BISHAN}} = 0.18$Bishan commands an 18% premium over Jurong West (the base), holding area and age constant

The ceteris paribus phrasing ("holding other predictors constant") is essential. The coefficient on age is the marginal effect of age after the model has already absorbed the variation due to area and town. Changing the set of predictors can change every coefficient.

Common mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Interpreting in isolation

"The coefficient on age is $-0.004$, so a 1-year older flat is 0.4% cheaper." Only in the presence of the other predictors. If you drop town from the model, the age coefficient changes — it picks up the correlation between age and town.

⚠ Mistake 2: Chasing high $R^2$ by adding predictors

$R^2$ only goes up as you add predictors. Always report adjusted $R^2$. And use cross-validation (M3) to check whether the extra variables actually help on unseen data.

⚠ Mistake 3: Multicollinearity

If two predictors are highly correlated, $X^\top X$ becomes nearly singular, the inverse blows up, and standard errors explode. Diagnose with variance inflation factors (VIF). Fix by dropping one, combining them, or using ridge regression (M3).

Split the data 80/20. Fit on train. Compute $R^2$ on train and test separately. Then add ten random noise predictors to the model. Watch train $R^2$ climb while test $R^2$ falls. This is overfitting — and the cue for cross-validation in M3.
