Lesson 3.3 The Complete Supervised Model Zoo

In the next 90 minutes you meet the five classical model families that together cover almost every non-deep supervised problem: Support Vector Machines, K-Nearest Neighbors, Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, and Random Forests. For each one you will see the geometry, derive the objective, compute the key quantity by hand, and train it on the same e-commerce churn dataset so the comparison is fair. By the end, you will know which model to reach for given a data shape — and why.

Why This Matters

Boosting and deep learning dominate competition leaderboards — but 60% of production ML in Singapore enterprises still runs on the five families below. They are fast, debuggable, interpretable, and most of them work with almost no hyperparameter tuning. Knowing their geometry is the difference between picking the right tool in 30 seconds and wasting a day on a model that was wrong for the data shape.

Support Vector Machines — Maximum-Margin Classification

Imagine two clouds of points in feature space, one class positive and the other negative. Infinitely many lines separate them. SVM picks the line that is as far as possible from the nearest point of either class — the maximum-margin hyperplane.

The primal optimisation problem

Let the labels be $y_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. A classifier $f(x) = \operatorname{sign}(\boldsymbol{w}^\top x + b)$ correctly classifies $x_i$ iff $y_i(\boldsymbol{w}^\top x_i + b) \ge 1$ (the $\ge 1$ rescales the margin to unit width). The distance from a point to the hyperplane is $|\boldsymbol{w}^\top x + b| / \|\boldsymbol{w}\|$, so the total margin width is $2 / \|\boldsymbol{w}\|$. Maximising the margin is therefore minimising $\|\boldsymbol{w}\|$:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{w}, b} \tfrac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 \quad \text{subject to}\quad y_i(\boldsymbol{w}^\top x_i + b) \ge 1 \;\forall i$$

This is a convex quadratic program. Real data is not linearly separable, so we introduce slack variables $\xi_i \ge 0$ and a penalty $C$:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{w}, b, \boldsymbol{\xi}} \tfrac{1}{2}\|\boldsymbol{w}\|^2 + C\sum_i \xi_i \quad \text{s.t.}\quad y_i(\boldsymbol{w}^\top x_i + b) \ge 1 - \xi_i,\; \xi_i \ge 0$$

$C$ is the bias-variance dial of SVMs. Small $C$ accepts many margin violations and tolerates noise (high bias, low variance). Large $C$ insists on separating everything (low bias, high variance).

The dual and the kernel trick

Applying Lagrange duality transforms the primal into:

$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_i \alpha_i - \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j \langle x_i, x_j\rangle \quad\text{s.t.}\quad 0 \le \alpha_i \le C,\; \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$$

Two beautiful things happen here. First, the inputs only enter through inner products $\langle x_i, x_j\rangle$. Second, at the optimum, most $\alpha_i$ are zero — only the support vectors have $\alpha_i > 0$. This is why SVMs are memory-efficient at inference: you only store the support vectors.

The kernel trick is the punchline. Replace $\langle x_i, x_j\rangle$ with a kernel function $K(x_i, x_j) = \langle \phi(x_i), \phi(x_j)\rangle$ where $\phi$ maps inputs into a higher-dimensional feature space. The SVM learns a linear classifier in $\phi$-space, which is a non-linear classifier in $x$-space — and you never compute $\phi$ explicitly. Common kernels:

Kernel$K(x, x')$Use for
Linear$x^\top x'$text, high-dim sparse
Polynomial$(x^\top x' + c)^d$interactions up to degree $d$
RBF (Gaussian)$\exp(-\gamma \|x - x'\|^2)$smooth non-linear boundaries
Sigmoid$\tanh(\kappa x^\top x' + c)$neural-net-like
🔵 Theory — Mercer's condition

Not every function is a valid kernel. Mercer's theorem says $K$ is a kernel iff the Gram matrix $K_{ij} = K(x_i, x_j)$ is symmetric and positive semi-definite for every finite sample. All the kernels in the table above satisfy this; custom kernels need verification.

K-Nearest Neighbors — Instance-Based Learning

KNN is almost embarrassingly simple: to classify a new point, find the $k$ closest training points and vote. No training, no parameters, no assumptions about the shape of the boundary.

The classifier is:

$$\hat{y}(x) \;=\; \text{majority vote}\;\{\,y_i : x_i \in \text{$k$-nearest neighbors of $x$}\,\}$$

The distance metric matters. Euclidean ($L_2$) is the default. Manhattan ($L_1$) is more robust to outliers. Cosine is scale-invariant and standard for text embeddings. Minkowski generalises both.

The curse of dimensionality

KNN works well in low dimensions and falls apart in high dimensions for a profound reason: in high-dim space, everything is equidistant from everything else. Consider $n$ points drawn i.i.d. uniformly from the unit cube $[0,1]^d$. As $d$ grows, the ratio of the distance to the nearest neighbor over the distance to the farthest neighbor approaches 1:

$$\lim_{d \to \infty} \frac{\text{dist}_{\max}(x) - \text{dist}_{\min}(x)}{\text{dist}_{\min}(x)} \to 0$$

Intuitively: with 10 features, the nearest neighbor is meaningfully closer than the average. With 500 features, the nearest neighbor is almost the same distance as a random point — the "nearest" is no longer informative. This is the distance concentration phenomenon, and it is why KNN is a bad default for high-dimensional data.

🟢 Foundations — Choosing k

Small $k$ (1–3) gives low bias and very high variance — every individual training point directly affects a nearby prediction. Large $k$ smooths the boundary, reducing variance but adding bias because faraway irrelevant points get a vote. The usual default is $k = \lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor$, tuned by CV. Tie-breaking with odd $k$ avoids the coin-flip at the boundary.

Naive Bayes — Probability with an Independence Assumption

Naive Bayes applies Bayes' theorem to classification and makes one extremely strong simplifying assumption: every feature is conditionally independent of every other given the class label. That assumption is almost never true. And yet the classifier often works — especially for text.

$$P(y \mid x_1, \ldots, x_d) \;=\; \frac{P(y)\,\prod_{j=1}^{d} P(x_j \mid y)}{P(x_1, \ldots, x_d)}$$

At prediction time, we pick the class that maximises the numerator:

$$\hat{y} \;=\; \arg\max_y P(y) \prod_{j=1}^{d} P(x_j \mid y)$$

Three variants cover most use cases:

  • GaussianNB: $P(x_j \mid y)$ is a normal distribution with class-specific mean and variance. Use for continuous features.
  • MultinomialNB: $P(x_j \mid y)$ follows a multinomial distribution. Use for count features like bag-of-words in text classification.
  • BernoulliNB: binary features, $P(x_j \mid y)$ is a Bernoulli. Use for presence/absence indicators.

The independence assumption is patently wrong for almost any real problem. But the classifier can still pick the right class even with biased probability estimates — rank-correct is easier than probability-correct. For spam filtering, sentiment classification, and anything with a "fast baseline" slot, MultinomialNB is a great choice.

Decision Trees — Recursive Partitioning

A decision tree splits the feature space into rectangles with axis-aligned cuts. At each internal node, it picks a feature and a threshold that best separates the classes; at each leaf, it predicts the majority class of the training points that landed there.

Splitting criteria: Gini and Entropy

What does "best separates" mean? Two quantities measure how impure a node is — how mixed its class labels are.

Gini impurity: the probability that two points drawn at random from the node belong to different classes.

$$G \;=\; 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k^2$$

For a perfectly pure node (all one class), $G = 0$. For a perfectly balanced binary node ($p_1 = p_2 = 0.5$), $G = 0.5$.

Entropy from information theory:

$$H \;=\; -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2 p_k$$

Again zero for pure, maximised (at $\log_2 K$) for uniform. In practice Gini and entropy give nearly identical trees; Gini is slightly faster because it avoids the logarithm.

Information gain

A tree picks the split that maximises the drop in impurity from the parent to the weighted children:

$$\text{IG} \;=\; H(\text{parent}) - \sum_{j=\text{child}} \frac{n_j}{n_{\text{parent}}} \, H(\text{child}_j)$$

where $n_j$ is the number of samples in child $j$. The weights are the child sample sizes as a fraction of the parent. The algorithm evaluates every feature and every candidate threshold, picks the split with the largest $\text{IG}$, and recurses.

📝 Compute Gini by hand

A node has 10 churned, 6 retained customers. Compute its Gini.

p_churn   = 10 / 16 = 0.625
p_retain  =  6 / 16 = 0.375
Gini      = 1 - (0.625² + 0.375²)
         = 1 - (0.3906 + 0.1406)
         = 0.469

A candidate split produces two children: {8 churn, 1 retain} and {2 churn, 5 retain}. Compute their Gini values and the weighted average.

G(left)   = 1 - (8/9)² - (1/9)²     = 0.198
G(right)  = 1 - (2/7)² - (5/7)²     = 0.408
Gweighted = (9/16)(0.198) + (7/16)(0.408) = 0.290
IG        = 0.469 - 0.290 = 0.179

The split reduces impurity by 0.179. The algorithm will try every other candidate split and keep whichever has the largest IG.

Pruning and overfitting

An unconstrained tree can keep splitting until every leaf is pure — perfectly memorising the training data. This is maximum variance. Two control strategies:

  • Pre-pruning: stop growing early via max_depth, min_samples_split, min_samples_leaf, or min_impurity_decrease. Simpler, but you have to pick the thresholds.
  • Post-pruning: grow the full tree, then prune weakest branches using cost-complexity pruning with parameter $\alpha$.

Random Forests — Bagging Many Trees

A single decision tree has high variance. A random forest reduces that variance by training many trees and averaging their predictions. Two tricks independently inject randomness:

  1. Bootstrap sampling: each tree is trained on a resample of the training data (with replacement) of the same size as the original.
  2. Random feature subsets: at each split, the tree considers only a random subset of features (typically $\sqrt{d}$ for classification).

The predictions of the $T$ trees are averaged (regression) or voted (classification). Because the trees are decorrelated by the two tricks, the variance of the ensemble is much lower than the variance of any individual tree, while the bias is unchanged. This is the bagging recipe in its cleanest form.

Out-of-bag error: 1/e and free cross-validation

Here is a beautiful fact. When you draw a bootstrap sample of size $n$ from $n$ points, each individual point has probability $(1 - 1/n)^n$ of not being drawn. For large $n$:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \;=\; \frac{1}{e} \;\approx\; 0.368$$

So roughly 36.8% of the training data is not used to build any given tree. Those out-of-bag (OOB) samples form a built-in validation set for each tree. Averaging the OOB predictions across all trees gives an unbiased estimate of generalisation error — no explicit cross-validation needed, no data wasted on a holdout.

🔵 Theory — Why 1/e?

Take the natural log: $n \log(1 - 1/n) \approx n \cdot (-1/n) = -1$ as $n \to \infty$ (using $\log(1 - x) \approx -x$ for small $x$). So $(1 - 1/n)^n \to e^{-1}$. A standard beautiful limit; it is the reason random forests get a free validation set.

Worked Example: Five Models on E-Commerce Churn

📝 Goal

Load the e-commerce customer dataset (~5000 customers, binary churn target, mixed behavioural and demographic features). Train all five model families with identical preprocessing and identical CV splits, compare accuracy, F1, and training time, and discuss which model to recommend.

Step 1 — Unified preprocessing. 
import polars as pl
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_validate, StratifiedKFold
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from kailash_ml.interop import to_sklearn_input

from shared import MLFPDataLoader
df = MLFPDataLoader().load("mlfp03", "ecommerce_customers.parquet")
X, y = to_sklearn_input(df, target="churned", standardise=True)
cv = StratifiedKFold(5, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
Step 2 — Train all five. 
from sklearn.svm import SVC
from sklearn.neighbors import KNeighborsClassifier
from sklearn.naive_bayes import GaussianNB
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeClassifier
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
import time

models = {
    "SVM (RBF)":     SVC(kernel="rbf", C=1.0, probability=True),
    "KNN (k=25)":    KNeighborsClassifier(n_neighbors=25),
    "GaussianNB":    GaussianNB(),
    "Decision Tree": DecisionTreeClassifier(max_depth=6, random_state=0),
    "Random Forest": RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200,
                                            oob_score=True, random_state=0),
}

results = {}
for name, model in models.items():
    t0 = time.time()
    cv_res = cross_validate(model, X, y, cv=cv,
        scoring=["accuracy", "f1", "roc_auc"], n_jobs=-1)
    results[name] = {
        "acc":      cv_res["test_accuracy"].mean(),
        "f1":       cv_res["test_f1"].mean(),
        "auc":      cv_res["test_roc_auc"].mean(),
        "train_s":  time.time() - t0,
    }
Step 3 — Build the comparison table. 
print(f"{'Model':<16}{'Acc':>8}{'F1':>8}{'AUC':>8}{'Time(s)':>10}")
for name, r in results.items():
    print(f"{name:<16}{r['acc']:>8.3f}{r['f1']:>8.3f}{r['auc']:>8.3f}{r['train_s']:>10.2f}")

Typical output on this dataset:

Model              Acc     F1     AUC   Time(s)
SVM (RBF)        0.812  0.708  0.874      8.4
KNN (k=25)       0.776  0.648  0.831      0.6
GaussianNB       0.731  0.604  0.796      0.1
Decision Tree    0.788  0.688  0.811      0.3
Random Forest    0.834  0.744  0.894      2.1

Random forest wins on AUC by a small margin over SVM and is 4× faster. Naive Bayes is eight times slower (on a small model, "slow" means "tenth of a second"), but its accuracy is weak because the independence assumption is violated by these correlated behavioural features. KNN lags because the dataset has 30+ features and distance concentration starts biting. For production, the recommendation is random forest — best metric, cheap inference, interpretable via feature importance.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1 — Unscaled features with SVM or KNN

Both SVM and KNN depend on distances. A feature measured in dollars (values 0–10000) will dominate one measured in years (values 0–100). Always standardise before using these models. Trees and NB are scale-invariant and do not need it.

⚠ Mistake 2 — Assuming "more neighbours is always better" in KNN

As $k \to n$ the prediction collapses to the majority class overall. At that point you are not doing KNN; you are reporting the base rate. The sweet spot is small enough to be informative, large enough to be stable — usually tuned by CV.

⚠ Mistake 3 — Reporting accuracy on imbalanced data

If 90% of customers churn, a model that predicts "churn" for everybody scores 90% accuracy. That is meaningless. Use F1, AUC, and precision/recall — Lesson 3.5 explains this trap in full detail.
