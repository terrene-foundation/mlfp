The Supervised Learning Setup

Foundations

We have a dataset of examples where we know the right answer:

\{(\mathbf{x}_1, y_1), (\mathbf{x}_2, y_2), \ldots, (\mathbf{x}_n, y_n)\}
Each x is a vector of features (e.g., floor area, storey, town). Each y is the target (e.g., price).

Our goal: learn a function f such that f(\mathbf{x}) \approx y for new, unseen examples.

Key word: "unseen." We do not care how well the model fits the training data. We care how well it predicts on data it has never seen before. This is called generalisation.
Emphasise generalisation. This is the single most important concept in ML. Everything else — regularisation, cross-validation, ensembles — exists to improve generalisation.

Training, Validation, and Test Splits

Foundations

Why you cannot grade your own homework.

Training Set (~60-70%)

The model learns from this data. It adjusts parameters to fit these examples.

Validation Set (~15-20%)

Used to tune hyperparameters and select between models. The model never trains on this.

Test Set (~15-20%)

Final exam. Touched once at the very end. If you peek, you cheat.

Analogy: Training = studying, Validation = practice exams, Test = the real exam. If you memorise the practice exams, you are overfitting to them.
Three-way split. Common beginner mistake: using the test set to tune hyperparameters. That turns the test set into a validation set, and you have no honest measure of generalisation.

Overfitting vs Underfitting

Foundations

Memorising the training data instead of learning the pattern.

Underfitting

Model too simple. Straight line through a curve.

High training error, high test error.

Just Right

Model captures the real pattern. Smooth curve following the trend.

Low training error, low test error.

Overfitting

Model memorises noise. Wiggly line through every point.

Low training error, high test error.

Key insight: More complex is not always better. The goal is to learn the signal, not the noise.

No Free Lunch Theorem

Foundations

There is no single best model for all problems.

Wolpert (1996): Averaged over all possible data distributions, every learning algorithm performs equally well. Any model that excels on one type of problem must perform worse on another.

What this means in practice:

  • There is no "always use XGBoost" rule
  • You must try multiple models and evaluate on your specific data
  • Domain knowledge helps you choose good candidates
This is why Kailash's TrainingPipeline and AutoMLEngine exist — to systematically compare models on your data rather than guessing.
Brief slide. The point is humility: no one model wins everywhere. This motivates why we will learn many models today.

Bias and Variance — The Darts Analogy

Foundations

Bias = how far the average prediction is from the truth. Variance = how spread out predictions are across different training sets.

The darts analogy is the most intuitive way to explain bias-variance. Point at each board. "Would you trust a model that is consistently wrong? What about one that is right on average but wildly unpredictable?"

Bias-Variance Decomposition — Setup

Theory

We want to predict target y = f(x) + \varepsilon, where \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).

Our model \hat{f}(x) is trained on a random sample D. The expected prediction error at a point x:

\text{EPE}(x) = \mathbb{E}_D\!\left[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right]
Expected Prediction Error: average squared error over all possible training sets D, and noise in y
Transition to the math. Tell beginners: "Follow the shape of the argument, not every symbol. The conclusion is what matters." Tell experts: "Watch how the cross terms vanish — that is the key step."

Derivation — Step 1: Expand

Theory
Substitute y = f(x) + ε
\mathbb{E}\!\left[(f(x) + \varepsilon - \hat{f}(x))^2\right]
Add and subtract E[f̂(x)]
= \mathbb{E}\!\left[\bigl(\underbrace{f(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)]}_{\text{bias term}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] - \hat{f}(x)}_{\text{variance term}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon}_{\text{noise}}\bigr)^2\right]
The "add and subtract the mean" trick. This is the same technique used in ANOVA. The key insight: we decompose the error into three independent pieces.

Derivation — Step 2: Cross Terms Vanish

Theory
Expand the square — three squared terms + three cross terms

The cross terms vanish because:

  • \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 0 — noise is zero-mean, independent of everything
  • \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}] - \hat{f}] = 0 — by definition of expectation
  • Bias term f(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] is a constant w.r.t. D
\text{EPE}(x) = \underbrace{\bigl(f(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)]\bigr)^2}_{\color{#DC2626}{\textbf{Bias}^2}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat{f}(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)])^2\right]}_{\color{#2563EB}{\textbf{Variance}}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\color{#6B7280}{\textbf{Irreducible Noise}}}
This is the key result. Colour-coded for clarity. Point to each term: "Red is how wrong you are on average. Blue is how unstable you are. Grey is the floor — you cannot beat the noise in the data."

The Three Terms Explained

Theory
TermWhat It MeasuresYou Control It By
Bias² How far your model's average prediction is from the truth More complex model (more features, deeper trees)
Variance How much predictions change across different training sets Simpler model, more data, regularisation, ensembles

The trade-off: Reducing bias (more complex model) typically increases variance. Reducing variance (simpler model) typically increases bias. The art of ML is finding the sweet spot.
Summary table. Key message: you cannot minimise both simultaneously. Every model choice is a position on the bias-variance curve.

Double Descent — When the Trade-off Breaks

Advanced

Classical wisdom: more parameters = more overfitting. But modern deep learning uses billions of parameters and generalises well. Why?

Double descent (Belkin et al., 2019): Test error first decreases (underfitting), then increases at the interpolation threshold (classical overfitting), then decreases again in the over-parameterised regime.

In the over-parameterised regime, the model fits training data exactly while finding a smooth, low-norm solution — benign overfitting. A January 2026 study (arXiv:2601.08316) showed this occurs even epoch-wise: models re-generalise after initial overfitting.

This does NOT mean "just make everything bigger." It means the classical bias-variance picture is incomplete for modern architectures.

Advanced slide. For beginners: "The simple version is — sometimes bigger models work better than medium ones, which surprised everyone." For experts: discuss the interpolation threshold and how it relates to the number of parameters equaling the number of training points.

Regularisation — Why Constrain Your Model?

Foundations

Regularisation adds a penalty for complexity. It tells the model: "I will accept slightly worse training accuracy if your weights stay small and reasonable."

Analogy: When writing an essay, your teacher says "explain it in 500 words, not 5000." The constraint forces clarity — you focus on the important ideas.
\text{Loss}_{\text{reg}} = \underbrace{\text{Loss}_{\text{data}}}_{\text{fit the data}} + \underbrace{\lambda \cdot R(\mathbf{w})}_{\text{penalty for large weights}}
λ controls how strongly we penalise — too high = underfitting, too low = overfitting
Regularisation is the primary tool for controlling the bias-variance trade-off. Lambda is the dial: turn it up for more bias/less variance, turn it down for less bias/more variance.

L1 vs L2 — Diamond vs Circle

Theory

L2 (Ridge) — Circle

R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_j w_j^2 \leq t
  • Constraint region is a sphere
  • Shrinks all weights toward zero, rarely to zero
  • All features kept, large weights penalised
  • Closed-form: \hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X} + \lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}

L1 (Lasso) — Diamond

R(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_j |w_j| \leq t
  • Constraint region is a diamond (corners on axes)
  • Loss contours hit corners — some w_j = 0
  • Produces sparse solutions (automatic feature selection)
  • No closed-form; solved by coordinate descent
The geometric intuition is crucial. Draw the diamond and circle on the whiteboard if possible. Show how the elliptical loss contours are more likely to touch the diamond at a corner (axis), setting a weight to exactly zero.

ElasticNet — Best of Both

Theory
R(\mathbf{w}) = \alpha \sum_j |w_j| + (1-\alpha) \sum_j w_j^2
α = 1 is pure Lasso, α = 0 is pure Ridge. In between: sparse but stable.

Why not just Lasso?

  • With correlated features, Lasso picks one arbitrarily and drops the rest
  • ElasticNet groups correlated features together
  • More stable feature selection across bootstrap samples
Zou & Hastie (2005): ElasticNet has the "grouping effect" — strongly correlated predictors tend to be in or out together, unlike Lasso which picks one arbitrarily.
Practical advice: when in doubt between Ridge and Lasso, use ElasticNet with alpha=0.5 as a starting point. Let cross-validation find the optimal alpha.

Bayesian Interpretation of Regularisation

Theory

Regularisation is equivalent to placing a prior on the weights and doing MAP estimation:

\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{w}} \;\underbrace{\log p(D|\mathbf{w})}_{\text{log-likelihood}} + \underbrace{\log p(\mathbf{w})}_{\text{log-prior}}
RegulariserPriorEffect
L2 (Ridge)w_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2) — GaussianShrink, none eliminated
L1 (Lasso)w_j \sim \text{Laplace}(0, b) — sharp peak at 0Shrink, many driven to exactly 0
ElasticNetMixture of Gaussian + LaplaceGrouped sparsity
The Laplace prior has heavier tails but a sharper peak at zero than the Gaussian — this is why L1 induces sparsity.
This connects regularisation to Bayesian thinking from M1. The strength of the prior (1/lambda) controls how much we trust the data vs our prior belief that weights should be small.

Simple Linear Regression

Foundations

The simplest supervised model: draw a straight line through the data.

\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x
w_0 = intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis), w_1 = slope (how much y changes when x increases by 1)
Example: If floor area increases by 1 sqm, HDB price increases by ~$5,200. That $5,200 is the slope w_1.
Start simple. Everyone understands a straight line. The question is: how do we find the BEST line?

OLS — Finding the Best Line

Theory

Ordinary Least Squares: find the line that minimises the sum of squared residuals.

\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \mathbf{x}_i^T \mathbf{w})^2 = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2
Take the gradient and set to zero
\nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2 = -2\mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}) = 0
Solve for w — the Normal Equations
\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}
The normal equations. Point out that this requires X^T X to be invertible — if features are perfectly correlated, it is not. That is one reason for regularisation (Ridge adds lambda*I to make it always invertible).

Geometric Interpretation of OLS

Theory

The columns of \mathbf{X} span a subspace. The OLS solution \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\hat{\mathbf{w}} is the orthogonal projection of \mathbf{y} onto this subspace.

The residual vector \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}} is perpendicular to the column space of X. This is why it is called "least squares" — the shortest distance from a point to a plane is always perpendicular.

The hat matrix \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T is the projection matrix: \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{y}.

For the geometrically-minded: OLS is just a projection. This perspective makes everything else (Ridge = projection with shrinkage, diagnostics = leverage from H) much more intuitive.

Multiple Linear Regression — Assumptions

Theory

y = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} requires four assumptions for valid inference:

Important nuance: these assumptions matter for INFERENCE (p-values, confidence intervals) more than for PREDICTION. If you just want to predict, violations are less critical — but if you want to understand which features matter, you need them.

  • Bad: Funnel shape = heteroscedasticity

Diagnostic slides. These are the "health check" for your linear model. Cook's distance is the most important — it tells you which individual data points are pulling the model toward them.

Ridge Regression — Full Derivation

Theory
\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|^2
Take derivative, set to zero
-2\mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}) + 2\lambda\mathbf{w} = 0
Solve for w
\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{ridge}} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X} + \lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}
Key insight: Adding \lambda\mathbf{I} guarantees invertibility even when features are correlated. Ridge is always well-defined. OLS is not.
Compare with the OLS normal equations — the only difference is the lambda*I term. This is what makes Ridge numerically stable and why it works when you have more features than observations.

Ridge — Effect on Coefficients

Theory

Using the SVD \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{V}^T, Ridge coefficients become:

\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{ridge}} = \sum_{j=1}^{p} \frac{d_j^2}{d_j^2 + \lambda} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{u}_j^T \mathbf{y}}{d_j} \mathbf{v}_j
Each component is shrunk by d_j^2 / (d_j^2 + λ). Small singular values (weak directions) are shrunk most.

Ridge shrinks coefficients along directions of low variance in the data — exactly where overfitting is most likely.

This is the SVD perspective on Ridge. The shrinkage factor d^2/(d^2+lambda) is close to 1 for large singular values (important directions) and close to 0 for small singular values (noisy directions). Ridge surgically targets the most overfit-prone directions.

Lasso — Sparsity and Feature Selection

Theory
\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \frac{1}{2n}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \lambda\sum_j |w_j|

No closed-form solution (the absolute value is not differentiable at zero). Solved by coordinate descent:

Soft-thresholding operator for each coordinate j
w_j \leftarrow S_\lambda\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_{ij}(y_i - \hat{y}_i^{(-j)})\right) \quad \text{where} \quad S_\lambda(z) = \text{sign}(z)\max(|z|-\lambda, 0)

The soft-thresholding operator sets coefficients to exactly zero when their contribution is smaller than \lambda. This is automatic feature selection.

The soft-thresholding operator is the mathematical reason Lasso produces zeros. If a feature's signal is weaker than the penalty lambda, it gets killed entirely. This is fundamentally different from Ridge, which only shrinks.

Polynomial Regression

Foundations

Linear regression can model non-linear relationships by creating polynomial features:

\hat{y} = w_0 + w_1 x + w_2 x^2 + w_3 x^3 + \cdots + w_d x^d
Still "linear" in the weights w — just non-linear in x. This is a feature engineering trick.
Danger: High-degree polynomials overfit badly at the edges of the data range. Degree 2-3 is usually enough. Always pair with regularisation (Ridge or Lasso on polynomial features).
Key insight: polynomial regression IS linear regression — we just engineer new features (x^2, x^3). This is the same principle behind kernel methods. "Linear in the parameters, non-linear in the features."

Logistic Regression — From Regression to Classification

Foundations

For binary classification, we need outputs between 0 and 1 (probabilities). Linear regression gives unbounded outputs. Solution: the sigmoid function.

p(y=1|\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}}}
The sigmoid squashes any real number into (0, 1)
Analogy: Think of a dimmer switch. The linear combination \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x} is the raw signal (can be any number). The sigmoid is the dimmer that converts it to a brightness between 0% and 100%.
Despite its name, logistic regression is a CLASSIFICATION algorithm. It outputs a probability. The decision boundary is where the probability equals 0.5 (equivalently, where w^T x = 0).

Logistic Regression — Log-Odds and MLE

Theory

The sigmoid is equivalent to modelling the log-odds as a linear function:

\log\frac{p}{1-p} = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}
Log-odds (logit) is linear in x. Every unit increase in x_j changes log-odds by w_j.
Maximum Likelihood Estimation
\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{w}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[y_i \log p_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right]

This is the cross-entropy loss (negated). No closed-form solution — solved by gradient descent or Newton's method (IRLS).

Two views of the same model: sigmoid on the probability, or linear on the log-odds. The log-odds view is more interpretable: exp(w_j) is the odds ratio — how much the odds change per unit increase in feature j.

Cross-Entropy Loss — Why Not MSE?

Theory

MSE for Classification

L = (y - p)^2
  • Non-convex with sigmoid — many local minima
  • Gradient vanishes when p is near 0 or 1
  • Slow, unreliable convergence

Cross-Entropy

L = -[y\log p + (1-y)\log(1-p)]
  • Convex with sigmoid — single global minimum
  • Gradient is large when prediction is wrong
  • Fast, reliable convergence
A common question. MSE seems natural but it creates a non-convex landscape. Cross-entropy is the "natural" loss for probabilities — it comes from maximum likelihood and information theory.

Multinomial Logistic Regression

Theory

For K > 2 classes, use the softmax function:

p(y=k|\mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{\mathbf{w}_k^T\mathbf{x}}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{\mathbf{w}_j^T\mathbf{x}}}
Each class k has its own weight vector w_k. Softmax normalises to sum to 1.

One-vs-Rest (OvR)

  • Train K binary classifiers
  • Each: "class k vs everything else"
  • Probabilities may not sum to 1

Multinomial (Softmax)

  • Train one K-class model
  • All classes compete simultaneously
  • Probabilities guaranteed to sum to 1
When to use which: OvR is simpler and works well when classes are well-separated. Multinomial is theoretically correct and better when classes overlap. In practice, the difference is often small.

Regularised Logistic Regression

Theory
\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left[y_i\log p_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right] + \lambda R(\mathbf{w})
R(w)NameWhen to Use
\|\mathbf{w}\|^2L2 (default in most libraries)All features relevant, prevent large weights
\|\mathbf{w}\|_1L1Feature selection needed, many irrelevant features
\alpha\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 + (1-\alpha)\|\mathbf{w}\|^2ElasticNetCorrelated features, grouped sparsity
Always regularise logistic regression. Without regularisation, if classes are linearly separable, the weights diverge to infinity (the sigmoid becomes a step function). Regularisation prevents this.
Practical tip: most libraries default to L2 regularisation. In scikit-learn, the C parameter is 1/lambda (smaller C = more regularisation). Always tune C via cross-validation.

Ordinal Regression

Advanced

When categories have a natural order (e.g., satisfaction: Poor < Fair < Good < Excellent).

p(y \leq k|\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\theta_k - \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x})
Cumulative link model: one set of weights w, but K-1 thresholds θ_k that separate categories
  • Preserves ordering information (unlike multinomial)
  • Proportional odds assumption: the same features shift you along the ordinal scale regardless of where you start
  • Common in healthcare (disease severity), surveys, credit ratings
Advanced slide. Most students will not need this immediately, but it is important to know it exists. The proportional odds assumption is often violated in practice — test it with the Brant test.

Generalised Linear Models (GLMs)

Theory

Linear and logistic regression are both special cases of the GLM framework:

g(\mathbb{E}[y|\mathbf{x}]) = \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}
g is the "link function" — it connects the linear predictor to the expected response

Bayesian Linear Regression

Advanced

Instead of a single best weight vector, compute a distribution over weights:

p(\mathbf{w}|\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w}) \cdot p(\mathbf{w})}{p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X})}
Posterior = (Likelihood x Prior) / Evidence

With a Gaussian prior p(\mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2\mathbf{I}) and Gaussian likelihood:

Posterior is also Gaussian (conjugacy)
p(\mathbf{w}|\mathbf{X},\mathbf{y}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left((\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X} + \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}, \;\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X} + \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}\mathbf{I})^{-1}\right)

The posterior mean is the Ridge solution. But now we also have a posterior variance — uncertainty quantification for free.

This is the Bayesian perspective. MAP estimation gives you Ridge. Full Bayesian gives you Ridge PLUS uncertainty. The posterior variance tells you where the model is confident and where it is not. This connects to calibration and conformal prediction later.

Bayesian Predictive Distribution

Advanced

For a new point \mathbf{x}_*, the predictive distribution integrates over all possible weights:

p(y_*|\mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y}) = \int p(y_*|\mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{w})\,p(\mathbf{w}|\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})\,d\mathbf{w} = \mathcal{N}(\mu_*, \sigma_*^2)

The predictive variance \sigma_*^2 has two components:

  • Epistemic uncertainty — from not knowing the true weights (reduces with more data)
  • Aleatoric uncertainty — noise in the data (irreducible)
Zillow connection: Bayesian regression would have told Zillow "I predict $500K, but my 90% interval is [$430K, $570K]." That $140K range might have prevented buying the house.
Connect back to Zillow. This is exactly the uncertainty quantification they were missing. Bayesian regression gives you prediction intervals naturally. We will see another approach — conformal prediction — later in the calibration section.

What Is a Decision Tree?

Foundations

A flowchart for making predictions. At each step, ask one question about the data and follow the answer.

Example: "Is the flat above 100 sqm? If yes, is it in a mature estate? If yes, predict $650K. If no, predict $520K."
Why trees are loved:
  • Easy to explain to anyone
  • Handle non-linear relationships
  • No feature scaling needed
  • Handle mixed types (numeric + categorical)
Why single trees are dangerous:
  • High variance — small data changes, big tree changes
  • Greedy splits are not globally optimal
  • Prone to overfitting without pruning
Decision trees are the foundation for the most powerful ML models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM). Start with a single tree, understand its weaknesses, then see how ensembles fix them.

CART — How Trees Split

Theory

At each node, CART finds the feature and threshold that best separates the data. "Best" means maximising the purity of child nodes.

Gini Impurity

G(t) = 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k^2

Probability that two randomly drawn samples from the node belong to different classes. G = 0 means pure.

Entropy / Information Gain

H(t) = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2 p_k

Information-theoretic measure of disorder. H = 0 means pure. Information gain = parent entropy minus weighted child entropy.

Two measures of impurity. In practice, Gini and entropy give nearly identical splits 99% of the time. Gini is faster to compute (no logarithm). Most libraries default to Gini.

Gini vs Entropy — When They Differ

Theory

For binary classification with class probability p:

G = 2p(1-p)
Gini: quadratic, max at p = 0.5
H = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p)
Entropy: concave, also max at p = 0.5

Both are concave, both are maximised at p = 0.5, both equal 0 at p = 0 and p = 1.

When they differ: Entropy penalises impure nodes slightly more than Gini. In multi-class problems with many classes, entropy can produce more balanced trees. In practice, the difference is rarely significant.
You can also mention gain ratio (entropy normalized by split info), which corrects for the bias of information gain toward features with many values. C4.5 uses gain ratio; CART uses Gini.

Regression Trees — MSE Splitting

Theory

For regression, each leaf predicts the mean of the training targets in that leaf. The split criterion minimises variance:

\text{Split quality} = \text{Var}(\text{parent}) - \left[\frac{n_L}{n}\text{Var}(L) + \frac{n_R}{n}\text{Var}(R)\right]
Choose the split that maximises variance reduction (equivalent to minimising weighted child MSE)

Each leaf prediction: \hat{y}_{\text{leaf}} = \frac{1}{n_{\text{leaf}}} \sum_{i \in \text{leaf}} y_i

Same principle as classification, just a different impurity measure. Variance instead of Gini/entropy. The prediction in each leaf is simply the average of the training targets that fell into that leaf.

Pruning — Preventing Overfitting

Theory

Pre-Pruning (Early Stopping)

  • max_depth — limit tree depth
  • min_samples_split — minimum samples to split a node
  • min_samples_leaf — minimum samples in a leaf
  • max_leaf_nodes — limit total leaves

Post-Pruning (Cost-Complexity)

R_\alpha(T) = R(T) + \alpha|T|

R(T) = training error, |T| = number of leaves, α = complexity parameter. Find α via cross-validation. Prune subtrees where the complexity cost exceeds the error reduction.

In practice: Pre-pruning is simpler and almost always sufficient. Post-pruning gives slightly better results but is rarely worth the effort when you are going to ensemble anyway.
Pre-pruning is what you tune in hyperparameter search. Post-pruning (minimal cost-complexity pruning) is available in scikit-learn via ccp_alpha. In practice, if you are using Random Forest or XGBoost, the ensemble itself controls overfitting.

From Single Tree to Forest

Foundations

A single decision tree is high variance. Small changes in data produce very different trees.

Solution: Train many trees on different random subsets of the data, then average their predictions. This is the fundamental idea behind Random Forests.

Why averaging works: If trees make independent errors, averaging cancels them out. The variance of the average is \frac{\sigma^2}{B} where B is the number of trees.

But: Trees trained on the same data are not independent — they are correlated. This limits the variance reduction. Random Forests solve this by adding extra randomness.
This is the key insight behind ensembles. "Wisdom of crowds" — many imperfect models, averaged, beat one perfect model. But independence matters. This motivates the two sources of randomness in Random Forests.

Random Forest — Bagging + Feature Randomness

Theory

Two sources of randomness to de-correlate trees:

RandomnessHowWhy
Bootstrap (bagging)Each tree trained on a bootstrap sample (n samples with replacement)Different trees see different data
Feature subsamplingAt each split, only consider m random featuresPrevents dominant features from appearing in every tree
\text{Var}(\bar{f}) = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{1-\rho}{B}\sigma^2
ρ = average pairwise correlation between trees. Feature randomness reduces ρ, which reduces ensemble variance.

Default m: \sqrt{p} for classification, p/3 for regression (where p = total features).

The variance formula is key. The first term (rho * sigma^2) does NOT decrease with more trees — it depends on correlation. Feature randomness is what drives rho down. This is why max_features is the most important RF hyperparameter, not n_estimators.

Out-of-Bag Error

Theory

Each bootstrap sample uses ~63.2% of the data (due to sampling with replacement). The remaining ~36.8% are out-of-bag (OOB).

Free cross-validation: For each training example, average the predictions from only the trees that did NOT include it in their bootstrap sample. This gives a nearly unbiased estimate of generalisation error — without needing a separate validation set.

OOB error closely approximates leave-one-out cross-validation, but is computed at no extra cost. Use oob_score=True.

OOB is a beautiful property of bagging. You get cross-validation for free. It is especially useful when your dataset is small and you cannot afford to hold out a validation set.

Feature Importance — Two Methods

Theory

Gini Importance (MDI)

Sum the impurity reduction from all splits using feature j, averaged across all trees.

Biased! Favours high-cardinality features (e.g., ID columns get high importance). Use with caution.

Permutation Importance

Randomly shuffle feature j, measure how much the model's performance drops. Large drop = important feature.

Unbiased and model-agnostic. Works for any model. Preferred in practice.
Neither handles correlated features well. If two features are correlated, permuting one does not hurt because the other compensates. Solution: drop-column importance (expensive) or SHAP (coming in Section H).
Gini importance is the default in scikit-learn. It is fast but biased. Always prefer permutation importance for final reporting. We will see SHAP later, which is the gold standard.

Gradient Boosting — The Key Insight

Foundations

Random Forests reduce variance by averaging. Gradient boosting reduces bias by correcting mistakes.

Analogy: A student takes an exam and gets some answers wrong. Instead of starting over, they study only the questions they got wrong, then take the exam again, focusing on their weaknesses. Each round corrects the previous round's mistakes.

Each new tree is trained on the residuals (errors) of the previous ensemble:

F_m(\mathbf{x}) = F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}) + \eta \cdot h_m(\mathbf{x})
F_m = ensemble after m trees. h_m = new tree fitted to residuals. η = learning rate (shrinkage).
This is the most important slide in the tree section. Random Forest = parallel, independent trees, averaged. Gradient boosting = sequential, dependent trees, accumulated. Each one addresses a different axis of the bias-variance trade-off.

Gradient Boosting — Gradient Descent in Function Space

Theory

The key mathematical insight: boosting is gradient descent, but in function space instead of parameter space.

Objective: minimise total loss
F^* = \arg\min_F \sum_{i=1}^{n} L(y_i, F(\mathbf{x}_i))
Negative gradient = "pseudo-residuals"
r_{im} = -\frac{\partial L(y_i, F(\mathbf{x}_i))}{\partial F(\mathbf{x}_i)}\bigg|_{F=F_{m-1}}

Fit tree h_m to these pseudo-residuals, then update: F_m = F_{m-1} + \eta h_m.

This is beautiful: instead of stepping in the direction of steepest descent in parameter space (like neural network training), we step in the direction of steepest descent in FUNCTION space. Each tree is a "step." The learning rate eta controls step size.

Gradient Computation for Different Losses

Theory

Absolute (L1)|y-F|\text{sign}(y_i - F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}_i))Yes
HuberL2 if |r|<δ, else L1Blend of L2 and L1Yes

The loss function defines the pseudo-residuals, which define what each tree learns. Choosing the right loss is choosing what "mistake" means. Huber loss is a robust compromise.
Show that for squared loss, the pseudo-residual is just the ordinary residual. For other losses, it is different. This is the power of gradient boosting: you can optimise ANY differentiable loss.

Stochastic Gradient Boosting

Theory

Friedman (2002) proposed training each tree on a random subsample of the data:

  • subsample=0.8 — each tree uses 80% of rows (without replacement)
  • Introduces randomness similar to bagging in Random Forests
  • Reduces variance, prevents overfitting, speeds up training
This combines the variance-reduction benefit of bagging with the bias-reduction benefit of boosting. Most modern implementations (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) support this via subsample / bagging_fraction.
Stochastic gradient boosting is to boosting what bagging is to decision trees — adding randomness to reduce variance. It is almost always better than full-sample boosting. Default subsample=0.8 is a good starting point.

XGBoost — The Breakthrough

Foundations

XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) dominated Kaggle competitions for years. Key innovations:

  • Regularised objective — penalises tree complexity directly
  • 2nd-order Taylor expansion — uses both gradient and Hessian for better splits
  • Sparsity-aware splitting — handles missing values natively
  • Column subsampling — like Random Forest, but within boosting
  • Parallel split finding — fast even on large datasets
Kailash's TrainingPipeline supports XGBoost as ModelSpec(algorithm="xgboost") with full hyperparameter access.
XGBoost was a paradigm shift. Before XGBoost, gradient boosting was slow and finicky. XGBoost made it fast, regularised, and practical. It is still one of the best choices for structured/tabular data.

XGBoost — Regularised Objective

Theory
\mathcal{L} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} L(y_i, \hat{y}_i) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \Omega(f_k) \quad \text{where} \quad \Omega(f) = \gamma T + \frac{1}{2}\lambda\sum_{j=1}^{T} w_j^2
T = number of leaves. w_j = leaf weight. γ penalises tree size. λ penalises large leaf weights.

Unlike standard gradient boosting, XGBoost explicitly penalises:

  • \gamma T — number of leaves (tree complexity)
  • \frac{1}{2}\lambda\sum w_j^2 — L2 on leaf weights (like Ridge on predictions)
The regularisation term Omega is what makes XGBoost different from plain gradient boosting. Gamma controls tree complexity (higher gamma = fewer splits). Lambda controls leaf weight magnitude (higher lambda = more conservative predictions).

XGBoost — 2nd-Order Taylor Expansion

Theory
Approximate the loss with a 2nd-order Taylor expansion at F_{m-1}
L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(m)}) \approx L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(m-1)}) + g_i f_m(\mathbf{x}_i) + \frac{1}{2}h_i f_m(\mathbf{x}_i)^2

Where:

  • g_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}^{(m-1)}} — first derivative (gradient)
  • h_i = \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial (\hat{y}^{(m-1)})^2} — second derivative (Hessian)
Why use the Hessian? Standard gradient boosting uses only g_i (first-order). The Hessian h_i provides curvature information — like Newton's method vs plain gradient descent. This leads to better splits and faster convergence.
The Taylor expansion is what makes XGBoost faster than standard gradient boosting. Newton's method converges quadratically (vs linearly for gradient descent). The Hessian tells us not just the direction but also the optimal step size.

XGBoost — Optimal Leaf Weight

Theory

For a leaf j containing data points I_j, the optimal weight is:

w_j^* = -\frac{\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i}{\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda} = -\frac{G_j}{H_j + \lambda}
G_j = sum of gradients in leaf j. H_j = sum of Hessians in leaf j. λ = L2 regularisation.

The optimal loss reduction for the entire tree:

\mathcal{L}^* = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{T}\frac{G_j^2}{H_j + \lambda} + \gamma T
This is a closed-form solution for the optimal prediction in each leaf. Notice the lambda in the denominator — it shrinks the leaf weight, which is the regularisation effect. The optimal loss formula is used to evaluate candidate splits.

XGBoost — Split Gain Formula

Theory

To decide whether to split a leaf, compute the gain:

\text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{G_L^2}{H_L + \lambda} + \frac{G_R^2}{H_R + \lambda} - \frac{(G_L + G_R)^2}{H_L + H_R + \lambda}\right] - \gamma
Left child gain + Right child gain - Parent gain - complexity penalty γ
  • Split only if Gain > 0 (the improvement exceeds the complexity cost \gamma)
  • \gamma acts as a minimum split gain — pruning built into the objective
  • Higher \lambda — more conservative splits (larger denominators reduce gain)
This is the most important formula in XGBoost. It tells you exactly when a split is worth making. The gamma term means XGBoost does built-in pre-pruning: a split must improve the objective by at least gamma to be accepted.

XGBoost — Sparsity-Aware Splitting

Advanced

Real data has missing values. XGBoost handles them natively:

  • At each split, try sending missing values to the left AND to the right
  • Keep whichever direction gives better gain
  • The "default direction" is learned from data, not hardcoded
This means you do not need to impute missing values before training. XGBoost learns the optimal handling of missingness at each split. This is a significant practical advantage.
This is one of XGBoost's underrated features. It often performs better with missing values left as-is than with imputation, because the optimal handling of missingness may differ at different points in the tree.

LightGBM — Speed and Scale

Foundations

LightGBM (Microsoft, 2017) is optimised for speed on large datasets. Three key innovations:

Histogram-Based Splitting

Bin continuous features into 256 discrete buckets. Find the best split on bins, not exact values. 8x-10x faster than exact splitting with negligible accuracy loss.

Leaf-Wise Growth

Grow the leaf with the largest loss reduction, not level-by-level. Produces deeper, more asymmetric trees. Lower loss with fewer leaves.

Leaf-wise growth can overfit on small datasets (<10K rows). Use num_leaves to control tree complexity instead of max_depth.
LightGBM is often the first choice for large datasets (>100K rows). The histogram trick reduces memory usage by 8x and speeds up split finding dramatically. Leaf-wise growth gives better accuracy per tree but requires careful regularisation.

LightGBM — GOSS and EFB

Theory

GOSS (Gradient-based One-Side Sampling)

Keep all instances with large gradients (poorly predicted). Randomly sample from instances with small gradients. Focus on the hard examples.

\text{Keep top } a\% + \text{sample } b\% \text{ from rest}

EFB (Exclusive Feature Bundling)

Mutually exclusive features (rarely non-zero together) are bundled into a single feature. Reduces feature count for sparse data.

Common with one-hot encoded categoricals: if feature A is 1, features B and C are 0.

GOSS is clever: samples with large gradients are the ones the model is getting wrong — so they carry more information. By keeping all of these and down-sampling the easy ones, LightGBM trains faster without losing accuracy. EFB is most useful for sparse data with many one-hot features.

CatBoost — Categorical Features Done Right

Foundations

CatBoost (Yandex, 2018) solves two problems that plague other boosting libraries:

Target Leakage in Target Encoding

Naive target encoding uses the target to encode features — the model sees information it should not have. CatBoost uses ordered target statistics: for each row, compute the encoding using only rows that came before it.

Prediction Shift in Boosting

Standard boosting uses the same data to compute gradients and fit trees — this is a subtle form of overfitting. CatBoost uses ordered boosting: each tree is trained on a different permutation.

CatBoost addresses two subtle but real problems. Target leakage from naive target encoding is a common mistake — CatBoost prevents it by design. Ordered boosting is CatBoost's unique contribution to the boosting literature.

CatBoost — Ordered Target Statistics

Theory

For a categorical feature with value c, the target statistic for row i is:

\hat{x}_i^k = \frac{\sum_{j: \sigma(j) < \sigma(i),\, x_j^k = c} y_j + a \cdot p}{\sum_{j: \sigma(j) < \sigma(i),\, x_j^k = c} 1 + a}
σ = random permutation. a = smoothing parameter. p = prior (global mean). Only uses rows BEFORE row i in the permutation.

This prevents target leakage: each row's encoding never uses its own target or the target of rows that come "after" it.

The key insight: by ordering the data randomly and only looking backward, CatBoost creates an encoding that is unbiased. The smoothing parameter 'a' controls the regularisation — for rare categories, the prior p dominates.

CatBoost — Symmetric Trees

Theory

CatBoost builds oblivious (symmetric) decision trees:

  • Same splitting condition at each level of the tree
  • All nodes at depth d use the same feature and threshold
  • Highly structured — acts as strong regulariser
  • Enables SIMD-optimised inference (very fast prediction)
Trade-off: Symmetric trees are less flexible than asymmetric trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) but generalise better on noisy data and are 3-8x faster at inference time. CatBoost compensates for reduced flexibility with more trees.
Symmetric trees are unusual but effective. The key advantage is inference speed: the tree structure can be compiled into a flat lookup table. This matters in production where you need sub-millisecond predictions.

NGBoost — Uncertainty from Boosting

Advanced

Standard gradient boosting predicts a point estimate. NGBoost (Duan et al., 2020) predicts a full probability distribution.

p(y|\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(\mathbf{x}), \sigma^2(\mathbf{x}))
Both the mean μ and variance σ² are output by the model. Each data point gets its own uncertainty estimate.

Uses the natural gradient to correctly handle the geometry of distribution parameter space. Modular: works with any base learner, any distribution family, any scoring rule.

2025 update: Neural-NGBoost replaces decision tree base learners with lightweight neural networks for improved performance on complex distributions (ScienceDirect, 2025).
NGBoost is the probabilistic extension of gradient boosting. Instead of "the house is worth $500K," it says "the house is worth $500K with standard deviation $40K." This is exactly what Zillow needed — uncertainty-aware predictions.

K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN)

Foundations

The simplest ML algorithm: to predict for a new point, find the k closest training examples and take a vote (classification) or average (regression).

Analogy: You move to a new neighbourhood. To estimate your rent, you ask your k nearest neighbours what they pay, and average their answers.

No training phase — the model is the entire training dataset. All computation happens at prediction time ("lazy learning").

KNN is the ultimate "lazy learner." It stores everything and computes at prediction time. Simple to understand, but the cost comes at inference — O(n) per prediction without indexing structures.

The Curse of Dimensionality

Theory

In high dimensions, all points become equidistant. KNN fails because "nearest" loses meaning.

Quantified: In d dimensions, to maintain the same local density, you need n \propto k^d samples. With 100 features and k=5, you need approximately 5^{100} samples — more than atoms in the universe.

Practical impact: KNN works well for <20 features. Above that, use dimensionality reduction first (PCA, UMAP) or switch to tree-based models which are curse-resistant.

The curse of dimensionality is the fundamental reason KNN is rarely used in production ML. It is a great teaching tool and useful for low-dimensional problems, but breaks down in high dimensions. Trees handle high dimensions naturally because they split on one feature at a time.

Efficient KNN — KD-Tree and Ball-Tree

Advanced

KD-Tree

  • Binary tree that recursively splits space along coordinate axes
  • Query time: O(log n) average case
  • Degrades to O(n) in high dimensions (>20)

Ball-Tree

  • Hierarchical nesting of hyper-spheres
  • Works with any metric (not just Euclidean)
  • Better than KD-tree in moderate dimensions (20-50)

For very large datasets, approximate nearest neighbour (ANN) libraries like FAISS and Annoy trade exact results for massive speed gains (millisecond queries on billions of points).

In practice, if you need KNN at scale, use an ANN library. Exact KNN is fine for small datasets but does not scale to production workloads with millions of points.

Support Vector Machines — Maximum Margin

Foundations

SVM finds the decision boundary that maximises the margin — the gap between the two classes.

Analogy: You are drawing a line between two groups of points. There are many possible lines, but the SVM picks the one with the widest gap between the line and the nearest points from each side. Wider gap = more room for error = better generalisation.

The nearest points to the boundary are called support vectors. They alone define the boundary — all other points could be removed without changing it.

SVMs are one of the most elegant algorithms in ML. The key insight: instead of fitting the data, find the boundary that separates with the largest margin. This is a fundamentally different objective from minimising error.

SVM — Hard and Soft Margin

Theory

Hard Margin

\min_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \;\text{s.t.}\; y_i(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i + b) \geq 1

Requires perfect separation. Fails if data is noisy or not linearly separable.

Soft Margin

\min_{\mathbf{w},b,\xi} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_i \xi_i

Slack variables \xi_i allow misclassification. C controls the penalty — high C = hard margin, low C = more slack.

The hinge loss: L = \max(0, 1 - y_i(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i + b)). Only penalises points within or on the wrong side of the margin. Points safely on the correct side contribute zero loss.
The soft margin is what makes SVM practical. C is the bias-variance knob: large C = low bias, high variance (try to classify everything correctly). Small C = high bias, low variance (allow more misclassification for a wider margin).

The Kernel Trick

Theory

Many datasets are not linearly separable in the original space. The kernel trick maps data to a higher-dimensional space where it becomes separable — without ever computing the mapping explicitly.

K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_i)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_j)
The kernel function computes the dot product in the high-dimensional space directly
KernelFormulaUse Case
Linear\mathbf{x}_i^T\mathbf{x}_jAlready linearly separable, text classification
Polynomial(\gamma\mathbf{x}_i^T\mathbf{x}_j + r)^dInteraction features
RBF (Gaussian)\exp(-\gamma\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|^2)General-purpose non-linear (most common)
Sigmoid\tanh(\gamma\mathbf{x}_i^T\mathbf{x}_j + r)Neural network analogy
The kernel trick is one of the most beautiful ideas in ML. The RBF kernel maps to INFINITE dimensions — yet we never need to compute those dimensions. We only need the dot product, which the kernel function gives us directly.

SVM for Regression (SVR)

Theory

Support Vector Regression fits a tube of width \varepsilon around the data:

\min_{\mathbf{w},b} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_i(\xi_i + \xi_i^*) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad |y_i - (\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{x}_i + b)| \leq \varepsilon + \xi_i
The ε-insensitive tube: points inside the tube incur zero loss

Only points outside the \varepsilon-tube are support vectors. This makes SVR robust to small noise — like a built-in noise filter.

SVR is underused in practice. The epsilon-tube is an elegant way to ignore small noise while fitting the trend. Useful for regression problems where you want robust predictions, but trees usually win in practice.

Naive Bayes

Foundations

Apply Bayes' theorem with the "naive" assumption that features are conditionally independent given the class:

p(y=k|\mathbf{x}) \propto p(y=k) \prod_{j=1}^{p} p(x_j|y=k)
Posterior ∝ Prior x Product of individual feature likelihoods

Variants

  • Gaussian: Features are continuous, modelled as Gaussian
  • Multinomial: Features are counts (text, word frequencies)
  • Bernoulli: Features are binary (0/1)
  • Complement: Correction for imbalanced classes

Why It Works Despite Being "Wrong"

The independence assumption is almost always violated. But Naive Bayes only needs the ranking of class probabilities to be correct, not the exact values. Misestimated probabilities can still produce correct rankings.

Naive Bayes is fast, simple, and surprisingly effective. Its biggest strength is text classification (spam filtering, sentiment analysis). The Multinomial variant with TF-IDF features is a strong baseline for NLP tasks.

Naive Bayes — Laplace Smoothing

Theory

If a word never appears in "spam" emails during training, p(\text{word}|\text{spam}) = 0, which makes the entire product zero — one unseen word overrides all other evidence.

p(x_j = v|y=k) = \frac{n_{kv} + \alpha}{n_k + \alpha V}
α = smoothing parameter (typically 1). V = vocabulary size. Adds a small "pseudo-count" to every feature-class combination.
Laplace smoothing is Bayesian: It is equivalent to a Dirichlet prior on the multinomial parameters. \alpha = 1 is a uniform prior (every word is equally likely a priori).
Without smoothing, Naive Bayes is fragile — one zero probability kills the entire prediction. Laplace smoothing is simple but essential. It is one of the earliest examples of regularisation via Bayesian priors.

Gaussian Processes

Advanced

A Gaussian Process (GP) is a distribution over functions, defined by a mean function and a kernel:

f(\mathbf{x}) \sim \mathcal{GP}\!\left(m(\mathbf{x}),\, k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')\right)
Any finite collection of function values is jointly Gaussian. The kernel k defines similarity between points.
KernelPropertiesBest For
RBF (Squared Exponential)Infinitely smoothSmooth, well-behaved functions
Matérn (ν=3/2, 5/2)Finite differentiabilityPhysical processes (most common)
PeriodicRepeating patternsSeasonal data
LinearBayesian linear regressionTrending data
GPs are powerful but scale as O(n^3) — practical for datasets up to ~10K points. Their key advantage is principled uncertainty quantification. They are widely used in Bayesian optimisation for hyperparameter tuning (coming next).

Gaussian Processes — Prediction

Advanced

Given training data, the GP posterior at a test point \mathbf{x}_* is:

\mu_* = \mathbf{k}_*^T (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{y}
\sigma_*^2 = k(\mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{x}_*) - \mathbf{k}_*^T(\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{k}_*

\mathbf{K} = kernel matrix between training points. \mathbf{k}_* = kernel between test and training points. The predictive variance \sigma_*^2 is large where training data is sparse and small where it is dense.

Connection to neural networks: An infinitely wide neural network with random weights is equivalent to a GP with a specific kernel (Neal, 1996). This is the Neural Tangent Kernel regime.
The GP posterior gives you both a prediction (mean) and uncertainty (variance). The uncertainty is not a scalar — it varies across the input space. Near training points, uncertainty is low. Far from training data, uncertainty is high. This is exactly what we want.

Theory

  2. Use an acquisition function to decide where to try next
  3. Evaluate the real model at that point, update the GP
  4. Repeat until budget exhausted

Bayesian optimisation is dramatically more efficient than grid or random search. It typically finds near-optimal hyperparameters in 20-50 evaluations vs thousands for grid search. The GP surrogate learns which regions of hyperparameter space are promising.

Model Evaluation — Why Accuracy Is Not Enough

Foundations

A model that predicts "no cancer" for every patient is 99% accurate if only 1% of patients have cancer. Is it useful?

No. It never catches cancer. Accuracy hides this because it rewards correct predictions on the majority class.

We need metrics that measure what we actually care about. Different problems need different metrics.

This is the most important evaluation slide. Start with the cancer example. Ask students: "Would you want this model making decisions about YOUR health?" The answer is always no. This motivates everything that follows.

The Confusion Matrix

Foundations

  • TP: Cancer patient correctly identified
  • FP: Healthy person scared unnecessarily (Type I error)

  • TN: Healthy person correctly cleared
Which error is worse depends on the problem. In cancer screening, FN is deadly. In spam filtering, FP is annoying but harmless. Choose your metrics accordingly.
The confusion matrix is the foundation of all classification metrics. Draw it on the whiteboard. Ask: "In a fraud detection system, which is worse — a false positive or a false negative?" (Depends on cost: FP = investigating a legitimate customer, FN = missing actual fraud.)

Precision, Recall, F1

Theory
\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
Of all predicted positives, how many are actually positive?

High precision = few false alarms. Important when false positives are costly (spam filter, fraud alert).

\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
Of all actual positives, how many did we catch?

High recall = few missed cases. Important when false negatives are costly (cancer screening, security).

F_1 = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}} = \frac{2TP}{2TP + FP + FN}
Harmonic mean of precision and recall. Low if EITHER is low.
The precision-recall trade-off is the classification equivalent of bias-variance. You can always increase recall by predicting everything as positive (but precision drops). F1 balances the two — but only use F1 when both errors are equally costly.

F-Beta — Weighting Precision vs Recall

Theory
F_\beta = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{(\beta^2 \cdot \text{Precision}) + \text{Recall}}
β > 1 weights recall more. β < 1 weights precision more. β = 1 is standard F1.
βEmphasisUse Case
0.5Precision >> RecallSpam filter (avoid marking real email as spam)
1.0EqualBalanced problems
2.0Recall >> PrecisionCancer screening (never miss a case)
F-beta lets you encode your business priorities into the metric. If missing a positive case costs 4x more than a false alarm, use F2. If false alarms cost 2x more, use F0.5.

ROC Curve and AUC

Theory

The ROC curve plots True Positive Rate vs False Positive Rate across all classification thresholds:

\text{TPR} = \frac{TP}{TP+FN} \quad \text{FPR} = \frac{FP}{FP+TN}
  • Random classifier: diagonal line (AUC = 0.5)
  • Perfect classifier: top-left corner (AUC = 1.0)
  • AUC = probability that model ranks a random positive higher than a random negative
ROC-AUC is the most popular classification metric. It is threshold-independent — it measures the model's ability to RANK examples, regardless of where you set the threshold. AUC = 0.5 means random, AUC = 1.0 means perfect.

Precision-Recall Curve — When ROC Lies

Theory

ROC-AUC can be misleadingly high with class imbalance. When the positive class is rare, even a bad model has low FPR.

Example: 1,000 positive, 1,000,000 negative. Model predicts 2,000 positives with 500 correct. FPR = 1500/1M = 0.15%. ROC looks great! But Precision = 500/2000 = 25%. PR curve reveals the truth.

Rule of thumb: Use ROC-AUC when classes are balanced. Use PR-AUC when the positive class is rare (<10% of data).

This is a common mistake in industry. Teams report AUC=0.95 and celebrate, but when you look at PR-AUC it is 0.3. The model is good at ranking negatives but terrible at actually finding positives. Always check PR-AUC for imbalanced problems.

Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) ranges from -1 to +1 and is the only metric that produces a high score ONLY when the model performs well on all four quadrants of the confusion matrix. It is the best single metric for imbalanced classification.
MCC is underused. Unlike F1, it accounts for true negatives. Unlike accuracy, it is not inflated by class imbalance. If you can only report one metric for imbalanced classification, MCC is the best choice.

Cross-Validation

Foundations

Problem: A single train/test split is noisy — the score depends on which data ended up in which set.

Solution: Split the data into K folds. Train on K-1 folds, test on the remaining fold. Rotate. Average the scores.

Final score = mean ± std of K scores. Typical: K = 5 or K = 10.

K-fold cross-validation is the standard way to estimate generalisation error. K=5 is the most common. K=10 gives lower bias but higher variance in the estimate. The standard deviation across folds tells you how stable the model is.

Nested Cross-Validation

Theory

If you use cross-validation to select hyperparameters AND to evaluate the model, you are overfitting to the validation folds.

Nested CV uses two loops:
  • Inner loop: Tune hyperparameters (e.g., 5-fold CV for grid search)
  • Outer loop: Evaluate the tuned model (e.g., 5-fold CV for honest error estimate)
This gives an unbiased estimate of the generalisation error of the model selection process.

Computationally expensive (5 × 5 = 25 training runs), but necessary when you want to report honest performance.

Nested CV is the gold standard for model evaluation. Without it, your reported performance is optimistically biased because the outer evaluation has seen the data used for hyperparameter selection. In papers, always use nested CV.

Class Imbalance — The Real-World Default

Foundations

Most real problems are imbalanced:

ProblemPositive RateImbalance Ratio
Credit card fraud0.17%1:600
Manufacturing defects1-5%1:20-100
Disease diagnosis0.5-5%1:20-200
Click-through rate1-3%1:30-100
Standard algorithms optimise accuracy, which rewards predicting the majority class. You need specialised techniques.
Class imbalance is the norm, not the exception. If your data is balanced, you are lucky. This section is about what to do when it is not. The techniques here will be used in almost every real project.

Resampling Strategies

Theory

Oversampling (More Positives)

  • Random oversampling: Duplicate minority samples
  • SMOTE: Create synthetic samples by interpolating between minority neighbours
  • ADASYN: Like SMOTE, but generates more synthetic samples where the boundary is harder

Undersampling (Fewer Negatives)

  • Random undersampling: Remove majority samples
  • Tomek links: Remove majority samples that are nearest neighbours to minority samples (clean the boundary)
  • Edited Nearest Neighbours: Remove majority samples whose nearest neighbours mostly belong to another class
Oversampling risks overfitting (duplicating minority data means training on the same examples multiple times). Undersampling loses information (throwing away majority data). SMOTE is the most popular compromise.

SMOTE — And Its Failure Modes

Theory

SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) creates new samples by interpolating between minority class neighbours:

\mathbf{x}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{x}_i + \lambda \cdot (\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i) \quad \text{where } \lambda \sim U(0,1)
x_j is a randomly chosen k-nearest minority neighbour of x_i
SMOTE failure modes:
  • Applied BEFORE train/test split — synthetic samples leak test information! Always apply SMOTE AFTER splitting.
  • High-dimensional data — interpolation in high dimensions creates unrealistic samples
  • Overlapping classes — synthetic samples created in the overlap region worsen the boundary
  • Small minority class (<20 samples) — not enough diversity to interpolate meaningfully
The number one SMOTE mistake: applying it before the train/test split. This creates synthetic samples in the test set that are derived from training data — a severe form of data leakage. Always split first, then SMOTE the training set only.

Cost-Sensitive Learning

Theory

Instead of resampling, modify the loss function to weight errors differently:

L_{\text{weighted}} = \sum_i w_{y_i} \cdot L(y_i, \hat{y}_i) \quad \text{where } w_k = \frac{n}{K \cdot n_k}
Rare classes get higher weight. Inversely proportional to class frequency.

In most libraries: class_weight="balanced"

Advantage over resampling: No synthetic data, no information loss, works with any algorithm. This is usually the first thing to try.
Class weighting is simpler, safer, and often more effective than SMOTE. It does not create fake data or throw away real data. Always try class_weight="balanced" before reaching for SMOTE.

Focal Loss

Advanced

From object detection (Lin et al., 2017), Focal Loss down-weights easy examples so the model focuses on hard ones:

FL(p_t) = -\alpha_t (1 - p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
γ = focusing parameter. γ = 0 is standard cross-entropy. γ = 2 is typical. (1-p_t)^γ is small when p_t is large (easy example).

When \gamma = 2: a well-classified example with p_t = 0.9 has its loss reduced by 100x compared to standard cross-entropy. Hard examples (p_t = 0.1) are barely affected.

Focal loss is more principled than SMOTE — it does not create synthetic data, and it dynamically adjusts focus during training. The gamma parameter is the key: higher gamma = more focus on hard examples. Start with gamma=2.

Threshold Moving

Foundations

Most classifiers output a probability. By default, we classify as positive if p > 0.5. But 0.5 is arbitrary.

With imbalanced data, lower the threshold. If only 1% of cases are positive, a threshold of 0.5 is too high — the model almost never predicts positive. Try thresholds of 0.1, 0.2, or 0.3.

Find the optimal threshold by:

  • Maximising F1 on the validation set
  • Choosing the point on the PR curve closest to (1, 1)
  • Cost-based: minimising C_{FP} \cdot FP + C_{FN} \cdot FN
Threshold moving is the simplest and most underrated technique for imbalanced classification. Train a well-calibrated model, then choose the threshold that minimises your business cost. This separates the modelling problem from the decision problem.

What Is Model Calibration?

Foundations

A model is calibrated if its predicted probabilities match actual frequencies.

Example: If the model says "there is a 30% chance of rain" for 1,000 days, then it should actually rain on ~300 of those days. If it only rains on 100, the model is overconfident.

This is exactly what Zillow missed. The model said "$500K" but did not say how confident it was. An uncalibrated model that says 90% confident when it should say 60% confident leads to bad decisions.

Calibration is the bridge between model output and real-world decision-making. A model that outputs probabilities can only be used for risk-based decisions if those probabilities are calibrated. This is critical in finance, medicine, and insurance.

Reliability Diagram

Foundations

Bin predictions by confidence. In each bin, compare predicted probability vs actual frequency.

Points on the diagonal = perfectly calibrated. Below the diagonal = overconfident. Above = underconfident.

The reliability diagram is the primary tool for assessing calibration. Show how to read it: if the blue dots are below the diagonal at p=0.8, it means that when the model says 80% confidence, the actual rate is lower (say 60%). The model is overconfident.

Theory
ModelCalibration QualityWhy

Random ForestModerate (biased toward 0.5)Averages bounded predictions from trees

Platt Scaling

Theory

Fit a logistic regression on the model's raw outputs to produce calibrated probabilities:

p_{\text{calibrated}} = \sigma(a \cdot f(\mathbf{x}) + b)
f(x) = raw model output (logit or decision function). a, b learned on validation set.
  • Learns only 2 parameters (a, b) — fast and stable
  • Assumes the calibration function is sigmoid-shaped
  • Works best when the miscalibration is monotonic
  • Must be fit on a held-out calibration set (not training data!)
Platt scaling is the most common calibration method. It is simple and effective. The key constraint: you need a separate calibration set — do NOT calibrate on training data, as that would overfit the calibration.

Isotonic Regression

Theory

A non-parametric calibration method: fit a monotonically increasing step function.

  • More flexible than Platt scaling — does not assume sigmoid shape
  • Can correct non-monotonic miscalibration
  • Needs more data (risk of overfitting with <1,000 calibration samples)
When to use which: Platt scaling with <1,000 calibration samples or when sigmoid assumption holds. Isotonic regression with >1,000 calibration samples or when miscalibration is non-monotonic.
Isotonic regression is more flexible but more data-hungry. In practice, try both and check the reliability diagram. With enough data, isotonic regression usually wins.

Temperature Scaling

Theory

The simplest calibration method for neural networks: divide logits by a learned temperature T:

p_k = \frac{e^{z_k / T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j / T}}
T > 1 softens probabilities (reduces overconfidence). T < 1 sharpens them.

Only one parameter to learn. Preserves the ranking of predictions (accuracy unchanged). Fixes the confidence, not the prediction.

Temperature scaling is the go-to for neural network calibration (Guo et al., 2017). It is remarkable that a single parameter can dramatically improve calibration without changing any predictions. T is typically learned by minimising NLL on a validation set.

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)

Theory
\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{n_b}{n} \left|\text{acc}(b) - \text{conf}(b)\right|
B bins. For each bin: |actual accuracy - average predicted confidence|, weighted by bin size.

ECE = 0 means perfectly calibrated. ECE = 0.1 means predictions are off by 10 percentage points on average.

ECE depends on the number of bins and bin edges. Always report the binning strategy (equal-width vs equal-frequency, number of bins). ECE with 10 equal-width bins is the convention.
ECE is the standard single-number summary of calibration. But it has known flaws: it depends on binning, and it can be gamed by choosing favourable bin edges. Always show the reliability diagram alongside ECE.

Proper Scoring Rules

Theory

A scoring rule is proper if it is optimised when the predicted probabilities equal the true probabilities. A strictly proper scoring rule has a unique optimum.

Brier Score (Proper)

BS = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (p_i - y_i)^2

MSE on probabilities. Decomposes into calibration + refinement + uncertainty.

Accuracy (NOT Proper)

\text{Acc} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \mathbb{1}[\hat{y}_i = y_i]

Only cares about the argmax, not the probability. A model predicting 0.51 and one predicting 0.99 are equally "correct."

Proof that accuracy is not proper: For a fair coin (p=0.5), predicting p=0.99 gives the same accuracy as p=0.5 (both predict "heads"). But the Brier score is 0.25 for p=0.5 and 0.2501 for p=0.99. Accuracy cannot distinguish them.
This is a subtle but important point. If you optimise accuracy, the model has no incentive to output well-calibrated probabilities. If you optimise log loss or Brier score (both proper), the model IS incentivised to be calibrated. This is why cross-entropy loss naturally produces better-calibrated models than hinge loss.

Conformal Prediction — Guaranteed Coverage

Advanced

Instead of a point prediction, output a prediction set that is guaranteed to contain the true value with probability 1 - \alpha.

P(y_{\text{new}} \in C(\mathbf{x}_{\text{new}})) \geq 1 - \alpha
C(x) = prediction set. α = 0.1 gives 90% coverage. Distribution-free — works with any model.

How it works:

  1. Compute a non-conformity score (e.g., |y - f(x)|) on a calibration set
  2. Find the (1-α) quantile of these scores
  3. For a new point, include all values within this quantile distance of the prediction
Conformal prediction is one of the most exciting developments in ML. It gives you uncertainty quantification that is valid for ANY model — no Bayesian assumptions, no parametric assumptions. The only requirement is exchangeability of the data.

Conformal Prediction — Recent Advances

Advanced
  • Adaptive Conformal Inference (ACI): Adjusts coverage online as data distribution changes — no exchangeability needed (2025)
  • Tail-Aware Conformal Prediction (TACP): Addresses under-coverage of rare classes in long-tailed distributions (AAAI 2026)
  • Density-Sorted Prediction Sets (DSPS): Non-convex prediction regions for multi-target regression using normalising flows (2025)
  • Rank-based conformal: Coverage for classifiers that rank labels correctly even when poorly calibrated (2026)
Conformal prediction is rapidly moving from theory to practice. It is now integrated into major ML frameworks and is being applied to medical imaging, solar forecasting, and financial risk.
Conformal prediction is having its moment. The key 2025-2026 advances make it practical: ACI removes the exchangeability assumption (critical for non-stationary data), TACP handles class imbalance, and DSPS handles multi-output regression.

SHAP — The Shapley Value Framework

Foundations

SHAP answers: "How much did each feature contribute to this specific prediction?"

Analogy: Three friends start a business. The business earns $100. How much did each person contribute? If A and B together earn $80 but A alone earns $30, then A's contribution is somewhere between $30 and ($80-$50)=$30. Shapley values compute the fair allocation by averaging over all possible orderings.

For ML: the "friends" are features, and the "earnings" are the prediction minus the average prediction.

SHAP is the single most important explainability method. It has a rigorous mathematical foundation (game theory) and produces both local (per-prediction) and global (per-model) explanations. Take time on this section.

Shapley Axioms

Theory

Shapley (1953) proved that the Shapley value is the unique allocation satisfying four axioms:

1. Efficiency: Feature contributions sum to the total prediction minus the average prediction. \sum_j \phi_j = f(\mathbf{x}) - \mathbb{E}[f(\mathbf{x})]
2. Symmetry: If two features contribute equally in all coalitions, they get equal Shapley values.
3. Dummy: A feature that never changes the prediction in any coalition gets Shapley value = 0.
4. Linearity (Additivity): For a combined model f = f_1 + f_2, Shapley values add: φ_j(f) = φ_j(f_1) + φ_j(f_2).
These axioms are what make SHAP unique. No other method satisfies all four. Efficiency means the explanation is complete — nothing is unaccounted for. Symmetry means fairness. Dummy means irrelevant features are correctly identified. Linearity enables ensemble explanations.

Computing Shapley Values

Theory
\phi_j = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!\,(p-|S|-1)!}{p!}\left[f(S \cup \{j\}) - f(S)\right]
Sum over all subsets S of features not including j. Compute marginal contribution of adding j to S.
Computational cost: O(2^p) — for p features, we must evaluate all subsets. With 20 features, that is 1 million subsets. With 100 features, it is impossible.

This is why we need approximations: TreeSHAP, KernelSHAP, sampling-based methods.

The exact computation is intractable for any realistic number of features. This slide motivates the approximation methods. TreeSHAP is exact for tree models but polynomial-time. KernelSHAP is approximate but works for any model.

TreeSHAP — Exact and Fast for Trees

Theory

TreeSHAP exploits the tree structure to compute exact Shapley values in polynomial time:

  • Complexity: O(TLD^2) where T = trees, L = max leaves, D = max depth
  • vs exact Shapley: O(TL \cdot 2^p)
  • Works for Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost
Two variants:
  • Interventional TreeSHAP: Breaks feature dependencies (each feature considered independently). Matches the "what if I changed this feature?" interpretation.
  • Observational TreeSHAP: Respects feature dependencies (uses conditional distribution). More accurate but can attribute importance to correlated features.
TreeSHAP is one of the most elegant algorithms in ML explainability. It gives you exact Shapley values in seconds for tree models. The interventional vs observational distinction matters when features are correlated — use interventional for actionable explanations.

SHAP Plots

Foundations

Summary Plot (Global)

Shows feature importance AND direction of effect. Each dot = one sample. Position = SHAP value. Colour = feature value.

Read: "High floor area (red dots) pushes predictions up (positive SHAP). Low floor area (blue) pushes them down."

Force Plot (Local)

Shows how each feature pushed a specific prediction up or down from the baseline (average prediction).

Read: "This loan was rejected because income was low (-0.3) and debt ratio was high (-0.2), partially offset by long employment (+0.1)."

Other Plots

  • Dependence plot: SHAP value vs feature value (like PDP but with interaction effects)
  • Waterfall plot: Cumulative feature contributions for one prediction
SHAP plots are the most informative visualisations in ML. The summary plot gives you global understanding. The force plot gives you local explanations. Together, they replace the need for a human to understand the model internals.

LIME — Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations

Theory

LIME explains individual predictions by fitting a simple, interpretable model (e.g., linear regression) to the model's behaviour in the local neighbourhood.

  1. Generate perturbed samples around the instance to explain
  2. Get the black-box model's predictions for each perturbation
  3. Weight samples by proximity (kernel weighting)
  4. Fit an interpretable model (e.g., sparse linear) on the weighted samples
  5. The coefficients of the interpretable model = the explanation
LIME is unstable: Different random perturbations can give different explanations. SHAP with exact computation is preferred when available. Use LIME when you need model-agnostic explanations for non-tree models.
LIME's key limitation: the explanation depends on the perturbation strategy and kernel width. Two runs of LIME on the same prediction can give contradictory explanations. SHAP is more theoretically grounded but slower for non-tree models.

PDP and ICE — Feature Effect Plots

Theory

Partial Dependence Plot (PDP)

\bar{f}_j(x_j) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_j, \mathbf{x}_{i,-j})

Average prediction as one feature varies, marginalised over all other features. Shows average effect.

Individual Conditional Expectation (ICE)

One line per observation. Shows how each individual prediction changes as the feature varies.

If ICE lines are parallel: no interaction. If they cross: interaction with other features.

PDP assumes feature independence. If features are correlated, PDP creates impossible data combinations (e.g., 10 sqm flat on the 50th floor). Use ALE plots instead.
PDP is the simplest way to visualise how a feature affects predictions. ICE adds per-observation detail. But the independence assumption is a real limitation — PDP can show effects that are not real if features are correlated.

Accumulated Local Effects (ALE)

Advanced

ALE fixes PDP's correlation problem by computing effects using small local differences instead of global marginalisation:

\text{ALE}_j(x) = \int_{z_{\min}}^{x} \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}\bigg| x_j = z\right] dz - \text{constant}
ALE accumulates the local effect of x_j by averaging the partial derivative within each bin
  • Unbiased even with correlated features
  • Faster than PDP (no need to predict on all data)
  • Less intuitive to interpret (accumulated effect, not direct effect)
ALE is the theoretically correct alternative to PDP when features are correlated. It avoids impossible data combinations by only looking at local perturbations. The trade-off is interpretability — ALE shows accumulated effects, which are less intuitive than PDP's marginal effects.

Counterfactual Explanations

Advanced

"What is the smallest change to the input that would change the prediction?"

Wachter et al. (2017): Instead of "why was I rejected?", ask "what would I need to change to be approved?"
\arg\min_{\mathbf{x}'} \; \lambda(f(\mathbf{x}') - y')^2 + d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}')

Find the closest input x' (in distance d) that changes the prediction to the desired outcome y'.

Example: "Your loan was rejected. If your annual income were $5,000 higher OR your credit utilisation 10% lower, you would be approved."

Counterfactuals are actionable — they tell the person what to change, not just why they were rejected.

Counterfactual explanations are the most actionable form of explainability. They answer the natural human question: "What would I need to change?" This is increasingly required by regulation (EU AI Act's "right to explanation").

A/B Testing Models

Theory

To compare a new model against the current production model:

  1. Route a random fraction of traffic to the new model (e.g., 10%)
  2. Measure the business metric (not just ML metric!) for both groups
  3. Run for a statistically significant duration (power analysis from M2)
  4. If the new model wins, gradually increase its traffic share (canary deployment)
ML metrics ≠ business metrics. A model with better AUC might not improve revenue, conversion, or user satisfaction. Always measure the metric that matters to the business.
A/B testing models is A/B testing applied to ML. The principles from M2 apply directly: randomisation, sample size calculation, statistical significance. The key addition: always measure the BUSINESS metric, not just the model metric.

  • Always split before resampling (SMOTE after split!)
  • Use the right metric for the right problem (F1 for imbalanced, PR-AUC, MCC)
  • Every model needs a model card and a lifecycle
If you followed the math:
  • Bias-variance decomposition explains why regularisation and ensembles work
  • XGBoost's 2nd-order Taylor expansion gives it an edge over standard boosting
  • SHAP is the unique allocation satisfying the four Shapley axioms
  • Brier score is a proper scoring rule; accuracy is not
For the experts:
  • Double descent means the classical bias-variance picture is incomplete
  • Conformal prediction provides distribution-free uncertainty guarantees
  • NGBoost extends boosting to probabilistic prediction via natural gradients
  • ALE plots fix PDP's correlation problem with local effects
