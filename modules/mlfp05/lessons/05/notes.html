Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.5Generative Models: GANs and Diffusion

Total time: ~90 minutes  ·  16 slides  ·  ~5–6 min per slide

This lesson covers three generative paradigms: GANs (with DCGAN and WGAN-GP variants), and diffusion models. The VAE from 5.1 is the third. Students should leave with a clear decision framework for when to use each. The training instability of GANs is the main teaching challenge — show the mode collapse diagram and WGAN fix side by side. The DDPM section is conceptual (no from-scratch implementation) — enough to understand the idea and compare with GANs.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Generative Models: GANs and Diffusion

Frame: "In 5.1 we generated images with a VAE — reconstruction loss plus KL regulariser. Today we meet two alternatives: GANs (adversarial game) and diffusion models (progressive denoising). By the end you will know when to use each and how to evaluate them."

→ "The GAN setup."
Slide 2~5 minGAN setup

The GAN setup

Walk the diagram. "Two networks: the generator creates fake samples from noise, the discriminator classifies real vs fake. They train against each other — the generator tries to fool the discriminator, the discriminator tries not to be fooled. When training succeeds, the generator produces samples indistinguishable from real data."

Counterfeiter analogy: "The generator is a counterfeiter making fake banknotes. The discriminator is a bank teller detecting fakes. Both get better over time until the fakes are perfect."
→ "The formal objective."
Slide 3~5 minObjective

GAN minimax objective

Walk the formula. "D maximises: it wants to correctly identify real (log D(x) high) and fake (log(1-D(G(z))) high). G minimises: it wants D to classify fakes as real (log(1-D(G(z))) low). The alternating training steps are critical — D must stay slightly ahead of G."

→ "DCGAN architecture."
Slide 4~5 minDCGAN

DCGAN architecture

Walk the pipeline. "The generator uses transposed convolutions to upsample noise into an image. Key rules: no max pooling (use strided convolutions), no fully connected layers, batch norm everywhere except the output layer, Tanh activation at the output." These rules were empirically discovered and remain the standard for convolutional generators.

→ "What goes wrong."
Slide 5~5 minMode collapse

Mode collapse

Walk the side-by-side diagram. "Left: healthy training — diverse samples covering the data distribution. Right: mode collapse — the generator found one output that fools D and repeats it with minor variations. This is the most common GAN failure mode."

→ "WGAN fixes this."
Slide 6~6 minWGAN

WGAN — Wasserstein distance

Walk the formula. "WGAN replaces the JS divergence with the Wasserstein distance. This provides gradients everywhere — even when the distributions do not overlap. The gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) enforces the Lipschitz constraint without weight clipping. The result: more stable training, a loss curve that actually correlates with sample quality, and reduced mode collapse."

For experts: the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality gives us the dual form that is tractable. The 1-Lipschitz constraint is enforced via gradient penalty rather than the original weight clipping (which had its own issues).
→ "Diffusion models."
Slide 7~6 minDDPM

DDPM — Denoising Diffusion

Walk the diagram left to right (forward process: add noise progressively until pure Gaussian), then right to left (reverse process: a UNet learns to predict and remove the noise at each step). "Diffusion models are more stable than GANs — no minimax game, no mode collapse. The cost: sampling is slow because you need many denoising steps. Active research (DDIM, consistency models) is making them faster."

→ "How do we evaluate?"
Slide 8~4 minFID

FID evaluation

Walk the formula. "FID compares the statistics (mean and covariance) of real vs generated features extracted by a pre-trained Inception network. Lower is better. FID = 0 means the distributions are identical. Always report FID — visual inspection is subjective."

→ "The decision framework."
Slide 9~4 minComparison

When to use what

Walk the table. "VAE: smooth latent space for interpolation, but outputs are blurry. GAN: sharp images and fast sampling, but training is unstable. Diffusion: best quality and diversity, but slow. For synthetic data generation where quality matters, diffusion. For real-time generation where speed matters, GAN. For controlled generation with latent space manipulation, VAE."

→ "Let's see the code."
Slides 10–11~8 minCode

DCGAN and WGAN-GP code

Walk the training loops. Point out the key differences: "DCGAN uses binary cross-entropy and a sigmoid in D. WGAN-GP removes the sigmoid (critic, not discriminator), uses the Wasserstein loss, and adds the gradient penalty. The GP computation requires autograd — let PyTorch compute it for you."

→ "Singapore applications."
Slide 12~3 minApplications

Singapore applications

Walk all three. The synthetic financial data use case resonates most — "Singapore's banking regulations (MAS) restrict data sharing. GANs can generate realistic transaction data that preserves statistical properties without exposing real customer records."

→ "Mistakes."
Slide 13~3 minMistakes

Common mistakes

Walk all four. #1 is the most common: training G too aggressively causes D to lose its discriminative ability, and then G has no gradient signal to improve from.
