Speaker Notes — Lesson 6.3 Preference Alignment — DPO and GRPO

Total time: ~60 minutes · 19 slides · ~3 min per slide average

This is the most math-heavy lesson in M6. The core move is the derivation of DPO from the RLHF objective — walk it slowly, on the whiteboard if possible, and let students see that DPO is not "different from RLHF" but rather "RLHF's closed-form solution". Once they see that, the rest of the lesson (GRPO, evaluation) slots in easily.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Preference Alignment — DPO and GRPO

Open with the framing: "Yesterday you made the model fit Singapore domain Q&A. Today we make it good. Fitting is not the same as being good."

Deliverable: one DPO run that takes the SFT adapter from 6.2 and measurably raises the refusal rate on a set of unsafe Singapore prompts without raising it on benign ones.

→ "Why SFT alone is not enough."
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

Why alignment is not just more fine-tuning

Read the MAS derivative quote aloud. Let them sit with it. "What does SFT tell the model? Answer the question in the shape of the training data. What does alignment tell the model? Prefer responses that a human auditor would approve of over responses that a human auditor would flag."

Introduce pairwise preferences as the native data shape. "It is much easier for a labeller to pick A over B than to score A on a scale of 1 to 10. Human judgement is naturally comparative."

→ "The classical recipe — three stages."
Slide 3~3 minRLHF three stages

Classical RLHF — three stages

Walk the diagram left to right. Stage 1 is what you did yesterday — SFT. Stage 2 trains a separate reward model on preference pairs. Stage 3 runs PPO to optimise the policy against the reward model.

Tell them why the community wanted a shortcut: "Stage 2's reward model can be exploited — the policy finds prompts that maximise reward without actually being good. Stage 3's PPO has many hyperparameters and is unstable. Two separate networks, two separate training loops, many ways to fail."

→ "The RLHF objective, written out."
Slide 4~3 minRLHF objective

The RLHF objective

Read the objective aloud. "Maximise expected reward under the policy, minus beta times KL to the reference. Beta controls how far we let the policy drift from SFT."

Point out the KL as a soft constraint — it does not forbid drift, it penalises it. "The higher beta is, the harder it is to drift. Low beta is aggressive alignment; high beta is conservative alignment."

For experts: recognise this as a KL-regularised bandit problem. The closed-form solution on the next slide is standard stat-mech — the maximum-entropy policy under a linear-in-reward constraint.
→ "The key insight — closed form."
Slide 5~4 minClosed form

Closed-form optimum

This is the linchpin. Walk the formula slowly. "The optimum policy is the reference policy, reweighted by the exponent of reward over beta, normalised by Z." Pause. "What this means: given the reference and the reward, you can write down the optimum without training. Training is just a way to find the policy that matches this formula."

Then rearrange for r_phi. "Rewards are implicitly determined by the ratio of the optimum policy to the reference." Pause again. "In other words, the reward model is redundant — if we know pi_ref and pi_star, we know r_phi up to a prompt-dependent constant."

If beginners are lost: "Do not try to memorise the formula. The takeaway is: the optimal aligned policy has a closed-form relationship to the reference and the reward. That means we can train without ever building the reward network."
→ "Bradley-Terry turns the ratio into a probability."
Slide 6~3 minBradley-Terry

Bradley-Terry preference model

Introduce the Bradley-Terry model. "Given two items with scores, the probability that one beats the other is the sigmoid of the score difference. It is the standard model for pairwise preferences — used in chess Elo rankings long before LLMs."

Substitute our expression for r_phi. Pause at the Z(x) cancellation. "Notice that Z(x) depends only on x, not on y. When you subtract r_phi(y_w) from r_phi(y_l), the Z(x) terms cancel. That means we never have to compute the partition function. The reward model vanishes."

→ "And here is the DPO loss — almost free."
Slide 7~3 minDPO loss

The DPO loss

Read the loss aloud. "Negative log of sigmoid of beta times the difference in log-ratios of chosen to reference, minus log-ratios of rejected to reference. Take the expectation over preference pairs. Minimise."

Deliver the punchline: "DPO turns preference alignment into binary classification. The classifier's weights are the aligned policy's weights. No reward model. No PPO. No critic. One loss, one optimiser, done."

For experts: mention that DPO is a special case of a broader family called preference optimisation, alongside IPO, KTO, ORPO. All start from the same RLHF objective with different assumptions about the preference model.
→ "Let's run it."
Slide 8~2 minDPO config

DPO in 15 lines of config

Walk the config. Highlight three things: (1) method="dpo" — the only meaningful change from SFT, (2) dpo_beta=0.1 — the new hyperparameter, (3) learning_rate=5e-6 — 40x smaller than SFT.

Explain the LR difference: "DPO's gradients are larger than SFT's because the loss is a log-sigmoid of a log-ratio difference. Same learning rate as SFT would blow up training in the first few steps."

→ "And the one hyperparameter that matters."
Slide 9~3 minBeta

Choosing $\beta$

Walk the table. Low beta = aggressive, high beta = conservative. Default to 0.1.

Tell them the tuning rule: "Only tune beta if (a) training plateaus with no eval gain, or (b) the eval-set MMLU score drops noticeably. Otherwise leave it at 0.1."

→ "GRPO is 2024-25's evolution."
Slide 10~4 minGRPO

GRPO — 2024–25's upgrade

Walk the diagram. "DPO uses two samples per prompt and a binary label. GRPO uses G samples per prompt and real-valued scores. The extra samples carry more information per rollout."

The key trick: no separate value network. "In PPO you needed a critic to estimate the baseline reward. GRPO replaces the critic with the group mean — the advantage of each sample is just its reward minus the average reward of its siblings."

Name the provenance: "This is the DeepSeek recipe. DeepSeek-Math was the first big win in 2024, DeepSeek-R1 in January 2025 pushed it to the state of the art on reasoning benchmarks."

→ "The objective, compared to PPO."
Slide 11~3 minGRPO objective

GRPO objective

Walk the loss. "If you know PPO, this is PPO without a critic and with group-normalised advantages. The min-of-clipped-ratio is the same PPO trick to bound per-step updates. The KL-to-reference keeps the policy from collapsing."

Tell them when it matters: "GRPO shines on tasks where you can automatically score a response — unit test pass/fail, math answer correctness, rubric-based grading. Human-labelled pairwise preferences do not benefit as much; DPO is the right tool for that regime."

→ "Quick comparison."
Slide 12~2 minDPO vs GRPO table

DPO vs GRPO — when to use which

Walk the table. The summary: "DPO for pairwise human feedback, GRPO for rollout-based automatic reward. On Singapore domain safety (this lesson), you almost certainly want DPO because the feedback is human pairs."

→ "Training is half the job. Evaluation is the other half."
Slide 13~3 minLLM-as-judge biases

LLM-as-judge — biases to know

Walk the three biases. Position: the judge prefers whichever response appears in position A or B. Verbosity: longer responses look more authoritative. Self-enhancement: judges prefer responses from their own model family.

Give the remediation for each. Position → swap and average. Verbosity → normalise length or instruct for conciseness. Self-enhancement → use a different judge family.

For experts: Liang et al. 2023 quantified these biases on MT-Bench. Position bias is typically 5–15% of the judgement variance; verbosity bias is smaller but persistent.
→ "Standard benchmarks."
Slide 14~2 minBenchmarks

Standard benchmarks — the big four

Walk the list. MMLU is the headline general-knowledge benchmark. HellaSwag catches memorisation. HumanEval tests code. MT-Bench tests conversation.

Tell them the rule: "Run all four before and after alignment. Expect task-specific metrics to improve and general-capability benchmarks to stay flat. If MMLU drops, you have catastrophic forgetting — stop or raise beta."

→ "How to run them."
Slide 15~2 minlm-eval-harness

lm-eval-harness in 10 lines

Walk the code. Point out the peft argument — it loads the LoRA adapter on top of the base model, so you get the aligned policy under evaluation. This is how the registry pays off: one line and you are benchmarking the thing you trained.

→ "Now measure the actual safety win."
Slide 16~3 minRefusal detection

Measure the safety win

Walk the code. Simple substring match on common refusal phrases. Not perfect, but it catches 90%+ of real refusals with zero cost.

Give them expected numbers: "SFT-only model might refuse 30% of unsafe Singapore prompts because it learned some polite refusals from its generic instruction data. After DPO, that should jump to 85%+. A 50-point delta is the quantitative evidence that your alignment run worked."

→ "And the critical sanity check."
Slide 17~3 minBenign sanity check

Sanity check: benign refusal rate

This is the guardrail. "A model that refuses every prompt has a 100% refusal rate on unsafe prompts — and is useless. You must also measure refusal rate on benign prompts and verify it stays near zero."

Name the failure mode: "catastrophic refusal". Give the fix: stop training or raise beta. "There is a Pareto frontier between safety and helpfulness. Your job is to find the operating point your product needs, not the extreme."

→ "Four things that will bite you."
Slide 18~3 minCommon mistakes

Four common mistakes

Walk each one. Wrong LR is the most common day-one error. Noisy preferences is the subtle one that quietly ruins results. Self- enhancement judging is the inflation trap. Catastrophic refusal is the "my alignment worked too well" trap.

→ "Reflect and move on."
Slide 19~3 minReflection / bridge

What you can now do

Read the bullets. Pause on "derive the DPO loss from the RLHF objective" — that is the mathematical unlock of this lesson.

Tease 6.4: "Sometimes the model does not need to be re-trained — it just needs the right facts in front of it. Lesson 6.4 builds a full RAG pipeline: chunk documents, embed them, retrieve the most relevant chunks, inject them into the prompt, and let the model generate a grounded answer. We use Singapore regulatory documents as the corpus. By the end you will know when to reach for fine-tuning and when to reach for RAG — they solve different problems."

Take 5 minutes for questions. Common ones: "Which beta should I start with?" (0.1), "Can I combine SFT and DPO?" (yes — train SFT first, then DPO on the SFT model), "When should I use GRPO instead?" (when you have automatic scoring), "How do I know my judge is unbiased?" (measure position-swap flip rate).

→ End of Lesson 6.3.
