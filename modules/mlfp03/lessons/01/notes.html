Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.1 Feature Engineering, ML Pipeline, and Feature Selection

Total time: ~75 minutes  ·  16 slides  ·  ~4.5 min per slide average

This is the first lesson of the ML engine room. Most learners finished Module 2 comfortable with linear regression and statistics but nervous about moving to "real" ML. Your job is to signal that the transition is mostly a shift in objective and a new discipline around features — not a mountain of new algorithms. Model algorithms start in Lesson 3.2.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Welcome to the ML engine room

Open with the framing sentence: "Module 2 gave you one model. Module 3 gives you the whole zoo — but today we do not train a single model. Today we build the raw material every model in the zoo needs: features." That framing lowers the anxiety of learners who feared Module 3 would throw twenty algorithms at them on day one.

For nervous beginners: "If you finished Module 2 you already know 80% of what happens in Module 3. We just swap the loss from MSE to log-loss and add more model families. The rest is the discipline we learn today."
→ "Before the zoo, the single most important principle."
Slide 2~3 minData > Models

Data > Models > Hyperparameters

Drive the headline home. "I will say this at least three times in Module 3 and I will keep meaning it: data beats models, and features beat data." Give a real example from your own experience — a project where a carefully engineered feature made a 0.05 AUC jump that no amount of XGBoost tuning could match.

Tell them today's dataset is a real Singapore ICU cohort. The target is in-hospital mortality. The prediction is made 24 hours after admission — that 24-hour deadline is what makes the feature-engineering discipline non-trivial.

→ "Where does feature engineering sit in the full pipeline?"
Slide 3~4 minThe seven-stage pipeline

The seven-stage ML pipeline

Walk the diagram left to right, naming each stage and saying who owns it. "Ingest — your data engineer or you. Preprocess — cleaning, imputation, encoding. Feature engineering — today's lesson, stage three. Feature selection — today's lesson, stage four. Training — lessons 3.2 through 3.4. Evaluation — lesson 3.5. Deploy — lessons 3.7 and 3.8."

Emphasise the arrows. "Every single arrow is a potential leakage site. The most common place a leakage bug hides is in the arrow between feature engineering and training — a feature that was computed on the whole dataset before you split."

For experienced folks: "Yes, stages 3 and 4 can overlap — embedded feature selection does both in a single fit. We'll come back to that on slide 7."
→ "First, the objective function that justifies everything."
Slide 4~4 minStats vs ML

Statistics vs ML — two different objective functions

Read the equation aloud: "minimise the expected loss over the true data distribution". Point out that we never see $\mathcal{D}$ directly — that's what makes ML hard. We see a sample from $\mathcal{D}$, fit on that sample, and hope to generalise.

The contrast: "In Module 2 we reported coefficients — a statistical exercise. In Module 3 we do not care what the coefficients say; we care what the model does on next week's applicants. Same math, different goal, different feature-engineering rules."

If the expectation notation is scary: "Read the big E as 'average over all possible new customers we haven't seen yet'. Our job is to make that average loss small. We can't compute it exactly, so we estimate it with cross-validation."
→ "So what kinds of features should we engineer?"
Slide 5~4 minFour families

Four feature families that cover most of the work

Walk each row. For aggregation, use the ICU example: "we have 200 heart-rate readings in the first 24 hours; the model wants one row per admission; so we compute mean, std, max and forget the raw series." For lag, use Module 1's rainfall example: "yesterday's rain predicts today's rain, seven days ago predicts today." For interaction, use the medical cliché age × systolic_bp.

The most important row is domain-derived. "The shock index — heart rate divided by systolic blood pressure — is a feature you would never discover from 5000 rows of data, but every ICU clinician knows it. Your job as an ML engineer is to talk to those clinicians."

→ "Now the most expensive mistake in ML."
Slide 6~5 minLeakage timeline

Point-in-time correctness

Slow down here. Leakage is the single biggest mistake in applied ML, and if learners leave today understanding how to spot it you have justified the whole lesson.

Walk the timeline. "The prediction is made at t = 24 hours. Everything to the left of that line — age, sex, comorbidities, first-24h vitals — is legal. Everything to the right — length of stay, total medications administered over the whole admission, the final ICD-10 code — is a crime scene. If your model uses it, your CV score will be beautiful and your deployment will fail."

Give a war story: "I once worked on a model that predicted hospital mortality with 99.2% AUC. Best model I ever trained. Turned out one of the features was 'was the patient admitted to palliative care' — which is only set in the discharge record. The model had memorised death. That's leakage."

For confused beginners: "Think of it as a rule: if the feature uses a number from after the moment you are predicting, it is cheating. Draw the timeline, put a vertical line at the prediction moment, and everything to the right is illegal."
→ "Now — how do we pick the best subset of the features we did engineer?"
Slide 7~5 minThree selection families

Filter / wrapper / embedded

Walk the three definitions. Emphasise the tradeoff: filter methods are milliseconds per feature but ignore interactions; wrapper methods catch interactions but retrain the model every time you add or drop a feature; embedded methods are one fit but tied to a specific model family.

The punchline is the intersection trick: run all three, keep features that survive all three rankings as your robust core, and use the union of the three as your candidate pool for hyperparameter tuning. This is a real practitioner pattern, not a textbook cliché.

For experts who ask about SHAP-based selection: "Yes, SHAP-based selection is an advanced embedded method. We'll see it in Lesson 3.6 alongside interpretability. For today stick with L1 sparsity and tree importance."
→ "The go-to filter statistic is mutual information."
Slide 8~4 minMutual information

Mutual information — the default filter

Read the equation. "$H(Y)$ is the entropy of $Y$ — how uncertain we are about the label. $H(Y \mid X)$ is the entropy of $Y$ after we learn $X$. If $X$ is perfectly predictive, $H(Y \mid X) = 0$ and $I(X; Y) = H(Y)$. If $X$ is useless, $H(Y \mid X) = H(Y)$ and $I(X; Y) = 0$."

Then the two wins over Pearson correlation. "First, MI captures non-linear dependencies — Pearson is zero for $Y = X^2$ when $X$ is symmetric; MI is not. Second, MI is invariant to monotonic transforms — scaling or log-transforming a feature doesn't change its MI, which is exactly what you want in a pre-model scoring step."

→ "And for categorical targets with categorical features, chi-squared."
Slide 9~3 minChi-squared

Chi-squared — when the target is categorical

Explain the contingency-table intuition. "Count how often each feature value co-occurs with each target value. Compare those counts to what you'd expect if they were independent. Sum the squared differences, normalised by the expected counts. Big number = feature and target are not independent = the feature is predictive."

Don't get into the chi-squared distribution. They'll see it again in statistics courses. For today it is just a scoring function.

→ "Enough theory — let's meet the Kailash engines."
Slide 10~4 minKailash engines

FeatureEngineer, PreprocessingPipeline, FeatureStore

Introduce the three verbs. "FeatureEngineer generates columns. PreprocessingPipeline chains transforms with train-only fitting enforced. FeatureStore registers a schema so inference time can validate incoming batches before they hit the model."

Walk the code block. Point out the declarative style: .aggregate(...).then_join(...).then_derive(...). That is the Kailash polars-native pipeline idiom you will see all module.

→ "Now the worked example on real ICU data."
Slide 11~5 minClip to 24h

Load and clip vitals to the first 24 hours

Walk the code. "Join vitals onto admissions so every vital row knows its admission's start time. Compute hours since admission. Filter to keep only readings within the first 24 hours."

Emphasise again: "This filter is where point-in-time correctness is enforced. If I forget it, my hr_mean feature would include readings from hour 48 — information the real model will never have."

For beginners worried about dates: "We already cast both columns to datetime at the top of the script. Polars subtracts datetimes and gives you a duration; .dt.total_hours() converts it to a number."
→ "Now collapse the time series into a fixed row."
Slide 12~5 minAggregate

Aggregate into a fixed-shape row per admission

Walk the agg list. Mean, std, max of heart rate; mean and min of systolic BP; min of SpO₂; count of readings. "Each of these is a legitimate, clinically meaningful summary. And that last one — n_vital_readings — is the informative-missingness trick."

Tell them: "In clinical data, how often a patient is measured is itself a signal. Sicker patients get monitored every 15 minutes. Stable patients get measured once an hour. A tree-based model will happily split on this count and pick up on it as a sickness proxy. This is one of those features you wouldn't invent if you weren't paying attention to your domain."

→ "And now the domain-derived feature."
Slide 13~4 minShock index

Shock index — a clinical ratio

"The shock index is heart rate divided by systolic BP. Clinicians have used it since the 1960s as an early warning for circulatory collapse. A normal patient sits around 0.5–0.7. A patient above 1.0 is in trouble. This ratio is a better predictor of ICU mortality than either of its inputs on their own — because a tachycardia of 110 is worrying if the BP is 90, but benign if the BP is 140."

"Could a random forest learn this split on its own? Maybe, with enough data. In our 5000-row dataset, encoding the ratio directly is the difference between 0.78 AUC and 0.81 AUC. Domain knowledge is a shortcut through statistical learning."

→ "Now the three selection methods, side by side."
Slide 14~5 minThree selections

Filter / wrapper / embedded — run them all

Walk the three code blocks. For each one say what it does in plain English: "Mutual information ranks every column against the target. RFE starts with all columns, fits logistic regression, drops the least important one, refits, and repeats until only ten are left. Random forest fits once and returns an importance score per feature."

Then the punchline: "They will disagree. That is not a bug, that is a feature. The filter ranks age high because it has strong MI with death. The wrapper ranks shock_index high because logistic regression loves it. The embedded RF ranks hr_std high because it captures instability. Different lenses, different rankings. Take the intersection as your robust core."

→ "Three mistakes to not make."
Slide 15~4 minCommon mistakes

Three expensive mistakes

Walk each one. Mistake 1 (scaler leakage) catches everyone. "If you fit StandardScaler on the full dataset before you split, the mean and std were computed using test data. You have leaked. Always fit on train only."

Mistake 2 (future features) is what the timeline slide was about. Repeat the war story if you didn't already: "99.2% AUC because one feature was set in the discharge record. Leakage."

Mistake 3 (selection on full data) is the subtle one. "If you rank features on the full data and then split, your selection already saw the test labels. The right order is: split, then fit the selector on the train split, then apply the same selector to the test split."

→ "Reflection and bridge to 3.2."
Slide 16~3 minReflection + bridge

What you can now do

Run the bullet list. Pause after each one — let learners silently tick them off. The psychological payoff is important: Module 3 feels huge, and they just mastered a meaningful chunk of it in 75 minutes.

Then tease Lesson 3.2: "Today we engineered features. Tomorrow we ask: given a feature set, why does a model with more parameters not always win? The answer is the bias-variance decomposition, and once you see it you will understand why every ML training loop looks the way it does. We will also derive L1 and L2 regularisation geometrically — the diamond and the circle — and you will see where all the sparsity properties of Lasso come from."

→ End of Lesson 3.1. Take a 10-minute break.
