Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.6 Logistic Regression and Classification Foundations

~60 minutes · 14 slides · ~4 min per slide

Logistic regression is the most practically important model in MLFP. Every bank credit decision, every clinical risk score, every churn prediction you will meet uses it or a close cousin. Land three things today: the sigmoid shape, the odds ratio interpretation, and the numerical MLE pattern (which reappears in Module 4 for neural nets).

Slide 1~1 minTitle

Logistic Regression and Classification

Open: "Linear regression is for continuous outcomes. But half the interesting questions in business and medicine are binary — did the customer convert, did the patient survive, will the loan default. Today: logistic regression. Same coefficient-and-$t$-test story, different link function."

→ "Why can't we just use linear regression?"
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

Binary outcomes — why not linear?

Three reasons. One, linear regression will predict probabilities outside $[0, 1]$ — nonsense. Two, the variance of a binary outcome is $p(1-p)$, not constant — so OLS standard errors are wrong. Three, the right likelihood is Bernoulli, and MLE under Bernoulli gives us logistic regression directly.

→ "Enter the sigmoid."
Slide 3~3 minSigmoid picture

The sigmoid

Walk the curve. "At $z=0$, probability is 0.5. As $z$ goes positive, probability climbs toward 1 — asymptotically. As $z$ goes negative, it drops toward 0. It's S-shaped — the middle is approximately linear, the tails flatten out."

"This is the function that squashes the linear predictor into a valid probability. Every logistic regression you'll ever see is: take the linear combination $\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots$, push it through sigmoid, get a probability."

→ "Where does the sigmoid come from?"
Slide 4~4 minLog-odds derivation

From log-odds to sigmoid

Do the derivation live. "Start with the assumption: the log-odds is a linear function of the predictors. Log-odds lives in the full real line, so this is a sensible thing to model with linear regression."

Then solve for $p$. Four algebra steps. "$p/(1-p) = e^z$. Multiply out. Collect. Divide. You get $p = 1/(1 + e^{-z})$. That's the sigmoid."

If beginners freeze on algebra: "You don't need to redo this at home. What you need is: log-odds in, sigmoid out. The derivation is on the slide for reference."
→ "The likelihood."
Slide 5~4 minBernoulli likelihood

Bernoulli log-likelihood

Write the Bernoulli. "For each observation, the probability of seeing $y_i$ is $p^{y_i}(1-p)^{1-y_i}$. When $y_i = 1$, that's $p$; when $y_i = 0$, that's $1 - p$. One formula for both cases."

Take log and sum. "Sum over all observations gives the log-likelihood. But here's the problem: the gradient with respect to $\beta$ is not linear — the sigmoid makes it non-linear. No closed form."

Connection back: "In 2.2 I told you we'd come back to scipy.optimize. Here we are. Logistic regression is why you learned the numerical MLE pattern."

For experts: mention that the loss is convex — so there's a unique global minimum. BFGS or Newton-Raphson both find it reliably. With $L_2$ regularisation (coming in M3), you also get strict convexity and numerical stability.
→ "Interpret the coefficients."
Slide 6~4 minOdds ratios

Odds ratios — the multiplicative story

Walk the derivation on the slide. "A one-unit increase in $x_j$ adds $\beta_j$ to the log-odds. Exponentiate: it multiplies the odds by $e^{\beta_j}$. That number is called the odds ratio."

Read the table. "+0.69 → OR 2.0, odds double. −0.22 → OR 0.80, odds drop 20%. Zero → odds unchanged. These are the reporting units for logistic regression in medicine and finance."

→ "But odds ≠ probability."
Slide 7~3 minOdds not probability

Odds ≠ probability

This is the trap. "Doctor tells a patient 'this drug doubles your odds of recovery'. Patient hears 'doubles my probability'. They're different. At baseline 10%, doubling the odds goes 1/9 → 2/9, which is 18% — not 20%."

Tell them to always convert back. "When reporting results to clinicians or product managers, give them the predicted probabilities at representative inputs. Don't leave them to compute $e^\beta / (1 + e^\beta)$ in their heads."

→ "Evaluation."
Slide 8~3 minConfusion matrix

Evaluation: confusion matrix

Walk the 2x2. "Threshold probabilities at 0.5 to get binary predictions. Then count TN, FP, FN, TP. Accuracy is the diagonal sum divided by total. Precision is TP over predicted positives — 'how often was I right when I said yes'. Recall is TP over actual positives — 'how many of the real yeses did I catch'."

Warn: "Accuracy is misleading on imbalanced data. If 5% of patients die in the ICU, a classifier that always predicts 'survives' has 95% accuracy and is useless. Always report the confusion matrix."

Quick mnemonic: "Precision: was I right when I pointed? Recall: did I point at everything I should have?"
→ "Hands on the ICU data."

→ "Evaluate."
Slide 10~3 minEvaluate

Worked: evaluate the classifier

Compute predictions, threshold at 0.5, confusion matrix, accuracy, AUC. Read the numbers aloud. "Accuracy 0.82. AUC 0.88. Confusion matrix shows we miss some deaths — the false negative rate is a concern in medical settings."

"For production, you might move the threshold lower than 0.5 to catch more deaths at the cost of more false alarms. The right threshold depends on business or clinical cost, not on statistics."

→ "Switching gears to ANOVA."
Slide 11~4 minANOVA

ANOVA: generalising the $t$-test

Motivate: "With two groups, you run a $t$-test. With four groups, you could run six pairwise $t$-tests — but that inflates your false-positive rate. ANOVA asks one question instead: are any of the groups different?"

Walk the variance decomposition. "Total variance splits into between-group and within-group. If the groups are really different, between-group variance dominates. The $F$-statistic is the ratio."

Key fact: "With two groups, ANOVA's $F$ equals $t^2$. ANOVA is not a different test — it's the same test generalised."

→ "And Tukey tells you which groups."
Slide 12~3 minTukey HSD

Post-hoc: Tukey's HSD

Walk the formula. "HSD is a threshold. Any pair of means whose difference exceeds HSD is significantly different, with family-wise error control built in. No Bonferroni needed."

Show the statsmodels one-liner. "This is what you'll use in practice. The math is under the hood."

→ "Run it on HDB."
Slide 13~3 minHDB ANOVA

Worked: HDB prices across flat types

Walk the code. ANOVA first, huge $F$ and tiny $p$ because the sample is large and the differences are obvious. Then Tukey to get the pairwise table.

"All six pairs will come back significant because the groups are genuinely different. In smaller samples or smaller effects, Tukey is where you find the actual story."
