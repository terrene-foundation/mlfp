Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.5 Model Evaluation, Imbalance, and Calibration

Total time: ~75 minutes · 14 slides · ~5 min per slide average

This is the "stop losing money to bad metrics" lesson. Open it by framing evaluation as a business decision tool, not a report card. The running cost matrix (100:1) gives every concept a concrete dollar number to bite on.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Opening

Frame the stakes: "So far in Module 3 we have been trying to make classifiers better. Today we ask a harder question: how do we know whether we actually did? With imbalanced data and uneven costs, the usual metrics lie to you. This lesson is where evaluation becomes an engineering discipline."

→ "Start at the foundation."
Slide 2~3 minConfusion matrix

The confusion matrix

Walk the four cells. Name each one twice: "True positive — correctly caught a defaulter. True negative — correctly passed a good loan. False positive — false alarm, we rejected a good loan. False negative — we approved a loan that then defaulted." Put the cost sticker on FP and FN: $100 and $10 000.

→ "Every metric is a ratio of these four cells."
Slide 3~4 minFormulas

Precision, Recall, F1

Walk the equations. "Precision: of everyone we flagged, how many were right? Recall: of everyone who actually defaulted, how many did we catch? F1 is the harmonic mean — it punishes extreme lopsidedness between the two."

Then the bomb: "Predict 'no default' for everyone. Accuracy = 88%. F1 = 0. You cannot tell from accuracy alone that the model is useless. That's why accuracy is banned in this lesson."

For beginners: "Harmonic mean means F1 is dragged down by whichever of precision and recall is smaller. So to get high F1 both must be decent. You can't game it with one extreme value."
→ "And now the two curves every analyst needs."
Slide 4~5 minROC vs PR curves

ROC vs PR

Walk both diagrams. "ROC plots true positive rate against false positive rate as the threshold moves. PR plots precision against recall. They show the same model but emphasise different cells."

The critical point: "Point at ROC — 0.87 AUC, looks gorgeous. Point at PR — 0.48 average precision, honest number. On a 12%-positive dataset, ROC-AUC of 0.87 can mean the model only catches 10% of defaults at 50% precision. That is not 'great performance'. It's '10% of defaulters caught, half of flags are false alarms'. That is a very different story."

→ "Why ROC lies on imbalanced data."
Slide 5~4 minWhy ROC fails

The math behind the lie

"FPR is false positives divided by false positives plus true negatives. When the negative class dominates, TN is enormous, so even 1000 false positives barely moves FPR. The ROC curve still hugs the corner. Meanwhile precision, which is TP over TP plus FP, is catastrophic."

"PR-AUC has no such blind spot. It completely ignores TN. Every false positive hurts your precision proportionally. Use PR-AUC when your positive class is under 20 percent."

→ "Four techniques for handling imbalance."
Slide 6~5 minFour techniques

The toolbox

Walk all four. SMOTE first, with the honest caveat that it is not a free lunch. Cost-sensitive learning next, emphasising "no fake data, no distribution distortion". Focal loss third, with the promise of detail on the next slide. Threshold tuning last, with the promise of a closed-form answer on slide 8.

Tell them: "In 2026 practice, modern practitioners usually skip SMOTE and go straight to cost-sensitive learning plus threshold tuning. That is the cleanest combination. SMOTE is a legacy tool that still has niche uses but no longer leads the toolbox."

→ "Focal loss in detail."
Slide 7~4 minFocal loss

Focal loss — the down-weighting trick

Walk the formula. "Ordinary cross-entropy on an easy example — say $p_t = 0.9$ — still contributes about 0.1 to the loss. Multiply by a million easy examples and that dominates the gradient. Focal loss multiplies by $(1 - p_t)^\gamma$. At $\gamma = 2$ and $p_t = 0.9$, that factor is 0.01. The easy example's contribution drops 100x. The hard examples — $p_t$ around 0.3 — barely change. Now the gradient is dominated by the hard cases and the model keeps learning."

"Focal loss was invented for dense object detection where the imbalance is extreme — thousands of background boxes per foreground box. For credit scoring at 12% positive it helps but is not life-changing. Cost-sensitive learning is usually enough."

→ "Threshold tuning — the cheapest improvement in ML."
Slide 8~5 minCost threshold

The cost-optimal threshold

Walk the derivation. "Expected cost of predicting positive is $c_{FP}(1-p)$. Expected cost of predicting negative is $c_{FN} p$. Predict whichever is smaller. Setting them equal gives $p^* = c_{FP} / (c_{FP} + c_{FN})$."

Plug in the credit numbers. "FP cost $100, FN cost $10 000. Optimal threshold is 100 / 10100, about 0.0099. Not 0.5. Not 0.3. One percent. Any time the model gives more than 1% probability of default, flag the loan. Because the cost of missing a defaulter is 100 times the cost of a false alarm, you should be very eager to flag."

Practical impact: "Changing the threshold from 0.5 to 0.01 on an already-trained model, with no retraining, can cut your business cost in half. It is the single cheapest improvement in applied ML and almost nobody does it in the first draft."

→ "Now the last piece — calibration."
Slide 9~4 minReliability diagram

Reliability diagram

Walk the diagram. "This is how you see whether your model's probabilities are trustworthy. X-axis is what the model says. Y-axis is what the data shows. Perfect calibration is the identity line. The red curve is a typical raw gradient boosting model — over-confident, pushing predictions toward 0 and 1 more than the data justifies. The blue dashed line is after calibration, which brings the curve close to the identity."

"Why do you care? Because if you feed 'probability of default' into a downstream cost calculation and the probability is lying, your cost calculation is garbage. Calibration is what makes the model's numbers usable as real probabilities."

→ "Two ways to calibrate."
Slide 10~4 minPlatt + isotonic

Platt scaling and isotonic regression

"Platt scaling fits a sigmoid $\sigma(Ax + B)$ on top of the model scores. One parameter plus a bias. Cheap. Works when miscalibration is sigmoidal — which it often is for SVM and gradient boosting."

"Isotonic regression is non-parametric — it fits a non-decreasing piecewise-constant function. More flexible but needs more data. Use it when Platt under-performs."

Practical: "Scikit-learn's CalibratedClassifierCV wraps any base model, does the cross-validated fit for you, and returns a calibrated classifier. Three lines of code."

→ "How do we know calibration actually helped? Brier score."
Slide 11~4 minBrier score

Brier score — the honest metric

Walk the formula. "Mean squared error between predicted probability and actual label. Zero is perfect. Higher is worse."

Key property: "Brier is a proper scoring rule. The unique minimiser is reporting the true probability. A model that cheats by pushing everything to 0 or 1 gets punished. A model that reports honest uncertainty gets rewarded. That is what you want."

Report Brier before and after calibration. "On credit data, Brier often drops from 0.11 to 0.08 after isotonic calibration. That is the improvement you couldn't see from AUC-PR alone."

→ "Full pipeline, live."
Slide 12~5 minFull pipeline

End-to-end demo

Walk the four-step code. "Step 1: baseline. Step 2: add cost-sensitive weights. Step 3: derive the cost-optimal threshold. Step 4: post-hoc calibrate."

If you can run it live, do. Compare the baseline business cost (large) to the post-calibration business cost (much smaller). That dollar number is what the stakeholder cares about.

→ "Three traps."
Slide 13~3 minTraps

Three traps

Walk them. Trap 2 — SMOTE-ing the test set — is the subtle one you will catch at least one learner making during the exercise. Warn them pre-emptively.

Reflection + Lesson 3.6
