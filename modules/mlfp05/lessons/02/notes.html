Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.2 CNNs and Computer Vision

Total time: ~60 minutes  ·  17 slides  ·  ~3.5 min per slide average

This lesson has three load-bearing ideas that must land: the convolution arithmetic formula, the ResNet skip connection, and the ONNX export workflow. Everything else is context and history. If you run short on time, cut the training-recipes slide (13) and the ViT slide (15) first — those can be reviewed offline. Do not cut the ResNet block diagram or the conv arithmetic; they are the load-bearing concepts.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

CNNs and Computer Vision

Open with the promise of the lesson. "By the end of the next hour you will have built a ResNet with squeeze-and-excitation blocks — a model that would have won ImageNet four years after ResNet itself was published — and exported it to ONNX for deployment. We will walk the entire history from LeNet 1998 to ViT 2020 in one lesson."

Warm-up question: "Who has ever trained a CNN before?" If many hands: "great, this lesson goes faster on the basics and spends more time on SE blocks and ONNX." If few hands: "then we go slow on the conv formula."

→ "Start with the obvious question: why not just use an MLP?"
Slide 2~4 minWhy conv?

Why convolutions?

Walk the parameter-count example. "Flatten a 224×224 RGB image, you get 150 thousand pixels. One fully connected layer to 1000 hidden units has 150 million parameters. That is before you even stack layers. It is unaffordable, and worse — it does not work."

Explain why it does not work, not just that it does not. "An MLP sees pixel (10, 10) and pixel (10, 11) as completely unrelated inputs. It has to learn from data that they are neighbours. A convolution builds that in — the filter sees a 3×3 window, so neighbouring pixels always land in the same window."

Then translation equivariance. "A cat in the top left and the same cat in the bottom right should produce the same features. A conv layer guarantees this because the same filter slides over the whole image."

→ "That is the op. Here is a picture."
Slide 3~3 minConv diagram

The convolution op

Walk the diagram. "Input is 6×6 — the grey grid. The teal highlighted square is the receptive field of one output pixel — a 3×3 window. The kernel (also 3×3) is applied to that window by elementwise multiply and sum. Slide the kernel one column right, get the next output pixel. Slide down a row, next. After sliding over the whole input you have a 4×4 output."

Critical teaching moment: "the same kernel is used at every position. That is what 'shared weights' means. Nine weights for the whole layer, not nine weights per output pixel."

For beginners: compare to Excel conditional formatting. "A rule like 'highlight any cell where the three cells above sum to more than 100' works the same way regardless of where the cell is in the sheet. The rule is applied everywhere."
→ "One formula governs all conv arithmetic."
Slide 4~3 minConv arithmetic

Conv arithmetic — the one formula

Put the formula on the board and walk through both examples. "Same padding means stride 1 and padding equal to (F-1)/2. For a 3×3 kernel that is padding 1. Plug in: (28 - 3 + 2*1)/1 + 1 = 28. Output same size as input. Used everywhere you want the spatial size preserved."

"Halving: stride 2, padding 1, F=3. (28 - 3 + 2)/2 + 1 = 14. Output is half the input. Used everywhere you want to downsample — ResNet stages, conv autoencoder encoder, everything."

Do one worked example live: "what's the output of a 7×7 conv with stride 2 and padding 3 on a 224×224 image?" Wait for answers. Correct: 112×112. "Good — that is literally the first layer of every ResNet."

→ "Now the history tour, fast."
Slide 5~4 minHistory

A short history

Walk the table quickly. Names, years, key idea. Do not dwell on LeNet and VGG — they are historical. Focus on:

  • AlexNet: "the moment deep learning won. 2012 ImageNet challenge, they crushed the hand-crafted feature entries by 10%. Shocked everyone."
  • ResNet: "the moment deep learning got really deep. We will spend the next three slides on why."
  • ViT: "the moment computer vision stopped being exclusively convolutional."
→ "The ResNet idea is one equation."
Slide 6~4 minResNet idea

The ResNet idea

Set up the problem first. "Before ResNet, everyone believed deeper networks would always be better. Then they weren't. People ran experiments with 20-layer and 50-layer plain CNNs — the 50-layer network had worse training accuracy than the 20-layer. Not test accuracy, which would suggest overfitting — training accuracy. The optimiser literally could not fit the training data with more layers. That was the degradation problem."

Then the ResNet fix. "He et al. said: let each block learn the residual rather than the whole output. H(x) = F(x) + x. The skip connection gives the gradient a direct additive path back. In the worst case, F learns zero and the block becomes the identity, so adding depth cannot hurt. They trained a 152-layer ResNet and won ImageNet."

For experts: the deeper insight is that the skip makes the loss landscape much smoother — see Li et al 2018 "Visualising the Loss Landscape of Neural Nets". Without skips, deep nets have chaotic loss surfaces that are basically unoptimisable.
→ "Diagram to lock it in."
Slide 7~3 minBasicBlock diagram

ResNet basic block

Walk the diagram. "Input x enters. Two paths. Main path: conv+BN+ ReLU, conv+BN. Skip path: plain identity — x goes straight to the add. The two paths meet at the yellow +. Then ReLU. That is a BasicBlock."

Emphasise the yellow dashed shortcut. "This is the entire trick. One dashed arrow. That is what made 152 layers trainable."

→ "And in PyTorch it is nine lines."
Slide 8~3 minBasicBlock code

BasicBlock in PyTorch

Walk line by line. "Two Conv2d layers with kernel 3, padding 1 (so same-size), and bias=False because BatchNorm has its own bias. Two BatchNorm2d layers. In forward: save identity, run conv1+bn1+relu, run conv2+bn2, add identity, relu, return. The magic is one line: out = out + identity."

Question for the class: "what happens if the input and output have different shapes?" Wait. "Then you cannot just add. You need a 1×1 conv on the shortcut to match. The full ResNet handles that; we are keeping it simple here."

→ "SE blocks — modern upgrade, same spirit."
Slide 9~4 minSE blocks

Squeeze-and-Excitation

The motivation: "Not all channels in a feature map are equally informative for every input. For one image, the 'edge' channels matter most; for another, the 'texture' channels. SE blocks let the network decide per-input which channels matter."

Walk the three steps. Squeeze: global average pool each channel to one number. Excite: tiny MLP, bottleneck and back up, sigmoid so outputs are in (0, 1). Scale: multiply each channel by its weight.

"One-shot channel attention. Few parameters (the reduction ratio 8 is standard). 1-2% extra accuracy on ImageNet benchmarks. Works as a drop-in on any CNN — plain, ResNet, EfficientNet — all use it."

→ "Code."
Slide 10~2 minSE code

SE block code

Walk the forward method. "Squeeze: x.mean(dim=[2,3]) — average over spatial dimensions, keep batch and channel. Excite: two linear layers with a ReLU and sigmoid. Scale: broadcast back via view(b, c, 1, 1) and multiply."

Point at the view: "this is standard broadcast — (b, c, 1, 1) tensor multiplied by (b, c, H, W) tensor gives the channel-wise scaling we want."

→ "Put it all together."
Slide 11~3 minStacked model

Stacking them

Walk the architecture. "Stem: 1 channel to 16. Block 1: BasicBlock with 16 channels, SE block on output. Down: 16 to 32 channels with stride-2 conv — halves the spatial size. Block 2: 32 channels, SE. Head: global average pool to (b, 32), flatten, linear to 3 classes."

"This is a minimal ResNet-with-SE in fifteen lines. Real ResNet-18 has four such stages with growing channels. Same pattern."

→ "Training loop."
Slide 12~2 minTraining loop

Training loop

"Identical to 5.1, to 4.8, to every PyTorch training loop you will ever write. The only difference is cross-entropy instead of MSE because this is classification, not reconstruction. Everything else — optimiser, .backward, .step — is muscle memory by now."

→ "Modern training tricks in one slide."
Slide 13~3 minTraining recipes

Modern training recipes

Walk the table. Do not dwell — these are optimisations you will revisit later. The key takeaways: Kaiming init is correct for ReLU (Xavier is for tanh and is wrong), mixed precision doubles throughput on modern GPUs, Mixup and label smoothing are free accuracy, and cosine LR always helps.

For experts: mention that SGD with momentum and cosine LR outperforms Adam on ImageNet-scale ResNets, but Adam is fine for small models and faster convergence.
→ "Deployment — the Kailash engine for it."
Slide 14~3 minOnnxBridge

Exporting with OnnxBridge

Introduce the engine. "ONNX — Open Neural Network eXchange — is the standard portable format for trained models. Every production runtime supports it: NVIDIA Triton, Rust, browsers, Android, iOS. But PyTorch's native torch.onnx.export is finicky — op coverage varies, opset versions matter, and numerical drift is common. OnnxBridge wraps it with validation."

Show the three-line code. "Hand it your model (in eval mode, on CPU, trained), a sample input of the right shape, and an output path. It traces, writes, and then loads the ONNX file in ONNX Runtime and compares outputs on the sample. If they drift beyond tolerance, it raises — which saves you from shipping a silently-broken model."

→ "One slide on ViTs."
Slide 15~3 minViT

Vision Transformers in one slide

"ViT arrived in 2020 and surprised everyone. It treats an image as a sequence of flattened patches and feeds it to a Transformer encoder — exactly the architecture you will meet in 5.4 for text. No convolutions at all."

The caveat: "ViTs need enormous datasets. On ImageNet alone, ResNet-50 still beats a plain ViT-B because CNNs have a stronger inductive bias — translation equivariance is baked in. ViTs have to learn it from data. Once you scale to JFT-300M or similar, ViTs win."

"For most enterprise problems with thousands (not millions) of examples, a pre-trained ResNet fine-tuned on your data is still the pragmatic choice. We do that in 5.7."

→ "Four traps."
Slide 16~3 minTraps

Four traps

Walk each one. The model.eval() one is the most common bug. "If you forget it, BN uses batch statistics from the validation batch — which might have 1 sample, giving you NaN. Symptom: your validation loss is nonsense. Fix: always model.eval() before with torch.no_grad():."

The skip-connection shape trap: "when you downsample or change channels, the main path has a different shape from the input, so you cannot just add. You need a 1×1 conv on the shortcut to match."

The LR trap is short: "start at 1e-3 for Adam. If NaN in 100 steps, halve it."

The view vs flatten trap: "after adaptive pooling, some tensors are non-contiguous. view fails. torch.flatten is always safe."

→ "Wrap up."
