When to Use Unsupervised ML

Foundations

Use USML when:

  • You have no labels (or unreliable labels)
  • You need to discover structure you didn't know existed
  • You want to compress features for a downstream model
  • You need preprocessing before supervised learning
  • You want to visualise high-dimensional data

Don't use USML when:

  • You have clean labels and a clear prediction task
  • You need interpretable predictions (use M5 methods)
  • Your data is too small for pattern discovery
  • The patterns are already known (manual features suffice)
"USML is powerful but not always the right tool. If you have clean labels and a clear target, go straight to supervised methods from M5. Use USML when discovery is the goal." Timing: ~2 min.

6.2 K-Means Clustering

Find k centroids that minimise distance to cluster members

"We begin with the simplest, most widely-used clustering algorithm. K-means is to clustering what linear regression is to supervised learning: the starting point." Timing: ~1 min.

What Is Clustering?

Foundations

Imagine you have 10,000 customers and no labels. You notice some buy luxury goods, others buy bulk groceries, others only buy on sale days.

Clustering finds these natural groups automatically.

"Think of sorting a box of mixed Lego bricks by colour without being told what the colours are. You just group similar things together." Timing: ~2 min.

K-Means: The Simplest Clustering Algorithm

Foundations

Think of it as "musical chairs with data points":

  1. Pick k chairs (centroids) — place them randomly
  2. Everyone sits at their nearest chair — assign each point to the closest centroid
  3. Move each chair to the middle of its group — recompute centroids
  4. Repeat until nobody changes chairs
You choose k. That's the big decision — and getting it wrong gives you meaningless groups. We'll see how to choose k shortly.
Use the musical chairs analogy. If beginners look confused, say: 'Imagine 3 magnets on a table covered with iron filings. Each filing goes to its nearest magnet. Then you move each magnet to the centre of its filings. Repeat.' Timing: ~2 min.

Lloyd's Algorithm: K-Means Formally

Theory

Objective: Minimise the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS):

$$J = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{x_i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2$$
x_i = data point  |  μ_k = centroid of cluster k  |  C_k = set of points in cluster k
Assignment step
$$c_i = \arg\min_k \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2$$
Update step
$$\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{x_i \in C_k} x_i$$
"Notice this looks like EM already: assign (E-step), update (M-step). K-means IS a special case of EM for a simplified Gaussian mixture. We'll see the full EM later." Timing: ~3 min.

Convergence and Local Minima

Theory

Convergence guarantee

  • J is non-increasing at each step
  • Finite number of possible assignments
  • Therefore: always converges
  • But: converges to a local minimum, not necessarily the global one
Bad initialisation → bad clusters. The starting positions of centroids determine which local minimum you find.

Why local minima matter

Imagine two well-separated clusters. If both initial centroids land inside one cluster, the algorithm converges with one cluster split in half and the other containing everything else.

Solution: Smart initialisation (k-means++) or multiple restarts.

"K-means always converges, but it may converge to a terrible solution. Imagine dropping your magnets in the wrong places. The fix: k-means++ gives you smart starting positions." Timing: ~2 min.

K-Means++ Initialisation

Theory

Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007): Spread initial centroids to avoid bad starts.

  1. Pick first centroid uniformly at random
  2. For each remaining centroid, pick point with probability proportional to $D(x)^2$
  3. Points far from existing centroids are more likely chosen
$$P(x) = \frac{D(x)^2}{\sum_{x'} D(x')^2}$$
D(x) = distance from x to nearest existing centroid. Guarantees O(log k)-competitive with optimal.
Plain English: "Pick your first centroid randomly. Then pick the next one as far as possible from the first. Repeat. This spreads your centroids across the data."
"K-means++ is now the default in every library. It makes k-means much more reliable without adding significant computation." Timing: ~2 min.

Choosing k: The Elbow Method

Theory

Plot WCSS (J) vs k. Look for the "elbow" where adding more clusters gives diminishing returns.

Problem: the elbow is often ambiguous. That's why we need additional methods.

"The elbow method is intuitive but subjective. Two people can look at the same plot and disagree on where the elbow is. We need quantitative alternatives." Timing: ~2 min.

Choosing k: Silhouette & Gap Statistic

Theory

Silhouette coefficient

$$s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))}$$
a(i) = avg distance to same-cluster points
b(i) = avg distance to nearest-other-cluster points

Range [-1, 1]. Higher = better separation. Choose k with highest average silhouette.

Gap statistic (Tibshirani et al., 2001)

$$\text{Gap}(k) = E^*[\log W_k] - \log W_k$$
Compare observed WCSS to WCSS on uniformly distributed reference data

Choose smallest k where: $\text{Gap}(k) \geq \text{Gap}(k+1) - s_{k+1}$

"Silhouette gives you an intuitive score: how well does each point fit its cluster versus the next best cluster? Gap statistic compares your clustering to what you'd get from random noise." Timing: ~3 min.

Choosing k: Information-Theoretic Methods

Advanced

X-means (Pelleg & Moore, 2000)

Run k-means, then test whether each cluster should be split using BIC:

$$\text{BIC} = -2 \ln \hat{L} + p \ln n$$
Penalise model complexity. Split only if BIC improves.

G-means (Hamerly & Elkan, 2004)

Test whether each cluster's data is Gaussian using the Anderson-Darling test. If not, split it.

Key idea: k-means assumes Gaussian clusters. G-means tests that assumption and increases k where it fails.

"For experts: X-means and G-means automatically determine k without you having to run k-means repeatedly. X-means uses BIC, G-means uses a normality test. Both are principled alternatives to the elbow method." Timing: ~2 min.

K-Means Limitations

Foundations

Assumes:

  • Spherical clusters — equal in all directions
  • Equal-size clusters — small clusters absorbed by large ones
  • No outliers — outliers pull centroids away
  • Convex shapes — cannot handle crescents, rings, etc.

When it fails:

  • Elongated clusters → use GMM
  • Varying density → use HDBSCAN
  • Arbitrary shapes → use spectral clustering
  • Many outliers → use K-medoids or DBSCAN
K-means is a starting point, not a destination. When it fails, the failure mode tells you which algorithm to try next.
"K-means is like a hammer: excellent for nails, terrible for screws. The key is recognising when your data is a screw." Timing: ~2 min.

Mini-Batch K-Means

Theory

For large datasets, update centroids using random subsets (mini-batches) instead of the full dataset:

  1. Sample a mini-batch B of b points
  2. Assign each to nearest centroid
  3. Update centroids using streaming average: $\mu_k \leftarrow (1-\eta)\mu_k + \eta \bar{x}_{B,k}$
Tradeoff: Slightly worse clustering quality for dramatically faster runtime. On 1M points, mini-batch can be 100x faster than standard k-means.
"When your dataset has millions of rows, standard k-means is too slow. Mini-batch k-means gives you 95% of the quality in 1% of the time." Timing: ~2 min.

K-Medoids / PAM

Theory

Key difference: Use actual data points (medoids) instead of means as cluster centres.

PAM Algorithm

  1. Select k initial medoids
  2. Assign each point to nearest medoid
  3. For each medoid, try swapping with each non-medoid
  4. Keep the swap if total cost decreases
  5. Repeat until no swap improves cost

Advantages over K-means

  • Robust to outliers — medoid is an actual point, not pulled by extreme values
  • Works with any distance — not just Euclidean (Manhattan, cosine, etc.)
  • Interpretable — the medoid IS a real example
Scalability: PAM is O(n²k). For large datasets, use CLARA (subsample + PAM) or CLARANS (randomised swaps).
"K-medoids: instead of computing the mean (which might be in empty space), use a real data point as the centre. More robust, more interpretable, but slower." Timing: ~2 min.

Foundations

This is the bridge. Cluster labels ARE features for supervised models.

6.3 Density-Based Clustering

DBSCAN and HDBSCAN — clusters of any shape, automatic noise detection

"K-means assumes spherical clusters. Real-world data rarely cooperates. Density-based methods find clusters of ANY shape and automatically identify noise." Timing: ~1 min.

DBSCAN: Core Concepts

Foundations

Key idea: Clusters are dense regions separated by sparse regions.

Three types of points

  • Core point — has at least minPts neighbours within radius ε
  • Border point — within ε of a core point but not core itself
  • Noise point — neither core nor border

Two parameters

  • ε (epsilon) — radius of the neighbourhood
  • minPts — minimum density threshold

No k! The number of clusters is discovered automatically.

"DBSCAN asks: is this area of space crowded or empty? Crowded areas are clusters. Empty areas are boundaries. Points in the void are noise. You don't need to specify k." Timing: ~2 min.

DBSCAN Algorithm

Theory
  1. Pick an unvisited point p
  2. Find all points within ε of p (neighbourhood query)
  3. If |neighbourhood| ≥ minPts, p is core — create or expand cluster
  4. Density-reachable expansion: recursively add all core-reachable points
  5. If |neighbourhood| < minPts and p is not reachable from any core → noise
Advantages: Arbitrary cluster shapes (crescents, rings, spirals). Automatic k. Built-in noise detection. No assumption about cluster size or shape.
Limitations: Struggles with varying density (one ε cannot fit both dense and sparse clusters). Sensitive to parameter choice.
"DBSCAN's weakness: one epsilon for the whole dataset. If your data has both tightly packed clusters and loose clusters, no single epsilon works. Enter HDBSCAN." Timing: ~3 min.

Choosing ε: The k-Distance Plot

Theory

For each point, compute the distance to its k-th nearest neighbour. Sort in descending order and plot.

The "elbow" in the k-distance plot gives a good ε. Points above the elbow are noise; points below are in clusters.

"The k-distance plot is to DBSCAN what the elbow plot is to k-means. Find the sharp bend, that's your epsilon." Timing: ~2 min.

HDBSCAN: Hierarchical DBSCAN

Foundations

HDBSCAN removes the biggest weakness of DBSCAN: you don't need to choose ε.

HDBSCAN runs DBSCAN at every possible ε simultaneously and picks the most stable clusters.
Key advantage: Handles varying density. A dense urban cluster and a sparse rural cluster can coexist. Points that don't belong to any cluster are labelled as noise (-1).
"HDBSCAN is the 'just do the right thing' version of DBSCAN. Instead of you choosing epsilon, it tries all possible values and picks the clusters that persist across the widest range." Timing: ~2 min.

HDBSCAN: The Algorithm

Theory
  1. Core distance: distance to the k-th nearest neighbour: $\text{core}_k(x)$
  2. Mutual reachability distance:
    $$d_{\text{mreach}}(a,b) = \max\{\text{core}_k(a), \text{core}_k(b), d(a,b)\}$$
    Smooths out density: no two points are "closer" than their core distances allow
  3. Minimum spanning tree on mutual reachability graph
  4. Build condensed cluster tree by cutting MST edges in order
  5. Extract clusters using excess of mass (stability-based selection)
"Step by step: compute a smoothed distance, build a tree, and extract the most stable clusters. The key insight is mutual reachability distance: it ensures sparse regions don't artificially connect dense clusters." Timing: ~3 min.

HDBSCAN: Cluster Stability

Theory

The condensed cluster tree shows how clusters merge as ε increases. Stability measures how long a cluster persists:

$$\text{stability}(C) = \sum_{x \in C} \left(\lambda(x, C) - \lambda_{\text{birth}}(C)\right)$$
λ = 1/distance. Larger stability = cluster persists over a wider range of scales

Cluster selection

Walk up the tree. At each split, keep the children if their combined stability exceeds the parent's. Otherwise, keep the parent.

Soft clustering

HDBSCAN provides membership probabilities — how confident is the assignment? Low probability = borderline point.

Soft clustering → k probability features per point.

"Stability is the key concept. A cluster that only exists at one specific epsilon is fragile. A cluster that persists across many epsilon values is real." Timing: ~3 min.

HDBSCAN*: Flat Clustering Extraction

Advanced

HDBSCAN* adds a flat clustering mode via the cluster_selection_epsilon parameter:

  • If a cluster would split at a distance below the threshold, keep it merged
  • Produces clusters similar to DBSCAN at a given epsilon, but with hierarchical noise handling
  • Useful when you want "DBSCAN-like" output with HDBSCAN's robustness
Computational advances (2025): LSH-based approximate HDBSCAN achieves subquadratic runtime on high-dimensional data. Hybrid methods like FLASC (flare-sensitive clustering) extend HDBSCAN to detect elongated structures within clusters.
"For experts: the original HDBSCAN paper used excess-of-mass. The asterisk variant adds the ability to enforce a minimum cluster separation distance. Recent work on approximate HDBSCAN makes it feasible for truly massive datasets." Timing: ~2 min.

6.4 Hierarchical Clustering

Build a tree of clusters — cut at the level that makes sense

"Hierarchical clustering gives you a dendrogram: a tree that shows how clusters merge from individual points up to a single cluster. You choose where to cut." Timing: ~1 min.

Agglomerative Clustering (Bottom-Up)

Foundations
  1. Start with every point as its own cluster
  2. Find the two closest clusters and merge them
  3. Repeat until everything is one cluster
  4. Cut the dendrogram at the desired level

The key question: how do you measure "distance between clusters"?

LinkageDistance Between ClustersBehaviour
SingleMinimum distance (closest pair)Chaining effect — elongated clusters
CompleteMaximum distance (farthest pair)Compact, equal-size clusters
AverageAverage of all pairwise distancesCompromise between single and complete
WardMinimise within-cluster varianceSimilar to k-means; compact, spherical
"Four linkage methods, four different behaviours. Ward linkage is the most popular because it minimises variance, just like k-means. Single linkage chains clusters together, which can be useful or problematic depending on your data." Timing: ~3 min.

The Dendrogram

Foundations

A dendrogram is a tree that shows the merge history. The y-axis is the distance at which clusters merge.

Cut horizontally at any height to get clusters. Higher cut = fewer, larger clusters. Lower cut = more, smaller clusters.

"The dendrogram is the most informative visualisation in clustering. You can see the hierarchy: which points merged first (most similar), which last. You choose the granularity by choosing where to cut." Timing: ~3 min.

Ward Linkage and the K-Means Connection

Theory

Ward's method merges clusters that cause the smallest increase in total within-cluster variance:

$$\Delta(A, B) = \frac{|A| \cdot |B|}{|A| + |B|} \|\mu_A - \mu_B\|^2$$
Merge cost proportional to cluster sizes and centroid distance
Connection to K-means: Ward linkage minimises the same WCSS objective as k-means. The difference: Ward builds a hierarchy (try all k at once), k-means optimises for a fixed k.
"Ward linkage and k-means are cousins. They optimise the same objective, but Ward gives you the entire hierarchy. You can read off the k-means-like solution for any k from the dendrogram." Timing: ~2 min.

Cophenetic Correlation

Advanced

How well does the dendrogram preserve the original pairwise distances?

$$c = \text{corr}(d(x_i, x_j), \hat{d}(x_i, x_j))$$
d = original distance, d-hat = cophenetic distance (height where x_i and x_j first merge)
  • c close to 1: dendrogram faithfully represents the data
  • c below 0.7: dendrogram is distorting distances significantly
  • Use to compare linkage methods: which one preserves your data's structure best?
"For experts: cophenetic correlation is a diagnostic tool. If it's low, your dendrogram is lying to you about which points are similar. Try a different linkage." Timing: ~2 min.

Divisive Clustering: DIANA

Theory

Top-down approach: Start with all data as one cluster, recursively split.

  1. Start with everything in one cluster
  2. Find the cluster with highest diameter (most dissimilar pair)
  3. Split it using the point most different from the rest
  4. Repeat until desired number of clusters
When to prefer divisive: When you expect a small number of large clusters. Divisive makes the most important splits first, while agglomerative makes the smallest merges first.
"Divisive is the opposite of agglomerative: top-down instead of bottom-up. Less commonly used in practice, but can be better when you have a few well-separated groups." Timing: ~2 min.

6.5 Gaussian Mixture Models & EM

Soft clustering — when points belong to multiple groups with different probabilities

"K-means says 'you belong to cluster 3.' GMM says 'you're 70% cluster 3, 25% cluster 1, 5% cluster 2.' Much more realistic for real-world data." Timing: ~1 min.

Hard vs Soft Clustering

Foundations

K-means (hard)

Every point belongs to exactly one cluster.

"This customer IS a luxury buyer."

But what about the customer who buys luxury items on weekdays and bulk groceries on weekends?

GMM (soft)

Every point has a probability of belonging to each cluster.

"This customer is 70% luxury buyer, 25% bulk buyer, 5% sale hunter."

Captures the real-world ambiguity in group membership.

Gaussian Mixture Model assumes data comes from a mix of bell curves (Gaussians). Each cluster is one bell curve with its own centre, spread, and weight.
"Soft clustering is more honest about reality. Most things don't belong neatly to one category. The probabilities from GMM are powerful features for downstream models." Timing: ~2 min.

GMM: The Generative Model

Theory

A GMM assumes each data point is generated by:

  1. Pick a component k with probability $\pi_k$ (mixing weight)
  2. Draw from that component's Gaussian: $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k)$
$$p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \; \mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)$$
π_k = mixing weight (sums to 1)  |  μ_k = mean of component k  |  Σ_k = covariance of component k

Parameters to learn: $\theta = \{\pi_k, \mu_k, \Sigma_k\}_{k=1}^K$

Problem: We can't directly maximise the log-likelihood because we don't know which component generated each point.

"Imagine K bell curves superimposed on each other. Each data point was generated by ONE of these curves, but we don't know which one. The EM algorithm figures this out." Timing: ~3 min.

The Log-Likelihood Problem

Theory

The log-likelihood we want to maximise:

$$\ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \; \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k) \right]$$
The log of a sum has no closed-form MLE solution

Setting $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu_k} = 0$ gives:

$$\mu_k = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \gamma_{ik} \, x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \gamma_{ik}} \quad \text{where} \quad \gamma_{ik} = \frac{\pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\sum_{j} \pi_j \, \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)}$$
Circular: μ_k depends on γ_ik, which depends on μ_k. EM breaks this circularity.
"This is the chicken-and-egg problem. To compute the means, we need the responsibilities. To compute the responsibilities, we need the means. EM solves this by alternating." Timing: ~3 min.

EM Algorithm: E-Step

Theory
E-step: Compute Responsibilities (Posterior)

For each point $x_i$ and component k, compute the posterior probability that component k generated $x_i$:

$$\gamma_{ik} = \frac{\pi_k \; \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \pi_j \; \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)}$$
Bayes' rule: prior × likelihood / evidence
Plain English: "Given our current best guess of the bell curves, how likely is it that this particular point came from bell curve k?" A point sitting between two bell curves gets partial credit from both.
"The E-step is just Bayes' rule. Given our current parameters, compute the probability that each point belongs to each cluster. This gives us soft assignments." Timing: ~3 min.

EM Algorithm: M-Step

Theory
M-step: MLE Updates for Gaussian Mixture

Using the responsibilities from the E-step, update all parameters:

Effective count:

$$N_k = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \gamma_{ik}$$

Updated mean:

$$\mu_k^{\text{new}} = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \gamma_{ik} \, x_i$$

Updated mixing weight:

$$\pi_k^{\text{new}} = \frac{N_k}{N}$$

Updated covariance:

$$\Sigma_k^{\text{new}} = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \gamma_{ik} \, (x_i - \mu_k^{\text{new}})(x_i - \mu_k^{\text{new}})^T$$
"The M-step is weighted MLE. Every formula is the same as the normal MLE for a Gaussian, except each point is weighted by its responsibility. More responsible = more influence on the parameter update." Timing: ~3 min.

EM Convergence

Theory

Guarantee

EM monotonically increases the log-likelihood:

$$\ell(\theta^{(t+1)}) \geq \ell(\theta^{(t)})$$
  • Proof: EM maximises a lower bound (ELBO) on $\ell(\theta)$
  • Converges to a local maximum
  • Multiple random restarts help find global
  • BIC/AIC for model selection (choosing K)

EM as a Template

The EM pattern works beyond GMMs:

"EM is not just for GMMs. It's a general template for any model with hidden variables. We'll see it again when we cover topic models (LDA uses a variant of EM)." Timing: ~3 min.

EM and ELBO

Advanced

EM maximises the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

$$\log p(X|\theta) \geq \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q}[\log p(X, Z|\theta)]}_{\text{expected complete-data log-lik}} - \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q}[\log q(Z)]}_{\text{entropy of q}} = \text{ELBO}(q, \theta)$$
E-step optimises q (tightens the bound). M-step optimises θ (pushes the bound up).
Connection to variational inference: EM is a special case where the E-step uses the exact posterior. When the exact posterior is intractable, we use variational inference (approximate q). This is how modern deep generative models (VAEs) work — preview of M7.
"For experts: the ELBO perspective unifies EM with variational autoencoders. In VAEs, you can't compute the exact posterior, so you use a neural network to approximate it. Same principle, different implementation." Timing: ~2 min.

6.6 Spectral & Other Clustering Methods

Graph-based clustering, mean shift, and beyond

"Spectral clustering uses the mathematics of graphs to find clusters of arbitrary shape. It's the most theoretically elegant clustering method." Timing: ~1 min.

Spectral Clustering: The Idea

Theory

Transform data into a graph, then use eigenvalues to find clusters:

  1. Build similarity graph — connect nearby points (k-NN or ε-ball)
  2. Compute graph Laplacian: $L = D - W$ where D is degree matrix, W is adjacency
  3. Eigendecompose L — take the k smallest eigenvectors
  4. Run k-means in eigenspace — clusters now linearly separable
$$L_{\text{norm}} = D^{-1/2} L \, D^{-1/2} = I - D^{-1/2} W \, D^{-1/2}$$
Normalised Laplacian — invariant to cluster size differences
"Think of it this way: your data points are nodes in a graph, connected by edges weighted by similarity. Spectral clustering finds the best way to cut the graph into pieces by analysing its eigenvalues." Timing: ~3 min.

The Graph Laplacian

Theory

Properties of L = D - W

  • Symmetric, positive semi-definite
  • Smallest eigenvalue is always 0
  • k connected components → k eigenvalues = 0
  • Eigenvectors encode cluster structure

Eigengap heuristic

The eigenvalues of L reveal the number of clusters. Look for the largest gap between consecutive eigenvalues.

Eigengap at position k means k is the right number of clusters.

"The graph Laplacian encodes the connectivity structure of your data. Its eigenvalues tell you how many natural clusters exist. This is mathematically beautiful and practically useful." Timing: ~3 min.

Normalised Cuts (Shi & Malik, 2000)

Advanced

Spectral clustering minimises the normalised cut:

$$\text{Ncut}(A,B) = \frac{\text{cut}(A,B)}{\text{vol}(A)} + \frac{\text{cut}(A,B)}{\text{vol}(B)}$$
vol(A) = sum of edge weights in A. This balances cluster sizes.
Why normalise? Minimum cut trivially isolates single outlier points. Normalised cut balances cluster sizes by dividing by volume. The relaxed optimisation leads to the eigenvector solution.
"For experts: the connection between normalised cuts and eigenvectors of the Laplacian is one of the most elegant results in spectral graph theory. The NP-hard discrete problem has a beautiful continuous relaxation." Timing: ~2 min.

Advanced

Are your clusters reproducible or artifacts of random noise?

"For experts: stability-based validation is the gold standard. If your clusters change dramatically when you remove 10% of the data, they're not real. If they're stable across subsamples, you can trust them." Timing: ~2 min.

6.8 Principal Component Analysis

Find the directions of maximum variance

"PCA is the most important dimensionality reduction technique. It finds the 'best angles' to project your data so you lose as little information as possible." Timing: ~1 min.

The Curse of Dimensionality

Foundations

Your e-commerce dataset has 200 features: purchase history, browsing time, click patterns, demographics...

You cannot visualise 200 dimensions. You cannot compute distances reliably in 200 dimensions.

Dimensionality reduction = finding a smaller set of "super-features" that capture most of the information.

Analogy: A shadow is a 2D projection of a 3D object. You lose some information (depth), but a good shadow preserves the shape. PCA finds the best "angle" to cast the shadow.
"200 features means 200 dimensions. Humans can visualise 2 or 3. Algorithms struggle with 200 because most of the space is empty. PCA compresses 200 dimensions to a handful that capture the important patterns." Timing: ~2 min.

PCA: The Mathematical Formulation

Theory

Goal: Find the direction that maximises variance of projected data.

Step 1: Centre the data
$$\tilde{X} = X - \bar{X}$$
Step 2: Compute covariance matrix
$$C = \frac{1}{n-1} \tilde{X}^T \tilde{X}$$
Step 3: Eigendecomposition
$$C v = \lambda v$$

Eigenvectors $v$ = principal directions. Eigenvalues $\lambda$ = variance along each direction.

Step 4: Project
$$X_{\text{proj}} = \tilde{X} V_k$$

Keep top k eigenvectors (largest eigenvalues).

"Four steps: centre, covariance, eigendecompose, project. The eigenvectors are the new axes. The eigenvalues tell you how much variance each axis captures." Timing: ~3 min.

PCA and SVD

Theory

PCA is equivalent to Singular Value Decomposition of the centred data matrix:

$$\tilde{X} = U \Sigma V^T$$
U = left singular vectors (scores)  |  Σ = singular values (related to eigenvalues: λ_k = σ_k²/(n-1))  |  V = right singular vectors = principal components
SVD is preferred computationally. Eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix can be numerically unstable. SVD of the data matrix directly is faster and more stable for tall matrices (n >> d).
"In practice, every PCA implementation uses SVD under the hood. The eigendecomposition formulation is for understanding; SVD is for computing." Timing: ~2 min.

Variance Explained & the Scree Plot

Theory

Variance explained ratio

$$\text{VER}_k = \frac{\lambda_k}{\sum_i \lambda_i}$$

Cumulative variance explained: choose k where cumulative VER exceeds 90-95%.

Scree plot

Plot eigenvalues in decreasing order. The "elbow" indicates the right number of components.

Rule of thumb: keep components until adding one more captures less than 5% additional variance.

Example: 200 features, first 10 PCs explain 95% of variance. You've compressed 200 dimensions to 10 with only 5% information loss. Those 10 PCs are your new features.
"The scree plot tells you how many components matter. If the first 3 components explain 95% of variance, the remaining 197 are noise. Compression achieved." Timing: ~2 min.

Reconstruction and Loadings

Theory

Reconstruction

$$\hat{X} = X_{\text{proj}} V_k^T + \bar{X}$$

Reconstruction error measures information loss.

Loadings

Each PC is a linear combination of original features. Loadings tell you which features contribute to each PC.

PC1 = 0.8 × income + 0.5 × spending — this PC captures "financial capacity".

Biplot: Plot observations AND loadings together. Observations as points, loadings as arrows. Direction of arrow = which PC the feature contributes to. Length = contribution strength.
"Loadings are the key to interpretation. Without them, PCs are abstract numbers. With them, you can name your components: 'PC1 = financial capacity', 'PC2 = risk appetite'." Timing: ~2 min.

Standardisation for PCA

Foundations
You MUST standardise features before PCA (unless they share the same unit and scale).

Why? PCA maximises variance. If "income" ranges 0–1,000,000 and "age" ranges 0–100, income will dominate the first PC purely because of scale, not because it's more important.

PCA Variants

Advanced

Probabilistic PCA

Latent variable model: $x = Wz + \mu + \epsilon$ where z ~ N(0,I). MLE of W recovers PCA. Enables missing data handling and Bayesian model selection.

Incremental PCA

Process data in mini-batches. Never loads full dataset into memory. For streaming or very large datasets.

Sparse PCA

Add L1 penalty: $\max_{v} v^T C v - \alpha \|v\|_1$. Forces loadings to be sparse (mostly zero). More interpretable components.

Kernel PCA

PCA in high-dimensional feature space via kernel trick. Handles non-linear structure. Kernels: RBF, polynomial, sigmoid.

"For experts: Probabilistic PCA gives a principled way to handle missing data and choose the number of components via BIC. Sparse PCA trades some variance explained for interpretability. Kernel PCA handles non-linear manifolds." Timing: ~2 min.

  • Multicollinearity: PCA produces orthogonal components — zero correlation between PCs. Eliminates multicollinearity for linear models.
  • Noise reduction: Drop low-variance components that capture noise, not signal.
  • Visualisation: Project to 2D for scatter plots and cluster inspection.
  • Speed: Fewer features = faster training for downstream models.

6.9 t-SNE & UMAP

Non-linear dimensionality reduction for visualisation

"PCA is linear: it finds straight axes. t-SNE and UMAP are non-linear: they can unravel complex manifolds. They're primarily used for visualisation." Timing: ~1 min.

t-SNE: Intuition

Foundations

t-SNE preserves local structure: neighbours in high-D stay neighbours in 2D.

How it works (simplified)

  1. Compute pairwise similarities in high-D (using Gaussian)
  2. Place points randomly in 2D
  3. Compute pairwise similarities in 2D (using Student's t)
  4. Move points in 2D to match the high-D similarities

Result

Clusters that are close in high-D appear as visible clusters in 2D. The MNIST digits dataset, visualised with t-SNE, shows clear digit groups.

"t-SNE is like crumpling a piece of paper: nearby points stay nearby, but the overall shape changes. It's brilliant for seeing clusters in 2D." Timing: ~2 min.

t-SNE: The Mathematics

Theory

High-dimensional similarities (Gaussian kernel):

$$p_{j|i} = \frac{\exp(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2 / 2\sigma_i^2)}{\sum_{k \neq i} \exp(-\|x_i - x_k\|^2 / 2\sigma_i^2)}$$
Symmetrised: $p_{ij} = \frac{p_{j|i} + p_{i|j}}{2n}$

Low-dimensional similarities (Student's t with 1 degree of freedom):

$$q_{ij} = \frac{(1 + \|y_i - y_j\|^2)^{-1}}{\sum_{k \neq l} (1 + \|y_k - y_l\|^2)^{-1}}$$
Student's t has heavier tails than Gaussian: allows moderate distances in 2D for points that are far in high-D
"Two key ideas: Gaussian in high-D (local neighbourhood), Student's t in low-D (prevents crowding). The heavy tails of the t-distribution are what make t-SNE work where SNE failed." Timing: ~3 min.

t-SNE: Objective and Optimisation

Theory

Objective: Minimise KL divergence between P and Q:

$$\text{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_{i \neq j} p_{ij} \log \frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}}$$
Make the 2D similarities (Q) match the high-D similarities (P)

Optimisation: Gradient descent on the positions $y_i$ in 2D.

Perplexity: Controls the effective number of neighbours. Low perplexity (5) = very local. High perplexity (50) = considers more neighbours. Typical range: 5–50. Always try multiple values.
"KL divergence measures how different two probability distributions are. We want the 2D similarities to be as close as possible to the high-D similarities. The gradient tells us which direction to move each point." Timing: ~3 min.

t-SNE: Critical Warnings

Foundations
t-SNE is for VISUALISATION ONLY. Do not use t-SNE embeddings as features.
  • Cluster sizes are MEANINGLESS — a large cluster in t-SNE may be smaller than a tiny one in reality
  • Distances between clusters are MEANINGLESS — two far-apart clusters might be closer in reality than two nearby ones
  • Different runs give DIFFERENT results — stochastic algorithm, non-convex objective
  • Perplexity changes everything — same data, different perplexity = completely different plot
Only trust local structure. Neighbours in t-SNE are likely real neighbours. Everything else (cluster size, inter-cluster distance) is an artifact.
"This is the most common mistake with t-SNE. Students see beautiful clusters and conclude they're well-separated. But t-SNE can create fake clusters and hide real distances. Use it for exploration, never for modelling." Timing: ~3 min.

Barnes-Hut t-SNE

Advanced

Standard t-SNE is O(n²). Barnes-Hut approximation reduces this to O(n log n):

  • Build a quad-tree (2D) or oct-tree (3D) over the low-dimensional space
  • For distant point groups, approximate the repulsive force as if the group were a single point
  • Same trick used in N-body simulations in physics
Practical impact: Standard t-SNE handles ~5,000 points. Barnes-Hut handles ~100,000. For larger datasets, use UMAP.
"For experts: Barnes-Hut is the standard implementation now. But for datasets larger than 100K points, UMAP is preferred because it scales better and preserves global structure." Timing: ~2 min.

UMAP: Intuition

Foundations

UMAP = Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (McInnes et al., 2018)

What UMAP does better

  • Preserves global structure better than t-SNE
  • Much faster (scales to millions of points)
  • Deterministic with fixed random seed
  • Arbitrary output dimensions (not just 2D)
  • Usable as features (unlike t-SNE)

Key parameters

  • n_neighbors — local vs global (like perplexity). Low = more local detail. High = more global structure.
  • min_dist — how tightly points are packed. Low = tighter clusters. High = more spread.
"UMAP is the modern replacement for t-SNE in most cases. Faster, better global structure preservation, and the embeddings can actually be used as features for downstream models." Timing: ~2 min.

UMAP: Theoretical Foundation

Theory

UMAP is grounded in topological data analysis:

  1. Assumptions: Data is uniformly distributed on a Riemannian manifold; the metric is locally constant; the manifold is locally connected.
  2. Build fuzzy simplicial set: Construct k-nearest neighbour graph. Assign fuzzy membership using exponential decay.
  3. Fuzzy union: Combine local fuzzy sets into a global topological representation.
  4. Optimise: Find low-dimensional embedding that minimises cross-entropy between the high-D and low-D fuzzy graphs.
$$\text{CE} = \sum_{e} \left[ \mu_h(e) \log\frac{\mu_h(e)}{\mu_l(e)} + (1-\mu_h(e))\log\frac{1-\mu_h(e)}{1-\mu_l(e)} \right]$$
μ_h = high-D fuzzy membership, μ_l = low-D fuzzy membership
"UMAP's theoretical grounding in Riemannian geometry and algebraic topology is what makes it work better than t-SNE for global structure. The cross-entropy objective is symmetric, unlike t-SNE's KL divergence." Timing: ~3 min.

Supervised and Parametric UMAP

Advanced

Supervised UMAP

Incorporate label information to influence the embedding. Points with the same label are pulled closer, different labels pushed apart.

Use case: Semi-supervised clustering where you have partial labels.

Parametric UMAP

Replace the direct optimisation with a neural network encoder. The network learns the UMAP mapping, which can then be applied to new data without re-fitting.

Bridge to M7: this IS a neural network doing dimensionality reduction.

Curvature-adaptive UMAP (2025): Recent work adjusts the UMAP distance metric based on local manifold curvature, improving embeddings on data with varying intrinsic dimensionality.
"For experts: Parametric UMAP is a neural network that learns the UMAP embedding function. This bridges directly to autoencoders in M7. Same idea, different formalisms." Timing: ~2 min.

6.10 Other Dimensionality Reduction Methods

Factor Analysis, ICA, MDS, and the Autoencoder Bridge

"Beyond PCA and UMAP, several specialised methods exist for specific types of data and problems." Timing: ~1 min.

Factor Analysis

Theory

Latent factor model: $x = \Lambda f + \epsilon$

Difference from PCA

  • PCA: no noise model. Variance = signal.
  • FA: separates signal from noise. $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Psi)$ where Ψ is diagonal.
  • FA recovers latent factors; PCA recovers directions of max variance.

When to use

  • Psychometrics (intelligence, personality tests)
  • Survey data (latent constructs like "satisfaction")
  • When you believe features are driven by hidden causes

Rotation: Varimax (orthogonal) or promax (oblique) to improve interpretability.

"Factor Analysis is PCA's philosophical cousin. PCA says 'find the axes of maximum variance.' FA says 'find the hidden causes that generated the data, separating signal from noise.'" Timing: ~2 min.

Independent Component Analysis (ICA)

Theory

Blind source separation: Unmix mixed signals.

The cocktail party problem

Three microphones record three people talking simultaneously. Each microphone captures a different mix. ICA recovers the individual voices.

Key assumptions

  • Sources are statistically independent
  • Sources are non-Gaussian (at most one can be Gaussian)
  • FastICA: maximise non-Gaussianity (negentropy)
Difference from PCA: PCA finds uncorrelated components (2nd-order). ICA finds independent components (all orders). Independence is stronger than uncorrelation.
"ICA goes beyond PCA: uncorrelated is not the same as independent. ICA separates sources that PCA cannot. Applications: EEG signal processing, audio separation, financial signal extraction." Timing: ~2 min.

Text Preprocessing: The Pipeline

Foundations
  1. Tokenisation — split text into words (or subwords or characters)
  2. Lowercasing — "The" = "the"
  3. Stop word removal — remove "the", "is", "at" (common but uninformative)
  4. Stemming — "running", "runs", "ran" → "run" (Porter/Snowball)
  5. Lemmatisation — "better" → "good" (dictionary-based, more accurate)
  6. N-grams — "machine learning" as a single token (bigram)
For classical topic models (LDA, NMF): preprocessing matters enormously. For BERTopic, the embedding model handles most of this internally.
"Preprocessing is the unsung hero of NLP. Garbage in, garbage out. For LDA and NMF, the quality of your term-document matrix depends entirely on how well you preprocess." Timing: ~2 min.

TF-IDF: Words That Matter

Theory
$$\text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \underbrace{\frac{f_{t,d}}{\sum_{t'} f_{t',d}}}_{\text{Term Frequency (TF)}} \times \underbrace{\log \frac{N}{|\{d : t \in d\}|}}_{\text{Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)}}$$
TF: how often this word appears in this document  |  IDF: how rare this word is across all documents
Plain English: A word is important to a document if it appears frequently in that document but rarely in others. "The" appears everywhere (low IDF). "Reinforcement" appears only in RL papers (high IDF).

Why IDF? Without it, common words like "the" dominate. IDF downweights words that appear in many documents.

"TF-IDF is the foundation of classical topic modeling. It converts raw text into a numerical matrix where each cell represents how important a word is to a document." Timing: ~2 min.

Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA/LSI)

Theory

Apply SVD to the term-document matrix:

$$X \approx U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T$$
U_k = term-topic matrix  |  Σ_k = topic importance  |  V_k = document-topic matrix

What LSA gives you

  • Documents as vectors in k-dimensional topic space
  • Handles synonymy ("car" and "automobile" in same topic)
  • Simple: just truncated SVD

Limitations

  • No probabilistic interpretation
  • Components can have negative values (hard to interpret)
  • Topics are orthogonal (real topics overlap)
Historical importance: LSA (Deerwester et al., 1990) was the first topic model. It showed that SVD on term-document matrices captures semantic structure. This insight led to LDA.
"LSA is PCA for text. Simple and fast, but no probabilistic foundation. LDA fixed this by providing a generative model." Timing: ~2 min.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA): The Generative Model

Theory

LDA (Blei, Ng, Jordan, 2003) tells a story about how documents are "created":

  1. For each document d, draw topic proportions: $\theta_d \sim \text{Dir}(\alpha)$
  2. For each word position n in document d:
    1. Draw a topic: $z_n \sim \text{Multinomial}(\theta_d)$
    2. Draw a word: $w_n \sim \text{Multinomial}(\beta_{z_n})$
Plain English: Each document is a mix of topics (e.g., 60% politics, 30% economics, 10% sports). Each topic is a distribution over words (e.g., "politics" = high probability for "election", "vote", "parliament"). To "write" a document, pick a topic for each word, then pick the word from that topic.
"LDA is a generative model: it describes how documents could have been generated. Inference reverses the process: given the words, infer the topics. This is the same E-step/M-step pattern from EM." Timing: ~3 min.

LDA: Plate Notation

Theory

Shaded = observed (words). Unshaded = latent (topics, proportions). Plates = repetition.

"Plate notation is compact: the D plate means 'repeat for D documents', the N plate 'for N words in each document', K plate 'for K topics'. Only w is observed. Everything else must be inferred." Timing: ~3 min.

LDA Inference: Collapsed Gibbs Sampling

Theory

Collapsed Gibbs: Integrate out θ and β. Sample only the topic assignments z:

$$p(z_i = k \mid z_{-i}, w, \alpha, \eta) \propto \underbrace{(n_{dk}^{-i} + \alpha_k)}_{\text{doc-topic count}} \times \underbrace{\frac{n_{kw}^{-i} + \eta}{n_{k\cdot}^{-i} + V\eta}}_{\text{topic-word proportion}}$$
n_dk = count of topic k in document d (excluding current word). n_kw = count of word w in topic k.
Plain English: For each word, ask: "What topic should this word belong to?" The answer depends on: (1) what topics are common in this document, and (2) how common this word is in each topic. Repeat for every word, many times, until stable.
"Collapsed Gibbs sampling is the most common inference method for LDA. It's simple to implement and scales reasonably well. For very large corpora, use online variational inference instead." Timing: ~3 min.

LDA Variants

Advanced

Online LDA (Hoffman et al., 2010)

Stochastic variational inference. Process documents in mini-batches. Scales to millions of documents.

Hierarchical LDA

Topics form a tree: broad topics at the top, specific sub-topics below. Number of topics learned automatically (nonparametric Bayesian).

Correlated Topic Model (CTM)

LDA assumes topics are independent. CTM models topic correlations using a logistic normal distribution instead of Dirichlet.

Dynamic Topic Models

Track how topics evolve over time. Each time slice has its own topic distributions, linked to adjacent slices.

"For experts: Online LDA is the production version. Hierarchical LDA is elegant but computationally expensive. CTM models topic correlations that LDA misses." Timing: ~2 min.

Non-Negative Matrix Factorisation (NMF)

Theory

Factor the document-term matrix into non-negative parts:

$$X \approx WH \quad \text{where} \quad W, H \geq 0$$
X (n×d) = document-term matrix  |  W (n×k) = document-topic matrix  |  H (k×d) = topic-word matrix
Key insight: non-negativity means "parts-based". Each topic is an additive combination of words (no subtraction). This makes NMF topics naturally interpretable: a topic IS a weighted list of words.
"NMF's constraint of non-negativity is what makes it interpretable. You can't subtract a word from a topic. Topics are purely additive collections of words. This matches human intuition about what a topic is." Timing: ~2 min.

NMF: Optimisation

Theory

Objective: Minimise reconstruction error:

$$\min_{W,H \geq 0} \|X - WH\|_F^2$$
Frobenius norm. Alternative: KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(X \| WH)$

Multiplicative update rules (Lee & Seung, 2001):

Update H
$$H \leftarrow H \circ \frac{W^T X}{W^T W H}$$
Update W
$$W \leftarrow W \circ \frac{X H^T}{W H H^T}$$

ˆ = element-wise multiplication. Non-negativity preserved by construction.

"The multiplicative updates are elegant: they guarantee non-negativity because you're multiplying positive numbers. No projection step needed." Timing: ~2 min.

Result: each document activates only a few topics. Each topic uses only a few words. More interpretable.

Semi-supervised NMF

Seed some topics with known keywords. The algorithm respects these seeds while discovering the rest.

Useful when you know some topics should exist (e.g., "billing", "technical") but want to discover others.

"For experts: Sparse NMF produces cleaner topics by forcing most weights to zero. Semi-supervised NMF lets you guide the algorithm with domain knowledge." Timing: ~2 min.

Isolation Forest

Foundations

Key insight: Anomalies are easier to isolate (fewer random splits needed).

Analogy: Imagine playing "20 questions" with data points. Normal points need many questions to isolate (they're in crowded areas). Anomalies need very few (they're alone in sparse areas).
"Isolation Forest is elegant in its simplicity. Instead of modelling what 'normal' looks like (hard), it measures how easy each point is to isolate (simple). Anomalies are isolated quickly." Timing: ~2 min.

Isolation Forest: Algorithm

Theory
  1. Randomly select a feature and a split value
  2. Partition the data
  3. Repeat recursively until each point is isolated
  4. Record the path length (number of splits to isolate each point)
  5. Build many trees (forest). Average the path lengths.
$$s(x, n) = 2^{-\frac{E[h(x)]}{c(n)}}$$
h(x) = path length  |  c(n) = average path length in BST: $c(n) = 2H(n-1) - 2(n-1)/n$  |  Score near 1 = anomaly. Score near 0.5 = normal.
Contamination parameter: Expected fraction of anomalies. Sets the score threshold. Typical: 0.01-0.1 (1-10% anomalies).
"The score formula normalises path length against what you'd expect for a binary search tree. Short paths (easy isolation) get scores near 1. Average paths get scores near 0.5." Timing: ~3 min.

Extended Isolation Forest

Advanced

Standard Isolation Forest uses axis-aligned splits. Extended Isolation Forest uses random hyperplanes:

  • Random direction (slope) instead of single feature
  • Eliminates bias from axis-aligned splits
  • Better for correlated features
Deep anomaly detection (2025): Reconstruction-based methods using autoencoders are now a major paradigm. Normal data reconstructs well; anomalies have high reconstruction error. Foundation models for anomaly detection enable cross-domain generalisation without task-specific training.
"For experts: Extended Isolation Forest fixes the axis-alignment bias. Autoencoder-based anomaly detection is gaining ground because it naturally handles high-dimensional, complex data." Timing: ~2 min.

Local Outlier Factor (LOF)

Theory

Key idea: Compare a point's local density to its neighbours' densities.

Step 1: Reachability distance
$$\text{reach-dist}_k(A, B) = \max\{k\text{-dist}(B), d(A, B)\}$$
Step 2: Local reachability density
$$\text{lrd}_k(A) = \left(\frac{\sum_{B \in N_k(A)} \text{reach-dist}_k(A,B)}{|N_k(A)|}\right)^{-1}$$
Step 3: Local Outlier Factor
$$\text{LOF}_k(A) = \frac{\sum_{B \in N_k(A)} \text{lrd}_k(B) / \text{lrd}_k(A)}{|N_k(A)|}$$

LOF ≈ 1: normal density. LOF >> 1: much less dense than neighbours = anomaly.

"LOF is the density-based approach. It doesn't ask 'is this point isolated?' (Isolation Forest), but 'is this point in a much sparser region than its neighbours?' This makes it robust to varying-density data." Timing: ~3 min.

LOF: Connection to DBSCAN

Theory
LOF and DBSCAN share the same intuition: both use local density and reachability distance. DBSCAN uses density for clustering (dense = cluster, sparse = noise). LOF uses density ratio for anomaly scoring (much sparser than neighbours = anomaly).

Advantages of LOF

  • Scores, not binary labels
  • Works with varying density
  • Identifies local anomalies

Limitations

  • O(n²) without spatial indexing
  • Sensitive to k (number of neighbours)
  • Not great for high dimensions
"LOF and DBSCAN are siblings. LOF gives continuous scores, DBSCAN gives binary noise labels. Use LOF when you want ranked anomaly scores rather than hard labels." Timing: ~2 min.

One-Class SVM

Theory

Idea: Learn a boundary around "normal" data in feature space.

  • Map data to high-dimensional space via kernel
  • Find a hyperplane that separates data from the origin with maximum margin
  • Points on the wrong side of the boundary = anomalies
$$\min_{w, \rho, \xi} \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 + \frac{1}{\nu n}\sum_i \xi_i - \rho$$
ν = expected fraction of anomalies. Controls the tightness of the boundary.
ν parameter: Upper bound on the fraction of anomalies and lower bound on the fraction of support vectors. Set it to your expected anomaly rate.
"One-Class SVM is useful when you can only train on normal data (no anomaly examples). It learns the 'shape' of normality and flags anything outside. The kernel trick lets it handle non-linear boundaries." Timing: ~2 min.

Theory

Combine multiple detectors for robustness:

  1. Run multiple detectors: Isolation Forest + LOF + One-Class SVM
  2. Normalise scores: Each detector has different scales
  3. Aggregate: Average, max, or weighted combination

6.13 Association Rules

Market basket analysis — "customers who bought X also bought Y"

"Association rules discover relationships between items. The classic example: diapers and beer are bought together on Friday evenings. This is pattern discovery in transaction data." Timing: ~1 min.

Support, Confidence, and Lift

Foundations

Support: How often do X and Y appear together?

$$\text{Support}(X \cup Y) = \frac{|\text{transactions containing } X \text{ and } Y|}{|\text{all transactions}|}$$

Confidence: If X, how likely Y?

$$\text{Conf}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \frac{P(X \cup Y)}{P(X)}$$

Lift: Does X actually help predict Y?

$$\text{Lift}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \frac{P(Y|X)}{P(Y)}$$
Lift > 1: X and Y are positively associated. Lift = 1: independent. Lift < 1: negatively associated.
"Support is frequency. Confidence is conditional probability. Lift is the key metric: does knowing X actually tell you anything about Y beyond base rate?" Timing: ~3 min.

Additional Interestingness Measures

Advanced
MeasureFormulaAdvantage
Conviction$\frac{1 - P(Y)}{1 - \text{Conf}(X \Rightarrow Y)}$Directional; infinity = X always predicts Y
Leverage$P(X \cup Y) - P(X)P(Y)$Absolute difference from independence
Cosine$\frac{P(X \cup Y)}{\sqrt{P(X) \cdot P(Y)}}$Symmetric, bounded [0,1]
Why not just confidence? Confidence can be misleading. If Y is very common (e.g., bread), confidence for any X → Y will be high even if X and Y are independent. Lift and conviction correct for this.
"For experts: conviction is especially useful because it's asymmetric and handles the case where confidence is misleading due to high base rates of the consequent." Timing: ~2 min.

Apriori Algorithm

Theory

Key insight: If an itemset is infrequent, all its supersets are also infrequent (anti-monotone property).

  1. Pass 1: Count single items. Prune items below minimum support.
  2. Generate C_2: All pairs of frequent items.
  3. Pass 2: Count pairs. Prune pairs below minimum support.
  4. Generate C_3: All triples from frequent pairs. And so on.
  5. Stop: When no new frequent itemsets are found.
Anti-monotone property: If {A, B} is infrequent, then {A, B, C} is also infrequent. This prunes the search space dramatically — no need to check any superset of an infrequent itemset.
"Apriori is a breadth-first algorithm: single items, then pairs, then triples. The anti-monotone property is the key pruning mechanism that makes it tractable." Timing: ~3 min.

FP-Growth: Faster Than Apriori

Theory

No candidate generation. Compress the database into an FP-tree, then mine directly.

  1. Build FP-tree: Compressed representation of all transactions. Shared prefixes save space.
  2. Header table: Links all nodes with the same item.
  3. Conditional FP-trees: For each item, build a sub-tree of transactions containing that item.
  4. Recursively mine conditional FP-trees for frequent patterns.
Advantage: Only two passes over the database (build tree, mine). Apriori needs one pass per itemset size. FP-Growth is 10-100x faster on large datasets.
"FP-Growth is the modern standard for association rule mining. It avoids the expensive candidate generation and counting steps of Apriori." Timing: ~3 min.

  • Bioinformatics: Gene co-expression patterns
  • Medical diagnosis: Symptom combinations → disease patterns

6.14 Drift Monitoring

Your model in production — detecting when the world changes

"You've deployed your unsupervised model. Clusters were perfect last month. But the data is shifting. Without monitoring, your clusters silently become meaningless." Timing: ~1 min.

Why Models Degrade

Foundations

You deploy your model on Monday. By Friday, it's giving worse predictions. What happened?

Data drift: P(X) changes

The input distribution shifted.

Example: Your customer segmentation was trained on 2023 data. In 2024, a new competitor changed buying patterns.

Concept drift: P(Y|X) changes

The relationship between inputs and outputs changed.

Example: During a recession, the relationship between spending patterns and default risk inverts.

Models don't know they're wrong. Without monitoring, they silently degrade until someone notices the business impact — which could be months later.
"This connects directly to our opening case. The AML model's 99.9% accuracy was useless because it didn't detect when criminal patterns shifted. Monitoring would have caught the degradation early." Timing: ~2 min.

PSI: Population Stability Index

Theory
$$\text{PSI} = \sum_{i=1}^{B} (p_i^{\text{actual}} - p_i^{\text{expected}}) \times \ln\frac{p_i^{\text{actual}}}{p_i^{\text{expected}}}$$
Bin the feature into B buckets. Compare proportions in each bucket.
PSI ValueInterpretationAction
< 0.1No significant shiftContinue monitoring
0.1 - 0.25Moderate shiftInvestigate, consider retraining
> 0.25Significant shiftRetrain immediately
"PSI is the industry standard for credit risk. It's simple, interpretable, and every regulator understands it. The thresholds (0.1 and 0.25) are well-established conventions." Timing: ~2 min.

KS Test for Drift Detection

Theory
$$D = \sup_x |F_{\text{ref}}(x) - F_{\text{prod}}(x)|$$
Maximum vertical distance between two cumulative distribution functions

Advantages over PSI

  • No binning required
  • Distribution-free (non-parametric)
  • Exact p-value available

When to use which

  • PSI: industry dashboards, regulatory reporting
  • KS: automated drift detection, statistical rigour
  • Both: belt and braces for critical models
"PSI needs binning (which introduces choices). KS is distribution-free and gives a proper p-value. Use PSI for communication, KS for automation." Timing: ~2 min.

Multivariate Drift and Concept Drift

Advanced

Multivariate drift

PSI and KS are univariate. Multivariate drift can occur even when each feature individually looks stable.

Methods: Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), domain classifier (train a model to distinguish old vs new data).

Concept drift detection

  • DDM (Drift Detection Method): monitor error rate, trigger when increase exceeds threshold
  • ADWIN (Adaptive Windowing): maintain a window of recent observations, shrink when drift detected
  • Page-Hinkley: sequential analysis for detecting mean shifts
"For experts: univariate drift detection is necessary but not sufficient. Correlations can change even when marginals stay the same. DDM and ADWIN are online methods for streaming concept drift." Timing: ~2 min.

Key Takeaways: Theory Level

Theory
If you followed the math:
  • K-means is a special case of EM with spherical, equal covariance
  • EM monotonically increases the ELBO — local convergence guaranteed
  • PCA = eigendecomposition of covariance = truncated SVD of centred data
  • t-SNE minimises KL(P||Q); UMAP minimises cross-entropy of fuzzy sets
  • LDA is a generative model with Dirichlet priors; NMF is non-negative matrix factorisation
  • Isolation Forest measures path length; LOF measures density ratio
  • PSI is a symmetric KL-like divergence; KS is supremum of CDF difference
"The theory-level takeaway: understand the connections. K-means, GMM, LDA, and PCA are all connected through the EM algorithm and matrix factorisation." Timing: ~2 min.

Key Takeaways: Advanced Level

Advanced
For the experts:
  • EM → ELBO → Variational Inference → VAEs (M7 bridge)
  • Parametric UMAP is a neural network doing dim reduction (M7 bridge)
  • Autoencoder bottleneck IS learned PCA (non-linear generalisation)
  • BERTopic = UMAP + HDBSCAN + c-TF-IDF — modular and extensible
  • Stability-based validation is the gold standard for real-world clustering
  • Multivariate drift requires domain classifiers or MMD, not just univariate PSI
"The expert takeaway: every USML method has a deep learning generalisation. This is the bridge to M7. Autoencoders generalise PCA. VAEs generalise EM. Neural topic models generalise LDA." Timing: ~2 min.
