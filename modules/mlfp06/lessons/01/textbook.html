Lesson 6.1 LLM Fundamentals, Prompting, and Structured Output

You finished Module 5 with a working transformer. This lesson picks up exactly where that left off: a transformer, scaled up a thousand times, trained on a trillion tokens, and reached through an API. In the next 60 minutes you will learn how large language models are pre-trained, why prompting works at all, how to squeeze quality out of a model without touching its weights, and how to force its output into typed Python objects you can put into a data pipeline.

Why This Matters

Every deployed AI product you will build from this point forward will contain at least one call to a large language model. Not because LLMs are fashionable, but because they are the only general-purpose tool in the ML toolbox: one API, one prompt, and you have a classifier, an extractor, a summariser, a translator, an agent. The cost of trying an idea has dropped to pennies. The skill that separates an engineer who ships from one who burns budget is no longer "can you fine-tune a model" — it is "can you write a prompt that works the first time".

This lesson is the foundation for every other lesson in Module 6. You cannot build a RAG system (6.4) without prompting skill. You cannot align a model with DPO (6.3) without understanding how the base model was pre-trained. You cannot build an agent (6.5) without a Delegate that reliably emits a typed object. So we start here.

From Transformer to Large Language Model

In Lesson 5.4 you built a small transformer block: multi-head attention, a feed-forward layer, residual connections and layer normalisation. A large language model is that same block, repeated 32–96 times, trained on a trillion tokens of text for weeks on thousands of GPUs.

Pre-training: next-token prediction at scale

GPT-style models are trained on exactly one objective: given the previous $t$ tokens, predict token $t+1$. Formally, given a sequence $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T$ the model learns parameters $\theta$ that minimise the negative log-likelihood of the next token:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta\!\left(x_t \,\mid\, x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}\right)$$
$p_\theta$ — model distribution $T$ — sequence length $x_t$ — token at position $t$

That is it. No labels, no human annotation — just text that already exists on the internet. The model reads "The Singapore monsoon arrived in" and is rewarded for placing high probability on "December". Repeat this across a trillion tokens of Wikipedia, GitHub, books, forums, news and you eventually get something that has absorbed a staggering fraction of human written knowledge as a side-effect of predicting one token at a time.

🔵 Theory — why next-token prediction yields intelligence

Predicting the next token under wide distribution coverage forces the model to implicitly learn grammar, world knowledge, arithmetic, programming, translation, style, and reasoning — because every one of those skills is useful for lowering the cross-entropy loss on some subset of the training data. The task is simple; the data is everything ever written. Scaling laws (Kaplan 2020, Hoffmann 2022) show loss decays as a power law in parameters, data, and compute: $$\mathcal{L}(N, D) \approx \mathcal{L}_\infty + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta}$$ where $N$ is parameters and $D$ is dataset tokens. This is why the frontier keeps moving — the law has not broken yet.

Tokenisation

Before text reaches the transformer it is chopped into tokens. Modern LLMs use byte-pair encoding (BPE) or similar subword schemes: common words are a single token ("Singapore" → 1 token), rare words are split ("HDB" might be 2 tokens), and every character is representable as a fallback. A rule of thumb: 1 English token $\approx$ 0.75 words $\approx$ 4 characters.

This matters for two practical reasons. First, you pay per token, not per word. Second, context windows are measured in tokens. GPT-4 Turbo allows 128k tokens; a typical Singapore regulatory document is 5–20k tokens. You have room.

Inference: the KV-cache and why generation is fast

When you ask an LLM to produce 500 tokens, it does not run the full stack 500 times from scratch. At step $t$ it already computed the Key and Value projections for tokens $1, \ldots, t-1$ — and attention at those positions does not change. The KV-cache stores them and reuses them. Generation becomes "compute Q for the new token, attend against the cached K/V, sample, append". This is why the first token takes longer (prompt prefill) than subsequent tokens (decode).

🟣 Advanced — inference optimisations

Production LLM serving stacks like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM add more: continuous batching packs requests of different lengths into one forward pass; speculative decoding uses a small draft model to propose tokens that the big model verifies in parallel; flash attention rewrites softmax to avoid materialising the full attention matrix. You do not need to implement these — but you should know they exist, because they determine your cost-per-token.

Why Prompting Works

Here is the paradox: a model trained to predict the next token on random internet text is somehow able to classify company reports, translate legal documents, and write code. How?

A prompt is not a command — it is a context. When you type "Classify this report: <text>. Category:" you are asking the model "what tokens are most likely to follow this exact string?". And because the training corpus contained billions of classification-shaped examples (stack overflow Q&A, form labels, tutorials), the most likely continuation is a category name. You are not teaching the model anything — you are priming it with a context in which the desired output is the natural continuation.

🟢 Foundations — the prompting mindset

A prompt is a way of selecting a neighbourhood of the training distribution. "Answer like a senior compliance officer" places the model in the neighbourhood of compliance documents. "Let's think step by step" places it in the neighbourhood of math tutorials where authors actually showed their work. You are not changing the model's knowledge — you are moving a spotlight across it.

Four Prompting Techniques You Must Know

1. Zero-shot

No examples. Just describe the task and ask. Works whenever the task is well-represented in pre-training — classification, simple extraction, summarisation in English. Cheapest, fastest, most brittle.

2. Few-shot (in-context learning)

Include $k$ labelled examples in the prompt. The model pattern-matches: "I have seen four input → output pairs of this shape; the fifth input is probably resolved the same way". Typical $k$ is 3–8. Diminishing returns beyond 10. Use diverse, representative examples — the model learns the pattern, not the specific content.

3. Chain-of-thought

Add "Let's think step by step" (or provide reasoning in your few-shot examples) and let the model write out its intermediate work before the final answer. Discovered in Wei et al. 2022; shockingly effective on arithmetic, logic, and multi-step decisions. The cost is more output tokens.

🔵 Theory — why chain-of-thought works

An LLM emits tokens autoregressively. Each next token is conditioned on everything generated so far. When you force the model to emit reasoning first, those reasoning tokens become part of the context for the final answer — effectively giving the model scratchpad space to break a hard problem into easy sub-problems. It also commits the model to a path; without CoT, the model has to "decide" the answer in a single forward pass through its layers, which is harder.

4. Self-consistency

Sample the same CoT prompt $N$ times at high temperature ($T \approx 0.7$). You get $N$ different reasoning chains that may reach different conclusions. Take the majority vote. Wang et al. 2022 showed this beats plain CoT across many benchmarks — it trades compute for accuracy in a principled way. Cost scales linearly with $N$.

The Kailash Engine: Delegate and Signature

⚙ Kailash — Kaizen Delegate

Delegate is Kailash's LLM call primitive. It wraps any provider (OpenAI, Anthropic, Ollama, local models) behind one interface. Every call emits a stream of events — text, tool-call start, tool-call result, completion — and every call enforces a cost budget. Use it for anything more serious than a quick experiment; the budget is the difference between a bug and a bill. 

from kaizen_agents import Delegate

delegate = Delegate(model="gpt-4o-mini", max_llm_cost_usd=0.50)

response = ""
async for event in delegate.run("Classify this report: ..."):
    if hasattr(event, "text"):
        response += event.text

print(response)

The async iterator is important. LLM responses come in chunks; Delegate streams each chunk as soon as it arrives, which lets you display partial text in a UI and abort early if the response is going somewhere bad. The max_llm_cost_usd guard trips hard — once your accumulated spend exceeds it, the call raises and nothing more is charged.

Structured output with Signature

Free-form text is fragile. If you write response.strip().split(":")[-1] to extract a category, one stray colon in the LLM's prose and your pipeline breaks. The production answer is to force the LLM to return a typed object, not prose. In Kaizen, a Signature is a Python class that declares typed input and output fields. You hand it to an agent and the agent produces an instance of that class — guaranteed schema, automatic validation, no string parsing. 

from kaizen import Signature, InputField, OutputField
from kaizen_agents.agents.specialized.simple_qa import SimpleQAAgent

class ReportExtraction(Signature):
    """Extract structured info from a Singapore company report."""

    report_text: str = InputField(description="Report excerpt (max ~800 tokens)")

    category: str = OutputField(
        description="One of: Financial, Technology, Healthcare, Real Estate, Manufacturing"
    )
    key_entities: list[str] = OutputField(
        description="Named entities (companies, products, locations)"
    )
    financial_metrics: list[str] = OutputField(
        description="Financial figures or percentages mentioned"
    )
    sentiment: str = OutputField(description="positive | negative | neutral")
    confidence: float = OutputField(description="Confidence in [0, 1]")


agent = SimpleQAAgent(
    signature=ReportExtraction,
    model="gpt-4o-mini",
    max_llm_cost_usd=1.0,
)

result = await agent.run(report_text=doc_text)
print(result.category, result.confidence, result.key_entities[:3])

Notice what this buys you. result.confidence is a float — not the string "high". You can write if result.confidence < 0.6: escalate_to_human(result) with a straight face. result.key_entities is already a list[str] — you can index, iterate, and persist to a DataFrame column without any parsing.

Worked Example: Classifying Singapore Company Reports

📝 Goal

Classify a small corpus of Singapore company reports into five categories, comparing zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompting on the same inputs. Then upgrade to a Signature for production-grade structured output.

Step 1.

Set up the environment and load the dataset.

import asyncio, os
import polars as pl
from kaizen_agents import Delegate
from kaizen import Signature, InputField, OutputField
from kaizen_agents.agents.specialized.simple_qa import SimpleQAAgent
from shared import MLFPDataLoader
from shared.kailash_helpers import setup_environment

setup_environment()
model = os.environ["DEFAULT_LLM_MODEL"]

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
reports = loader.load("mlfp06", "sg_company_reports.parquet")
sample = reports.head(10)
Step 2.

Define the five categories and a zero-shot classifier.

CATEGORIES = ["Financial", "Technology", "Healthcare", "Real Estate", "Manufacturing"]

async def zero_shot_classify(text: str) -> str:
    delegate = Delegate(model=model, max_llm_cost_usd=0.5)
    prompt = f"""Classify this Singapore company report into one category.
Categories: {', '.join(CATEGORIES)}

Report excerpt:
{text[:800]}

Respond with ONLY the category name."""
    response = ""
    async for event in delegate.run(prompt):
        if hasattr(event, "text"):
            response += event.text
    return response.strip()

Read the prompt carefully. The category list is inline so the model sees exactly which labels are allowed. The text is truncated to ~800 characters — this bounds cost and avoids hitting the context limit on longer reports. The closing instruction "ONLY the category name" is the format constraint.

Step 3.

Add few-shot examples. A good few-shot example set is diverse (covers all categories), clean (unambiguous), and short (token-efficient).

FEW_SHOT = [
    ("Revenue increased 15% driven by loan growth and net interest margin expansion.", "Financial"),
    ("The company launched its cloud-native SaaS platform serving enterprise clients.", "Technology"),
    ("Clinical trials for the oncology drug showed 40% improvement in patient outcomes.", "Healthcare"),
    ("The integrated township development in Jurong added 2,500 residential units.", "Real Estate"),
    ("Factory automation reduced production cycle time by 30% at the Tuas fab.", "Manufacturing"),
]

async def few_shot_classify(text: str) -> str:
    delegate = Delegate(model=model, max_llm_cost_usd=0.5)
    examples = "\n".join(f'Text: "{t}"\nCategory: {c}\n' for t, c in FEW_SHOT)
    prompt = f"""Classify Singapore company reports. Examples:

{examples}
Text: "{text[:800]}"
Category:"""
    response = ""
    async for event in delegate.run(prompt):
        if hasattr(event, "text"):
            response += event.text
    return response.strip()
Step 4.

Add chain-of-thought. The prompt asks for reasoning first, then a final line with the category.

async def cot_classify(text: str) -> tuple[str, str]:
    delegate = Delegate(model=model, max_llm_cost_usd=0.5)
    prompt = f"""Classify this report into one of: {', '.join(CATEGORIES)}.
Think step by step:
1. Identify key terms and topics in the text
2. Match those terms to the most relevant category
3. State your final classification on the last line

Report excerpt:
{text[:800]}

Step-by-step reasoning:"""
    response = ""
    async for event in delegate.run(prompt):
        if hasattr(event, "text"):
            response += event.text
    lines = response.strip().split("\n")
    final = lines[-1].strip()
    return response, final
Step 5.

Upgrade to Signature-based extraction for production use.

class ReportExtraction(Signature):
    report_text: str = InputField(description="Report excerpt")
    category: str = OutputField(description=f"One of: {', '.join(CATEGORIES)}")
    key_entities: list[str] = OutputField(description="Named entities")
    financial_metrics: list[str] = OutputField(description="Financial figures")
    sentiment: str = OutputField(description="positive | negative | neutral")
    confidence: float = OutputField(description="Confidence in [0, 1]")

agent = SimpleQAAgent(
    signature=ReportExtraction,
    model=model,
    max_llm_cost_usd=1.0,
)

result = await agent.run(report_text=sample["text"][0][:800])
print(f"Category: {result.category}")
print(f"Confidence: {result.confidence:.2f}")
print(f"Entities: {result.key_entities}")

The agent writes a prompt for you, calls the model, parses the response into the ReportExtraction schema, and hands you a typed Python object. If the model's output does not match the schema, the agent retries. You never see string parsing.

Step 6.

Compare accuracy. On the 10-document sample, agreement between strategies is the proxy for confidence: rows where all three agree are almost certainly correct; rows where strategies disagree are exactly where you would escalate to a human in production.

Mathematical Foundations: Cost, Temperature, and Tokens

The cost model

LLM providers charge per token, with different rates for input (prompt) and output (completion). The bill for one call is:

$$\text{cost} \;=\; N_\text{in}\cdot r_\text{in} + N_\text{out}\cdot r_\text{out}$$
$N_\text{in}$ — prompt tokens $N_\text{out}$ — completion tokens $r$ — rate per token

For gpt-4o-mini at the time of writing, $r_\text{in} \approx \$0.15$ per million tokens and $r_\text{out} \approx \$0.60$ per million tokens — output is 4× more expensive than input. That is why chain-of-thought is more expensive than zero-shot: you are asking the model to produce more output tokens (the reasoning) before the final answer.

Temperature and sampling

At inference, the model outputs logits $\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_V$ over the vocabulary. The probability of token $i$ under temperature $T$ is:

$$p_i \;=\; \frac{\exp(\ell_i / T)}{\sum_{j=1}^{V} \exp(\ell_j / T)}$$

When $T \to 0$, the softmax concentrates on the arg-max — the model always picks the highest-logit token (greedy decoding, fully deterministic). When $T \to \infty$, the distribution becomes uniform (pure randomness). In practice: $T = 0$ for classification and extraction; $T \approx 0.7$ for creative generation and self-consistency sampling. Use $T = 0$ whenever you want the same input to produce the same output.

🟢 Rules of thumb for $T$

Classification: $T = 0$. Structured extraction: $T = 0$. Summarisation: $T = 0.2$. Creative writing: $T = 0.7$–$1.0$. Self-consistency sampling: $T = 0.7$ so the chains diverge.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Parsing free-form text in production

"It worked on my three test cases" is not production-ready. The moment a user types a report that triggers a colon, a newline, or an em-dash inside the model's output, your .split(":")[-1] breaks. Use Signature.

⚠ Mistake 2: Asking for N answers at once

"Here are 20 reports, classify all of them in one prompt" sounds efficient. It usually is not. The model's error rate compounds — if each row is 95% correct, 20 rows in one prompt is $0.95^{20} \approx 36\%$ fully correct. Parallelise with asyncio.gather instead.

⚠ Mistake 3: No cost budget

A bug in a loop that accidentally calls the model 10,000 times with a 2000-token prompt is a real event. It has happened to people you know. Always pass max_llm_cost_usd to Delegate — it is one line that saves you from a surprise invoice.

⚠ Mistake 4: "Temperature = 0 means deterministic"

With most providers, $T = 0$ is nearly deterministic, not fully. GPU kernel non-determinism, batching effects, and minor provider-side jitter can still produce different outputs across calls. If you need byte-identical reproducibility for audit, log every prompt and response.
