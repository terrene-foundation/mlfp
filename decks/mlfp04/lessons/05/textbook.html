Lesson 4.5 Association Rules and Market Basket Analysis

Customers don't buy one item — they buy baskets. Patients don't have one symptom — they have syndromes. Web users don't visit one page — they follow paths. Association rule mining is the classical unsupervised technique for discovering these co-occurrence patterns without assuming any particular model. Today you will meet the Apriori and FP-Growth algorithms, the support / confidence / lift triangle, and how the rules you discover become features for supervised models downstream.

Why This Matters

Thirty years ago, a researcher at a supermarket chain found that customers who bought nappies on Thursday evenings also tended to buy beer. The story is probably apocryphal but the technique it inspired — market basket analysis — is very real. Today it powers "frequently bought together" widgets on every major e-commerce site, menu recommendations in restaurant apps, and cross-sell engines in telecoms. It is one of the oldest unsupervised techniques in the book and still one of the most useful.

Core Concepts

Support, confidence, lift

Let $T$ be the set of all transactions and let $X$ and $Y$ be (disjoint) sets of items. The three fundamental metrics are:

$$ \text{support}(X) \;=\; \frac{|\{t \in T : X \subseteq t\}|}{|T|} $$ $$ \text{confidence}(X \to Y) \;=\; \frac{\text{support}(X \cup Y)}{\text{support}(X)} $$ $$ \text{lift}(X \to Y) \;=\; \frac{\text{confidence}(X \to Y)}{\text{support}(Y)} $$

Support is "how often do we see this combination" — a pure frequency. Confidence is "given that we see $X$, how likely are we to also see $Y$" — a conditional probability. Lift is "is $Y$ more likely when $X$ is present than it would be by random chance?" — lift > 1 means positive association, lift < 1 means negative, lift ≈ 1 means independent.

Example: if 20% of baskets contain bread and 10% contain butter, and 5% contain both, then:

  • support(bread ∧ butter) = 0.05
  • confidence(bread → butter) = 0.05 / 0.20 = 0.25 — 25% of bread-buyers also buy butter
  • lift(bread → butter) = 0.25 / 0.10 = 2.5 — bread-buyers are 2.5× more likely to buy butter than a random customer

Lift is the metric you report to a marketing manager. A rule with high confidence but low lift tells you the consequent is common, not that there is a real association.

🔵 Theory

Lift is the ratio of the joint probability to the product of the marginals: $\text{lift}(X \to Y) = P(X \cap Y) / (P(X) P(Y))$. For independent events this is exactly 1. For perfectly correlated events (both present iff both present) it is $1 / P(X)$. This matches the mutual-information view of dependency.

Apriori — the classical algorithm

Association rule mining has two stages: (1) find all itemsets with support above a minimum threshold, and (2) generate rules from those itemsets. Stage (1) is the expensive one — there are $2^d$ possible itemsets over $d$ items, so brute-forcing is impossible. The Apriori property saves you:

🔵 Apriori property

If an itemset $X$ is not frequent (support below threshold), then no superset of $X$ is frequent either. Equivalently: every subset of a frequent itemset is itself frequent.

Apriori uses this property to prune the search space. It proceeds level by level: find all frequent 1-itemsets, use them to generate candidate 2-itemsets, prune those that contain an infrequent subset, count the survivors, repeat. In practice it scales to thousands of items and millions of transactions, but slows dramatically when itemsets get long.

FP-Growth — Apriori's faster cousin

Apriori has one big drawback: it scans the transaction database once per level. For long itemsets that is many scans. FP-Growth (Frequent Pattern Growth) avoids the candidate-generation step entirely by building a compressed data structure called an FP-tree on a single pass, then mining frequent itemsets by recursively walking the tree. FP-Growth is typically 2 – 10× faster than Apriori on dense data and is the default in modern libraries (mlxtend, SparkML, polars' own extensions).

You do not need to implement FP-Growth from scratch — understanding that it exists, that it is faster than Apriori, and that it gives the same answer is enough. In the exercise you will use both.

From rules to features

A key design point: association rules are not an end in themselves. The discovered rules become features for supervised models. Example: if you find that "purchased item A AND item B → 75% probability of purchasing item C", you can add a binary feature has_AB_pattern to your feature store and let a classifier learn how to use it. This is the feed-forward from unsupervised discovery into supervised decisions — the same story as using cluster labels as features in Lesson 4.1.

Mathematical Foundations

The combinatorial explosion

With $d$ items there are $2^d - 1$ non-empty itemsets. For $d = 100$ that is $\approx 10^{30}$ — a thousand million million million billion itemsets. Apriori's pruning is what makes the problem tractable: under a minimum support threshold, the number of itemsets you actually examine is typically a few orders of magnitude smaller than the worst case.

Conviction — beyond lift

An alternative to lift is conviction:

$$ \text{conv}(X \to Y) \;=\; \frac{1 - \text{support}(Y)}{1 - \text{confidence}(X \to Y)} $$

Conviction measures how much the rule improves over a model where $X$ and $Y$ are independent: conviction of 1 means the rule is no better than random, higher values mean stronger implication. Conviction is asymmetric (unlike lift) and is the metric of choice when direction matters — e.g., "does buying A cause buying B?"

Kailash integration: rules as feature columns

⚙ Kailash Engine

Kailash does not ship a dedicated association-rule engine — you use mlxtend.frequent_patterns.apriori and association_rules as the computational core. What Kailash provides is the FeatureEngineer engine to materialise discovered rules as binary feature columns in a polars DataFrame, which then flows into the training pipeline. This is how you turn an offline rule-mining exercise into a production feature.

Worked Example: Singapore Retail Baskets

import polars as pl
from mlxtend.frequent_patterns import apriori, association_rules, fpgrowth

from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
baskets = loader.load("mlfp04", "sg_retail_baskets.parquet")
# Each row: one transaction. Columns: item codes as booleans.

# Run both algorithms with the same threshold
freq_apriori = apriori(baskets.to_pandas(), min_support=0.01, use_colnames=True)
freq_fp = fpgrowth(baskets.to_pandas(), min_support=0.01, use_colnames=True)
print(f"Apriori found {len(freq_apriori)} frequent itemsets")
print(f"FP-Growth found {len(freq_fp)} frequent itemsets")  # should agree

# Generate rules with lift >= 2
rules = association_rules(freq_fp, metric="lift", min_threshold=2.0)
rules = rules.sort_values("lift", ascending=False).head(20)
print(rules[["antecedents", "consequents", "support", "confidence", "lift"]])

Interpretation walk-through: look at the top rule. If the antecedent is {instant_noodles, canned_tuna} and the consequent is {rice} with lift 3.5, the business reading is "customers buying instant noodles and canned tuna are 3.5× more likely to also buy rice than a random customer". That suggests a three-item promotion, a shelf placement decision, or a recommendation widget.

Common Mistakes

  1. Setting min_support too low. If you allow itemsets that appear in only 0.001% of baskets, you get millions of meaningless rules. Start at 1% and adjust.
  2. Reporting confidence instead of lift. High confidence means "the consequent is common", not "there is an association". Lift is the metric stakeholders should see.
  3. Ignoring reverse direction. $A \to B$ and $B \to A$ have different confidences — present both if direction matters.
  4. Treating rules as causal. Lift measures association, not causation. A rule is a correlation, not a causal claim.

Reflection

Association rules are the oldest trick in the unsupervised book and they still earn their keep. The algorithm is simple, the metrics are interpretable, and the output is a list of rules that a non-technical stakeholder can read and argue about. This is unusual in modern ML — most algorithms produce predictions, not explanations. When you need to have a meeting about why, association rules are often the easiest place to start.
