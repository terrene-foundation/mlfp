Lesson 2.1 Probability and Bayesian Thinking

In Module 1 you learned how to look at data. Now we ask the harder question: how confident can you be in what you saw? This lesson introduces the language of uncertainty — probability, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, and probability distributions — and uses it to update beliefs about Singapore HDB resale prices the moment new data arrives.

Why This Matters

A property analyst at HDB says the average 4-room flat in Tampines sells for about S$550,000. Then two transactions land in the database: one at S$620,000 and one at S$590,000. Should the analyst's estimate change? By how much? If a hundred more transactions arrive, should they trust the new average and discard the old belief, or blend them? Probability is the machinery that answers these questions, and Bayes' theorem is its central instrument.

Core Concepts

Probability as a weight on an event

The probability of an event $A$, written $P(A)$, is a number between 0 and 1 that expresses how likely $A$ is to happen. $P(A) = 0$ means impossible; $P(A) = 1$ means certain. For any event $A$ and its complement $A'$ ("not $A$"), the total probability is 1:

$$P(A) + P(A') = 1$$

For a fair six-sided die, each face has probability $1/6$. The probability of rolling an even number is $P(\{2,4,6\}) = 3/6 = 1/2$. The probability of the die landing at all is $1$ — one of the outcomes must happen.

Joint and conditional probability

Two events can happen together. The joint probability $P(A, B)$ is the probability that both $A$ and $B$ occur. The conditional probability $P(A \mid B)$ — read "probability of $A$ given $B$" — is the probability of $A$ under the restriction that $B$ has already happened. Conditional probability is defined as:

$$P(A \mid B) \;=\; \frac{P(A, B)}{P(B)} \qquad (\text{when } P(B) > 0)$$
$P(A, B)$ — joint probability $P(B)$ — probability of the condition The fraction is a "renormalisation" inside $B$

The intuition is simple. The condition $B$ shrinks the sample space to only the outcomes where $B$ holds. Inside that shrunken world, we ask what fraction also contains $A$. The division by $P(B)$ is just rescaling so the new world has total probability 1 again.

Rearranging the definition gives the multiplication rule:

$$P(A, B) \;=\; P(A \mid B)\,P(B) \;=\; P(B \mid A)\,P(A)$$

The fact that $P(A \mid B)\,P(B)$ and $P(B \mid A)\,P(A)$ both equal $P(A, B)$ is the seed of Bayes' theorem.

Deriving Bayes' theorem

Set the two expressions for $P(A, B)$ equal to each other and divide by $P(B)$:

$$P(A \mid B) \;=\; \frac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}$$
$P(A \mid B)$ — posterior $P(B \mid A)$ — likelihood $P(A)$ — prior $P(B)$ — evidence (normaliser)

That is Bayes' theorem. It is nothing more than algebra on the definition of conditional probability, yet it is the single most important formula in applied statistics. It tells you how to update a prior belief ($P(A)$) into a posterior belief ($P(A \mid B)$) after observing evidence $B$, using the likelihood $P(B \mid A)$ to quantify how well the evidence fits.

🔵 Theory: the denominator as a sum

In most applications, $P(B)$ is computed by the law of total probability: $P(B) = \sum_i P(B \mid A_i)\,P(A_i)$ where the $A_i$ partition the sample space. It is a normalising constant that ensures the posterior integrates (or sums) to 1. When we do continuous Bayes later, it becomes an integral: $P(B) = \int P(B \mid A)\,P(A)\,dA$. Many modern methods (MCMC, variational inference) exist precisely because this integral is hard to evaluate in closed form.

A Worked Example: COVID ART Tests

📝 The question

Singapore's Ministry of Health reports a current COVID prevalence of about 1% in the community. A rapid antigen test (ART) has sensitivity 90% (true-positive rate) and specificity 98% (so 2% false-positive rate). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have COVID? Most people — including doctors — guess around 90%. The answer is dramatically smaller.

Step 1.

Name the events. Let $T$ = "has COVID", $T'$ = "does not", $+$ = "tests positive".

Step 2.

Write down what we are given:

  • Prior: $P(T) = 0.01$, so $P(T') = 0.99$
  • Sensitivity: $P(+ \mid T) = 0.90$
  • False-positive rate: $P(+ \mid T') = 0.02$
Step 3.

Compute the total probability of testing positive:

$$P(+) \;=\; P(+ \mid T)P(T) + P(+ \mid T')P(T') \;=\; (0.90)(0.01) + (0.02)(0.99) \;=\; 0.0090 + 0.0198 \;=\; 0.0288$$
Step 4.

Apply Bayes' theorem:

$$P(T \mid +) \;=\; \frac{P(+ \mid T)\,P(T)}{P(+)} \;=\; \frac{0.0090}{0.0288} \;\approx\; 0.3125$$

A positive ART in a low-prevalence setting gives only a 31% chance of infection. Two out of three positive tests will be false positives. This is not a flaw of Bayes — it is a property of the numbers. When the prior is very small, even a "90% accurate" test is dominated by the false positives from the huge non-infected population.

🟢 Intuition: the 10,000-person ward

Imagine 10,000 people. 100 have COVID (1%). Of those 100, the test catches 90 (sensitivity). Of the 9,900 uninfected, the test wrongly flags 198 (2%). So $90 + 198 = 288$ people test positive; only $90/288 \approx 31\%$ of them are actually sick. The tree diagram above is exactly this counting exercise written in probability notation.

Probability Distributions

A random variable $X$ is a function from outcomes to numbers (the number of bedrooms in a flat, the resale price, the test score). The distribution of $X$ assigns a probability to each possible value (discrete) or a density to each region (continuous). Five distributions cover almost everything you will need in this course.

DistributionSupportWhen to reach for it
Normal $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$$(-\infty, \infty)$Measurements that cluster around a mean: temperature, height, HDB log-price
Beta $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$[0, 1]$Probabilities and proportions: conversion rate, click-through rate
Binomial $\text{Bin}(n, p)$$\{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$Counts of successes in $n$ trials: conversions in 1,000 visitors
Poisson $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$$\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$Counts over time or space: taxi arrivals per hour, bugs per KLOC
Exponential $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$$(0, \infty)$Waiting times: seconds between MRT arrivals, days until churn

Expected value

The expected value of a discrete random variable is the probability-weighted average of its possible values:

$$E[X] \;=\; \sum_{i} p_i\, x_i$$
$p_i$ — probability of outcome $i$ $x_i$ — the value at outcome $i$

For a continuous random variable, the sum becomes an integral: $E[X] = \int x\,f(x)\,dx$ where $f$ is the probability density function. The expected value is the long-run average if you repeated the experiment many times — the "gravitational centre" of the distribution.

Variance is the expected squared deviation from the mean. There are two equivalent forms, and the algebra between them is worth seeing now because it will reappear throughout Module 2:

$$\text{Var}(X) \;=\; E[(X - \mu)^2] \;=\; E[X^2] - \mu^2$$

The derivation expands the square inside the expectation:

$$\begin{aligned} E[(X - \mu)^2] &\;=\; E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] \\ &\;=\; E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2 \\ &\;=\; E[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 \\ &\;=\; E[X^2] - \mu^2 \end{aligned}$$

The second form ($E[X^2] - \mu^2$) is often easier to compute numerically; the first form makes the geometric meaning (squared distance from the centre) obvious.

Conjugate Priors: Bayes Without Integrals

Bayes' theorem in its continuous form involves an integral that is usually hard to compute:

$$P(\mu \mid \text{data}) \;=\; \frac{P(\text{data} \mid \mu)\,P(\mu)}{\int P(\text{data} \mid \mu')\,P(\mu')\,d\mu'}$$

A conjugate prior is a family of priors chosen so that the posterior stays in the same family — turning Bayes into algebra on the parameters. Two conjugate pairs cover most of what we do:

Normal–Normal (for a mean)

Prior: $\mu \sim N(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$. Observe $n$ data points with known variance $\sigma^2$. The posterior is also Normal with:

$$\mu_{\text{post}} = \frac{\sigma^2\,\mu_0 + n\,\sigma_0^2\,\bar{x}}{\sigma^2 + n\,\sigma_0^2}$$ $$\sigma^2_{\text{post}} = \frac{\sigma_0^2\,\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + n\,\sigma_0^2}$$

The posterior mean is a weighted average of the prior mean and sample mean, with weights determined by the precision (inverse variance) of each. As $n \to \infty$, the data dominates the prior.

Beta–Binomial (for a proportion)

Prior: $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$. Observe $k$ successes in $n$ trials. The posterior is:

$$p \mid \text{data} \;\sim\; \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\, \beta + n - k)$$

You literally add the successes to $\alpha$ and the failures to $\beta$. This is why Beta is the canonical prior for conversion rates: the update rule is elementary arithmetic and the posterior is instantly interpretable.

Singapore HDB Prices — A Bayesian Update

📝 Goal

The URA's 2019 annual report said the average 4-room Tampines resale price was $\mu_0 = \$480{,}000$ with standard deviation $\sigma_0 = \$40{,}000$ across neighbourhoods (your prior belief). You now pull $n = 30$ recent transactions from hdb_resale.parquet and compute a sample mean of $\bar{x} = \$542{,}000$ with assumed sampling standard deviation $\sigma = \$60{,}000$. What is your updated belief about the true mean?

Step 1.

Load the data with the standard M1 ritual.

import polars as pl
from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
df = loader.load("mlfp02", "hdb_resale.parquet")
recent = df.filter(
    (pl.col("town") == "TAMPINES") &
    (pl.col("flat_type") == "4 ROOM") &
    (pl.col("year") >= 2024)
)
print(recent.shape)
print(recent.select("resale_price").describe())
Step 2.

Encode the prior and likelihood parameters.

mu_0, sigma_0 = 480_000, 40_000     # prior from 2019 report
x_bar = recent["resale_price"].mean()
sigma = recent["resale_price"].std()
n = recent.height
Step 3.

Apply the Normal–Normal conjugate update.

precision_prior = 1 / sigma_0**2
precision_data  = n / sigma**2

mu_post  = (precision_prior * mu_0 + precision_data * x_bar) / (precision_prior + precision_data)
var_post = 1 / (precision_prior + precision_data)
sigma_post = var_post**0.5

print(f"Prior:     mu_0 = {mu_0:,.0f}, sigma_0 = {sigma_0:,.0f}")
print(f"Data:      x̄   = {x_bar:,.0f}, sigma = {sigma:,.0f}, n = {n}")
print(f"Posterior: mu   = {mu_post:,.0f}, sigma = {sigma_post:,.0f}")
Step 4.

Read the result. The posterior mean sits between the prior and the sample mean, pulled more towards the sample because $n = 30$ gives the data higher precision. The posterior std $\sigma_{\text{post}}$ is smaller than either input — we are more certain than we were before.

A 95% Bayesian credible interval is:

lo = mu_post - 1.96 * sigma_post
hi = mu_post + 1.96 * sigma_post
print(f"95% credible: [{lo:,.0f}, {hi:,.0f}]")

A credible interval is what most people incorrectly think a frequentist CI means: "there is a 95% probability the true mean lies in this range, given our prior and our data". In Lesson 2.2 we will see the frequentist CI and its more awkward interpretation.

Sampling Bias: the Friendship Paradox

Your friends, on average, have more friends than you do. This sounds insulting, but it is a mathematical fact — a consequence of how we sample. If you pick a random person from a social network, each person is equally likely. If you pick a random friend of a random person, people with many friends are more likely to be picked because they appear in more friendship lists.

🟣 Advanced: the algebra

Let $X$ be the number of friends of a uniformly chosen person. Let $Y$ be the number of friends of a uniformly chosen friendship endpoint (i.e., pick a random edge and then one of its two ends). Then $E[Y] = E[X^2]/E[X]$, and by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality $E[X^2]/E[X] \ge E[X]$ with equality only if everyone has the same number of friends. So sampling through friendships systematically over-represents high-degree nodes.

The lesson for data work: the way you sample shapes what you see. Surveys that recruit through existing users over-sample engaged people. Customer satisfaction data over-samples the very angry and the very happy. Bayesian thinking does not rescue you from biased sampling — you must model the selection mechanism explicitly.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Confusing $P(A \mid B)$ with $P(B \mid A)$

The COVID example is the canonical trap. $P(+ \mid T) = 0.90$ ("given you have COVID, the test is positive 90% of the time") is not the same as $P(T \mid +) \approx 0.31$ ("given the test is positive, you have COVID 31% of the time"). Bayes' theorem is the conversion factor between the two, and the prior $P(T)$ is what makes them differ.

⚠ Mistake 2: Ignoring the base rate

Saying "the test is 90% accurate" tells you nothing about $P(T \mid +)$ without the base rate $P(T)$. Journalists, managers, and even physicians routinely forget this. Always ask: what was the prior before the data arrived?

⚠ Mistake 3: Treating $P(A)$ as a fixed number when it is a belief

In Bayesian statistics, probabilities quantify beliefs about uncertain quantities — they are not frequencies of repeatable experiments. The prior $P(\mu)$ is your belief about the true mean before seeing the data; it is reasonable for two analysts to have different priors and therefore different posteriors. Over time, enough data makes the posteriors converge.
