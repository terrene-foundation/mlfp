Lesson 3.2 Bias-Variance, Regularisation, and Cross-Validation

In the next 75 minutes we derive, from first principles, the identity that governs every supervised-learning algorithm: expected test error equals squared bias plus variance plus irreducible noise. You will see why a more flexible model is not always a better model, meet L1 and L2 regularisation as two different geometric constraints on the coefficient vector, learn the Bayesian reading of both penalties, and pick the right cross-validation strategy for any data structure you are likely to see in practice.

Why This Matters

In Lesson 3.1 you engineered features. The moment you start training models on those features, a new problem appears: your training error keeps going down as you add capacity, but your test error starts going up. You are overfitting. Every regularisation, every dropout, every early stopping, every cross-validation protocol in modern ML exists to manage the tradeoff you are about to formalise. This lesson is the mathematical backbone of the rest of the course.

The Bias-Variance Decomposition — Full Derivation

Let us nail it down from scratch. Assume the true process that generates our data is:

$$y \;=\; f(x) + \varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon \sim (0,\,\sigma^2),\quad \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 0$$

where $f$ is the (unknown) true function and $\varepsilon$ is irreducible noise with zero mean and variance $\sigma^2$ — the part of the world that our features cannot explain no matter how hard we try. We fit a model $\hat{f}$ on a finite training sample $\mathcal{T}$; because $\mathcal{T}$ is random, $\hat{f}$ is also random. We want to understand the expected squared error at a fixed test point $x$:

$$\mathrm{Err}(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{T},\,\varepsilon}\bigl[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2\bigr]$$

Expand $y = f(x) + \varepsilon$ and add–subtract $\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{T}}[\hat{f}(x)]$:

$$\mathrm{Err}(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[(f(x) + \varepsilon - \hat{f}(x))^2\bigr] \;=\; \mathbb{E}\Bigl[\bigl(\underbrace{(f - \mathbb{E}\hat{f})}_{\text{bias}} \;+\; \underbrace{(\mathbb{E}\hat{f} - \hat{f})}_{-\text{fluctuation}} \;+\; \underbrace{\varepsilon}_{\text{noise}}\bigr)^2\Bigr]$$

The three terms are uncorrelated: $\varepsilon$ is independent of the training data, and the deviation $\mathbb{E}\hat{f} - \hat{f}$ has mean zero. All cross-terms therefore vanish in expectation, leaving:

$$\boxed{\;\mathrm{Err}(x) \;=\; \underbrace{\bigl(f(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)]\bigr)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\bigl[(\hat{f}(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)])^2\bigr]}_{\text{Variance}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Irreducible}}\;}$$

Read this equation slowly. It says the expected test error at any point $x$ decomposes into three strictly non-negative pieces, each with a distinct meaning:

  • Bias$^2$: how far, on average, our model's prediction is from the truth. A model that is too simple to represent $f$ has systematic error regardless of how much data you give it.
  • Variance: how much the prediction wobbles as the training set changes. A model that is too flexible fits noise and changes its answer when shown a different sample.
  • Irreducible noise $\sigma^2$: the part of $y$ that $x$ simply cannot explain. No amount of cleverness reduces this.
🔵 Theory — Why the cross-terms vanish

The clean decomposition relies on three facts: (1) $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 0$, so noise is uncorrelated with the deterministic part; (2) $\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x) - \mathbb{E}\hat{f}(x)] = 0$, so the fluctuation has mean zero; (3) $\varepsilon$ is independent of the training sample. Expand the square, apply linearity of expectation, and every cross-term is the product of a mean-zero quantity with something independent — so its expectation is zero. The only survivors are the three squared pieces.

Regularisation: Trading Variance for Bias

A perfectly flexible model ($\hat{f}$ = "memorise every training point") has zero bias and catastrophic variance. Regularisation deliberately introduces bias to reduce variance — and if done well, total expected error drops.

The standard linear regression loss is the residual sum of squares:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top x_i)^2$$

Regularised versions add a penalty on the coefficient vector:

$$\text{Ridge (L2):}\;\; \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) + \lambda \sum_{j}\beta_j^2 \qquad \text{Lasso (L1):}\;\; \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) + \lambda \sum_{j}|\beta_j|$$

The geometric picture: diamond vs circle

The regularised objective is equivalent, by Lagrange duality, to a constrained optimisation: minimise $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta})$ subject to $\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_p \le t$. L2 constrains $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ to a ball; L1 constrains it to a diamond (hyperoctahedron).

Why does L1 produce sparse solutions while L2 does not? Because the isocontours of the residual sum of squares are ellipses in $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-space, and the constrained optimum is the point where the smallest ellipse first touches the constraint region. The diamond has corners on the coordinate axes; those corners are likely first-contact points, and at a corner one or more coefficients are exactly zero. The circle has no corners and almost never lands with a coefficient exactly at zero. That single picture explains the entire sparsity story.

L2 = Gaussian prior, L1 = Laplace prior

There is a second reading of both penalties that will bring Module 2's Bayesian thinking back. Posit a prior on $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and derive the maximum a-posteriori estimate.

Assume Gaussian likelihood $y \mid x, \boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top x, \tau^2)$ and a Gaussian prior $\boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{0}, \tfrac{\tau^2}{\lambda} I)$. The log-posterior is, up to a constant:

$$\log p(\boldsymbol{\beta} \mid \text{data}) \;\propto\; -\frac{1}{2\tau^2}\sum_{i}(y_i - \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top x_i)^2 \;-\; \frac{\lambda}{2\tau^2}\sum_j \beta_j^2$$

Maximising this log-posterior is algebraically identical to minimising the ridge loss $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) + \lambda\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_2^2$. Ridge regression is Bayesian regression with a Gaussian prior. The regularisation strength $\lambda$ is the inverse prior variance: small $\lambda$ = flat prior = weak shrinkage, large $\lambda$ = narrow prior = strong pull toward zero.

Swap the Gaussian prior for a Laplace prior $p(\beta_j) \propto \exp(-|\beta_j|/b)$ and the log-prior becomes $-\sum_j |\beta_j|/b$. The MAP estimate is now the Lasso. So:

$$\text{Ridge} \;\equiv\; \text{MAP under Gaussian prior}\qquad \text{Lasso} \;\equiv\; \text{MAP under Laplace prior}$$

The Laplace prior has a sharp point at zero that the Gaussian lacks. That sharp point is the Bayesian image of the diamond corner — probability mass concentrates on exactly zero. Two pictures, same story.

🟣 Advanced — Elastic Net

Elastic Net combines both penalties: $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) + \lambda\bigl(\alpha\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_1 + (1-\alpha)\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_2^2\bigr)$. It is the MAP under a mixed Gaussian–Laplace prior. In practice, Elastic Net is what you reach for when Lasso oversimplifies correlated features (it picks one arbitrarily) and Ridge refuses to zero anything.

Cross-Validation — Estimating the Gap

The decomposition tells us what error is made of. It does not tell us how to measure it on a finite dataset. For that we need cross-validation.

The plain k-fold above assumes exchangeability — any row can go in any fold. Real datasets often have structure that demands a different strategy.

Nested cross-validation: honest model selection

If you use one CV loop to pick a hyperparameter and the same CV loop to report performance, you are reporting an optimistic number — you chose whatever happened to look best on those folds. Nested CV fixes this with an outer loop that estimates performance and an inner loop that tunes hyperparameters on each outer training split independently. 

from sklearn.model_selection import KFold, GridSearchCV
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge
import numpy as np

outer = KFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
outer_scores = []

for train_idx, test_idx in outer.split(X):
    X_tr, X_te = X[train_idx], X[test_idx]
    y_tr, y_te = y[train_idx], y[test_idx]

    inner = KFold(n_splits=3, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
    search = GridSearchCV(
        Ridge(),
        param_grid={"alpha": [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100]},
        cv=inner, scoring="neg_mean_squared_error",
    )
    search.fit(X_tr, y_tr)
    outer_scores.append(search.score(X_te, y_te))

print(f"Nested CV score: {np.mean(outer_scores):.4f} ± {np.std(outer_scores):.4f}")

Worked Example: Overfitting HDB Prices

📝 Goal

Use the HDB resale dataset from Module 1. Fit polynomial models of increasing degree (1, 2, 3, 5, 9) and plot training vs validation MSE. Then fit Ridge and Lasso at a range of $\lambda$ values and plot the coefficient paths. Observe bias-variance in action.

Step 1 — Polynomial fits. 
import polars as pl, numpy as np
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge, Lasso
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline

from shared import MLFPDataLoader
df = MLFPDataLoader().load("mlfp01", "hdb_resale.parquet")
X = df.select(["floor_area_sqm", "flat_age", "distance_to_mrt_km"]).to_numpy()
y = df["resale_price_sgd"].to_numpy()

for degree in [1, 2, 3, 5, 9]:
    model = Pipeline([
        ("poly", PolynomialFeatures(degree, include_bias=False)),
        ("lin",  LinearRegression()),
    ])
    mse = -cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5,
                            scoring="neg_mean_squared_error").mean()
    print(f"degree={degree:<2d}  CV MSE = {mse:,.0f}")

You will see CV MSE fall from degree 1 to degree 3, then rise again at degree 5 and explode at degree 9. The training MSE (not shown) falls monotonically. That gap between train and validation is variance.

Step 2 — Regularisation path. 
from kailash_ml import PreprocessingPipeline, ModelVisualizer

for alpha in [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000]:
    ridge = Ridge(alpha=alpha).fit(X, y)
    lasso = Lasso(alpha=alpha, max_iter=10_000).fit(X, y)
    print(f"alpha={alpha:<6g}  "
          f"ridge_coef={ridge.coef_.round(2)}  "
          f"lasso_coef={lasso.coef_.round(2)}")

Lasso zeros out coefficients as $\alpha$ grows; Ridge only shrinks them. At $\alpha = 100$ Lasso may already have set one or two coefficients to exactly zero; Ridge simply pulled them closer to zero.

Step 3 — Nested CV to pick $\alpha$. 
from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV, KFold

outer = KFold(5, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
scores = []
for tr, te in outer.split(X):
    inner = KFold(3, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
    search = GridSearchCV(Ridge(), {"alpha": np.logspace(-2, 3, 20)},
                          cv=inner, scoring="neg_mean_squared_error")
    search.fit(X[tr], y[tr])
    scores.append(search.score(X[te], y[te]))

print(f"Unbiased MSE estimate: {-np.mean(scores):,.0f}")

Compare this number against the best-score reported by a single-loop GridSearchCV. The single-loop number is always smaller — that gap is the bias you get when the same data chose the hyperparameter and reported the score.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1 — Reporting the best CV score as the test score

GridSearchCV.best_score_ is the score of the best hyperparameter, chosen because it looked best. It is optimistically biased. Report the nested-CV mean as your honest estimate of future performance.

⚠ Mistake 2 — Ignoring grouping structure

If multiple rows belong to the same patient / firm / city, plain k-fold splits them arbitrarily and the model sees one row of a group in training and another in validation. That is leakage. Use GroupKFold.

⚠ Mistake 3 — Shuffling time-series data

Shuffling destroys temporal order and lets the model see the future while training. TimeSeriesSplit validates on later dates only. If you are predicting next month's HDB prices, your CV must respect the arrow of time.
