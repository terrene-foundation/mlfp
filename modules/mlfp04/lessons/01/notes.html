Speaker Notes — Lesson 4.1 Clustering

Total time: ~90 minutes  ·  20 slides  ·  ~4 minutes per slide average

This is the first lesson of the unsupervised module. Students have been in "label-and-loss" mode for all of M3, so the first 10 minutes are about mindset shift. Keep reminding them: no ground truth, no error signal, just structure. The most common blocker is students trying to "validate" their clustering against some true label — there is no such thing today.

Slide 1~3 minTitle

Clustering

Open with a framing: "In M3 we built models that predicted a known outcome. Today we stop predicting and start discovering. The data does not tell you what it means — you have to find meaning." Set the 90-minute expectation up front. By the end, they should be able to run the full clustering pipeline on the e-commerce customer data.

If beginners look nervous: "We are not adding a new algorithm on top of M3 — we are removing one piece (the labels). If anything, the first clustering algorithm you will see is simpler than linear regression."
For experts: "You have almost certainly used K-means. The parts you have probably not seen carefully are the gap statistic, HDBSCAN's stability extraction, and the double opt-in pattern in AutoMLEngine."
→ "Let us start with why we even need unsupervised learning."
Slide 2~4 minM3 → M4 motivation

From M3 to M4: the no-label problem

Describe the scenario concretely: "Your marketing manager walks in with 10,000 rows. She says 'I want three or four segments — something we can price differently.' There is no label. There is no RMSE to minimise. What do you do?" Pause. Let them sit with it for 5 seconds. The answer — clustering — is the whole module.

→ "Clustering in one picture."
Slide 3~3 minWorkflow diagram

Clustering in one picture

Walk the diagram left to right. Unlabelled blob → coloured groups → business meaning. Emphasise the last box: the algorithm finds the groups, the human finds the meaning. If all you do is produce "cluster 0, cluster 1, cluster 2" and walk away, you have failed the task.

→ "Let us start with the workhorse — K-means."
Slide 4~5 minK-means algorithm

K-means: alternate two trivial steps

Walk the two-step loop on the board. Emphasise that both steps are monotonic — assign strictly decreases or holds inertia, update strictly decreases or holds inertia — which is why the algorithm must converge. Converging to a local optimum is a different problem (solved by k-means++ init).

"If the formula looks scary: read it as 'for every cluster, sum the squared distance from each point to its centroid, then add all those up'. That's it. K-means tries to make that number as small as possible."
"The inertia is the unnormalised variance — minimising it is equivalent to maximising the Calinski-Harabasz ratio for fixed K. This is why CH often agrees with K-means' own choice: it is measuring the same thing."
→ "Here's what convergence looks like visually."
Slide 5~3 minConvergence snapshots

K-means convergence in three snapshots

Walk the three panels. In iteration 0 the centroids are randomly initialised (grey points, badly placed centroids). By iteration 3 the centroids are migrating toward the true cluster centres. At convergence, every point's colour matches its nearest centroid and no further movement is possible.

→ "But you still have to pick K."
Slide 6~4 minPicking K

Picking K: elbow vs silhouette

Show the code. Emphasise that the elbow method is visual and three analysts will pick three different elbows. The silhouette score gives an objective number. In practice, try silhouette for K from 2 to 10, and take the max. If the silhouette is flat, your data does not have a clear K.

→ "And what exactly is the silhouette?"
Slide 7~5 minSilhouette derivation

Silhouette in one equation

Walk through $a(i)$, $b(i)$, and $s(i)$ one at a time. $a$ is the intra-cluster distance (small is good). $b$ is the nearest-neighbour cluster distance (large is good). The silhouette rewards $b$ being much larger than $a$.

"$s(i) = 1$ means point $i$ is sitting right on top of its own cluster and miles from any other. $s(i) = 0$ means it could go either way. $s(i) = -1$ means it is in the wrong cluster entirely."
"Silhouette is well-defined for any metric, not just Euclidean. For cosine distance on TF-IDF vectors (we will see this in 4.6), it is still a valid quality score — the denominator normalises out the scale."
→ "K-means is not the only game. Let us meet its cousin — the dendrogram."
Slide 8~5 minDendrogram

Hierarchical clustering: one tree, many cuts

The beauty of hierarchical clustering is that you do not have to commit to $K$ up front. Build the tree once, then cut it at whatever level produces the right number of clusters for your question. Point out the red dashed line: cutting there yields {A,B,C}, {D,E}, {F,G,H}. Move the line up and you get two clusters; move it down and you get five.

→ "The choice that matters most is linkage."
Slide 9~4 minLinkage table

Linkage: how do you measure cluster distance?

Walk the table row by row. Single linkage is the "friend-of-a-friend" approach and produces long chains — good for connectivity problems, bad for blob detection. Complete linkage is paranoid and produces tight balls. Average linkage is the compromise. Ward's linkage is the K-means-compatible choice and is usually the default.

→ "K-means and Ward both assume blobs. Real data has outliers."
Slide 10~5 minDBSCAN

DBSCAN: density, not centroids

Describe the three-way classification: core, border, noise. Walk the diagram. Two knobs: $\varepsilon$ (how far is "nearby") and min_samples (how many neighbours make a dense region). Key selling point: DBSCAN does not force every point into a cluster. Outliers get label $-1$.

"If a point has at least 5 friends within 0.3 units of it, it is a core point. If it has fewer friends but one of them is a core point, it is a border point. If neither, it is noise."
→ "One $\varepsilon$ does not fit clusters of different densities. Enter HDBSCAN."
Slide 11~3 minHDBSCAN

HDBSCAN: DBSCAN at every density

Explain the key insight: HDBSCAN builds the full hierarchy of DBSCAN clusterings across all values of $\varepsilon$ and picks the single clustering that is most stable across the range. In practice, you only set min_cluster_size and HDBSCAN does the rest. This is our default when we do not know the right $K$ and suspect varying density.

→ "One more algorithm for the topology-aware case."
Slide 12~3 minSpectral

Spectral clustering in one line

Keep this brief. Mention that it exists, that it handles non-convex clusters, and that it is $O(n^3)$. Most students will never use it on business data but should recognise it on a scikit-learn cheatsheet.

"Spectral clustering on the k-nearest-neighbour graph is equivalent to minimising the normalised cut of the graph. This connects to graph neural networks — we will see why in M5 optional content."
→ "How do we judge which clustering is best?"
Slide 13~4 minThree metrics

Three metrics, one decision

Walk the table. No single metric is sufficient. In practice, if silhouette, DB, and CH all agree, you have a good clustering. If they disagree, something is off — usually the number of clusters or the feature set.

→ "And there is a deeper question — do the clusters even exist?"
Slide 14~4 minGap statistic

Gap statistic — does the structure even exist?

This is the most underused concept in clustering. All the other metrics will happily give you an "optimal" $K$ for pure noise. The gap statistic compares your data's dispersion to the dispersion under a null reference — if your data is no better than noise, the gap is not significantly positive, and the honest answer is $K = 1$ (no clusters).

"The null reference is typically uniform over the bounding box of your data or uniform over the principal-component box. Sample ~50 null datasets, compute $W_K^\star$ on each, take the mean for the expectation."
→ "The Kailash way to sweep all of this automatically."
Slide 15~4 minAutoMLEngine

Kailash AutoMLEngine — clustering sweep with guardrails

Show the config. Emphasise two things: (1) the engine does the whole sweep for you with agent=False — no LLM involvement, just random search over K-means/GMM/HDBSCAN with silhouette scoring; and (2) the double opt-in pattern. You must both set agent=True AND install kailash-ml[agents]. This is deliberate — LLM calls cost money and introduce non-determinism.

→ "One universal rule before we run anything."
Slide 16~3 minStandardisation

Standardise, always

Drill this hard. Every distance-based algorithm — K-means, hierarchical, DBSCAN, HDBSCAN, spectral — needs standardised features. Otherwise the feature with the biggest range (usually "income" or "total spend") dominates and the clustering reduces to a 1D partition of that column.

From cluster to business meaning

"If you can't name a cluster, you haven't profiled it enough. Go back to the data and ask: what makes these customers different from the average? Three or four features should jump out."
