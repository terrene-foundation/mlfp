Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.2 Bias-Variance, Regularisation, and Cross-Validation

Total time: ~75 minutes · 14 slides · ~5 min per slide average

This is the most mathematically dense lesson in Module 3. The derivation is short (three lines) but the consequences run through every subsequent lesson. Use the dart-board metaphor early and often — it is the bridge between the equation and the intuition.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

The single most important equation in applied ML

Open boldly: "Today we derive the one equation that governs every ML algorithm you will ever use. Bias-variance is not a slide in a textbook; it is the reason every model has hyperparameters, every training loop has regularisation, and every reported number has a confidence interval."

→ "Let's start with the canonical picture."
Slide 2~4 minCanonical curve

The U-shaped test error

Walk the diagram. "Training error — blue — only ever goes down as you add capacity. Test error — red — is U-shaped. The gap between them is variance. The floor at the bottom is irreducible noise."

Anchor it: "Underfitting is the left side — too simple to capture the signal. Overfitting is the right side — too flexible, memorising noise. The sweet spot is the minimum of the red curve, and everything we do today is about finding it."

→ "Now the math."
Slide 3~3 minThe setup

Setting up the expectation

Walk the equations. "The world has a true function $f$ we don't know. Every observation has irreducible noise. We fit $\hat{f}$ on a random training set. Because the training set is random, $\hat{f}$ is random. We care about expected squared error at a new test point $x$."

For beginners: "Two sources of randomness. The noise in $y$ — we can't predict it. The training sample — if we had drawn different data, we would have fitted a different model. The expectation averages over both."
→ "The trick: add and subtract the expected prediction."
Slide 4~5 minThe decomposition

The three-term identity

Walk the boxed equation slowly. Name each term: bias squared, variance, irreducible. "Each term is non-negative. Total error is a sum. To reduce total error, we must reduce at least one of the first two — the third is fixed by nature."

Sketch the vanishing cross-terms on the board if you have time. It takes 90 seconds and cements the "it's exactly three terms, nothing more, nothing less" point.

For experts who ask about regression to the mean: "Yes, Stein's paradox says the sample mean is inadmissible as an estimator of a multivariate Gaussian mean. Shrinkage estimators dominate it. That is the deep reason ridge regression works even when you have infinite data — biasing toward zero reduces total risk."
→ "And the dart-board picture to make it stick."
Slide 5~3 minDart board

Four quadrants of bias and variance

Walk the four boards. "Top-left: tight cluster on the bullseye — our dream. Top-right: tight cluster off-centre — the model is consistently wrong, systematic bias. Bottom-left: scattered around the centre — unbiased on average but noisy on any single run. Bottom-right: scattered and off — the disaster case."

Map each to a real model. "Linear regression on a non-linear dataset is high bias, low variance. A very deep decision tree on a small dataset is low bias, high variance. Random forests reduce variance by averaging many low-bias trees."

→ "So how do we engineer the tradeoff?"
Slide 6~3 minRegularisation intro

Regularisation — trade variance for bias

"Here is the move: take a zero-bias model and deliberately add bias to kill the variance. If we pick the right penalty strength, total error drops. That's regularisation in one sentence."

Introduce the two penalties. "L2 penalises squared coefficients. L1 penalises absolute values. Both shrink. Only L1 drives coefficients to exactly zero. Why is a geometric question."

→ "Enter the diamond and the circle."
Slide 7~5 minL1 vs L2 geometry

Diamond vs circle

Walk the diagram. "The OLS solution is the centre of those ellipses — the unconstrained optimum. The regularised optimum is the point where the smallest ellipse touches the constraint region."

The circle has no corners — the hit point is almost always in the interior of any axis and both coefficients are nonzero. The diamond has corners on the axes — when the ellipse touches a corner, one coefficient is exactly zero. That is the entire sparsity story.

If geometry is unfamiliar: "Picture dropping a ball onto a diamond-shaped table — it rolls to a corner. Drop it on a circular table — it stops wherever it first touches. The corners are the axis crossings, and that's where coefficients become zero."
→ "A second, equivalent way to see it — Bayesian."
Slide 8~5 minBayesian reading

Ridge = Gaussian prior, Lasso = Laplace prior

This is where Module 2's Bayesian thinking pays off. Walk the log-posterior equation. "The first term is the Gaussian log-likelihood — it becomes the residual sum of squares. The second term is the log-prior. A Gaussian prior becomes a squared coefficient penalty. So ridge regression is literally maximum-a-posteriori Bayesian regression with a Gaussian prior."

Then swap to Laplace. "The Laplace distribution has a sharp peak at zero and exponential tails. Its log is $-|\beta|/b$. MAP under that prior is Lasso. The peak at zero is the Bayesian twin of the L1 diamond corner — probability mass concentrates on 'coefficient exactly zero'."

For experts: "Why MAP and not the full posterior? Full posterior is Bayesian linear regression — gives you a distribution over $\boldsymbol\beta$. MAP collapses that to the mode for computational convenience. If you keep the full posterior you get predictive intervals, which we pick up in Lesson 3.8 with conformal prediction."
→ "Elastic net for when you can't choose."
Slide 9~3 minElastic Net

Elastic Net — mix and match

"In practice, you reach for Elastic Net when you have groups of correlated features. Lasso will arbitrarily pick one feature from each correlated group; Ridge will spread the coefficient across the group. Elastic Net does both — it shrinks groups together (L2 behaviour) while still zeroing truly irrelevant features (L1 behaviour)."

"The mixing parameter $\alpha$ is itself a hyperparameter. $\alpha = 1$ recovers Lasso; $\alpha = 0$ recovers Ridge. In practice you tune it on a grid or just use ElasticNetCV."

→ "Now the other half of the lesson — how we estimate error."
Slide 10~4 minCV strategies

Five cross-validation strategies

Walk the table. "KFold for i.i.d. data. Stratified when classes are imbalanced — otherwise a fold with zero positive samples breaks the metric. GroupKFold when the same entity appears in multiple rows — patients, companies, users. TimeSeriesSplit for anything with a clock. Nested CV when you are both tuning and evaluating."

Give a Singapore example for each. "Credit applications: i.i.d., KFold with stratification on default. ICU admissions: 1–5 per patient, use GroupKFold by patient_id. Monthly HDB prices: temporal, use TimeSeriesSplit respecting the calendar."

→ "Here is what KFold looks like."
Slide 11~3 min5-fold diagram

5-fold CV — the diagram

Walk the five rows. "Each row is one fold. The red square is the validation set. Five models get trained in total. You average the five validation scores and that's your CV estimate."

Mention $k=5$ as the default. "$k=2$ wastes half the data on every fit. $k=n$ — leave-one-out — has very high variance because the training sets are nearly identical across folds. $k = 5$ or $10$ is the bias-variance sweet spot for the CV estimate itself."

→ "Now the harder question: how do we tune and evaluate honestly?"
Slide 12~5 minNested CV

Nested CV — honest model selection

The core pitch: "If you use GridSearchCV and report best_score_ as your performance estimate, you are lying to yourself. That score is the score of the hyperparameter you chose because it happened to win the lottery on those folds. It is optimistically biased."

Walk the code. "Outer loop: five folds. Inside each outer fold, we run a complete GridSearchCV on the outer training set to pick the best hyperparameter. Then we evaluate that hyperparameter on the outer test set — which the inner loop never saw. Average the five outer scores. That number is unbiased."

For experts who ask if this is wasteful: "Yes, it's 5× more compute. But it's the only way to get an honest estimate when tuning. In practice, in Lesson 3.7 you will use Kailash's HyperparameterSearch which runs this pattern automatically with Bayesian inner loops for speed."
→ "Let's see it live on HDB."
Slide 13~5 minHDB worked example

Watching the U-shape form on real data

Walk the code. "We fit polynomials of degree 1, 2, 3, 5, 9 on HDB prices. Cross-validation gives us the test error curve." Run it live if you can.

The expected output: "degree 1 high error (underfitting — a plane can't capture the real pricing surface), degree 3 minimum (just enough curvature), degree 9 catastrophic (fitting noise). That's the U-shape from slide 2, live."

→ "Three mistakes to not make."
Slide 14~4 minMistakes + reflection

Three mistakes + what you can do

Walk the three traps. Emphasise that #1 (reporting best_score_) is the one almost every practitioner has made at least once, and it causes production embarrassment every time.

Close with the bullet list. "You can derive the decomposition. You can explain sparsity two ways. You can pick the right CV strategy for any data structure. That's the theoretical backbone of Module 3."

Bridge: "In Lesson 3.3 we meet the model zoo — SVM, KNN, Naive Bayes, decision trees, random forests. Every one of them has hyperparameters that trade bias for variance. You will spot the pattern every time."

→ End of Lesson 3.2. Break.
