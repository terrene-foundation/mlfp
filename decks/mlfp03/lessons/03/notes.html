Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.3 The Complete Supervised Model Zoo

Total time: ~90 minutes · 15 slides · ~5.5 min per slide average

This is the lesson where the zoo opens. Learners will be excited — five model families in one session is a lot of new toys. Pace yourself: SVM gets the most time because its math is the hardest; NB gets the least because it is conceptually cheap. Leave twelve minutes at the end for the live comparison run and the discussion.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Opening — "The zoo opens today"

Open with the promise: "By the end of this lesson you will have trained five different model families on the same dataset, watched them compete, and know which one to reach for given a data shape. No more 'try everything and hope' — you will be able to predict which family wins, and why."

→ "Here are the five."
Slide 2~3 minFive families

The zoo map

Preview the five. Give each one a one-line geometric identity: SVM is a hyperplane. KNN is a neighborhood. NB is independent per feature. Trees are axis-aligned rectangles. Forests are averaged trees.

Tell them you will derive, hand-compute, or geometrically intuit something concrete for each — this is not a tour, it is a working lesson.

→ "Start with the one with the prettiest geometry."
Slide 3~4 minSVM picture

SVM — maximum margin

Walk the diagram. "Two classes of points. Many lines separate them. SVM picks the line that is as far as possible from the nearest point of either class. The distance from the line to the nearest points is the margin. The points touching the margin are the support vectors."

Key intuition: "The support vectors are the only points that matter. You could delete every other point and get the same model. That's why SVMs are memory-efficient at inference — you only store the SVs."

→ "Now the math that makes it a quadratic program."
Slide 4~5 minSVM math

The soft-margin quadratic program

Walk the primal. "Minimise the norm of $\boldsymbol{w}$ — that maximises the margin. Subject to every point being on the right side with at least unit margin. In real data, not every point fits, so we add slack variables $\xi_i$ and pay a penalty $C$ per violation."

$C$ is the bias-variance dial. "Small $C$ = tolerant = smooth boundary = high bias / low variance. Large $C$ = strict = complex boundary = low bias / high variance. Same story as Lesson 3.2."

For experts asking about the dual: "Yes, we get the dual via Lagrange. It only depends on inner products and at the optimum most $\alpha_i$ are zero — exactly the support vectors. That's how the kernel trick plugs in on the next slide."
→ "The kernel trick — why SVMs became famous."
Slide 5~5 minKernel trick

Non-linear with a linear model

Explain the trick in plain language: "Imagine mapping every point into a higher-dimensional space where it becomes linearly separable. Then do linear SVM there. The problem is the mapping $\phi$ could be expensive — infinite dimensional even. The kernel trick says: you don't need $\phi$. You just need the inner products $\langle \phi(x), \phi(x')\rangle$, and for common kernels you can compute those directly without ever touching $\phi$."

Walk the kernel table. "Linear kernel — just a dot product, useful for text where everything is already sparse and high-dim. RBF kernel — Gaussian bump around each support vector, smooth non-linear boundaries. Polynomial — interactions up to degree $d$."

→ "Now something very different — KNN."
Slide 6~6 minKNN + curse

KNN and the curse of dimensionality

Spend time here — this is the intuition learners will remember. "KNN is almost trivial: find the $k$ nearest training points to your query, take the majority vote. No training. No model. The magic is in the distance metric."

Then pivot to the curse. Walk the diagram. "In 2D, your nearest neighbor is meaningfully closer than a random point. In 500D, it is almost exactly the same distance as a random point. That's distance concentration — and it kills KNN as soon as you have enough features."

Quantify: "With 10 features and enough data, KNN can be competitive. With 100 features, it starts to struggle. With 1000 features, it is essentially random. That's why KNN is not a default choice in modern tabular ML."

For beginners lost in the math: "The intuition is 'everything is equidistant from everything else in high dimensions'. Picture a 1000-dimensional unit cube — there is so much room that every pair of points is roughly the same distance apart. So 'nearest' stops meaning anything."
→ "Now the simplest probabilistic model — Naive Bayes."
Slide 7~4 minNaive Bayes

Naive Bayes — wrong but often right

Walk the equation. "Bayes theorem, with one aggressive simplification: assume every feature is independent given the class. That is never true — 'age' and 'income' are correlated even within churners. But the classifier still often works."

Why it works despite being wrong: "The classifier only needs to pick the right class, not output a correctly calibrated probability. Even if the probability estimates are biased, the ranking can still put the right class on top. NB is rank-correct more often than it is probability-correct."

Three variants. "GaussianNB for continuous features. MultinomialNB for counts — bag-of-words in text. BernoulliNB for binary flags. The multinomial one is the best spam baseline ever invented."

→ "Now the interpretable favourite — decision trees."
Slide 8~4 minTree splitting

Decision trees — Gini and information gain

Walk both impurity measures. "Gini is 1 minus the sum of squared class probabilities. Zero for a pure node, 0.5 for a balanced binary split. Entropy is the same idea with logarithms. In practice they build nearly identical trees; Gini is faster."

Then information gain. "IG is the drop in impurity from the parent to the weighted average of the children. The algorithm tries every feature and every threshold and picks the split with the largest IG. Recursive: each child becomes a parent for the next split."

→ "Now do one by hand."
Slide 9~5 minGini by hand

Compute Gini by hand

This is the hand-computation they will remember. Walk the numbers slowly. "Parent node: 10 churn, 6 retain. $p_{\text{churn}} = 0.625$. Gini = 1 - 0.625² - 0.375² = 0.469."

"Candidate split: {8 churn, 1 retain} on the left, {2 churn, 5 retain} on the right. Compute Gini of each child. Weight by child size. Subtract from parent Gini. That's IG = 0.179."

Tell them: "The algorithm does exactly this arithmetic for thousands of candidate splits per node and keeps the best. That's decision-tree training in one slide."

→ "One tree overfits. Average many, get a forest."
Slide 10~4 minRandom forest diagram

Random forests — decorrelating trees

Walk the diagram. "Training data at the top. Bootstrap sample 1 grows tree 1. Bootstrap sample 2 grows tree 2. Continue for $T$ trees. At each split, only a random subset of features is considered — this is the second decorrelation trick."

"Prediction: pass the input through every tree and average (regression) or vote (classification). The individual trees are high-variance and decorrelated; their average has much lower variance. That's bagging."

→ "And the beautiful side effect — free validation."
Slide 11~4 minOOB = 1/e

OOB = 1/e

This is the slide where you prove a beautiful limit. "When you draw a bootstrap sample of size $n$ from $n$ points, each individual point has probability $(1 - 1/n)^n$ of never being drawn. As $n$ gets large, that probability converges to $1/e$, about 36.8 percent."

"So every tree has ~37% of the original data unused. Those out-of-bag samples form a validation set for free. Aggregate across all trees and you get an unbiased generalisation estimate without any explicit CV. That's why random forests are so convenient — you don't even need to hold out data."

For experts: "The proof is one line. $\log(1-1/n)^n = n\log(1-1/n) \approx -1$ for large $n$, so the whole thing converges to $e^{-1}$. Standard calculus-1 limit."
→ "Now the live comparison."
Slide 12~4 minComparison code

Five models, fair competition

Walk the dictionary. "Same StratifiedKFold, same X, same y, same scorers. That makes the comparison honest — any difference we see is due to the model, not the preprocessing or the splits."

Mention inline that SVM needs probability=True to get AUC, KNN doesn't need training but its inference is slow, and random forest gets OOB for free.

→ "Here's what typically happens."
Slide 13~5 minLeaderboard

Typical leaderboard

Walk the table row by row. Comment on each: "Random forest wins AUC by a narrow margin. SVM is very close second but 4× slower — not worth it here. KNN lags because we have 30+ features and distance concentration has started. Naive Bayes is decent but handicapped by the independence assumption; the behavioural features are correlated with each other. Decision tree is halfway between — decent AUC, very fast, fully interpretable."

Make the production call explicit: "For this problem, I would ship the random forest. Best metric, cheap inference, feature importance for free, and OOB means I trust my generalisation estimate. Decision tree is my backup if the business demands a human-readable rule set."

→ "A pocket guide for future projects."
Slide 14~3 minPick the right tool

Which model when

Walk the table. Tell them to screenshot this slide. "This is the cheat sheet. Memorise which row fits your data shape and you will pick the right family in 30 seconds instead of burning a day."

Emphasise that "random forest is the right default when you don't yet know what you're doing". That is not lazy — it is correct. Most tabular ML problems in production run on random forests or their boosted cousins (next lesson).

→ "Three traps."
Slide 15~3 minTraps + reflection

Three traps + reflection

Walk the three traps. Distance-based models without scaling is the single most common mistake on this lesson's exercises — warn them loudly.

Close with the bullet list and the bridge. "Next lesson: we ditch the random-forest family and replace it with the single model family that has won nearly every tabular competition of the last decade — gradient boosting. We will derive the XGBoost split gain from a second-order Taylor expansion. The math is gorgeous."

→ End of Lesson 3.3. Break.
