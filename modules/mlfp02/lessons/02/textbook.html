Lesson 2.2 Parameter Estimation and Inference

You have data but no prior — how do you pick the "best" parameter value? This lesson derives maximum likelihood estimation from first principles, proves Bessel's correction, introduces the central limit theorem as the engine of frequentist confidence intervals, and shows when MLE fails and how MAP estimation fixes it.

Population versus sample

A population is the idealised infinite set of all possible observations. A sample is the finite collection you actually have. Parameters (denoted with Greek letters like $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$) describe the population; statistics (denoted $\bar{x}$, $s^2$) are what we compute from the sample in an attempt to estimate those parameters.

The subtlety is that $\bar{x}$ and $s^2$ are themselves random — different samples give different values. A good estimator is one whose distribution is concentrated around the true parameter.

The sampling distribution and the CLT

Take a population with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Draw samples of size $n$ and compute the mean of each sample. Those sample means form their own distribution — the sampling distribution of the mean. Two facts are true for any finite-variance population:

$$E[\bar{X}] \;=\; \mu \qquad \text{Var}(\bar{X}) \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$
the sample mean is unbiased variance shrinks like $1/n$

The standard deviation of $\bar{X}$ is $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, called the standard error. The CLT adds a remarkable third fact: whatever the shape of the original population, for large enough $n$, the sampling distribution of $\bar{X}$ is approximately Normal.

🔵 Theory: the central limit theorem

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. draws from a distribution with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$. Then as $n \to \infty$,

$$\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \;\xrightarrow{d}\; N(0, 1)$$

This is why the Normal distribution dominates statistics: it shows up whenever you average enough independent things, regardless of where they came from. The HDB price distribution is right-skewed, but the distribution of mean prices computed from samples of 100 flats is beautifully Normal.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Suppose you observe $n$ data points $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ that you believe were drawn independently from a Normal distribution $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. You do not know $\mu$ or $\sigma^2$. What values should you pick? The maximum likelihood principle says: pick the values that make the observed data most probable.

Deriving the Normal MLE

The probability density of a single Normal observation is:

$$f(x_i \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

For $n$ independent observations, the joint density — the likelihood $L(\mu, \sigma^2)$ — is the product:

$$L(\mu, \sigma^2) \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Products are hard to differentiate, so we take the logarithm — the log-likelihood $\ell = \log L$. Since log is monotonic, the maximum is in the same place:

$$\ell(\mu, \sigma^2) \;=\; -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\log\sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2$$

Solving for $\hat{\mu}$

Take the partial derivative with respect to $\mu$ and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu) \;=\; 0$$

This gives $\sum x_i = n\mu$, so:

$$\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \;=\; \bar{x}$$
The MLE of the mean is the sample mean — the familiar $\bar{x}$.

Solving for $\hat{\sigma}^2$

Take the partial derivative with respect to $\sigma^2$ (treating $\sigma^2$ as a single variable) and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma^2} \;=\; -\frac{n}{2\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{2\sigma^4}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2 \;=\; 0$$

Multiplying through by $2\sigma^4$ and substituting $\hat{\mu} = \bar{x}$:

$$\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2$$

Look carefully — this divides by $n$, not $n - 1$. The MLE of the variance is biased: it systematically underestimates the true population variance. This is where Bessel's correction comes in.

Bessel's correction: why divide by $n - 1$

We want an estimator $s^2$ whose expected value equals the true variance $\sigma^2$. Let's compute the expected value of the naive MLE variance and see the gap.

🔵 Theory: the derivation

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Define $\tilde{s}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (X_i - \bar{X})^2$.

Expand $(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = ((X_i - \mu) - (\bar{X} - \mu))^2$ and sum:

$$\sum_i (X_i - \bar{X})^2 \;=\; \sum_i (X_i - \mu)^2 - n(\bar{X} - \mu)^2$$

Take expectations:

$$E\!\left[\sum_i (X_i - \bar{X})^2\right] \;=\; \sum_i E[(X_i - \mu)^2] - n\,E[(\bar{X} - \mu)^2]$$

Each term $E[(X_i - \mu)^2] = \sigma^2$, and $E[(\bar{X} - \mu)^2] = \sigma^2/n$:

$$= n\sigma^2 - n \cdot \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \;=\; (n-1)\sigma^2$$

Therefore:

$$E[\tilde{s}^2] \;=\; \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$$

To fix the bias, multiply by $n/(n-1)$ — which is the same as dividing the sum by $n-1$ instead of $n$:

$$s^2 \;=\; \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i - \bar{X})^2 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad E[s^2] \;=\; \sigma^2$$

The intuition: using $\bar{X}$ (instead of the unknown $\mu$) to center the squared deviations makes them systematically smaller, because $\bar{X}$ is the value that minimises $\sum(x_i - c)^2$. The $n/(n-1)$ factor exactly compensates for this self-centering. One degree of freedom was spent computing $\bar{X}$; only $n - 1$ remain.

🟢 The intuition

If you only have two data points, say $10$ and $14$, their sample mean is $12$. The squared deviations from the mean are both $4$, total $8$. Dividing by $n = 2$ gives variance $4$; dividing by $n - 1 = 1$ gives variance $8$. With only two points, the $n - 1$ version is the honest one — you cannot have zero uncertainty after seeing two numbers.

Confidence intervals

Once you know the sampling distribution of an estimator, you can build a confidence interval: a range that would contain the true parameter in a specified fraction of repeated experiments.

For the sample mean with large $n$, the CLT says $\bar{X} \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. A 95% confidence interval for $\mu$ is then:

$$\bar{x} \;\pm\; 1.96 \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$
$1.96$ — the 97.5th percentile of $N(0,1)$ $s/\sqrt{n}$ — standard error

When $\sigma$ is unknown and estimated by $s$, for small $n$ we use the Student's $t$ distribution instead of Normal. The $t$ distribution is slightly wider (heavier tails) to account for the extra uncertainty in estimating $\sigma$.

⚠ Interpreting a CI correctly

A 95% CI does not mean "there is a 95% probability the true mean is in this range". That is the Bayesian credible interval from Lesson 2.1. A frequentist CI means "if I repeated the experiment many times and computed a CI each time, 95% of the CIs would contain the true mean". For any specific CI you computed, it either contains the true mean or it does not — you don't know which.

MAP estimation: MLE with a prior

MLE uses only the likelihood. MAP — Maximum A Posteriori — combines the likelihood with a prior belief (exactly the Bayesian update from Lesson 2.1) and returns the mode of the posterior:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} \;=\; \arg\max_\theta P(\text{data} \mid \theta)\,P(\theta) \;=\; \arg\max_\theta \big[\ell(\theta) + \log P(\theta)\big]$$

The $\log P(\theta)$ term is a regularizer. It pulls the estimate toward the prior mean, trading off fit against prior knowledge. When $n$ is small, the prior dominates; when $n$ is large, the likelihood dominates and MAP converges to MLE.

Worked Example: Fitting a Normal to Singapore GDP growth

📝 Goal

Load Singapore quarterly GDP growth rates from economic_indicators.csv, compute the MLE of $(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for a Normal model, and construct a 95% confidence interval for the mean.

Step 1 — Load. 
import polars as pl
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
df = loader.load("mlfp02", "economic_indicators.csv")
growth = df["gdp_growth_pct"].drop_nulls().to_numpy()
n = len(growth)
print(f"n = {n}")
Step 2 — Closed-form MLE.

For a Normal, the MLE is the sample mean and (biased) sample variance: 

mu_hat    = growth.mean()
sigma2_ml = growth.var(ddof=0)   # divide by n (MLE)
sigma2_s  = growth.var(ddof=1)   # divide by n-1 (unbiased)
print(f"MLE variance        = {sigma2_ml:.4f}")
print(f"Unbiased variance   = {sigma2_s:.4f}")
print(f"Ratio (n/(n-1))     = {sigma2_s/sigma2_ml:.4f}")

The ratio should be exactly $n/(n-1)$ — Bessel's correction in numbers.

Step 3 — Numerical MLE with scipy.

For models without a closed-form solution, you optimise the log-likelihood. We do it here for the Normal just to see the machinery. 

from scipy.optimize import minimize

def neg_log_lik(params, x):
    mu, log_sigma = params
    sigma = np.exp(log_sigma)           # keep sigma > 0
    return -np.sum(stats.norm.logpdf(x, loc=mu, scale=sigma))

result = minimize(
    neg_log_lik,
    x0=[0.0, 0.0],
    args=(growth,),
    method="BFGS",
)
mu_fit, log_sigma_fit = result.x
print(f"Numerical MLE: mu = {mu_fit:.4f}, sigma = {np.exp(log_sigma_fit):.4f}")

The numerical fit should match $\bar{x}$ and the biased sample std to within floating-point precision.

Step 4 — 95% CI via CLT. 
s  = float(growth.std(ddof=1))
se = s / np.sqrt(n)
t_crit = stats.t.ppf(0.975, df=n - 1)
lo, hi = mu_hat - t_crit * se, mu_hat + t_crit * se
print(f"mu_hat = {mu_hat:.3f} ± {t_crit * se:.3f}")
print(f"95% CI  = [{lo:.3f}, {hi:.3f}]")
