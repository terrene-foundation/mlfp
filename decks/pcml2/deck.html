Opening Case: The Feature That Killed a Clinical Trial

Foundations
2019 — A major hospital built a readmission-prediction model. It achieved 0.97 AUC on the test set. Leadership approved a clinical trial. Three months later, the trial was halted. The model was useless in production.

What went wrong?

One of the input features was "discharge medication list" — information that is only available after the outcome (discharge) has already happened.

The model was predicting the past, not the future.

What Is an Experiment?

Foundations
An experiment is a structured test where you change one thing and measure the effect.

Everyday Example

You suspect that adding a balcony photo increases HDB listing clicks.

  • Control group: listings without balcony photo
  • Treatment group: listings with balcony photo
  • Metric: click-through rate
  • Question: Is the difference real or just luck?

Key Ingredients

  1. A clear hypothesis
  2. A control (no change)
  3. A treatment (the change)
  4. Random assignment
  5. A metric to measure

Correlation vs. Causation

Foundations

Correlation

Two things move together.

"When ice cream sales go up, drowning incidents go up."

Does ice cream cause drowning? No. Both are caused by hot weather.

Causation

One thing produces the other.

"Taking this medicine reduces blood pressure."

We know this because of randomised controlled trials.

ML models learn correlations. They cannot, by themselves, tell you what causes what. That is why causal inference (Lesson 2.6) matters.

What Is Probability?

Foundations
Probability measures how likely something is to happen, on a scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

Frequentist View

"If I flip this coin 1,000 times, about 500 will be heads."

Probability = long-run frequency.

Bayesian View

"Based on what I know, I believe there is a 70% chance this flat sells above $500K."

Probability = degree of belief, updated with evidence.

Both views are useful. Module 2 teaches you both.

Distributions — The Shape of Uncertainty

Foundations

A distribution describes all the possible values something can take and how likely each value is.

Normal (Bell Curve)

Most values cluster around the average. Heights, test scores, measurement errors.

$$\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$$
$\mu$ = centre (mean), $\sigma$ = spread (std dev)

Beta Distribution

Values between 0 and 1. Perfect for modelling proportions, conversion rates, probabilities.

$$\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$
$\alpha$ = "pseudo-successes", $\beta$ = "pseudo-failures"

Bayes' Theorem — Updating Beliefs

Foundations
Analogy: You think there is a 30% chance of rain today. Then you see dark clouds. You update your belief to 70%. Bayes' theorem is the formula for that update.
$$\underbrace{P(\theta | \text{data})}_{\color{#4F46E5}{\text{posterior}}} = \frac{\overbrace{P(\text{data} | \theta)}^{\color{#0D9488}{\text{likelihood}}} \cdot \overbrace{P(\theta)}^{\color{#D97706}{\text{prior}}}}{\underbrace{P(\text{data})}_{\color{#6B7280}{\text{evidence}}}}$$
Posterior = what you believe after seeing data  |  Likelihood = how well data fits your theory  |  Prior = what you believed before

2.1 Conjugate Priors — The Shortcut

Theory

When the prior and likelihood belong to the same "family", the posterior has a closed-form solution — no numerical integration needed.

Beta-Binomial

Prior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)$

Data: $k$ successes in $n$ trials

$$\text{Posterior} = \text{Beta}(\alpha_0 + k,\; \beta_0 + n - k)$$

Use case: conversion rates, click-through rates

Normal-Normal

Prior: $\mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$, known data variance $\sigma^2$

$$\mu_{\text{post}} = \frac{\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2}}{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}}$$

Use case: HDB price estimation with prior knowledge

Bayesian Update — Step by Step

Theory

Scenario: What is the true conversion rate of a new HDB listing page?

1Prior: Based on similar pages, we believe $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(2, 8)$ — mean 20%, wide uncertainty.
2Data: After 100 visitors, 35 clicked (converted). $k=35$, $n=100$.
3Posterior: $\text{Beta}(2+35,\; 8+65) = \text{Beta}(37, 73)$ — mean ≈ 33.6%.
495% credible interval: [25.0%, 42.8%] — we are 95% sure the true rate is in this range.
Key insight: The posterior is a compromise between your prior belief (20%) and the data (35%). With more data, the posterior shifts toward the data and the interval narrows.

Prior Sensitivity

Theory

How much does the prior matter? It depends on how much data you have.

Priorn=10, k=4n=100, k=40n=1000, k=400
Beta(1,1) — flatMean: 41.7%Mean: 40.2%Mean: 40.0%
Beta(2,8) — skepticalMean: 30.0%Mean: 38.2%Mean: 39.8%
Beta(20,80) — strongMean: 21.8%Mean: 30.0%Mean: 38.2%
Advanced: As $n \to \infty$, all priors converge to the same posterior. This is Bernstein-von Mises theorem — the Bayesian CLT. In practice, with $n > 100$, moderate priors barely matter.

2.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Theory
Plain English: Find the parameter values that make the observed data most probable.
$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_\theta \; \underbrace{\prod_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i | \theta)}_{\text{likelihood}} = \arg\max_\theta \; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \ln p(x_i | \theta)}_{\text{log-likelihood}}$$
Product → sum via logarithm (numerically stable, same optimum)

We take the log because products of small numbers underflow. The log turns products into sums, which are easier to differentiate and optimise.

MLE for the Normal Distribution — Derivation

Theory

Given data $x_1, \ldots, x_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

1Log-likelihood: $\ell(\mu, \sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\ln(\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2$
2Differentiate w.r.t. $\mu$: $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu) = 0$
3Solve:
$$\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = \bar{x}$$
The MLE for the mean is simply the sample mean — intuitive and optimal
4Variance: $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$   (biased — divides by $n$, not $n-1$)

Fisher Information & Cramér-Rao Bound

Theory

How good can an estimator be? Fisher information sets a hard floor on variance.

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -E\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \ln p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta^2}\right]$$
Fisher information = expected curvature of the log-likelihood
$$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{n \cdot \mathcal{I}(\theta)}$$
Cramér-Rao bound: no unbiased estimator can have variance lower than $\frac{1}{n \cdot \mathcal{I}(\theta)}$
Intuition: Sharp curvature = lots of information = lower variance. If the log-likelihood is flat, the data does not help you pin down $\theta$.

MAP Estimation — MLE with a Prior

Theory
Plain English: MAP finds the most probable parameter value after combining data with prior knowledge.
$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \; \underbrace{\ln p(\text{data}|\theta)}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{log-likelihood}}} + \underbrace{\ln p(\theta)}_{\color{#D97706}{\text{log-prior}}}$$
MAP = MLE + regularisation from the prior

When to Use MLE

  • Lots of data ($n > 100$)
  • No strong prior knowledge
  • Simpler to implement

When to Use MAP

  • Small datasets
  • Domain knowledge available
  • Regularisation needed
  • Preview: L2 regularisation = Gaussian prior (Module 3)

2.3 Hypothesis Testing — The Framework

Theory

Neyman-Pearson Framework

  • $H_0$: Null hypothesis (no effect)
  • $H_1$: Alternative (there is an effect)
  • $\alpha$: Type I error rate (false positive)
  • $\beta$: Type II error rate (false negative)
  • Power = $1 - \beta$: probability of detecting a real effect

Decision Table

$H_0$ true$H_0$ false
Reject $H_0$Type I ($\alpha$)Correct!
Fail to rejectCorrect!Type II ($\beta$)

P-Value — What It Really Means

Theory
$$p\text{-value} = P(\text{data as extreme or more} \mid H_0 \text{ is true})$$
The p-value is NOT:
  • The probability that $H_0$ is true
  • The probability that the result is due to chance
  • A measure of effect size
Correct interpretation: "If there were truly no effect, how surprising would our observed data be?" A small p-value means very surprising.

Power Analysis — Planning Your Experiment

Theory

Before running an experiment, you need to know: how many observations do I need?

$$n = \frac{2\left(\underbrace{z_{\alpha/2}}_{\color{#DC2626}{\text{significance}}} + \underbrace{z_{\beta}}_{\color{#2563EB}{\text{power}}}\right)^2 \cdot \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\color{#D97706}{\text{variance}}}}{\underbrace{\delta^2}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{MDE}^2}}}$$
$z_{\alpha/2}$ = 1.96 for 95% confidence  |  $z_\beta$ = 0.84 for 80% power  |  $\sigma^2$ = metric variance  |  $\delta$ = minimum detectable effect
Singapore example: Testing whether a new MRT station increases nearby HDB prices by $\delta = \text{S\$10,000}$, with $\sigma = \text{S\$50,000}$, at $\alpha=0.05$ and power $= 0.80$: you need $n = 2 \times (1.96+0.84)^2 \times 50000^2 / 10000^2 \approx 393$ flats per group.

Multiple Testing — The Bonferroni Trap

Theory

Testing 20 features at $\alpha = 0.05$? You expect 1 false positive by pure chance.

Bonferroni Correction

$$\alpha_{\text{adj}} = \frac{\alpha}{m}$$

Controls FWER (family-wise error rate). Conservative — many real effects missed.

Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR)

$$\text{Reject } H_{(i)} \text{ if } p_{(i)} \leq \frac{i}{m} \cdot q$$

Controls false discovery rate. Less conservative — preferred in practice.

Advanced — Permutation Tests: When distributional assumptions fail, permutation tests provide exact p-values by shuffling labels and recomputing the test statistic. Distribution-free but computationally expensive. Wild bootstrap handles heteroscedastic data where standard permutation tests break.

2.4 Bootstrap — Efron's Insight

Theory
Plain English: Cannot collect more data? Pretend you can by resampling with replacement from what you have. Each resample gives you a different estimate. The spread of those estimates tells you how uncertain you are.
1You have $n$ observations: $\{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}$
2Draw $B$ bootstrap samples, each of size $n$, with replacement
3Compute your statistic $\hat{\theta}^*_b$ on each sample
4The distribution of $\{\hat{\theta}^*_1, \ldots, \hat{\theta}^*_B\}$ approximates the sampling distribution
$$\text{SE}_{\text{boot}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{B-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\left(\hat{\theta}^*_b - \bar{\hat{\theta}}^*\right)^2}$$

BCa Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

Theory

The simple percentile interval (2.5th and 97.5th percentile) is biased. The BCa (bias-corrected and accelerated) method fixes two problems:

Bias Correction ($\hat{z}_0$)

$$\hat{z}_0 = \Phi^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\#\{\hat{\theta}^*_b < \hat{\theta}\}}{B}\right)$$

Measures how far the bootstrap distribution centre is from the original estimate.

Acceleration ($\hat{a}$)

$$\hat{a} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{\theta}_{(\cdot)} - \hat{\theta}_{(i)})^3}{6\left[\sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{\theta}_{(\cdot)} - \hat{\theta}_{(i)})^2\right]^{3/2}}$$

Corrects for skewness using jackknife influence values.

Adjusted quantiles: $\alpha_1 = \Phi\!\left(\hat{z}_0 + \frac{\hat{z}_0 + z_{\alpha/2}}{1 - \hat{a}(\hat{z}_0 + z_{\alpha/2})}\right)$ — this shifts the interval to correct for bias and skew.

When Bootstrap Fails

Theory
SituationWhy It FailsAlternative
Extrema (min, max)Resampling cannot exceed observed rangeParametric bootstrap
Heavy-tailed dataVariance does not exist (Cauchy)Subsampling
Dependent data (time series)Resampling breaks temporal structureBlock bootstrap
Very small $n$ ($< 10$)Not enough variation to resampleBayesian methods
Advanced — Wild Bootstrap: For heteroscedastic data where standard errors vary across observations, the wild bootstrap multiplies residuals by random Rademacher variables ($\pm 1$). This preserves the heteroscedastic structure while still providing valid inference. Wu (1986), Liu (1988).

2.5 CUPED — The Variance Reduction Technique

Theory
Plain English: CUPED uses pre-experiment data to reduce noise in your A/B test. Less noise means you can detect smaller effects with fewer observations. This is the single most impactful technique in modern A/B testing.

CUPED = Controlled-experiment Using Pre-Experiment Data (Deng et al., Microsoft, 2013)

Core Idea

If you know each user's behaviour before the experiment, you can subtract out the predictable part, leaving only the treatment effect + residual noise.

$$\underbrace{Y_{\text{adj}}}_{\color{#4F46E5}{\text{adjusted metric}}} = Y - \underbrace{\theta}_{\color{#D97706}{\text{coefficient}}} \cdot \left(\underbrace{X}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{pre-experiment covariate}}} - \underbrace{\bar{X}}_{\color{#6B7280}{\text{mean of covariate}}}\right)$$
$Y_{\text{adj}}$ = what we analyse instead of $Y$  |  $\theta$ = regression coefficient  |  $X$ = pre-experiment behaviour

CUPED Variance Reduction — Full Derivation

Theory

Goal: Show that $\text{Var}(Y_{\text{adj}}) < \text{Var}(Y)$.

1Start: $Y_{\text{adj}} = Y - \theta(X - \bar{X})$
2Variance: $\text{Var}(Y_{\text{adj}}) = \text{Var}(Y) - 2\theta\text{Cov}(Y,X) + \theta^2\text{Var}(X)$
3Minimise over $\theta$: $\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} = -2\text{Cov}(Y,X) + 2\theta\text{Var}(X) = 0$
$$\theta^* = \frac{\text{Cov}(Y, X)}{\text{Var}(X)}$$
The optimal $\theta$ is the OLS regression coefficient of $Y$ on $X$
4Substitute back:
$$\boxed{\text{Var}(Y_{\text{adj}}) = \text{Var}(Y)\left(1 - \rho_{XY}^2\right)}$$
$\rho_{XY}^2$ = squared correlation between pre- and post-experiment metric.
If $\rho = 0.7$, variance drops by 51%. If $\rho = 0.9$, variance drops by 81%.

CUPED — Practical Impact

Theory

Variance reduction translates directly to needing fewer observations or detecting smaller effects.

Pre-post correlation $\rho$Variance reductionEquivalent sample multiplier

Singapore example: Testing a new HDB listing recommendation algorithm. Without CUPED: need 50,000 users. With CUPED ($\rho = 0.8$): need only 14,000 users. You run the experiment 3.6x faster.

2.6 Causal Inference — Beyond Correlation

Theory
Plain English: ML models predict. Causal inference tells you what would happen if you intervened. "Will this policy actually change outcomes, or just correlate with them?"

Rubin's Potential Outcomes Framework

Every individual $i$ has two potential outcomes:

$$Y_i(1) = \text{outcome if treated}$$
$$Y_i(0) = \text{outcome if not treated}$$
The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference: For each individual, you can only observe one of the two potential outcomes. The other is the counterfactual — it never happened.

Treatment Effects — ATE, ATT, CATE

Theory
$$\underbrace{\text{ATE}}_{\color{#4F46E5}{\text{Average Treatment Effect}}} = E[Y(1) - Y(0)]$$
Average effect across the entire population
$$\underbrace{\text{ATT}}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{Avg. Treatment Effect on Treated}}} = E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid T = 1]$$
Average effect only among those who actually received treatment
$$\underbrace{\text{CATE}}_{\color{#8B5CF6}{\text{Conditional ATE}}} = E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid X = x]$$
Treatment effect for a specific subgroup defined by covariates $X$
Singapore policy example: ATE of cooling measures on all HDB prices. ATT on flats that were actually affected by the policy. CATE for 3-room vs 5-room flats (heterogeneous effects).

Pearl's DAGs — Graphical Causal Models

Theory

Judea Pearl's Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) encode causal assumptions visually.

Z ↙ ↘ T Y         →

Z Confounder — causes both treatment and outcome. Must be adjusted for.

T Treatment — the intervention.

Y Outcome — what we measure.

d-Separation

Two variables are d-separated if every path between them is "blocked". Conditioning on a confounder blocks the backdoor path. Conditioning on a collider opens a spurious path.

Backdoor & Front-Door Criteria

Theory

Backdoor Criterion

A set $Z$ satisfies the backdoor criterion for $(T, Y)$ if:

  1. $Z$ blocks all backdoor paths from $T$ to $Y$
  2. $Z$ does not contain any descendant of $T$
$$P(Y | do(T)) = \sum_z P(Y|T,z) \cdot P(z)$$

Adjust for confounders → identify causal effect.

Front-Door Criterion

When confounders are unobserved, but a mediator $M$ is observed:

$T \to M \to Y$ with unobserved $U \to T, U \to Y$

$$P(Y|do(T)) = \sum_m P(m|T) \sum_t P(Y|m,t)P(t)$$

Identify causal effect without observing the confounder.

Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Theory
Plain English: Compare the change in a treated group to the change in an untreated group over the same period. If both groups were trending similarly before, the difference in their changes is the causal effect.
Before ($t=0$)After ($t=1$)Change
Treatment$\bar{Y}_{T,0}$$\bar{Y}_{T,1}$$\Delta_T$
Control$\bar{Y}_{C,0}$$\bar{Y}_{C,1}$$\Delta_C$
$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{DiD}} = \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{T,1} - \bar{Y}_{T,0})}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{treatment change}}} - \underbrace{(\bar{Y}_{C,1} - \bar{Y}_{C,0})}_{\color{#2563EB}{\text{control change}}}$$
The ATT estimator: treatment change minus counterfactual change

DiD — ATT Estimator Derivation

Theory

Key assumption: parallel trends. Without treatment, both groups would have changed by the same amount.

1Observed: $\bar{Y}_{T,1}$ (treated, after) and $\bar{Y}_{C,1}$ (control, after)
2Counterfactual: $E[Y_T(0) | t=1] \approx \bar{Y}_{T,0} + (\bar{Y}_{C,1} - \bar{Y}_{C,0})$
3ATT:
$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{ATT}} = \bar{Y}_{T,1} - \underbrace{\left[\bar{Y}_{T,0} + (\bar{Y}_{C,1} - \bar{Y}_{C,0})\right]}_{\color{#D97706}{\text{counterfactual for treated}}}$$
4Regression form: $Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta_1 D_i + \beta_2 T_t + \underbrace{\delta}_{\color{#DC2626}{\text{DiD estimate}}} (D_i \times T_t) + \epsilon_{it}$

where $D_i = 1$ if treated, $T_t = 1$ if post-treatment, and $\delta = \hat{\tau}_{\text{DiD}}$.

DiD — Parallel Trends & Placebo Tests

Theory
Parallel trends is the critical assumption. If treatment and control groups were already diverging before the intervention, DiD gives a biased estimate.

How to Validate

Visual Inspection

Plot both groups over time. Before the intervention, trends should be parallel (not identical — just moving in the same direction at the same rate).

Placebo Tests

  1. Fake treatment time: Apply DiD at $t-1$ — should find no effect
  2. Fake treatment group: Apply treatment to an unaffected group — should find no effect
  3. Pre-trend test: Regress outcome on time $\times$ group before treatment — coefficient should be zero

DiD — Singapore Cooling Measures Example

Theory
Case study: In December 2021, Singapore introduced additional buyer's stamp duty (ABSD) increases. Did this reduce HDB resale prices in affected segments?

Design

  • Treatment: Segments most affected by ABSD (investment purchases)
  • Control: First-time buyer segment (minimal ABSD impact)
  • Before: Jan-Nov 2021
  • After: Jan-Jun 2022

Validation

  • Pre-trend: both groups rising at similar rates
  • Placebo: no effect at fake date (July 2021)
  • $\hat{\tau}_{\text{DiD}} = -\text{S\$}18,200$ (significant at $p < 0.01$)
  • Cooling measures reduced investment-segment prices

Double ML / Debiased ML

Advanced
Chernozhukov et al. (2018): Use ML models to estimate nuisance parameters (confounders), then use the residuals for causal estimation. The "double" comes from two ML models.
1Model 1: Predict outcome $Y$ from confounders $X$ → residual $\tilde{Y} = Y - \hat{m}(X)$
2Model 2: Predict treatment $T$ from confounders $X$ → residual $\tilde{T} = T - \hat{e}(X)$
3Causal estimate: Regress $\tilde{Y}$ on $\tilde{T}$ → coefficient is the ATE
$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{DML}} = \frac{\sum_i \tilde{T}_i \cdot \tilde{Y}_i}{\sum_i \tilde{T}_i^2}$$
Partialling out confounders with ML, then OLS on residuals

Key requirement: use cross-fitting (sample splitting) to avoid overfitting bias.

Heterogeneous Treatment Effects — Causal Forests

Advanced

CATE tells you who benefits most from a treatment. Causal forests (Athey & Imbens, 2018) estimate $\tau(x) = E[Y(1) - Y(0) | X = x]$ non-parametrically.

How It Works

  1. Build a forest of "causal trees"
  2. Each split maximises treatment effect heterogeneity
  3. Honesty: use separate samples for splitting and estimation
  4. Predictions are local ATEs for each leaf

Singapore Application

Estimate CATE of MRT station openings on HDB prices:

  • Mature estates: moderate effect (+S$15K)
  • Non-mature estates: strong effect (+S$42K)
  • Near existing MRT: minimal effect
  • Policy insight: target transit investment where it matters most

Bayesian A/B Testing

Advanced

Instead of p-values, compute the posterior probability that B beats A.

$$P(\theta_B > \theta_A | \text{data}) = \int\!\!\int_{\theta_B > \theta_A} p(\theta_A | \text{data}_A) \cdot p(\theta_B | \text{data}_B) \, d\theta_A \, d\theta_B$$
Direct probability answer: "There is an 94% chance B is better than A"

Advantages

  • Intuitive: direct probability, not "reject/fail-to-reject"
  • No fixed sample size required
  • Natural early stopping (credible intervals)
  • Handles optional stopping without inflation

Computation

For Beta-Binomial (conversion rates): Monte Carlo sampling from both posteriors, count fraction where $\theta_B > \theta_A$.

For Normal metrics: closed-form using the difference distribution.

Interference & Network Effects

Advanced
SUTVA violation: The Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption assumes one user's treatment does not affect another user's outcome. This breaks in social networks, marketplaces, and shared resources.

Examples of Interference

  • Ride-hailing: offering discounts to some riders affects wait times for all
  • Social media: a user's new feature affects their friends' behaviour
  • HDB listings: promoting some listings reduces clicks on nearby ones

Solutions

  • Cluster randomisation: randomise at neighbourhood/region level
  • Switchback experiments: alternate treatment on/off over time
  • Ego-cluster randomisation: randomise users and their network neighbours together

2.7 Feature Selection — Mutual Information

Theory
Plain English: How much does knowing one variable tell you about another? Mutual information measures this in bits — it captures any relationship, not just linear ones.
$$I(X; Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x) \cdot p(y)}$$
If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $p(x,y) = p(x)p(y)$ and $I(X;Y) = 0$.
Higher MI = more information shared = more useful feature.
Why not just use correlation? Correlation only captures linear relationships. A feature with zero correlation can have high mutual information (e.g., $Y = X^2$).

Feature Selection — Boruta Algorithm

Theory

Boruta answers: "Is this feature more important than random noise?"

1Create "shadow features" — random permutations of each original feature
2Train a Random Forest on original + shadow features together
3Record feature importance for all features (original and shadow)
4Compare: if an original feature's importance consistently exceeds the maximum shadow importance, it is confirmed
5Repeat $T$ times. Use binomial test to decide: confirmed, rejected, or tentative
Key insight: By comparing against the best random noise, Boruta is more conservative than simple importance ranking. It finds all relevant features, not just the top-$k$.

Stability Selection

Theory

Run feature selection on many random subsamples. Features that are consistently selected are truly important.

$$\hat{\Pi}_j = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \mathbb{1}[\text{feature } j \text{ selected in subsample } b]$$
Selection probability $\hat{\Pi}_j$: how often feature $j$ is chosen across $B$ subsamples.
Threshold: keep features with $\hat{\Pi}_j > \pi_{\text{thr}}$ (typically 0.6-0.9).
Meinshausen & Bühlmann (2010): Stability selection provides finite-sample error control: $E[V] \leq \frac{q^2}{(2\pi_{\text{thr}} - 1) \cdot p}$ where $V$ = false positives, $q$ = expected selected features, $p$ = total features.

Temporal Features — Lag, Rolling, Fourier

Theory

Lag Features

$x_{t-k}$ = value $k$ periods ago

    pl.col("price").shift(3).alias("price_lag3"),

Rolling Features

$\bar{x}_{t,w}$ = mean over window $w$

df.with_columns(
    pl.col("price")
      .rolling_mean(window_size=6)

Fourier Features (Seasonal Decomposition)

$$f_k(t) = \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi k t}{P}\right), \quad g_k(t) = \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi k t}{P}\right)$$
$P$ = period (12 for monthly, 7 for weekly), $k$ = harmonic number. Captures cyclical patterns without explicit seasonal dummies.

Target Encoding — James-Stein Shrinkage

Theory
Problem: You have a categorical feature with many levels (e.g., 1,000 postcodes). One-hot encoding creates 1,000 columns. Target encoding replaces each category with the mean target value for that category — but naive encoding overfits.
$$\hat{\mu}_j^{\text{JS}} = \underbrace{\lambda_j}_{\color{#D97706}{\text{shrinkage}}} \cdot \underbrace{\bar{y}_j}_{\color{#0D9488}{\text{category mean}}} + (1 - \lambda_j) \cdot \underbrace{\bar{y}}_{\color{#4F46E5}{\text{global mean}}}$$
$\lambda_j$ = $\frac{n_j}{n_j + \frac{\sigma^2_{\text{within}}}{\sigma^2_{\text{between}}}}$ — shrinks small categories toward the global mean
Intuition: A postcode with 3 observations and mean price S$800K is unreliable. Shrink it toward the global mean (S$520K). A postcode with 3,000 observations keeps its own mean. This is exactly the Bayesian posterior mean under a Normal-Normal model.

Feature Engineering — Interaction & Polynomial Features

Theory

Interaction Features

Capture combined effects of two features.

# Floor area matters MORE in central locations
df.with_columns(
    (pl.col("floor_area_sqm")
     * pl.col("is_central"))
    .alias("area_x_central")
)

This lets the model learn that floor area has a different effect depending on location.

Polynomial Features

Capture nonlinear relationships.

# Diminishing returns on floor area
df.with_columns(
    (pl.col("floor_area_sqm") ** 2)
    .alias("area_squared"),
    (pl.col("floor_area_sqm").log())
    .alias("area_log"),
)

Be careful: $p$ features with degree $d$ creates $\binom{p+d}{d}$ features. Feature explosion is real.

A point-in-time join retrieves the feature value that was most recent as of the event timestamp — never future values.

Data Lineage & Reproducibility

Theory
Data lineage = a complete record of where each feature came from, how it was computed, and what version is being used. Without lineage, you cannot debug a model or reproduce results.

What Lineage Tracks

  • Source data and timestamp
  • Transformation code (hash)
  • Feature version
  • Schema definition
  • Downstream consumers (which models use this feature)

Why It Matters

  • Debugging: "Why did this prediction change?" → trace to feature version change
  • Compliance: Regulators require audit trails (MAS, EU AI Act)
  • Reproducibility: Re-train a model with exactly the same features

Key Takeaways — By Level

Everyone should remember:

  • Never use future information to predict the past (feature leakage)
