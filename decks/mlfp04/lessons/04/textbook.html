Lesson 4.4 Anomaly Detection and Ensembles

Clustering asked which group a point belongs to. Anomaly detection asks the opposite question: which points do not belong to any group? Today you will learn four methods — statistical (Z-score, IQR), Isolation Forest, and Local Outlier Factor — and the ensemble pattern for blending their scores into a single trustworthy signal. By the end you will be able to detect fraudulent financial transactions without any labelled fraud examples.

Why This Matters

Fraud, defective products, faulty sensors, network intrusions, churn precursors — every business has rare but critical events that matter more than the common ones. You usually do not have enough labelled examples to train a supervised classifier (fraud may be 0.1% of transactions). Anomaly detection finds the strange points without labels, by modelling what "normal" looks like and flagging everything that deviates.

🟢 Foundations

Anomaly detection is an unsupervised problem with a supervised framing: you are implicitly classifying points as "normal" or "anomalous". But you do not have labels — you have to infer normality from the shape of the data itself.

Core Concepts

Statistical outliers: Z-score and IQR

The simplest anomaly detectors look at one feature at a time. The Z-score method standardises and thresholds:

$$ z_i \;=\; \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{s} \qquad\text{flag as outlier if } |z_i| > 3 $$

The "3-sigma rule" catches the ~0.27% of points that would be in the tails of a normal distribution. The trade-off is obvious: the method assumes the data is normally distributed, which is rarely true for business quantities (revenue, session duration, basket size are right-skewed), and a few extreme values inflate both $\bar{x}$ and $s$, hiding everything else.

The IQR method is more robust:

$$ \text{outlier if } x < Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR}\quad\text{or}\quad x > Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR} $$

where $\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$ is the interquartile range. Because quartiles are not affected by extreme values, IQR is the standard univariate outlier rule for skewed data. It is also the method behind box plot whiskers.

Winsorisation

Winsorising is a related technique — instead of removing outliers, cap them at a percentile (typically 1st and 99th). This preserves sample size and makes downstream statistics robust without losing information about the fact that a point was extreme. In production, winsorise before you cluster or regress, not instead of investigating outliers.

Isolation Forest

Statistical methods only look at one feature at a time. Real anomalies are usually strange combinations of normal values — a small transaction is normal, a midnight transaction is normal, but a small midnight transaction from a new device is suspicious. You need a multivariate method.

Isolation Forest is a beautifully simple idea: anomalies are points that can be "isolated" with fewer random splits. Build a random tree by picking a feature and a split value uniformly at random. Count how many splits are needed to isolate each point into its own leaf. Normal points require many splits (they are in the dense core); anomalies require few (they are out on their own).

Formally, the anomaly score is

$$ s(\mathbf{x}, n) \;=\; 2^{-E(h(\mathbf{x}))/c(n)} $$

where $E(h(\mathbf{x}))$ is the average path length from root to the leaf containing $\mathbf{x}$ across all trees, $n$ is the sample size, and $c(n)$ is the expected path length of a random binary tree with $n$ leaves. $s$ is near 1 for strong anomalies, near 0.5 for normal points, and below 0.5 if the point looks "more typical than average". Typical threshold: $s > 0.6$ flags the point as anomalous.

LOF (Local Outlier Factor)

Isolation Forest is a global method — it does not care whether a point is in a dense region or a sparse one. LOF is local: it measures the density around a point and compares it to the density around its neighbours. A point in a region much sparser than its neighbours is flagged.

Formally, let $N_k(\mathbf{x})$ be the $k$ nearest neighbours of $\mathbf{x}$ and let $\text{lrd}_k(\mathbf{x})$ be the local reachability density (inverse of the average reachability distance to neighbours). Then

$$ \text{LOF}_k(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \frac{1}{|N_k(\mathbf{x})|}\sum_{\mathbf{o} \in N_k(\mathbf{x})}\ \frac{\text{lrd}_k(\mathbf{o})}{\text{lrd}_k(\mathbf{x})} $$

$\text{LOF} \approx 1$ means "same density as neighbours" (normal). $\text{LOF} > 1.5$ typically flags an anomaly. LOF is the right choice when your data has clusters of varying density — a point that is "close" by absolute distance can still be an outlier relative to its own neighbourhood.

Score blending and the EnsembleEngine

Each method catches different anomalies. Z-score catches tail events on individual features. Isolation Forest catches multivariate outliers. LOF catches local density anomalies. Running all three and blending the scores gives you a more robust detector than any single method.

The blending pattern is: (1) convert each method's output to a normalised score in $[0, 1]$; (2) average them (or take max, or weighted sum); (3) apply a single threshold. The EnsembleEngine in kailash-ml provides four modes — blend(), stack(), bag(), and boost() — for both classification and anomaly detection. 

from kailash_ml import EnsembleEngine

ensemble = EnsembleEngine()
scores = {
    "z_score": z_scores,
    "iforest": iforest.decision_function(X),
    "lof": -lof.negative_outlier_factor_,
}
blended = ensemble.blend(scores, normalise=True, agg="mean")
anomalies = blended > 0.7

Mathematical Foundations

Expected path length in a random binary tree

The normalisation constant $c(n)$ in Isolation Forest is the expected path length from root to leaf in a random binary search tree with $n$ elements — a classical result:

$$ c(n) \;=\; 2 H(n-1) - 2(n-1)/n \qquad\text{where } H(k) \approx \ln k + 0.5772 $$

This is what makes the anomaly score comparable across different sample sizes: without the $c(n)$ normalisation, a point's raw path length would scale with $\log n$ and the scores would not be interpretable.

Why unsupervised anomaly detection works at all

Every method here rests on a single assumption: anomalies are rare and different. If anomalies make up more than ~10% of your data, or if they look indistinguishable from normal points until you know the label, unsupervised detection fails and you need a supervised classifier (or semi-supervised methods with a small labelled set). Always check: is the assumption justified for your problem?

Worked Example: Detecting Fraud in Financial Transactions

import numpy as np
import polars as pl
from sklearn.ensemble import IsolationForest
from sklearn.neighbors import LocalOutlierFactor
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
tx = loader.load("mlfp04", "financial_transactions.parquet")

features = ["amount", "hour", "recipient_count_24h", "location_distance_km"]
X = StandardScaler().fit_transform(
    tx.drop_nulls(subset=features).select(features).to_numpy()
)

# Method 1: univariate Z-score on amount
z = np.abs((X[:, 0] - X[:, 0].mean()) / X[:, 0].std())
z_flag = z > 3

# Method 2: Isolation Forest
iforest = IsolationForest(contamination=0.01, random_state=42).fit(X)
iforest_scores = -iforest.decision_function(X)  # higher = more anomalous

# Method 3: LOF
lof = LocalOutlierFactor(n_neighbors=20, contamination=0.01)
lof.fit_predict(X)
lof_scores = -lof.negative_outlier_factor_

# Blend the three into a single score
def normalise(x):
    return (x - x.min()) / (x.max() - x.min() + 1e-9)

blended = (normalise(z.astype(float)) + normalise(iforest_scores) + normalise(lof_scores)) / 3
anomalies = blended > 0.7

print(f"Flagged {anomalies.sum()} of {len(anomalies)} transactions "
      f"({anomalies.mean():.2%}) as anomalous")

The key pedagogical point: look at the overlap between the methods' flags. Transactions caught by all three are almost certainly anomalous. Transactions caught by only one method deserve human review — the single flag might be a real signal or a quirk of that specific detector. Blending gives you a confidence gradient you can act on.

Common Mistakes

  1. Setting contamination too high. contamination tells Isolation Forest and LOF "approximately what fraction of the data should I flag". Setting it to 0.1 when your real anomaly rate is 0.001 forces the method to label many normal points as anomalies. Tune carefully and be honest about the base rate.
  2. Using Z-score on skewed data. For revenue, session duration, basket size — anything right-skewed — use IQR or log-transform first.
  3. Not standardising before multivariate methods. Same rule as clustering: scale-sensitive methods need standardised inputs.
  4. Treating anomaly scores as probabilities. They are not — different methods use different ranges and scales. Always normalise to $[0, 1]$ before blending or thresholding.

Reflection

Anomaly detection is deceptively hard in practice. The algorithms are simple; the difficulty is the labelling. Because you do not have a ground truth, you cannot compute precision or recall — the only way to evaluate a detector is to investigate the top-$k$ flagged points and ask a domain expert "is this an anomaly?". The engineering lesson is that unsupervised methods are starting points, not finish lines. They tell you where to look, and a human decides what to do.
