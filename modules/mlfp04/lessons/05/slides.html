Association Rules and Market Basket Analysis

Apriori · FP-Growth · Support · Confidence · Lift

⏱ 60 minutes   ·   Outcome: discover retail patterns, turn them into features

The oldest unsupervised trick in the book

  • Customers don't buy one item — they buy baskets.
  • Goal: find items that appear together more often than chance.
  • Output: rules of the form "A ⇒ B" with three statistics.
🟢 Foundations: still powers "frequently bought together" widgets everywhere.

The pipeline

Three metrics

\[ \text{support}(X) = P(X) \] \[ \text{confidence}(X \to Y) = P(Y\,|\,X) \] \[ \text{lift}(X \to Y) = \frac{P(X \cap Y)}{P(X)\,P(Y)} \]

Support = how common. Confidence = conditional. Lift = vs independence.

Lift is the metric you report

  • Lift = 1 → $X$ and $Y$ independent.
  • Lift > 1 → positive association.
  • Lift < 1 → negative (one substitutes the other).
  • High confidence + low lift means the consequent is just common. Not a finding.
⚠ Rookie mistake: reporting confidence without lift.

Numerical example

  • $P(\text{bread}) = 0.20$, $P(\text{butter}) = 0.10$, $P(\text{both}) = 0.05$.
  • confidence(bread → butter) = $0.05 / 0.20 = 0.25$
  • lift(bread → butter) = $0.25 / 0.10 = 2.5$

25% of bread-buyers buy butter; this is 2.5× random chance → real association.

Apriori property

If an itemset is not frequent, no superset of it is frequent.
Equivalently: every subset of a frequent itemset is itself frequent.

This is what saves us from $2^d$ itemsets. Prune infrequent subsets early.

Apriori in pictures

FP-Growth — faster cousin

  • Builds a compressed FP-tree in a single pass.
  • Avoids candidate generation.
  • Typically 2–10× faster than Apriori on dense data.
  • Same output — same frequent itemsets.

Default in modern libraries. Use it unless you have a specific reason for Apriori.

Worked example: Singapore retail


from mlxtend.frequent_patterns import fpgrowth, association_rules

freq = fpgrowth(baskets_df, min_support=0.01, use_colnames=True)
rules = association_rules(freq, metric="lift", min_threshold=2.0)

top = rules.sort_values("lift", ascending=False).head(10)
print(top[["antecedents", "consequents", "confidence", "lift"]])

Interpreting a rule


{instant_noodles, canned_tuna} → {rice}
  support: 0.035   confidence: 0.72   lift: 3.4

Business reading: "Customers buying instant noodles and canned tuna are 3.4× more likely than average to also buy rice."

Action: three-item promo, shelf placement, recommendation widget.

Conviction — direction matters

\[ \text{conv}(X \to Y) \;=\; \frac{1 - \text{support}(Y)}{1 - \text{confidence}(X \to Y)} \]
  • Measures improvement over independence.
  • Asymmetric (unlike lift).
  • Preferred when the rule direction is causally meaningful.

Four rookie mistakes

  1. min_support too low → millions of noise rules.
  2. Reporting confidence instead of lift.
  3. Ignoring the reverse rule direction.
  4. Treating rules as causal claims.
