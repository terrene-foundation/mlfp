Lesson 5.1 Autoencoders

An autoencoder is a neural network trained to reconstruct its own input after passing it through a narrow bottleneck. That single idea — compress, then reconstruct — gives you image denoising, anomaly detection, dimensionality reduction, and (in its variational form) a fully functional image generator. In this lesson you implement four flavours in PyTorch: vanilla, denoising, variational (VAE), and convolutional.

Why This Matters

Every deep‑learning architecture you will meet later in this module learns a representation — a vector that captures what the input is about. Autoencoders are the cleanest way to see that learning happen because the training target is the input itself. If the decoder can recover the input from a 16‑dimensional latent vector, then those 16 numbers must carry the essential information. Everything else — content, texture, noise — has been thrown away.

Three Singapore scenarios that autoencoders solve directly:

  • Credit card anomaly detection. Train on "normal" transactions. A fraudulent transaction reconstructs poorly — the reconstruction error itself becomes the alarm signal.
  • HDB listing photo denoising. Real estate photos taken in a Housing Board flat are often dim and noisy. A denoising autoencoder cleans them up without a human touching a slider.
  • Synthetic patient records for research. A hospital wants to share data with researchers but cannot expose real patients. A variational autoencoder trained on anonymised records can generate new, plausible records that preserve statistical structure.

Core Concepts

The bottleneck is the whole point

If the encoder could emit a vector as wide as the input, the network could learn the identity function: copy every pixel through, reconstruct perfectly, learn nothing. The bottleneck — a latent layer much smaller than the input — forces the encoder to discard information, and the decoder has to reconstruct from whatever survived. What survives is the essence of the data.

Formally, let $x \in \mathbb{R}^{D}$ be the input and $z \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ the latent with $d \ll D$. The encoder $f_\theta : \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}^d$ and decoder $g_\phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^D$ together minimise the reconstruction loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}}(\theta, \phi) \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \left\| x_i - g_\phi(f_\theta(x_i)) \right\|^2$$
$x_i$ — input sample $f_\theta$ — encoder $g_\phi$ — decoder $\|\cdot\|^2$ — squared L2 (MSE)

Both networks are trained jointly with backpropagation. No labels are required — the input is its own target — which makes the autoencoder unsupervised.

🟢 Foundations — a mental model

Think of the bottleneck as a one‑sentence summary of the input. The encoder is a student reading a news article and writing a one‑sentence summary; the decoder is another student who only sees the summary and tries to rewrite the original article. The quality of the summaries is whatever loss the decoder achieves. Train both students jointly and the summaries become genuinely informative.

Variational autoencoders — the generative leap

A vanilla autoencoder memorises a compressed version of each training example. It is good at reproducing seen data, but if you sample a random vector from the latent space, the decoder typically produces nonsense — the latent points "between" training examples are undefined.

The variational autoencoder fixes this by making the latent space a proper probability distribution. Instead of encoding $x$ to a single point $z$, the encoder emits a mean $\mu(x)$ and a log‑variance $\log \sigma^2(x)$. We then sample $z$ from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and decode. Two changes to the loss keep the latent space well‑behaved:

  1. A reconstruction term that rewards accurate decoding.
  2. A KL divergence term that pulls the encoded distribution toward a standard normal prior $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

The VAE ELBO

The full VAE objective is derived from variational inference. We want to maximise the data log‑likelihood $\log p(x)$, which is intractable, so we maximise a lower bound instead — the evidence lower bound or ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}}(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\!\bigl[\log p_\theta(x|z)\bigr] \;-\; \mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(q_\phi(z|x) \,\|\, p(z)\bigr)$$
$q_\phi(z|x)$ — encoder (approx. posterior) $p_\theta(x|z)$ — decoder (likelihood) $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ — prior

In training, the first term becomes MSE (up to a constant) when the likelihood is Gaussian with fixed variance. The second term, for a diagonal Gaussian encoder and standard‑normal prior, has a closed form:

$$\mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(0, I)\bigr) \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d}\left(1 + \log \sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2\right)$$

That is the exact line of code you write. No Monte Carlo, no tricks — just a sum over latent dimensions.

🔵 Theory — Why is maximising ELBO the right thing?

Because $\log p(x) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}} + \mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x)\bigr)$. The KL term on the right is non‑negative, so ELBO is a valid lower bound on the data log‑likelihood. Maximising ELBO simultaneously pulls the approximate posterior $q_\phi$ toward the true posterior $p(z|x)$ and fits the decoder. Derivation in any good graphical‑models text.

The reparameterisation trick

Sampling looks innocent — z = torch.randn(...) * sigma + mu — but it hides a problem: sampling is not differentiable with respect to its parameters. You cannot backpropagate through a random draw. The gradient of $\mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)}[\ell(z)]$ with respect to $\mu$ and $\sigma$ is not directly computable by autograd.

The reparameterisation trick pushes the randomness out of the computation graph:

$$z \;=\; \mu + \sigma \odot \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$
$\mu, \sigma$ — encoder outputs (differentiable) $\varepsilon$ — noise (no gradient needed) $\odot$ — elementwise product

Now $\varepsilon$ is a plain input to the network — it happens to be random, but autograd does not need to differentiate it. The gradient flows through $\mu$ and $\sigma$ naturally. This single trick is why the VAE exists as a trainable model.

Worked Example: Four autoencoders on synthetic images

📝 Goal

Build and train four autoencoders on a small synthetic image dataset (2000 tiny 16×16 Gaussian blobs). Compare reconstruction error. Sample new images from the VAE's latent space. You will recognise every line from M5 exercise 1.

Step 1 — imports and the dataset. 
import numpy as np
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader, TensorDataset
from kailash_ml import ModelVisualizer

torch.manual_seed(42)
device = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")

def make_blobs(n=2000, size=16):
    yy, xx = np.mgrid[0:size, 0:size].astype(np.float32)
    cx = np.random.uniform(4, size-4, n).astype(np.float32)[:, None, None]
    cy = np.random.uniform(4, size-4, n).astype(np.float32)[:, None, None]
    w  = np.random.uniform(1.5, 3.0, n).astype(np.float32)[:, None, None]
    return np.exp(-((xx - cx)**2 + (yy - cy)**2) / (2 * w**2))

X_np = make_blobs()
X = torch.from_numpy(X_np.reshape(len(X_np), -1)).to(device)  # (n, 256)
loader = DataLoader(TensorDataset(X), batch_size=64, shuffle=True)
Step 2 — a vanilla autoencoder. 
class VanillaAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_in=256, d_latent=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_in, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, d_latent),
        )
        self.decoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_latent, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, d_in), nn.Sigmoid(),
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        z = self.encoder(x)
        return self.decoder(z), z

The encoder maps 256 pixels down to an 8‑dimensional latent. The decoder mirrors the encoder shape. The final sigmoid keeps outputs in [0, 1] to match our blob pixels.

Step 3 — train it. 
ae = VanillaAE().to(device)
opt = torch.optim.Adam(ae.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
history = []
for epoch in range(20):
    running = 0.0
    for (batch,) in loader:
        recon, _ = ae(batch)
        loss = F.mse_loss(recon, batch)
        opt.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); opt.step()
        running += loss.item() * batch.size(0)
    history.append(running / len(loader.dataset))
print(f"Vanilla AE final MSE: {history[-1]:.5f}")
Step 4 — upgrade it to a denoising AE.

Denoising adds exactly one idea: corrupt the input before feeding it in, but keep the clean version as the target. 

class DenoisingAE(VanillaAE):
    def add_noise(self, x, sigma=0.3):
        return torch.clamp(x + sigma * torch.randn_like(x), 0.0, 1.0)

# In the training loop:
#   noisy = ae.add_noise(batch)
#   recon, _ = ae(noisy)
#   loss = F.mse_loss(recon, batch)   # target is the clean original

Forcing the encoder to produce a latent that decodes to the clean original is a powerful regulariser — it must throw away the noise and keep the signal.

Step 5 — the VAE with reparameterisation. 
class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_in=256, d_latent=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.enc = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(d_in, 64), nn.ReLU())
        self.fc_mu     = nn.Linear(64, d_latent)
        self.fc_logvar = nn.Linear(64, d_latent)
        self.decoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_latent, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, d_in), nn.Sigmoid(),
        )
    def encode(self, x):
        h = self.enc(x)
        return self.fc_mu(h), self.fc_logvar(h)
    def reparameterise(self, mu, logvar):
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)
        return mu + eps * std
    def forward(self, x):
        mu, logvar = self.encode(x)
        z = self.reparameterise(mu, logvar)
        return self.decoder(z), mu, logvar

The encoder now emits two vectors — $\mu$ and $\log \sigma^2$ — instead of a single latent. reparameterise implements $z = \mu + \sigma \odot \varepsilon$ exactly as in the annotated equation above.

Step 6 — the ELBO loss. 
def vae_loss(recon, x, mu, logvar):
    rec = F.mse_loss(recon, x, reduction="sum")
    kl  = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp())
    return rec + kl, rec, kl

Two terms — exactly the ELBO. The sum reduction is standard for VAE loss so the two terms are comparable per batch. The KL closed form is the one we derived above.

Step 7 — sample new images from the VAE. 
vae.eval()
with torch.no_grad():
    z = torch.randn(16, 8, device=device)       # draw from prior
    generated = vae.decoder(z).cpu().view(16, 16, 16)
print("Generated 16 new blobs — none of these are training images.")

This is the payoff: sampling $z$ from the standard normal prior and passing it through the decoder produces new images that resemble the training set but are not copies. The KL term in the loss made the latent space look like $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$, so random draws are in‑distribution.

Step 8 — the convolutional variant.

For image‑shaped inputs (rather than flat vectors), the encoder uses nn.Conv2d to downsample and the decoder uses nn.ConvTranspose2d to upsample: 

class ConvAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_latent=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv2d(1, 16, 3, stride=2, padding=1), nn.ReLU(),  # 16 → 8
            nn.Conv2d(16, 32, 3, stride=2, padding=1), nn.ReLU(), #  8 → 4
            nn.Flatten(),
            nn.Linear(32 * 4 * 4, d_latent),
        )
        self.decoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_latent, 32 * 4 * 4),
            nn.Unflatten(1, (32, 4, 4)),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(32, 16, 3, stride=2, padding=1, output_padding=1), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(16, 1, 3, stride=2, padding=1, output_padding=1), nn.Sigmoid(),
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        z = self.encoder(x)
        return self.decoder(z), z

Four architectures, one loss intuition (reconstruction error, possibly plus a KL regulariser), one optimiser. The same training loop runs all four with minor adjustments.

🟣 Advanced — Why not just do PCA?

A linear autoencoder with a single hidden layer and no non‑linearity converges to the same subspace as PCA. Non‑linear activations are what make autoencoders fundamentally more powerful — they learn curved manifolds, not just flat hyperplanes. The classical proof is in Baldi & Hornik (1989). Once you add ReLUs, the optimisation is non‑convex but the representational power is vastly greater.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Forgetting to train encoder and decoder together

An autoencoder is one network. You call loss.backward() once and update both encoder and decoder parameters in the same optimiser step. A common beginner error is building two separate optimisers — it works, but it is slower and never better.

⚠ Mistake 2: Treating log σ² as σ

The VAE encoder outputs log variance, not standard deviation — it makes the output unconstrained in sign. When you reparameterise, recover $\sigma$ with std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar). Mixing these up produces nan losses after a handful of epochs.

⚠ Mistake 3: Reconstruction loss that does not match the output activation

If the decoder ends in Sigmoid, pair it with F.binary_cross_entropy or MSE — never BCEWithLogitsLoss, which expects raw logits. If it ends in nothing, MSE is fine but BCE will misbehave. Match the loss to what the final layer actually emits.

⚠ Mistake 4: KL term swamps reconstruction (posterior collapse)

If the KL term is weighted too heavily, the encoder learns to ignore $x$ entirely and emits $\mu = 0, \sigma = 1$ for every input. Then sampling is fine but the decoder has nothing to condition on. Standard fix: $\beta$‑VAE with $\beta \in [0.1, 1.0]$, or a KL warm‑up schedule that starts at 0 and ramps to 1 over a few epochs.
