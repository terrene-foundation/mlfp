Lesson 1.6 Data Visualisation

A good chart answers a question faster than the best table in the world. A bad chart misleads its reader in seconds. Today you learn the six chart types that cover most of the questions a data analyst is asked, the Gestalt principles of visual perception that govern which shapes the eye groups together, the cardinal rules of truthful charts, and the ModelVisualizer engine that turns a Polars DataFrame into an interactive Plotly figure in one line.

Why This Matters

Every analysis has two audiences. The first is you — you are looking for patterns in the data. The second is the decision-maker you are writing the report for. For the first, a dense table works fine. For the second, a chart is usually the only thing they will actually read. The gap between the two audiences is the reason visualisation skill is a career-long differentiator.

Charts communicate by exploiting the human visual system. A reader's eye processes position, length, colour, and shape before any conscious thought. The Gestalt psychologists (Wertheimer, Koffka, Köhler, 1920s) catalogued the rules by which the eye groups shapes together. Those rules govern whether your chart communicates or confuses.

Core Concepts

The purpose of every chart

Before you pick a chart type, ask yourself one question: what decision should the reader make after seeing this? The chart exists to answer one question — not three, not ten. If you cannot state the question in a single sentence, the chart is not ready.

🟢 Single-question rule

Every chart should have exactly one question it answers. "Which town grew fastest last year?" is a question. "All the important patterns in the HDB market" is not. If your chart is trying to answer everything it will answer nothing.

Six chart types cover roughly 95% of the questions you will be asked. The rest (box plots, violin plots, sankey diagrams, maps) are specialist tools. Master the six and you can communicate almost anything; master the specialists when you actually need them.

Gestalt in practice

Six Gestalt principles matter for charts:

  • Proximity — elements close together appear grouped. Use spacing to separate clusters.
  • Similarity — elements with the same colour, shape, or size appear grouped. Use colour deliberately; random colour reads as random categories.
  • Closure — the eye completes partial shapes. You can suggest a trend line without drawing it.
  • Enclosure — a box around elements binds them tightly. Stronger than proximity.
  • Continuity — the eye follows smooth lines and curves. This is why line charts read as trends.
  • Connection — elements joined by a line read as related. Strongest grouping signal of all.

The attributes of a good chart

✅ Good chart

  • Answers one question
  • Truthful axes (0 baseline on bar, honest scale on line)
  • Direct labels instead of legends where possible
  • Colour used meaningfully
  • Text big enough to read at final size
  • No chartjunk (gratuitous gridlines, 3D, shadows)

❌ Bad chart

  • Tries to answer everything
  • Truncated y-axis exaggerates tiny differences
  • Legend forces eye saccades between chart and key
  • Colour is random or misleading
  • Text too small to read when embedded
  • 3D effects distort quantities

Charts to avoid

⚠ 3D charts

The 3D effect distorts the quantities. A reader cannot tell if the green bar is actually larger than the blue one because perspective shrinks the back bars. Never use 3D for quantitative communication.

⚠ Misleading pie charts

Pie charts ask the reader to compare angles — the single hardest perceptual task in charting. Five slices is the usable limit; ten slices is unreadable. Use a horizontal bar chart instead — position on a common axis is the easiest comparison for the human eye.

Worked Example: Six Charts from the HDB Data

📝 Goal

Produce six charts that together tell the story of Singapore HDB prices: distribution, price vs area, price by town, correlation heatmap, price trend over time, and flat-type composition by town.

Step 1.

Set up the visualiser and prepare the data.

import polars as pl
from kailash_ml import ModelVisualizer
from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
hdb = loader.load("mlfp01", "hdb_resale.parquet").with_columns(
    (pl.col("resale_price") / pl.col("floor_area_sqm")).alias("price_per_sqm"),
    pl.col("month").str.to_date("%Y-%m").alias("transaction_date"),
)

viz = ModelVisualizer()
Step 2 — Histogram.

Answering "how are prices distributed?". Bins = 40 gives enough resolution to see shape without over-fragmenting.

fig = viz.histogram(
    data=hdb,
    column="resale_price",
    bins=40,
    title="HDB Resale Price Distribution",
)
fig.write_html("ex6_price_histogram.html")

Expect right skew: most transactions in the 350k–600k range, a long tail of million-dollar flats. Mean will be higher than median as a result.

Step 3 — Scatter.

Answering "how does price relate to area?". Sample to 5,000 points — a scatter of 500,000 points is unreadable ink.

hdb_sample = hdb.sample(n=5_000, seed=42)
fig = viz.scatter(
    data=hdb_sample,
    x="floor_area_sqm",
    y="resale_price",
    title="HDB Resale Price vs Floor Area",
)
fig.write_html("ex6_price_vs_area.html")
Step 4 — Horizontal bar.

Answering "which towns have the highest median price?". Aggregate first, then feed the named dict to metric_comparison.

district_prices = (
    hdb.group_by("town")
    .agg(pl.col("resale_price").median().alias("median_price"))
    .sort("median_price", descending=True)
)

price_by_town = {
    town: {"Median Price (S$)": price}
    for town, price in zip(
        district_prices["town"].to_list(),
        district_prices["median_price"].to_list(),
    )
}

fig = viz.metric_comparison(price_by_town)
fig.write_html("ex6_median_by_town.html")
Step 5 — Line chart.

Answering "how has price per sqm changed over time?". Aggregate to monthly first.

monthly = (
    hdb.group_by("transaction_date")
    .agg(pl.col("price_per_sqm").median().alias("median_psm"))
    .sort("transaction_date")
)

fig = viz.training_history(
    {"median_price_sqm": monthly["median_psm"].to_list()}
)
fig.write_html("ex6_price_trend.html")

training_history was designed for ML training curves but works perfectly for any single-series line chart — that's the beauty of consistent engine APIs.

Step 6 — Heatmap.

Answering "how do numeric features correlate?".

numeric = hdb.select(
    "resale_price", "floor_area_sqm", "price_per_sqm"
).drop_nulls()

corr = numeric.corr()
fig = viz.confusion_matrix(
    matrix=corr.to_numpy(),
    labels=numeric.columns,
    title="HDB Numeric Feature Correlations",
)
fig.write_html("ex6_correlation.html")
Step 7 — Stacked bar.

Answering "how does flat-type composition vary across towns?".

composition = (
    hdb.group_by("town", "flat_type")
    .agg(pl.len().alias("count"))
    .sort("town", "flat_type")
)
# Plotly Express handles the stacking when given long-format data
import plotly.express as px
fig = px.bar(
    composition.to_pandas(),
    x="town", y="count", color="flat_type",
    title="HDB Flat Type Composition by Town",
)
fig.write_html("ex6_composition.html")

The practical rule: for quantitative comparisons, prefer charts that use position on a common scale (bar charts, dot plots, scatter plots). Avoid charts that rely on angle (pie charts) or area (bubble charts) when accurate comparison matters. Colour is the least reliable quantitative channel — use it for categories, not for magnitudes.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Too many points on a scatter

500,000 points on a scatter is a grey blob — you cannot see individual observations or the relationship. Sample down to 2,000–10,000 points for scatter, or switch to a 2D histogram / hexbin for density.

⚠ Mistake 2: Truncated y-axis on bar charts

If your bars start at 400 instead of 0, a 5% difference looks like a 50% difference. Bar charts must start at zero — the length of the bar encodes the value. Line charts may start at non-zero if you are emphasising change, but always label the axis clearly.

⚠ Mistake 3: Random colour choices

A chart with ten bars in ten random colours reads as chaos. Either use a single colour for non-categorical data, a sequential palette for ordered categories, or a diverging palette for values with a meaningful middle. Random rainbow palettes break the similarity principle.

⚠ Mistake 4: Title as header instead of takeaway

"HDB resale prices" is a label, not a takeaway. "Central districts command a 60% premium over peripheral towns" is a takeaway. Use chart titles to state the conclusion, not the topic.

Reflection

Visualisation is a design discipline as much as a technical one. The six chart types you met today cover almost every question you will be asked. The Gestalt principles tell you why one choice reads better than another. The Cleveland–McGill ranking tells you which perceptual task to pick for the comparison you want the reader to make. ModelVisualizer gives you the tools without the boilerplate.

The biggest habit you take away today: every chart answers one question. State the question before you build the chart. If you cannot, the chart is not ready. If you can, your chart-type choice follows automatically.
