Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.1 Autoencoders

Total time: ~60 minutes  ·  18 slides  ·  ~3 min per slide average

This is the first lesson of Module 5 and the class is in new territory: "unsupervised deep learning". The audience has just finished M4 (classical ML plus a neural‑network bridge), so they know forward passes, loss functions and optimisers. What they do not yet know is how to build a model with no labels. Autoencoders are the cleanest entry point — the target is the input — which makes this lesson a natural on‑ramp to the rest of the module.

Target distribution of time: 15 min on the vanilla AE (set up loop, show it works), 10 min on denoising (tiny tweak), 25 min on the VAE (the conceptually heavy part — ELBO + reparameterisation), 10 min on the convolutional variant and sampling demos.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Autoencoders

Open by naming the transition. "Module 4 was classical ML and the first neural networks you trained by hand. Module 5 is every major deep‑learning architecture, one per lesson. We start with the simplest — the autoencoder — because you already know every trick it needs."

Show the minute budget on the title slide. Tell them explicitly the ELBO and reparameterisation trick take the longest — not because they are hard, but because they are unfamiliar. Warn them that the code is short and they should focus on why, not how.

→ "Four tools, one idea."
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

One idea powers four tools

Walk the four bullets. Each one is a separate industry — retail, real estate, finance, healthcare — all solved by the same base architecture with a small twist. Emphasise that the first three (dimensionality reduction, denoising, anomaly detection) are applications of vanilla and denoising AEs; generation requires the VAE, which we build up to slowly.

For beginners: the anomaly detection one is the most intuitive. Train on normal fraud‑free transactions. A fraudster's transaction doesn't look like the training data, so it reconstructs badly. The reconstruction error becomes a fraud score for free.
→ "Now the architecture — it's simpler than it looks."
Slide 3~3 minArchitecture diagram

Encoder → bottleneck → decoder

Walk the diagram left to right. Point at the input block: "high dimensional — an image, a vector, whatever". Point at the encoder trapezoid: "getting narrower — that's the key shape". Point at the latent: "this is where learning happens". Then the decoder trapezoid widening back out, then the reconstruction.

Name the loss explicitly: "we train it by minimising ||x − x̂||². That's it. No labels, no targets — the input is the target."

Emphasise the bottleneck's role: "if the latent were as wide as the input, the network would trivially learn the identity and learn nothing. The narrowness is what forces it to compress."

→ "Now the 15 lines of PyTorch."
Slide 4~3 minVanilla AE code

Vanilla AE in 15 lines of PyTorch

Walk every line. Encoder is two Linear layers with a ReLU in between, ending at latent dimension 8. Decoder is the mirror — Linear up to 64, ReLU, Linear back to 256, sigmoid to squash outputs to [0, 1].

Stop at the sigmoid: "this is a common beginner trap — the activation at the output must match the range of your data. Our pixels are in [0, 1], so sigmoid is correct. If your data were in [-1, 1], you would use tanh. If unbounded, nothing."

For experts: mention that the encoder/decoder need not be symmetric — you can have asymmetric capacity. But for clarity, mirror designs are the default.
→ "And the training loop is familiar."
Slide 5~2 minTraining loop

Training loop — the usual suspects

Tell them: "this is exactly the loop from M4.8 — Adam optimiser, MSE loss, .backward(), .step(). The only difference is that the input is also the target. Both encoder and decoder parameters are in one optimiser, so one .step() call updates both. Never use two optimisers for an autoencoder."

Run it live if you have the time. 20 epochs on 2000 blobs takes about 10 seconds on CPU. Show the loss going down.

→ "Now a tiny tweak gives us the denoising AE."
Slide 6~3 minDenoising AE

Denoising AE — corrupt input, clean target

Emphasise: "this is exactly the vanilla AE with one change. We add noise to the input before feeding it in, but the target stays the clean original. So the loss is mse(ae(noisy)[0], clean)."

Why does this work? "Because the encoder now must throw away the noise and keep the signal. It cannot just memorise pixel patterns — those patterns change every mini‑batch. It has to learn features that are invariant to the noise. That's a powerful free regulariser."

For beginners: compare to studying for an exam with the answers blurry. You cannot memorise the exact words, so you have to understand the concepts. Same idea.
→ "But neither vanilla nor denoising can generate new data. Why not?"
Slide 7~3 minWhy AE cannot generate

Vanilla AE cannot generate new data

Demo it: pass a random latent vector to the decoder and show the output is nonsense. Ask the class: "why?" Take one or two answers.

Then give the real answer: "the latent space is not a probability distribution — it's a set of isolated points, one per training example. Each training image gets mapped to its own little spot and the in‑between is undefined. The decoder was never trained on anything between the points, so it has no idea what to output there."

Set up the need: "we want the latent space to be continuous and smooth so that random samples always land somewhere sensible. That's what the VAE gives us."

→ "Enter the variational autoencoder."
Slide 8~4 minVAE intuition

Variational AE — make the latent a distribution

The big idea: "instead of encoding x to a single point, encode it to a distribution. The encoder emits a mean and a variance. We sample from that distribution to get z. Then we decode."

"Why does this help? Because we can add a KL term that says 'hey, encoder, make your distribution look like a standard normal'. That forces all the training examples' distributions to overlap around the origin. Now sampling from N(0, I) always lands in populated territory."

For experts: mention this is variational inference — we're approximating the true posterior p(z|x) with a parametric family q_φ(z|x). The math drops out of the ELBO derivation.
→ "Now the math — don't panic, it's two lines."
Slide 9~4 minELBO derivation

The ELBO — two terms

Walk the ELBO equation. "Two terms. The first is a reconstruction likelihood — how well does the decoder explain x given z. For Gaussian likelihood it becomes MSE plus a constant. The second is a KL divergence — how far is the encoder's distribution from the prior N(0, I)."

Then show the closed form: "for Gaussian q and Gaussian prior, KL has a closed form — one line of Python, no Monte Carlo. That is why VAEs are fast to train."

If beginners flinch at KL: "Think of KL as a distance between distributions. Zero if they are identical, larger if they differ. We penalise the distance so the encoder doesn't wander off into unused parts of the latent space."
→ "But we still need to differentiate through the sample — that's the trick."
Slide 10~4 minReparameterisation

The reparameterisation trick

Set up the problem first: "autograd cannot differentiate through a random sample. If we write z = torch.normal(mu, sigma), then .backward() has no idea how to propagate a gradient through torch.normal — it's not a differentiable op."

Then the trick: "but we can rewrite the sample as z = mu + sigma * epsilon where epsilon is noise drawn once outside the computation graph. Now epsilon is just an input, and mu and sigma are regular tensors with gradients. Autograd sails through."

Show the three‑line Python. Pause on std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar) and explain why the encoder outputs log‑variance (it's unconstrained in sign).

→ "A picture makes it clearer."
Slide 11~2 minReparameterisation diagram

Why the trick is necessary

Walk the diagram. "Input flows into two encoder heads — one for mu, one for sigma. Both are differentiable. Epsilon enters from the bottom — it's an external input, not part of the graph. They get combined in the yellow z = mu + sigma*eps box, which is differentiable. Then decoder."

Emphasise the solid vs dashed arrows: "solid arrows carry gradients back during .backward(). Dashed arrow (epsilon) doesn't — autograd treats it as a constant. But because mu and sigma are on the solid path, their gradients still flow through the whole thing."

→ "The full VAE loss in code."
Slide 12~3 minVAE loss code

VAE loss in code

Walk the three lines. "Reconstruction is MSE with sum reduction — sum so that the two terms are on comparable scales per batch. KL is the closed form we derived on the previous slide, translated directly into PyTorch. Return the total and the components for logging."

Then warn about posterior collapse: "if KL dominates, the encoder learns mu = 0, sigma = 1 for every input. Then sampling is fine but the decoder has nothing to condition on — it becomes a plain generator with no connection to x. Two fixes: β‑VAE (multiply KL by a small constant) or KL warm‑up (start with β=0 and ramp to 1 over a few epochs)."

→ "Payoff time — generate new images."
Slide 13~3 minSampling new data

Generating new images

Show the three lines. Emphasise torch.randn — that's the prior sample. Pass through the decoder. Out comes 16 new images that are not in the training set.

Run the demo if you can. The blobs are not stunning — they are blurry Gaussians — but they are new Gaussians, different centres and widths from any you trained on. That's generation.

For experts: the blurriness is a known VAE problem — Gaussian likelihood favours mean reconstructions. GANs (Lesson 5.5) produce sharper samples but are harder to train. Diffusion models (also 5.5) give the best of both worlds.
→ "The convolutional variant — swap in Conv2d."
Slide 14~3 minConvolutional AE

Convolutional AE — for images

Walk the code. "For image‑shaped inputs, linear layers waste parameters. We use Conv2d to downsample in the encoder — stride 2 each layer halves the spatial size. For the decoder we use ConvTranspose2d to upsample back. Everything else stays the same — same loss, same training loop."

Mention that this is a preview of CNNs in Lesson 5.2. Students don't need to understand Conv2d in depth yet — they just need to recognise the mirror structure.

→ "Four variants, one table."
Slide 15~2 minComparison

Four variants at a glance

Walk the table. The key column is the last one — only the VAE can generate new data. All four can reconstruct, compress, and detect anomalies. Only the VAE's latent space is a proper distribution.

Tell them: "when you hear 'autoencoder' in the wild, 80% of the time they mean vanilla or denoising. When they mean generation, they say VAE explicitly. When they mean a specific architecture, they say convolutional."

→ "Kailash engine for visualisation."
Slide 16~2 minModelVisualizer

ModelVisualizer for training curves

Introduce the Kailash engine. "Throughout Module 5 you will use ModelVisualizer for training curves, latent scatter plots, reconstruction galleries, and confusion matrices. It is not a matplotlib wrapper — it handles layout, titles, and HTML export for you. Two lines of code instead of fifteen."

Run the two‑line example. Show the plot. Move on.

→ "Four common traps, then we close."
Slide 17~3 minCommon mistakes

Four traps

  1. log σ² is not σ. This is the single most common bug in VAE code. The symptom is NaN loss after a few epochs. Fix: always use std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar).
  2. Loss must match output activation. If you end with sigmoid, use MSE or binary cross‑entropy. If you end with nothing, MSE is fine. Never pair sigmoid with BCEWithLogitsLoss.
  3. Posterior collapse. Symptom: KL term drops to near zero, the decoder ignores z. Fix: β‑VAE or warm‑up.
  4. Linear AE ≈ PCA. If you forget ReLUs, your deep AE is just a roundabout PCA. Non‑linearity is where the representation power lives.
→ "Wrap up and preview 5.2."
Slide 18~2 minReflection

What you can now do

Walk the bullet list. Pause after each one and let them mentally confirm. This is a long lesson and they need the reinforcement.

Bridge to 5.2: "Today's autoencoder had a flat MLP bottleneck. Lesson 5.2 replaces the bottleneck with a stack of convolutional layers and skip connections — LeNet, AlexNet, VGG, ResNet — and introduces squeeze‑and‑excitation blocks that are standard in production CV models today. You will also meet ONNX export via OnnxBridge for the first time."

Take 5 minutes for questions. Common ones: "why are VAE samples so blurry?" (Gaussian likelihood favours means — GANs and diffusion fix this), "can I use a VAE for text?" (yes but it's tricky — posterior collapse is much worse for discrete data), "what if my data is not in [0, 1]?" (adjust the output activation and loss to match).
