Speaker Notes — Lesson 6.1 LLM Fundamentals, Prompting, and Structured Output

Total time: ~60 minutes · 19 slides · ~3 min per slide average

This is the first lesson of the final module. Students arrive hyped — "now we do the LLM stuff" — and with wildly different priors. Some have been hand-rolling prompts for a year; others have never called an LLM API. Your job in the first 10 minutes is to reset both groups on the same page: LLMs are just large transformers, prompting is distribution shifting, and structured output is how we move from toys to products.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

LLM Fundamentals, Prompting, and Structured Output

Open by naming what this lesson is not: not a GPT product demo, not a survey of all frontier models, not a debate about whether LLMs "really understand" anything. It is an engineering lesson about how you — today — turn an API call into a typed Python object with a cost budget.

Remind them of the arc. M1–M5 built the stack: DataFrames, statistics, classical ML, NLP, deep learning. M6 is where all six modules fuse into a shippable product.

If beginners look anxious: "You already know how to write a prompt — you have typed into ChatGPT. Today we turn that into code. The structured-output bit at the end is where you gain the real superpower."
If experts look bored: "Two things in this lesson that seasoned engineers often get wrong: the cost model (output is 4× input) and the Signature pattern for typed extraction. Even if the rest is revision, those two pieces change what you build."
→ "Where M6 starts and ends."
Slide 2~2 minModule arc

Where M6 starts — and ends

Walk them through the arc in 30 seconds each. Name each future lesson by number: 6.2 LoRA, 6.3 DPO, 6.4 RAG, 6.5 agents, 6.6 multi-agent and MCP, 6.7 PACT, 6.8 the capstone where it all wires together.

Plant the flag: 80% of real LLM work is prompting + Signatures + budgets. Fine-tuning and alignment are the remaining 20% — valuable, but not needed on day one.

→ "Let's remind ourselves what an LLM actually is."
Slide 3~3 minTransformer diagram

The transformer, one more time

Walk the diagram left to right. Tokens in, 32–96 layers of attention + feed-forward, a distribution over the vocabulary out, sample one token, append, repeat.

Stress two facts. (1) A modern frontier LLM has the same architecture as the mini-transformer they built in 5.4 — just scaled. (2) The thing that comes out is a probability distribution, not a word. Everything downstream is a function of that distribution.

For experts: mention KV-caching explicitly — "prompt prefill is the big cost; decode is cheap because we reuse K and V". That sets up the cost model later.
→ "And here is the one-line training objective."
Slide 4~3 minPre-training loss

Pre-training: one equation

Read the loss aloud. "Negative log-likelihood of the true next token, summed over every position in the sequence. That is the entire training objective for GPT-style models. No labels, no annotation. Every token in the training set is both input and target."

Spell out the consequence: "Every skill — grammar, arithmetic, code, translation, world knowledge — was learned as a side effect of lowering this one loss, because each skill is useful on some subset of internet text."

If beginners flinch at the equation: "Big sigma means add things up. Log p means 'how probable did the model think the correct answer was'. Minus sign means 'we want it high, so we minimise the negative'. That is all."
→ "Tokens — because that is what you get billed for."
Slide 5~2 minTokenisation

Tokenisation — you pay per token

Walk the diagram. Text → subword tokens → integer IDs. The integers are what actually enters the transformer. "Singapore" is one token in most vocabularies; "HDB" is usually two or three.

Hammer the rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ 0.75 words ≈ 4 characters. They will use this for the rest of their careers for rough cost estimates.

→ "So why does prompting work at all?"
Slide 6~3 minWhy prompting works

Why prompting works

This is the slide that reframes how they think. Read the blockquote slowly. "A prompt is not a command — it is a context."

Then the neighbourhood metaphor. "Your prompt selects a neighbourhood of the training distribution. 'Answer like a senior compliance officer' does not teach the model anything. It moves the spotlight to the part of the model that already knows what a compliance officer sounds like."

The mindset shift: "Stop thinking 'instruct the model'. Start thinking 'make the desired output the natural continuation'. That one shift is worth the whole lesson."
→ "Four prompting strategies, in order of cost."
Slide 7~2 minStrategy table

Four baseline prompting strategies

Walk the table. Zero-shot is cheapest. Few-shot adds examples. Chain-of-thought adds reasoning tokens. Self-consistency runs N CoT chains and votes. Each one typically trades more cost for more accuracy — but not always.

Flag the catch: "self-consistency only helps when the majority is actually right. On tasks where all your chains are wrong in the same way, voting amplifies the error."

→ "Zero-shot — the default."
Slide 8~3 minZero-shot prompt

Zero-shot

Read the f-string slowly. Point out three structural choices that make this prompt work: (1) the allowed categories are inlined, (2) the text is truncated to bound cost, (3) the closing instruction "Respond with ONLY the category name" gives the parser a chance.

Then warn: "The moment the model says 'Well, it could be Financial or Technology, but leaning Financial', your strip() breaks. That is why we will graduate to Signatures."

→ "Add examples — few-shot."
Slide 9~3 minFew-shot

Few-shot (in-context learning)

Walk the examples list. Point out three things that make these examples good: diversity (one per category), cleanliness (no ambiguity), and brevity (~15 words each — cheap in tokens).

Mention the magic number: 3–8 examples for most tasks, with diminishing returns past 10. The reason is that few-shot exploits attention over the example block — past a point, the signal from a new example is drowned by the noise.

For experts: mention ordering effects. Most positions are roughly equivalent but the last example has a slight recency bias. If you have a "hard" example, put it last.
→ "Now the magic phrase: think step by step."
Slide 10~3 minChain-of-thought

Chain-of-thought — "think step by step"

Read the prompt. Then read the model's output. Then pause and say: "This phrase — 'think step by step' — bought the field about 10 accuracy points on reasoning benchmarks overnight. It is a free lunch for complex problems."

Explain the mechanism in one sentence: "When the model emits reasoning tokens first, those tokens are in the context window when it emits the final answer. Scratchpad space beats direct answers on hard problems."

For beginners: "It is literally just four words. Put 'Let's think step by step' at the end of any reasoning prompt and try again. It will often work."
→ "Stack multiple chains — self-consistency."
Slide 11~3 minSelf-consistency

Self-consistency: vote across samples

Walk the diagram. "One prompt, temperature turned up, sample N times, collect the final answers, take the majority vote." Cost is N times the baseline, so $N = 5$–$10$ is typical.

Name the intuition: "Different CoT chains can make different mistakes. Majority vote lets you cancel out the non-systematic errors. You cannot cancel out a systematic error — if all N chains are wrong in the same way, voting just gives you a wrong answer with high confidence."

→ "Enough theory — let's talk tools."
Slide 12~3 minDelegate

Kaizen Delegate — the LLM primitive

This is the first code they will write. Walk it line by line. "Pick a model. Set a budget — this is not optional, it is the difference between a bug and a bill. Call .run(prompt) and iterate asynchronously. Accumulate the text from the events."

Mention provider neutrality: the same code works for OpenAI, Anthropic, Ollama, local models. The model string is the only thing that changes.

If async feels scary: "Inside a notebook or main script, you wrap it in asyncio.run(...). We will see that pattern in the worked example."
→ "String parsing is fragile. Here comes the fix."
Slide 13~3 minSignature

Stop parsing strings — use Signature

This is the slide that separates hobbyist prompting from production LLM engineering. Walk the class carefully. Name the types aloud: category is a str, key_entities is a list of str, sentiment is a str, confidence is a float.

Stress the "float, not 'high'" point. "When confidence is a float, you can write if result.confidence < 0.6. When confidence is the word 'high', you are parsing strings again. Types matter."

For experts: mention that Signatures compile to JSON schema under the hood, which the model provider enforces via function-calling / structured-output mode where available. Kaizen abstracts this so you write Python, not JSON schema.
→ "And here is how we use it."
Slide 14~3 minAgent call

Using the signature

Walk the code. "Create a SimpleQAAgent with the signature class. Await its .run() with the input field as a kwarg. You get a Python object back, not a string."

Point out the two post-processing lines — the float threshold and the list iteration — and emphasise that neither line requires parsing. They are native Python operations on a typed object. That is the production win.

→ "But what does it cost?"
Slide 15~3 minCost model

Cost model

Read the formula. Walk the price table. The headline number is "output tokens cost 4× input tokens". This is why CoT is expensive — you are paying the 4× rate for the reasoning prefix.

Give them a mental calculation: "One classification call is ~1000 input tokens + 5 output tokens. On gpt-4o-mini that is $0.00015 + $0.000003 = roughly $0.00015. Ten thousand classifications is $1.50. A million is $150. Keep those numbers in your head; they keep you honest."

→ "And temperature."
Slide 16~2 minTemperature

Temperature controls randomness

Walk the softmax. "At $T = 0$ the softmax collapses to the arg-max — always the top token. At high $T$ the distribution flattens and the model samples more uniformly."

Give them the cheat sheet: 0 for classification and extraction, 0.2 for summarisation, 0.7 for creativity and for self-consistency sampling. Warn them that "T=0 is deterministic" is a myth — nearly deterministic, but GPU non-determinism and provider jitter still cause occasional drifts.

→ "Let's put it together."
Slide 17~3 minWorked example

Worked example — 10 Singapore reports

Walk the code. Load the dataset. Iterate the sample. Call three classifiers per row. Build a comparison DataFrame. Print it.

Stop and point out the practical pattern they should remember: "Rows where all three strategies agree are high-confidence; rows where strategies disagree are exactly where you escalate to a human. This is called ensemble disagreement and it is how production LLM pipelines decide when to ask for human review."

For experts: mention that for a real pipeline you would parallelise with asyncio.gather, not a sequential loop. Sequential is for teaching clarity.
→ "Four traps."
Slide 18~3 minCommon mistakes

Four common mistakes

Walk each one. Mistake 1 (parsing free-form text) is the reason Signatures exist. Mistake 2 (batched answers) is the most common "helpful optimisation that actually hurts". Mistake 3 (no budget) is the one that has cost someone you know real money. Mistake 4 ("T=0 is deterministic") is a myth that surprises people during audits.

Give them the takeaway: "Signature, async, budget, logging. Those four are the baseline. Everything else is optimisation."

→ "Reflect, then move on."
Slide 19~3 minReflection / bridge

What you can now do

Read the bullet list slowly, pause after each one. Students silently confirm "yes I can do that".

Tease 6.2: "Today we worked with a frozen model and shaped its behaviour through prompts. Next lesson we crack open the model and change its weights. We will implement LoRA — low-rank adapters — from scratch, run it through AlignmentPipeline, and survey the full 10-technique landscape of modern fine-tuning. By the end of 6.2 you will understand when prompting is not enough, and exactly what to do about it."

Take 5 minutes for questions. The most common ones after 6.1 are: "Which provider should I use?" (any — the code is the same), "How do I know if my prompt is good?" (measure on a holdout set, that is the only answer), and "Can I just use ChatGPT instead?" (you can, but you cannot build products that way because there is no budget control, no structured output, no audit trail).

→ End of Lesson 6.1. Take a 5-minute break.
