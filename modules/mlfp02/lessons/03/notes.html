Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.3 Bootstrapping and Hypothesis Testing

~45 minutes · 14 slides · ~3 min per slide

This lesson introduces the decision framework of frequentist statistics. The p-value warning (slide 6) is the most important single sentence you will say today. Say it slowly. Repeat it. Learners will hear it wrong many times before they hear it right.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Bootstrapping and Hypothesis Testing

Open: "Yesterday MLE gave you point estimates and the CLT gave you a CI. Today we cover two things: a distribution-free alternative to the CLT (the bootstrap), and a decision framework for answering 'is this difference real?' (hypothesis testing)."

→ "Start with the bootstrap."
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

The Bootstrap: sampling without extra samples

Sell the idea: "You have one sample. You want the sampling distribution of the median — or a correlation, or a regression coefficient. There's no clean formula. What do you do? You pretend your sample is the population and draw from it with replacement. It works. It's called the bootstrap."

Why it works (intuition): "Your sample is the best guess you have about the population's shape. Resampling from it is the best cheap imitation of drawing new samples from the real population. It isn't magic — it just uses what you already have."
→ "Here's the picture."
Slide 3~3 minResampling loop

The resampling loop

Walk the diagram left to right. One sample, many resamples (each with replacement, each of size $n$), compute statistic on each, collect them into an empirical distribution.

Emphasise "with replacement". "If I draw without replacement I get back my original sample. With replacement, some points appear twice, others not at all — that's how each resample differs from the original."

→ "And then you read percentiles off."
Slide 4~3 minPercentile CI

Percentile CI (the simple version)

Walk the code. 5,000 bootstrap replicates. Compute the statistic on each. Take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. Done.

"That's your 95% CI. No formula, no Greek letters, no assumptions about shape. The only thing the bootstrap assumes is that your original sample was drawn independently."

For experts: mention the basic bootstrap CI (pivotal form) and when each is preferred. BCa is the default in most modern software.
→ "Percentile is biased when things are skewed. Meet BCa."
Slide 5~3 minBCa

BCa: when percentile is biased

Don't derive BCa fully. State the two corrections (bias and acceleration) and show the scipy one-liner. "For most real data, the difference between percentile and BCa is 5-10%. For highly skewed statistics it matters. Default to BCa and move on."

→ "Now hypothesis testing."
Slide 6~4 minHypothesis testing picture

Hypothesis testing in one picture

Walk the figure. Blue curve is the null distribution — the distribution of the test statistic if the null hypothesis were true. Red line is what you actually observed. The red tail area is the p-value.

Then THE sentence, slowly: "The p-value is the probability of seeing data at least as extreme as yours, given that the null is true. It is NOT the probability that the null is true."

Give the flip example: "If I test 'this coin is fair' with 100 flips and see 62 heads, I get a p-value around 0.02. That does NOT mean there's a 2% chance the coin is fair. It means: IF the coin were fair, I'd see results this extreme or more 2% of the time. Subtle but crucial."

If beginners glaze: "You don't have to love the wording. Just remember: small p-value = surprising if the null were true = probably the null is wrong. That's enough to get started."
→ "The most common test statistic."
Slide 7~3 mint-statistic

The $t$-statistic and its distribution

Write the formula. Explain each piece: numerator is the deviation from the null mean, denominator is the standard error. The ratio follows $t$ with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.

Tease forward: "In 2.5 you will see this exact same $t$-statistic applied to every regression coefficient — the hypothesis being 'this coefficient is zero'. Same math."

→ "Two error types and power."
Slide 8~3 minType I/II matrix

Type I / Type II / Power

Walk the 2x2. "Reject a true null — Type I, rate $\alpha$. Fail to reject a false null — Type II, rate $\beta$. Power is $1 - \beta$: the probability you catch a real effect."

"Rookie error: you run an experiment, don't see significance, and say 'the treatment doesn't work'. Maybe — or maybe you didn't have enough power to see it. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence."

→ "And power has a formula."
Slide 9~3 minPower and MDE

Power and MDE

State the power formula. Walk each piece: bigger effect $\delta$ gives more power, more variance $\sigma$ gives less, more data $n$ gives more, stricter $\alpha$ gives less.

"MDE is the smallest effect you could detect with 80% power given your sample size. This is the number you should know BEFORE running an experiment. If you're running an A/B test with 500 users expecting a 1% lift — check your MDE. You probably can't see anything smaller than 5%."

→ "Multiple testing: the 20-test trap."
Slide 10~3 minBonferroni

Multiple testing: Bonferroni

Open with the warning. "Run 20 tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ on random data. By chance alone, expect 1 of them to be 'significant'. This is where science goes to die."

State the Bonferroni fix. "Divide $\alpha$ by the number of tests. Guaranteed to control the family-wise error rate. Very conservative — wastes power when you have lots of real effects."

→ "More power: BH-FDR."
Slide 11~3 minBH-FDR

Multiple testing: BH-FDR

Walk the algorithm: sort p-values, find the largest $k$ with $p_{(k)} \le \frac{k}{m}\alpha$, reject everything at or below that rank.

"The guarantee is weaker — it controls the expected proportion of false positives among the rejects, not the probability of any false positive at all. In exchange you get much more power. For exploratory work, BH-FDR is almost always the right choice."

For experts: mention that under independence of p-values, BH controls FDR exactly; under positive regression dependence, it still controls FDR; under arbitrary dependence, use Benjamini–Yekutieli instead.
→ "And permutation tests when parametric assumptions fail."
Slide 12~3 minPermutation test

Permutation test

Walk the four steps. Emphasise the core idea: "If the null is true, the group labels don't matter. So if you shuffle them, you should see test statistics just as extreme as what you observed. If the observed is far out in the tail of the shuffled distribution, the labels DO matter — there's a real effect."

"This is the purest test you can run. No distributional assumptions. Just counting."

→ "Now the three-metric A/B example."
Slide 13~4 minWorked example

Worked: three-metric A/B test

Walk the code. Compute three p-values. Apply both corrections. Show what's different — typically BH rejects one or two that Bonferroni misses.

"The point isn't which correction to always use — it's that you MUST pick one. Running three tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ each and celebrating the one that crosses the line is how you get results that don't replicate."

→ "Recap."
Slide 14~2 minRecap

What you can now do

Walk the bullets. Tease 2.4: "You have the parts. 2.4 assembles them into an end-to-end A/B test: pre-registered hypothesis, power-based sample size, SRM check, test, report. The data collection framework comes in there too."

→ End. 10-min break.
