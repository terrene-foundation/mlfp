Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.3 RNNs and Sequence Models

Total time: ~60 minutes · 17 slides · ~3.5 min per slide average

The load-bearing content of this lesson is the six LSTM gate equations and the intuition that the cell state is a gradient highway. Everything else — vanilla RNN, GRU, attention preview — is supporting material. If you run short on time, the GRU slide (10) can be collapsed into a sentence; the temporal-attention slide (13) can be pushed to a homework. Do not cut the gate equations (7–9) or gradient clipping (12). The audience after this lesson must be able to explain why LSTMs work, not just how to instantiate one.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

RNNs and Sequence Models

Open by framing the module arc. "Yesterday we did CNNs — grids. Today we do RNNs — sequences. Different structure, different architecture, same training toolkit. By the end you will have trained a vanilla RNN, an LSTM, and a GRU on a synthetic SGX price series and seen why the vanilla version can't keep up."

→ "What makes sequences different?"
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

Sequences need their own architecture

Walk the two bullets. Variable length first — "a sentence has 5 words or 50, an ECG recording has 300 samples or 30,000. CNNs handle fixed-size inputs. RNNs naturally handle whatever length arrives."

Then temporal dependency. Use the "bank" example. "The word 'bank' means different things in 'river bank' versus 'central bank'. The RNN's hidden state is how context from earlier in the sequence influences the interpretation of later words."

→ "The recurrent idea in one equation."
Slide 3~3 minVanilla RNN equation

The vanilla RNN update

Walk the equation. "Two matrices — W_xh maps the current input into the hidden space, W_hh maps the previous hidden state forward. Add them, apply tanh, done. Same two matrices at every step, which is what gives the model its constant parameter count regardless of sequence length."

Analogy: "this is a conv kernel but across time instead of space. The kernel's weights are reused at every position; the RNN's weights are reused at every time step. Same idea, different axis."

→ "Picture: the RNN unrolled through time."
Slide 4~2 minUnrolled diagram

The RNN unrolled

Walk left to right. "Four time steps, four cells — but all four cells have the same weights. The orange arrows carry the hidden state forward. The blue arrows bring inputs in. At the end we take the final hidden state and project it to an output."

Clarify: "the four boxes in the diagram are the same cell drawn four times, not four different cells. Call this 'unrolling' the recurrence."

→ "It looks elegant. It has a cruel flaw."
Slide 5~4 minVanishing gradients

The cruel flaw: vanishing gradients

Explain the problem carefully. "When we backpropagate the loss back to the hidden state at time step 1, the gradient is a product of Jacobians — one per time step in between. It's literally a matrix product of T matrices. If each matrix has singular values less than 1, the product shrinks geometrically. After 20 or 30 steps it's effectively zero."

Consequence: "early time steps never get updated. The RNN forgets. It can handle short sequences — up to maybe 20 time steps — then falls apart."

Set up the fix: "for nearly 20 years this was the limit of recurrent models. Then Hochreiter and Schmidhuber solved it in 1997 with a clever trick: the LSTM cell."

→ "The LSTM idea in one sentence."
Slide 6~3 minLSTM idea

LSTM: cell state highway

The single idea: "add a second state — the cell state — that flows forward with only additive updates. Three gates decide what to forget, what to add, and what to reveal. The additive path means the gradient doesn't get multiplied at every step. It flows back through the cell state nearly unchanged."

Draw the parallel to ResNet: "you saw yesterday that ResNet's skip connection did the same thing for CNNs — give the gradient an additive path back. The LSTM had the same insight 18 years earlier for sequences. History rhymes."

→ "All six equations. Don't panic."
Slide 7~4 minGate equations

LSTM gate equations — all six

Put the six equations on screen. Tell the class: "don't try to memorise them right now. I will walk them in the next slide. Just notice there are six lines, three of them are sigmoid gates, one is a tanh candidate, one is the cell update with forget and input mixing, and one is the hidden state output."

→ "Now read them row by row."
Slide 8~4 minWalk the equations

Walk the equations

Walk each bullet with physical intuition.

  • Forget: "sigmoid output in [0, 1]. Multiply elementwise into the old cell state. Value of 1 means 'keep', 0 means 'erase'. The network learns per-dimension what to remember from the past."
  • Input: "another sigmoid gate. How much of the new candidate content to let in."
  • Candidate: "tanh, so the content is in [-1, 1]. This is the 'what to write' part."
  • Cell update: "this is the key line. Forget gate times old cell, plus input gate times candidate. Purely additive. That's the gradient highway."
  • Output: "how much of the cell state to expose as the visible hidden state."
  • Hidden: "output gate times tanh of cell. That's what downstream layers actually see."
→ "Why does this solve vanishing gradients?"
Slide 9~3 minGradient highway

Why it works: gradient highway

"In a vanilla RNN, the Jacobian ∂h_t/∂h_{t-1} is a product of W_hh and a tanh derivative. Exponentially decaying over time steps.

In an LSTM, look at the cell update: C_t = f_t ⊙ C_{t-1} + (new stuff). The Jacobian ∂C_t/∂C_{t-1} is approximately just f_t. If the network learns f_t ≈ 1, the gradient passes through unchanged. Over hundreds of time steps."

For experts: the bias on the forget gate is often initialised to +1 or +2 to make it start close to "remember everything". This tiny initialisation hack speeds up training dramatically on long sequences.
→ "The GRU — a simpler cousin."
Slide 10~3 minGRU

GRU — a simpler cousin (2014)

"Cho et al 2014 noticed that LSTM's forget and input gates are redundant — one can be derived from the other. The GRU merges them into a single update gate, drops the separate cell state, and adds a reset gate. Fewer parameters, faster training, usually competitive."

Rule of thumb: "try GRU first. If it underfits, upgrade to LSTM. If you need really long contexts (1000+ tokens), neither works — use a Transformer, which is tomorrow's lesson."

→ "Code."
Slide 11~3 minLSTM code

LSTM regressor in PyTorch

Walk the code. "Ten lines. nn.LSTM implements all six gate equations for you — you do not write them by hand. batch_first=True makes the input shape (B, L, F), which is the modern convention. The forward pass takes out the final time step and runs a linear head on it."

"Note that nn.LSTM returns both the output sequence out (B, L, H) and a tuple (h_n, c_n) of final states. We only need out, so we discard the tuple."

→ "Training with clipping."
Slide 12~3 minTraining + clipping

Training — clip those gradients

Emphasise the starred line. "clip_grad_norm_. This is non-negotiable for RNNs. Without it, about 1% of batches trigger an exploding gradient — the entire gradient vector shoots up to thousands in norm, the optimizer takes a huge step, parameters go to NaN, training over."

"Gradient clipping rescales the gradient vector so its L2 norm never exceeds max_norm. We use 1.0 by convention. Always on. Same for Transformers in 5.4."

For beginners: "this line looks obscure but it's the difference between working and NaN. Copy it into every sequence-model training loop you ever write and forget about it."
→ "Temporal attention — preview of 5.4."
Slide 13~3 minTemporal attention

Temporal attention

Motivate: "the last time step isn't always the most informative. For stock price prediction, the past few days matter most; for sentiment analysis, a single word somewhere in the sentence might flip the verdict. Attention lets the model learn which time steps to weight."

Walk the four lines. "Take the full output sequence (B, L, H). Project each step to a scalar score via a linear layer. Softmax over time to get weights. Multiply and sum — weighted combination of all time steps, not just the last one."

Tease 5.4: "this is an attention mechanism bolted onto an LSTM. Tomorrow's lesson asks a radical question: what if you kept the attention and dropped the LSTM entirely? That's the Transformer."

→ "Singapore scenario."
Slide 14~2 minSGX scenario

Singapore scenario: forecasting SGX

Set up the worked example. "We'll predict the next day's price given the past 50 days. Real SGX data has trend, seasonality, and noise — our synthetic generator mimics that. Optional features: technical indicators like RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands."

Expected result: "Vanilla RNN plateaus at a high MSE — it can't carry enough history. GRU and LSTM both get meaningfully lower. On 50-step sequences the gap is visible; on 200-step sequences the vanilla RNN is unusable."

→ "Metrics."
Slide 15~2 minMetrics

Metrics for sequence models

Walk the table. "MSE / MAE for regression — same as tabular. BLEU for machine translation — compares generated n-grams to reference. Perplexity for language modelling — lower is better, represents 'on average how many words was the model guessing between'. Sequence accuracy is the fraction of entire sequences classified correctly (not per-token)."

→ "Four traps."
Slide 16~3 minTraps

Four traps

Walk each. Gradient clipping first — the one you will definitely hit if you skip it. batch_first — the silent-failure one. Stateful leakage — the one that produces subtly wrong results you won't notice. BPTT memory — the one you hit when scaling up.

→ "Wrap up."
Slide 17~2 minReflection

Reflection

Walk the bullets. Ask: "who can recite the six LSTM equations from memory?" Take volunteers. If nobody: "it's a lot to absorb in one hour — but the story of each equation should stick: forget what, let in what, new content, combine, reveal what, output."

Bridge to 5.4: "We saw how LSTMs carry memory forward step by step. Tomorrow we'll see how Transformers replace that with parallel attention — no recurrence at all. It's faster to train and handles longer contexts. The price is quadratic memory in sequence length."

Break: 10 minutes. Come back for self-attention.

→ End of 5.3.
