Speaker Notes — Lesson 4.3 Dimensionality Reduction

Total time: ~90 minutes  ·  18 slides  ·  ~5 min per slide

This lesson has the best ratio of pedagogical payoff to mathematical density in M4. PCA is the one algorithm in classical ML with a clean closed-form derivation, and walking through it gives students a rare moment of "oh, the math actually works out". Spend extra time on the loadings — they are what makes PCA interpretable and what stakeholders care about.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Dimensionality Reduction

Open: "In 4.1 and 4.2 we asked 'which group'. Today we ask a different question: 'which axis'. Not 'where does this point live' but 'along which direction does the data vary the most'. That question has a beautiful closed-form answer, and it will teach you half of linear algebra along the way."

→ "Let's motivate it."
Slide 2~4 minMotivation

From "which group" to "which axis"

Give a concrete example: "Your feature table has 50 columns. Six of them are variants of 'total amount' (spend, revenue, basket value, lifetime value, etc). They are 95% correlated. The model treats them as independent information and overfits. PCA replaces the six with one or two linear combinations that capture the same signal without the redundancy."

"Think of it as 'find the single column that best summarises a bunch of related columns, then repeat'."
→ "Here's what it looks like geometrically."
Slide 3~4 minRotation diagram

PCA rotates the axes to the data

Walk the two panels. The left shows data elongated along the $y = -x$ diagonal in the original coordinates. Most of the variance is along that diagonal but PC1 does not exist yet — the axes are $x_1$ and $x_2$. On the right, PCA has found new axes aligned with the data: PC1 along the diagonal, PC2 perpendicular. Projecting onto PC1 alone recovers almost all the information.

→ "How does PCA find PC1? Variance maximisation."
Slide 4~6 minLagrangian derivation

Deriving PCA — one Lagrangian

Walk the derivation step by step on the board. "We want the direction $\mathbf{w}$ that maximises the variance of the projections. Constraint: $\mathbf{w}$ must be a unit vector, otherwise we could cheat by making it huge. Lagrangian, take the derivative, set to zero, and we get $C \mathbf{w} = \lambda \mathbf{w}$ — an eigenvalue equation."

Pause here: "This is rare. Most ML problems have no closed form. PCA is one of a handful of problems where the optimisation reduces to linear algebra you already know."

"If the algebra is hazy, remember the conclusion: PCA solves the problem by computing eigenvectors of the covariance matrix. The biggest eigenvalue gives you PC1."
"The Rayleigh quotient $\mathbf{w}^\top C \mathbf{w} / \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{w}$ is maximised at the top eigenvector — this is the variational characterisation of eigenvalues. Same idea appears in spectral clustering and graph Laplacians."
→ "In practice, we use SVD instead."
Slide 5~5 minSVD

SVD = PCA's computational engine

Walk the diagram. "Every $N \times D$ matrix factorises as $U \Sigma V^\top$. $V$'s columns are the principal component directions. Σ's diagonal gives you the singular values, and the variance captured by each component is $\sigma_i^2 / (N-1)$."

Explain why SVD is preferred: "Forming $X^\top X$ squares the condition number and loses precision. SVD skips this step and works on $X$ directly. Every production PCA library — sklearn, PyTorch, JAX — uses SVD under the hood."

→ "Now the practical question: how many components?"
Slide 6~4 minScree plot

Scree plot: how many components?

Walk the plot. "Bars descend because components are sorted by variance. The elbow is at $k \approx 4$ in this example — after that, adding more components buys you little." Explain the two heuristics: visual elbow (subjective) vs variance threshold (objective). Use both, agree on the answer.

→ "But components are just numbers unless you interpret them."
Slide 7~5 minLoadings

Loadings make PCA interpretable

Walk the code and the example. "If PC1 has large positive loadings on spend, visits, and items, it is an 'overall activity' axis. If PC2 has positive loading on account age and negative on recent clicks, it is a 'tenure vs novelty' axis. These names are what the stakeholder cares about. A scree plot without loadings is an academic exercise."

"You name PCs the same way you name clusters — look at which original features dominate, and give it a human-meaningful label."
→ "Next: what if the structure is curved?"
Slide 8~3 minKernel PCA

Kernel PCA — for curved structure

Brief. "PCA can only rotate. For S-curves, spirals, rings — curved manifolds — you need kernel PCA. It applies PCA in a higher-dim feature space via the kernel trick. RBF kernel is the default. Scales only to a few thousand points because it builds an $N \times N$ matrix."

→ "And for visualisation, two non-linear methods."
Slide 9~5 mint-SNE

t-SNE: good for plots, bad for features

Walk the KL formula at a high level. "t-SNE defines a notion of closeness in high-dim space and another in low-dim space, then shoves the low-dim points around until the two notions match. The result is gorgeous visualisations but non-deterministic, with no inverse transform."

"t-SNE's pairwise distributions use Gaussian kernels in high-dim and Student's t in low-dim. The heavy tails of the t distribution prevent the 'crowding problem' where many points want to be near the origin in 2D."

Most importantly: "Never use t-SNE output as features for a downstream model. The stochasticity means your model gets different inputs every time you run it. Use it only for visualisation."

→ "UMAP fixes most of t-SNE's problems."
Slide 10~3 minUMAP

UMAP: newer, faster, better defaults

Key points: faster, preserves more global structure, has inverse transform, deterministic with a seed. This is our default choice for any visualisation task.

→ "Side-by-side table."
Slide 11~3 minComparison table

t-SNE vs UMAP at a glance

Walk the table quickly. Recap: UMAP for almost everything. t-SNE only if you already know exactly why you need it. "In the exercise you will try both and see the difference."

→ "One more theoretical result."
Slide 12~4 minEckart-Young

Eckart-Young: PCA is optimal

State the theorem informally: "Among all rank-$k$ approximations of a matrix, the truncated SVD is the best in Frobenius norm. PCA is provably optimal for linear compression — you cannot beat it with a better linear method."

"The proof uses the fact that the Frobenius norm is orthogonally invariant and the singular values are ordered. Extends to nuclear norm via Mirsky's theorem, which is why nuclear-norm minimisation is the convex relaxation of rank minimisation in matrix completion."
→ "So how many components does your data actually need?"
Slide 13~3 minIntrinsic dimension

Intrinsic dimension

Brief. Three ways to estimate: scree elbow, variance threshold, participation ratio. If the intrinsic dimension is close to the number of features, PCA will not help much — you cannot compress data that has no redundancy.

→ "Let's see it on real data."
