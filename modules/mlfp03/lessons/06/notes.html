Speaker Notes — Lesson 3.6 Interpretability and Fairness

Total time: ~55 minutes  ·  13 slides  ·  ~4 min per slide average

This lesson shifts from "is the model accurate?" to "should the model be deployed?". It is the most ethically loaded lesson in the course. Handle the fairness section with care — learners may have strong opinions. Ground everything in mathematics (the impossibility theorem) and engineering (measure, report, mitigate). Avoid moral prescriptions.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Interpretability and Fairness

"We have spent five lessons building the best model we can. Today we ask two questions: can we explain it, and is it fair?"

→ "Why interpretability matters."
Slide 2~3 minWhy it matters

Why interpretability matters

Walk the three reasons. The Singapore MAS reference is important for local context — financial institutions are required to explain credit decisions. "If your model denies someone a loan, you need to say why."

→ "SHAP."
Slides 3-5~10 minSHAP

SHAP: Shapley values

Slide 3: Walk the formula and the four axioms. "Efficiency is the key one — SHAP values sum to the prediction. That means the explanation is complete — nothing is left unexplained."

Slide 4: Walk the waterfall diagram. "Base value is the average prediction. Each feature pushes it up or down. The final prediction is the sum." This is the visual learners will remember.

Slide 5: Run the code. Show the summary plot and one waterfall.

For beginners: "Don't worry about the formula. The waterfall is the key visual: each bar is one feature's contribution."
For experts: "TreeSHAP is polynomial, not exponential. It exploits the tree structure to avoid enumerating all coalitions. KernelSHAP approximates with weighted regression."
→ "A simpler alternative: LIME."
Slides 6-7~5 minLIME + comparison

LIME and the comparison table

Slide 6: Walk the LIME code. "LIME perturbs the input, gets predictions, fits a local linear model. The coefficients of that linear model are the explanation."

Slide 7: Walk the comparison table. "Use SHAP for tree models (fast, consistent). Use LIME when you need a quick model-agnostic answer. Use ALE when features are correlated and PDP would be misleading."

→ "Fairness."
Slides 8-10~12 minFairness

Fairness metrics and the impossibility theorem

Slide 8: Walk disparate impact. "The four-fifths rule: if you approve 80% of majority applicants, you must approve at least 64% of minority applicants. Below that ratio triggers investigation."

Slide 9: Walk equalised odds. "Same true positive rate and false positive rate across groups. If your model catches 90% of actual defaults in group A, it should catch 90% in group B too."

Slide 10: The impossibility theorem. This is the climactic moment. "When base rates differ — and they almost always do — you cannot have all three. This is not your model's fault. It is mathematics." Let this sink in. It is the most important conceptual takeaway.

For beginners: "Think of it like this: if group A defaults 5% of the time and group B defaults 15%, a perfectly calibrated model will approve group A more often. That violates demographic parity. Fixing demographic parity breaks calibration."
→ "Mistakes."
Slide 11~3 minMistakes

Three mistakes

Mistake 2 (removing protected features) is the most common in practice. Give the postcode example: "Remove gender, the model uses postcode. Remove postcode, it uses school district. The signal finds a way."

→ "Reflection."
Slide 12~3 minReflection

What you can now do

Walk the bullets. Bridge to 3.7: "You can now build, evaluate, explain, and audit a model. The next two lessons are about industrialising this: workflow orchestration (3.7) and production deployment (3.8)."

→ End. Break.
