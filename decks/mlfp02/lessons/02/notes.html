Speaker Notes — Lesson 2.2 Parameter Estimation and Inference

Total time: ~45 minutes  ·  17 slides  ·  ~2.5 min per slide average

This is the densest math lesson of M2. Your pacing must be careful: derive, pause, check. Bessel's correction is where most learners lose the thread — spend the time. The payoff is that every statistical test in the rest of M2 has a principled foundation after today.

Slide 1~1 minTitle

Parameter Estimation and Inference

Open with: "Yesterday Bayes gave us the update loop — prior, data, posterior. Today we ask: if I have no prior, just data, what's my best guess? The answer is MLE, and as a bonus we'll prove the $n-1$ you've been typing for two weeks."

→ "Population vs sample."
Slide 2~3 minPopulation vs sample

Population vs sample

Pound this in: parameters ($\mu, \sigma^2$) are fixed but unknown. Statistics ($\bar x, s^2$) are random — their value changes with each sample. "The population is a ghost. We never see it. All we have is a finite sample, and our job is to reason back to the ghost."

Concrete: "There is some true mean HDB price in Singapore right now. No one knows it exactly — prices change every minute, and we can't measure all of them. When I pull 30 transactions and average them, I get an estimate. Tomorrow I'll pull 30 different ones and get a slightly different estimate. Both are guesses at the same ghost."
→ "Those guesses have a distribution of their own."
Slide 3~3 minSampling distribution

The sampling distribution of $\bar X$

State the two facts: $E[\bar X] = \mu$ (unbiased) and $\text{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma^2 / n$ (variance shrinks with $n$). Then state the CLT — "whatever the shape of the population, the sample mean is approximately Normal for large $n$".

Walk the CLT statement: "take $(\bar X - \mu)$ — the centred sample mean. Divide by the standard error — that's the standardised sample mean. It converges to a standard Normal."

For experts: mention Lindeberg–Lévy for i.i.d. and Lindeberg–Feller when the $X_i$ have different distributions. Finite variance is the key condition; infinite variance (Cauchy) breaks it.
→ "The classic picture: skewed population, Normal averages."
Slide 4~2 minCLT visualisation

CLT: the HDB example

Walk the three panels: skewed population on the left, slightly less skewed with $n=5$ averages, nearly Normal with $n=100$ averages. "HDB prices are right-skewed: lots of cheap flats, a long tail of luxury units. But if I keep averaging 100 of them, the average is Normal. This is why CLT is magic."

→ "Now MLE."
Slide 5~3 minMLE intro

MLE: pick parameters that make the data likely

Say it in English first: "Given a parametric model, write down the probability of your data under any particular parameter values. Then find the parameter values that make that probability as large as possible."

Show the product form of the likelihood. "Because the data points are independent, the joint probability is just the product of the per-point densities." Then explain why we take logs: "Products are nasty to differentiate; sums are not. Logs turn products into sums, and log is monotonic so the location of the max is unchanged."

→ "Let's solve for $\hat\mu$."
Slide 6~3 minSolving for mu

Solving for $\hat\mu$

Take the partial, set to zero, solve. Walk each step: the derivative of $-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(x_i - \mu)^2$ with respect to $\mu$ is $\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum(x_i - \mu)$. Set to zero. $\sum x_i = n\mu$. Divide by $n$. Done.

Punchline: "The MLE of the mean is the sample mean. You've been using MLE since Lesson 1.1 without knowing it."

If beginners freeze on partial derivatives: "Think of the log-likelihood as a hill with two axes — $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$. The top of the hill is where both slopes are zero. The partial tells us the slope in the $\mu$ direction; setting it to zero means 'the hill is flat in this direction'."
→ "And for sigma squared."
Slide 7~3 minSolving for sigma²

Solving for $\hat\sigma^2$

Partial with respect to $\sigma^2$, set to zero, solve. The key rearrangement: multiply by $2\sigma^4$ to clear the fractions. The final formula divides by $n$, not $n-1$.

Pause here. "This is the MLE of the variance — and it's biased. It systematically underestimates the truth. Why? Because we used the sample mean $\bar X$ as our centre of mass. The sample mean is, by construction, the number that minimises the sum of squared deviations. Centering on it instead of on the unknown true $\mu$ makes the sum too small."

→ "Time to prove Bessel's correction."
Slide 8~4 minBessel derivation

Bessel's correction

Walk the three lines of the derivation:

  1. "Write $X_i - \bar X = (X_i - \mu) - (\bar X - \mu)$. Square it and sum."
  2. "Take expectations. Each $E[(X_i - \mu)^2]$ is $\sigma^2$. There are $n$ of them."
  3. "And $E[(\bar X - \mu)^2]$ is the variance of the sample mean, which is $\sigma^2/n$. Multiply by $n$."

The result: $n\sigma^2 - \sigma^2 = (n-1)\sigma^2$. Divide by $n$ and you get $\frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$ — biased low. Divide by $n-1$ and you get exactly $\sigma^2$ — unbiased.

If beginners can't follow the algebra: "You don't need to reproduce this proof. What you need to remember is: dividing by $n$ underestimates. Dividing by $n-1$ is the honest answer. That's why describe() has been using $n-1$ all along."
→ "Let's make the intuition stick."
Slide 9~2 minn-1 intuition

Why $n - 1$: the intuition

Give the two-point example. "Ten and 14. Mean is 12. Deviations are $-2$ and $2$. Squared deviations are $4$ and $4$. Sum is $8$. If I divide by $n=2$, I get variance 4. If I divide by $n-1=1$, I get 8."

"Which is right? With only two points, how certain can you really be about the spread? The $n-1$ answer — 8 — is honest. The $n$ answer — 4 — is pretending you know more than you do."

→ "Confidence intervals."
Slide 10~4 minConfidence intervals

Confidence intervals from the CLT

"CLT says the sample mean is approximately $N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. So $\bar X - \mu$ divided by the standard error is approximately $N(0,1)$. The 95% central region of $N(0,1)$ runs from $-1.96$ to $+1.96$. Solve for $\mu$: the CI is $\bar x \pm 1.96 \cdot s/\sqrt n$."

Then the small-$n$ caveat: "When $n$ is small and you have to estimate $\sigma$ with $s$, the ratio isn't quite Normal — it's Student's $t$ with $n-1$ degrees of freedom. Slightly fatter tails. Replace $1.96$ with the $t$ critical value."

Then the interpretation warning. This is the same point from 2.1 but with sharper teeth. "A 95% CI is a statement about the procedure — if you repeated the experiment, 95% of your intervals would cover the truth. For any specific interval you compute, it either contains the true mean or it doesn't. You don't know which."

For experts: point out that this is exactly why many practitioners prefer Bayesian credible intervals — the interpretation is cleaner when the data is fixed.
→ "And MAP bridges back to Bayes."
Slide 11~3 minMAP

MAP: MLE plus a prior

"MAP is just MLE with an extra term — the log of your prior. That term acts as a regulariser, pulling the estimate toward the prior mean. In machine learning it becomes L2 or L1 penalties; it's the same idea."

"When $n$ is small, the prior dominates. When $n$ is large, the likelihood dominates and MAP converges to MLE. This is where Bayesian and frequentist methods meet in the middle."

→ "Hands on the keyboard."
Slide 12~3 minGDP worked example

Worked: Singapore GDP growth (closed-form)

Load the economic indicators, pull GDP growth, compute both variances. Show that their ratio is $n / (n-1)$. "This is Bessel's correction in numbers. You can feel it now."

→ "And then the same thing with scipy.minimize."
Slide 13~4 minNumerical MLE

Worked: Numerical MLE with scipy

Walk the code. Define the negative log-likelihood (scipy minimises, so we negate). Wrap $\sigma$ in exp so the optimiser can range freely. Call minimize. Compare to the closed-form answer.

"Why do this for Normal, where we have a closed form? Because the next model — logistic regression — has no closed form. You'll reach for exactly this pattern in 2.6."

For experts: mention that BFGS uses a Hessian approximation — the inverse Hessian at the MLE is the asymptotic covariance of the parameters. That's where standard errors come from.
→ "Building the CI."
Slide 14~2 min95% CI

95% CI from the sample

Compute the standard error. Look up the $t$ critical value. Construct the interval. Print.

Then the simulation check: "Run 1,000 fake samples. Count how many CIs contain the truth. You should get close to 950. That's what '95% confidence' actually means."

→ "One last thing: when does MLE break?"
Slide 15~2 minMLE failure modes

When MLE fails

Walk the four failure modes quickly. Small $n$ → huge variance. Use MAP. Multimodal → local optimum. Restart from multiple points. Misspecified model → confidently wrong. Do goodness-of-fit. Heavy tails → outliers dominate. Use a robust likelihood.

"MLE is not magic. It assumes the model is right. When the model is right it's optimal. When it's wrong, MLE will tell you a very precise number that is also very wrong."

→ "Recap and bridge."
Slide 16~2 minRecap

What you can now do

Walk the bullet list. Pause after each. Tease 2.3: "Now that you can estimate parameters, we need to decide. Did the conversion rate actually go up, or is it noise? The answer is hypothesis testing — and when the formulas break, the bootstrap."
