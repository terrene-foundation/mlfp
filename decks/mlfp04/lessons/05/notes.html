Speaker Notes — Lesson 4.5 Association Rules and Market Basket Analysis

Total time: ~60 minutes  ·  16 slides  ·  ~3–4 min per slide

This is the shortest textbook lesson in M4 — the concepts are straightforward and the algorithm is well-known. Spend extra time on the confidence-vs-lift distinction and on the "rules as features" forward connection. The math is light; the pedagogical lift is in the business interpretation.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Association Rules and Market Basket Analysis

Frame: "This is the oldest unsupervised technique we will study. Thirty years old, still running on every e-commerce site. Today we learn how it works and how to turn its output into features for supervised models."

→ "The core idea."
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

The oldest unsupervised trick in the book

Give the apocryphal example: "Thirty years ago a researcher found that supermarket customers who bought nappies on Thursday nights also bought beer. Probably apocryphal, but it launched a whole subfield. Today you see it in 'customers also bought' and 'frequently viewed together' widgets everywhere."

→ "The pipeline: transactions → itemsets → rules."
Slide 3~4 minPipeline diagram

The pipeline

Walk the three-box pipeline. Transactions → frequent itemsets → rules. Emphasise the support threshold between boxes 1 and 2 — that is the expensive step. From itemsets to rules is cheap.

→ "Three metrics define every rule."
Slide 4~5 minThree metrics

Three metrics

Walk the three formulas. Support is "how common", confidence is "given X, how likely is Y", lift is "compared to random chance, is Y more likely when X is present?" These three must become second nature.

"If this still feels abstract: support is frequency, confidence is 'how reliable is the rule', lift is 'is the rule better than guessing'. That's it."
→ "Lift is the star of the show."
Slide 5~4 minLift

Lift is the metric you report

Key point: high confidence alone is not a finding. "If everyone buys bread, then confidence(anything → bread) is high. Lift corrects for this by dividing by the base rate of bread."

Emphasise: "When you present rules to a stakeholder, report lift, not confidence. Lift is the actionable signal."

→ "Numerical walkthrough."
Slide 6~4 minNumerical example

Numerical example

Walk the three calculations. Bread is 20% of baskets, butter is 10%, both is 5%. Confidence is 0.25, lift is 2.5. "2.5× is a strong signal — worth a cross-sell experiment."

→ "Computationally, how do we find frequent itemsets?"
Slide 7~4 minApriori property

Apriori property

State it clearly. "If {A,D} is infrequent, no itemset containing both A and D can be frequent. That is what saves us from $2^d$ itemsets — we prune aggressively at each level."

"This is the anti-monotone property of support — it is a monotone function under set inclusion and can be used in branch-and-bound search. The same property appears in closed frequent itemsets and generalisations to sequential patterns."
→ "Visually."
Slide 8~4 minApriori diagram

Apriori in pictures

Walk the three levels. At L1, {D} is infrequent (red). At L2, {A,D} is correctly dropped because {D} was infrequent. At L3, {A,B,D} is pruned without ever being counted because its subset {A,D} was infrequent — that is the property in action.

→ "Faster alternative: FP-Growth."
Slide 9~3 minFP-Growth

FP-Growth — faster cousin

Brief. "Single pass of the data, compressed FP-tree, recursive mining. 2 – 10× faster than Apriori on dense data. Same output." Do not go into the tree construction — students just need to know it exists and when to prefer it (almost always).

→ "Real example."
Slide 10~4 minWorked example

Worked example: Singapore retail

Walk the code. Emphasise that use_colnames=True makes the output readable with item names instead of indices. Sort by lift descending, take top 10. Point at modules/mlfp04/solutions/ex_5.py for the full script including data preparation.

→ "How to read a rule."
Slide 11~4 minInterpret rule

Interpreting a rule

Walk the noodles-tuna-rice example. "Translate the numbers into a business sentence: 'customers buying noodles and tuna are 3.4× more likely to also buy rice'. Then propose an action: promo bundle, shelf placement, widget. This translation is what separates a usable analysis from an academic exercise."

→ "And then: turn rules into features."
Slide 12~4 minFeatures

Rules as features — the feed-forward

This is the strategically important slide. "Association rules are not a dead end. The best rules become binary features — has_pattern columns — that you add to a feature store and feed into supervised models. The unsupervised discovery powers supervised prediction."

Connect this to the feature engineering spectrum: "This is a step between manual M3 features and the continuous embeddings we will see in 4.7."

→ "One more metric: conviction."
Slide 13~3 minConviction

Conviction — direction matters

Brief. "Lift is symmetric — $A \to B$ and $B \to A$ have the same lift. Conviction is asymmetric and measures the directional strength. Use it when rule direction is meaningful."

→ "Common mistakes."
