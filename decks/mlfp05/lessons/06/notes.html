Speaker Notes — Lesson 5.6Graph Neural Networks

Total time: ~75 minutes  ·  13 slides  ·  ~5–6 min per slide

GNNs are the least familiar architecture for most students. Spend extra time on the message passing concept (Slide 3) — once they get that, GCN/GraphSAGE/GAT are just different aggregation strategies. The torch_geometric code (Slide 8) is the practical anchor. The consolidation table (Slide 11) is critical — it is the architecture selection guide they will use for the rest of the course.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

Graph Neural Networks

Frame: "We have done grids (CNNs), sequences (RNNs/Transformers), and generation (VAE/GAN/Diffusion). Today: graphs. Not every dataset is a table or a sequence. Social networks, molecules, supply chains — these are graphs. GNNs are the architecture for graph-structured data."

→ "Why graphs matter."
Slide 2~4 minWhy graphs

Why graphs?

Singapore context: "The MRT network is a graph — stations are nodes, tracks are edges. DBS's fraud detection models transactions as a graph — accounts are nodes, transfers are edges. NUS researchers model proteins as graphs — atoms are nodes, bonds are edges. In every case, the relationships between entities carry information that a flat table loses."

For beginners: "Think of a social network. Knowing who is friends with whom tells you more about a person than their profile alone. That friendship information is what the graph captures."
→ "The key idea: message passing."
Slide 3~6 minMessage passing

Message passing

This is the core concept. Spend time here. "Each node looks at its neighbours, collects their features, aggregates them (sum, mean, or attention-weighted), and updates its own representation. One round of this is one GNN layer. Two layers means each node has information from nodes two hops away."

Gossip analogy: "Imagine a party. Each person talks to their friends and updates their understanding. After one round, you know what your friends know. After two rounds, you know what your friends' friends know. After three rounds — the whole room."
→ "GCN formalises this."
Slides 4–6~12 minGCN, GraphSAGE, GAT

Three architectures

Walk each in 4 minutes. GCN: fixed degree-normalised aggregation — simple, effective, requires all nodes at training. GraphSAGE: samples neighbours, aggregates with mean/LSTM/max — scales to large graphs, handles unseen nodes. GAT: learned attention weights per neighbour pair — most flexible, connects to Transformer attention.

→ "Three task types."
Slides 7–9~12 minTasks, code, vis

Tasks, code, and visualisation

Walk the task table (node/graph/link), then the PyG code (GCNConv is one line per layer), then the embedding visualisation. "If the GCN learned good representations, you will see nodes of the same class clustering together in t-SNE — without any labels used for the projection."

→ "Mistakes."
Slides 10–11~6 minMistakes + consolidation

Mistakes and architecture selection

Walk mistakes. Then the consolidation table — this is the architecture selection guide for M5. "Images: CNN or ViT. Sequences: RNN or Transformer. Generation: VAE/GAN/Diffusion. Graphs: GCN/GraphSAGE/GAT. Tabular: gradient boosting (not DL). This table guides every architecture decision."

→ "Recap."
Slide 12~2 minRecap

What you can now do

Checklist. Preview 5.7: "Transfer learning — instead of training from scratch, start with a pre-trained model and fine-tune. This works spectacularly for images (ResNet) and text (BERT)."
