Lesson 4.1 Clustering — Finding Structure Without Labels

In Module 3 every exercise handed you a column called y. You had prices, churn flags, risk categories — a ground truth to fit against. In Module 4 there is no y. Today you will organise ten thousand Singapore e-commerce customers into segments before anyone tells you what a segment is. By the end you will know five clustering algorithms, three evaluation metrics, and — most importantly — how to argue that the clusters you found are real rather than statistical fog.

Why This Matters

A marketing manager hands you a table of ten thousand online shoppers. She wants "three or four segments we can target differently". No one has labelled them. No one knows how many segments exist. You have spending, frequency, recency, and a dozen behavioural counters. Where do you start?

This is the typical unsupervised problem: the data is rich but there is no answer key. Clustering algorithms partition the rows into groups such that rows in the same group are more similar to each other than to rows in other groups. The choice of similar — and of how many groups — turns out to matter more than the choice of algorithm.

Clustering is the entry point of the Feature Engineering Spectrum you will walk across in M4. The cluster labels themselves become features — they can be fed back into a supervised model in M5 as a categorical column that encodes "which behavioural group does this customer belong to?". That is how unsupervised learning earns its keep: not as an end in itself, but as a feature factory.

🟢 Foundations

If you are new to clustering, the single most important thing to remember is that there is no correct answer. Two analysts with the same data will produce different clusterings depending on the algorithm, the distance metric, and the number of clusters. The goal is not to find the true clustering — it is to find one that is useful for the decision you need to make.

Core Concepts

K-means: the workhorse

K-means is the oldest, fastest, and most-used clustering algorithm. It works by alternating two trivial steps until nothing changes:

  1. Assign: each point goes to the nearest centroid.
  2. Update: each centroid moves to the mean of its assigned points.

Both steps monotonically decrease the total squared distance between points and their centroids, so the algorithm must converge (though not necessarily to the global optimum). The quantity it is minimising is the inertia:

$$ J \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K}\ \sum_{\mathbf{x}\in C_k}\ \lVert \mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k \rVert^2 $$

Here $K$ is the number of clusters you chose, $C_k$ is the set of points assigned to cluster $k$, and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ is the centroid of that cluster. The squared Euclidean distance is the reason K-means assumes spherical clusters — every direction away from the centroid is penalised equally.

Two things can go wrong. First, a random initialisation can land two centroids in the same cluster, leaving another cluster starved — the algorithm gets stuck in a local minimum. k-means++ fixes this by spreading the initial centroids out: the first is chosen uniformly, and each subsequent centroid is sampled with probability proportional to the squared distance to the nearest existing centroid. This is now the default in every serious library.

Second, you have to pick $K$. The two common heuristics are the elbow method (plot inertia against $K$ and look for the bend) and the silhouette score (pick the $K$ with the highest average silhouette). The elbow method is visual and subjective; the silhouette score gives you a number you can defend in a meeting.

Hierarchical clustering and dendrograms

Agglomerative hierarchical clustering takes the opposite approach. Start with every point in its own cluster. At each step, merge the two closest clusters. Continue until only one cluster remains. The result is a tree — a dendrogram — that you can cut at any height to produce any number of clusters.

The key choice is linkage — how you define the distance between two clusters.

🔵 Theory

Ward's linkage is mathematically equivalent to greedy K-means: at each step it picks the merge that minimises the sum of squared deviations around the new centroid. This is why Ward + Euclidean distance is the default in most applications — it is the hierarchical analogue of the algorithm you already understand.

DBSCAN and HDBSCAN: density, not distance to centroid

K-means and Ward assume clusters are blobs around centroids. Real data is often stringy, ring-shaped, or has outliers that must not be absorbed into any cluster. DBSCAN (density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise) solves both problems.

DBSCAN defines a point as a core point if it has at least min_samples neighbours within radius $\varepsilon$. Core points whose $\varepsilon$-neighbourhoods overlap form a cluster. Points that are reachable from a core point but are not themselves core are border points. Points that are neither are noise, labelled $-1$.

DBSCAN has two beautiful properties: you do not need to specify the number of clusters (the algorithm figures it out), and outliers are explicitly flagged rather than swept into the nearest blob. The price is that you must choose $\varepsilon$ and min_samples, and a single $\varepsilon$ does not work well if your clusters have different densities.

HDBSCAN fixes the last problem. Rather than a single $\varepsilon$, it builds a hierarchy of DBSCAN clusterings across all possible $\varepsilon$ and extracts the clustering that is most stable. In practice you only need to set min_cluster_size — HDBSCAN chooses the rest. It is our default when we do not know the right number of clusters and the data has varying densities.

Spectral clustering

K-means fails on non-convex clusters — think two interleaved crescents. Spectral clustering solves this by first embedding the points into a new space where the clusters are convex. The embedding comes from the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian of a similarity graph built from the data. Once in the new space, you run plain K-means.

You rarely need spectral clustering in business data — most segments are roughly elliptical — but it is the right choice when you know the clusters have topology: trajectories, manifolds, or ring structure.

Mathematical Foundations

Silhouette: is point $i$ in the right cluster?

For each point $i$ define:

$$ a(i) \;=\; \text{mean distance from } i \text{ to other points in its own cluster} $$ $$ b(i) \;=\; \min_{k' \neq k(i)}\ \text{mean distance from } i \text{ to points in cluster } k' $$ $$ s(i) \;=\; \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max\{a(i),\, b(i)\}} \in [-1,\,1] $$

Interpretation: $s(i) \approx 1$ means $i$ is much closer to its own cluster than to the next nearest. $s(i) \approx 0$ means $i$ sits on the boundary. $s(i) < 0$ means $i$ is actually closer to a different cluster — a misclassification. The average silhouette across all points is the silhouette score. Good clusterings score above 0.5; below 0.25 is typically statistical fog.

Davies-Bouldin and Calinski-Harabasz

The Davies-Bouldin index averages the worst-case ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-cluster separation:

$$ DB \;=\; \frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^{K}\ \max_{j \neq i}\ \frac{s_i + s_j}{d(c_i, c_j)} $$

where $s_i$ is the mean distance from points in cluster $i$ to centroid $c_i$, and $d(c_i, c_j)$ is the distance between centroids. Lower is better — a value of 0 would mean zero-diameter clusters infinitely far apart.

The Calinski-Harabasz index (also called the variance-ratio criterion) is the ratio of between-cluster variance to within-cluster variance, scaled by the degrees of freedom. Higher is better. Unlike silhouette and DB, it scales with sample size, so compare only within the same dataset.

The gap statistic

All three metrics above degenerate if the data has no clusters at all: they will happily rank a "best" $K$ for pure noise. The gap statistic compares the within-cluster dispersion $W_K$ of your data to the expected dispersion $\mathbb{E}[W_K^\star]$ under a null reference distribution (uniform in the bounding box):

$$ \text{Gap}(K) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[\log W_K^\star\bigr] - \log W_K $$

Pick the smallest $K$ such that $\text{Gap}(K) \geq \text{Gap}(K+1) - s_{K+1}$, where $s_{K+1}$ is the standard error under the null. If the best gap is achieved at $K = 1$, your data has no cluster structure — an honest answer every other metric refuses to give you.

🟣 Advanced

In production, we recommend computing all four metrics (silhouette, DB, CH, gap) and only trusting a clustering when at least three agree. Any single metric can be gamed by choosing a $K$ that flatters it.

Worked Example: Segmenting Singapore E-commerce Customers

Load the customer feature table from Module 3 and engineer three new behavioural features (frequency, recency, monetary value — the RFM triple). Then cluster. 

import numpy as np
import polars as pl
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from sklearn.mixture import GaussianMixture
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score, davies_bouldin_score
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
import hdbscan

from shared import MLFPDataLoader
from kailash_ml.interop import to_sklearn_input

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
customers = loader.load("mlfp03", "ecommerce_customers.parquet")

feature_cols = [
    c for c, d in zip(customers.columns, customers.dtypes)
    if d in (pl.Float64, pl.Float32, pl.Int64, pl.Int32) and c != "customer_id"
]
X, _, _ = to_sklearn_input(
    customers.drop_nulls(subset=feature_cols),
    feature_columns=feature_cols,
)

# STANDARDISE — mandatory for distance-based clustering
X_scaled = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X)

# Sweep K from 2 to 10 with silhouette
sils = []
for k in range(2, 11):
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, n_init=10)
    labels = km.fit_predict(X_scaled)
    sils.append(silhouette_score(X_scaled, labels))

best_k = 2 + int(np.argmax(sils))
print(f"Best K by silhouette: {best_k} (score={max(sils):.3f})")

The result will look something like Best K by silhouette: 4 (score=0.41). A silhouette of 0.41 says the clusters are real but not razor-sharp — which is honest for customer data. Now compare K-means, GMM, and HDBSCAN on the same data: 

km_labels = KMeans(n_clusters=best_k, random_state=42, n_init=10).fit_predict(X_scaled)

gmm = GaussianMixture(n_components=best_k, random_state=42, covariance_type="full")
gmm_labels = gmm.fit_predict(X_scaled)

hdb = hdbscan.HDBSCAN(min_cluster_size=50, min_samples=10)
hdb_labels = hdb.fit_predict(X_scaled)

for name, lbl in [("K-means", km_labels), ("GMM", gmm_labels), ("HDBSCAN", hdb_labels)]:
    valid = lbl != -1
    if valid.sum() < 2 or len(set(lbl[valid])) < 2:
        continue
    sil = silhouette_score(X_scaled[valid], lbl[valid])
    db = davies_bouldin_score(X_scaled[valid], lbl[valid])
    print(f"{name:<10} k={len(set(lbl[valid])):<3} sil={sil:.3f} DB={db:.3f}")

Once you have chosen the best method, profile each cluster by computing per-cluster means and comparing them to the global mean. A cluster where frequency is +50% and monetary value is −30% is a "frequent bargain hunter". A cluster where recency is +80 days and frequency is −60% is a "dormant user". These human labels are what your marketing manager will use; the algorithm cannot produce them.

End-to-end checkpoint

After running this, you should have:

  • A numeric best_k chosen by silhouette.
  • Three sets of labels from K-means, GMM, and HDBSCAN.
  • A comparison table showing silhouette and Davies-Bouldin for each.
  • A per-cluster profile table where each row is a cluster and each column is a feature's mean relative to the global mean.

Common Mistakes

  1. Not standardising the features. If one column is "income in dollars" and another is "number of visits", the first dominates every distance calculation. Always pass your data through StandardScaler (or equivalent) before clustering.
  2. Interpreting $K$ as the number of real segments. The silhouette-optimal $K$ is a property of the data and the algorithm, not a law of nature. Three analysts will get three different $K$ values on the same data. Use the metrics to rule out bad $K$ values, not to find the one true answer.
  3. Forgetting that HDBSCAN returns $-1$ for noise. If you naively pass HDBSCAN labels to a metric, the noise points will pollute the score. Always filter on labels != -1 first.
  4. Treating clustering as the end state. The cluster label is a feature, not a conclusion. Feed it back into a supervised model or use it to define a decision policy. A clustering that sits in a notebook is clustering that wasted your time.

Reflection

The deepest insight in clustering is that the algorithm does not determine the segmentation — the feature set does. If you cluster on {revenue, frequency, recency} you will get RFM segments. If you cluster on {click-through rate, time-on-page, bounce rate} you will get engagement segments. The same customers will appear in different groupings depending on which lens you look through. Choosing the feature set is the decision; choosing the algorithm is a rounding error in comparison.
