Lesson 4.3 Dimensionality Reduction — Finding the Axes That Matter

Clustering asked "which group does this point belong to?" Dimensionality reduction asks a different question: "which directions in feature space carry the most information?" Today you will derive principal component analysis from first principles, connect it to the singular value decomposition, and meet its non-linear cousins — kernel PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP. By the end you will know when to use each and why t-SNE is great for visualisation but dangerous for feature extraction.

Why This Matters

A modern business dataset easily has 50 – 500 features. Most of them are correlated: "total_spend" and "n_transactions" carry overlapping information; "minutes_on_site" and "pages_viewed" do too. When features are correlated, distance-based algorithms waste capacity and models overfit. Dimensionality reduction replaces the original features with a smaller set of new features — linear combinations of the originals — that are uncorrelated and ordered by variance.

The idea has three uses: visualisation (project high-dim data to 2D so humans can see it), compression (store less without losing much), and feature extraction (feed the reduced features into a downstream model to avoid the curse of dimensionality). Today we care most about feature extraction — the principal components are learned features, the first step on the spectrum from manual engineering to automated discovery.

Core Concepts

PCA in one sentence

Principal component analysis finds an orthonormal basis for your data such that the first axis captures as much variance as possible, the second axis captures as much of the remaining variance as possible, and so on. The first $k$ axes span a $k$-dimensional subspace that is the optimal low-rank approximation of your data in a least-squares sense.

Derivation via variance maximisation

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ be the centred data matrix (each column has mean zero). We want a unit vector $\mathbf{w}$ that maximises the variance of the projected points $X \mathbf{w}$:

$$ \max_{\mathbf{w}}\ \text{Var}(X\mathbf{w}) \;=\; \max_{\mathbf{w}}\ \mathbf{w}^\top C\,\mathbf{w}\quad \text{subject to } \lVert \mathbf{w}\rVert = 1 $$

where $C = \frac{1}{N-1} X^\top X$ is the sample covariance matrix. Using a Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$ for the norm constraint:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda) \;=\; \mathbf{w}^\top C\,\mathbf{w} - \lambda(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{w} - 1) $$ $$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}} \;=\; 2 C \mathbf{w} - 2\lambda \mathbf{w} \;=\; 0 \quad \Longrightarrow\quad C\mathbf{w} \;=\; \lambda \mathbf{w} $$

This is an eigenvalue equation. The vector $\mathbf{w}$ that maximises $\mathbf{w}^\top C \mathbf{w}$ is the eigenvector of $C$ with the largest eigenvalue — which equals the variance along that direction. The next principal component is the eigenvector with the second-largest eigenvalue, constrained to be orthogonal to the first, and so on.

🔵 Theory

Every symmetric positive semi-definite matrix (like the covariance $C$) has orthonormal eigenvectors with non-negative eigenvalues. So the spectral decomposition $C = V \Lambda V^\top$ always exists, and PCA reduces to sorting the columns of $V$ by the corresponding $\lambda_i$. This is a rare case in ML where the optimisation has a closed-form solution.

The SVD connection

The singular value decomposition of the data matrix writes

$$ X \;=\; U\,\Sigma\,V^\top $$

where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times r}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$ have orthonormal columns, $\Sigma$ is diagonal with non-negative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r \geq 0$ (the singular values), and $r = \min(N, D)$. The columns of $V$ are exactly the principal component directions, and $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2 / (N-1)$. This is why every modern PCA implementation uses SVD under the hood: it is numerically more stable than forming $X^\top X$ and is just as fast.

Reading a scree plot

The scree plot shows the variance explained by each principal component in descending order. You pick $k$ — the number of components to keep — by eyeballing the "elbow" (the same idea as elbow-method K-means) or by picking the smallest $k$ such that the cumulative variance explained exceeds a threshold (e.g., 0.90).

The loadings — the entries of $V$ — tell you which original features each principal component weights. A PC where "n_transactions" has loading 0.7 and "total_spend" has loading 0.6 roughly means "overall activity level". Interpreting loadings is how you turn PCA from a black box into a feature-engineering conversation.

Kernel PCA

Standard PCA is a linear method — it can only rotate the axes, not bend them. When the variance-maximising directions are curved (think data on a spiral or a torus), linear PCA fails. Kernel PCA applies the kernel trick: instead of computing PCA in the input space, compute it in an implicit higher-dimensional feature space defined by a kernel function $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \phi(\mathbf{x})^\top \phi(\mathbf{y})$. Common kernels are RBF (Gaussian) and polynomial. The cost is that you must store and eigendecompose an $N \times N$ kernel matrix, so kernel PCA only scales to a few thousand points.

t-SNE and UMAP — for visualisation

t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbour Embedding) is a non-linear embedding designed specifically for visualisation. It defines a probability distribution over pairs of points in the high-dimensional space — $p_{ij}$ is high if points are close — and another distribution $q_{ij}$ in the low-dimensional space, then minimises the KL divergence between them:

$$ \text{KL}(P \,\|\, Q) \;=\; \sum_{i \neq j}\ p_{ij}\ \log \frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}} $$

t-SNE preserves local structure well — points that were close stay close — but it distorts global structure, and distances in the t-SNE plot are not meaningful. It also has no inverse mapping, so it cannot be used to project new points, and it is non-deterministic (different random seeds give different layouts). Use it for visualisation, never for feature extraction.

UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) is newer and generally better. It is faster, preserves more global structure, and has an approximate inverse transform. It also scales to millions of points. In practice, try UMAP first for any visualisation task; fall back to t-SNE only when you need its specific behaviour.

Intrinsic dimension

Your data might live in a 500-feature space but truly only vary along 10 dimensions — the other 490 are noise or redundancy. The intrinsic dimension is the number of components needed to capture most of the variance. The scree plot's elbow gives a visual estimate; the participation ratio $\bigl(\sum_i \lambda_i\bigr)^2 / \sum_i \lambda_i^2$ gives a scalar one. Knowing the intrinsic dimension tells you whether feature selection will help at all: if your data has intrinsic dimension 200, reducing to 10 will lose too much information.

Mathematical Foundations

Eckart-Young theorem

The deepest result in PCA is the Eckart-Young theorem: the best rank-$k$ approximation of a matrix $X$ in Frobenius norm is obtained by truncating its SVD. Formally, among all rank-$k$ matrices $\hat{X}$,

$$ \min_{\text{rank}(\hat{X}) \leq k}\ \lVert X - \hat{X}\rVert_F^2 \;=\; \sum_{i=k+1}^{r}\ \sigma_i^2 $$

and the minimum is achieved by $\hat{X} = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top$ where the subscript $k$ means "first $k$ columns". In English: PCA is provably the best linear dimensionality reduction method, and the reconstruction error equals the sum of the dropped singular values squared.

Reconstruction error and variance explained

Because $\sum_i \sigma_i^2 = \lVert X \rVert_F^2$, the fraction of variance explained by the first $k$ components is $\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i^2 / \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^2$. Setting this to 0.9 or 0.95 is the standard heuristic for choosing $k$.

Worked Example: Compressing the Customer Feature Set

import numpy as np
import polars as pl
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
customers = loader.load("mlfp03", "ecommerce_customers.parquet")

num_cols = [c for c, d in zip(customers.columns, customers.dtypes)
            if d in (pl.Float64, pl.Float32, pl.Int64, pl.Int32) and c != "customer_id"]
X = customers.drop_nulls(subset=num_cols).select(num_cols).to_numpy()
X = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X)

pca = PCA().fit(X)
cum = np.cumsum(pca.explained_variance_ratio_)
k90 = int(np.searchsorted(cum, 0.90)) + 1
print(f"Need {k90} components to reach 90% variance "
      f"(down from {X.shape[1]} features)")

# Interpret the first 3 components
for i in range(3):
    top = np.argsort(-np.abs(pca.components_[i]))[:3]
    print(f"PC{i+1} (var={pca.explained_variance_ratio_[i]:.3f}):")
    for j in top:
        print(f"  {num_cols[j]:<25}  loading={pca.components_[i][j]:+.3f}")

A typical result: 15 original features collapse to 6 – 8 principal components at the 90% threshold. PC1 is usually an "overall activity" axis (all positive loadings on spend, frequency, page views). PC2 is often a "tenure vs novelty" axis (positive on account age, negative on recent activity). PC3 varies by dataset. After PCA, downstream models train faster, overfit less, and often perform slightly better.

Common Mistakes

  1. Not centring the data. PCA requires zero mean. If you skip standardisation, the first PC will align with the direction of the mean rather than the direction of maximum variance.
  2. Using t-SNE as a feature extractor. t-SNE has no inverse transform and is non-deterministic. Always UMAP if you need embeddings; t-SNE is visualisation-only.
  3. Keeping too few components. If your scree plot does not have a sharp elbow, your data's variance is diffuse. Trust the 90% threshold rather than forcing a dramatic number.
  4. Ignoring the loadings. PCA results without loadings are a black box. The loadings are what let you explain the analysis to a stakeholder.

Reflection

PCA is the quiet workhorse of machine learning. It is not glamorous — there are no LLMs involved, no deep networks, just eigenvectors — but it shows up in almost every production pipeline. It is also the cleanest example in the curriculum of optimisation with a closed form: you write down the objective, take a derivative, and read off the answer. Most ML optimisation is not this kind — but when you get one that is, cherish it.
