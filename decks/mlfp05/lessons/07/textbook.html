Lesson 5.7 Transfer Learning

Training deep networks from scratch requires millions of images or billions of tokens — data and compute that most teams do not have. Transfer learning sidesteps this: take a model pre-trained on a massive general dataset, freeze the early layers that capture universal features, and fine-tune the later layers on your specific task. Today you fine-tune ResNet-18 for Singapore image classification and BERT for text classification, export both to ONNX with Kailash's OnnxBridge, and serve predictions with InferenceServer — the complete pipeline from research to production.

Why This Matters

A Singapore hospital wants to classify chest X-rays (normal vs pneumonia). They have 5,000 labelled images — enough to fine-tune, not enough to train from scratch. A Singapore fintech wants to classify customer complaints by department. They have 2,000 labelled examples — enough to fine-tune BERT, not enough to train a language model. In both cases, transfer learning takes a model that learned general patterns from millions of examples and adapts it to the specific task with a fraction of the data.

This is the most practically impactful technique in modern deep learning. The vast majority of production ML systems do not train from scratch — they fine-tune pre-trained models. Understanding when, why, and how to transfer is the difference between a model that takes a week to train and one that takes an hour.

Core Concepts

Feature hierarchy in deep networks

The fundamental insight behind transfer learning: features in deep networks form a hierarchy from universal to specific. In a CNN trained on ImageNet:

  • Layer 1: Gabor filters — edges at various orientations (universal to all images)
  • Layer 2: Texture detectors — combinations of edges (universal)
  • Layer 3-4: Part detectors — shapes like wheels, eyes, petals (domain-specific)
  • Layer 5+: Object detectors — complete objects like cars, cats, flowers (task-specific)

The same hierarchy exists in language models. In BERT:

  • Layers 1-3: Syntax — part-of-speech, dependency relations (universal to all English)
  • Layers 4-8: Semantics — word meaning, coreference (partially transferable)
  • Layers 9-12: Task-specific — sentiment, topic, NER (not transferable)

Three transfer learning strategies

StrategyApproachData neededWhen to use
Feature extractionFreeze entire backbone, train new head onlyHundredsVery small dataset, similar domain
Fine-tuning (top)Freeze early layers, fine-tune top layers + headThousandsModerate dataset, different domain
Full fine-tuningUnfreeze all layers, low learning rateTens of thousandsLarge dataset, very different domain
Foundations

Start with feature extraction (freeze everything, train only the new head). If accuracy is insufficient, unfreeze the top few layers and fine-tune. Only unfreeze all layers if you have enough data to prevent overfitting. This progressive unfreezing strategy is safe — you cannot make things worse by starting frozen and gradually unfreezing.

Math Foundations

Why freezing helps: the learning rate perspective

When you fine-tune, the pre-trained weights are already in a good region of the loss landscape. Large updates would push them out of this region, destroying the learned features. The solution:

$$ \theta_l^{(t+1)} = \theta_l^{(t)} - \eta_l \cdot \nabla_{\theta_l} \mathcal{L} $$
$\eta_l$ — learning rate for layer $l$ $\theta_l$ — parameters of layer $l$ $\nabla_{\theta_l} \mathcal{L}$ — gradient of loss w.r.t. layer $l$

Frozen layers have $\eta_l = 0$ (no updates). Fine-tuned layers use a small $\eta_l$ (typically $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$). The new head uses a normal $\eta_l$ (typically $10^{-3}$). This discriminative learning rate strategy prevents catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained features.

LLRD — Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay

A more sophisticated approach: decay the learning rate exponentially from top layers to bottom layers:

$$ \eta_l = \eta_{\text{top}} \cdot \xi^{L - l} $$
$\eta_{\text{top}}$ — learning rate for the top layer $\xi \in (0, 1)$ — decay factor (typically 0.9) $L$ — total number of layers $l$ — current layer index

With $\xi = 0.9$ and $L = 12$ (BERT), the bottom layer's learning rate is $0.9^{12} \approx 0.28$ times the top layer's rate. This preserves the low-level features that are most universal while allowing the high-level features to adapt to the new task.

Theory

LLRD is connected to the Fisher Information Matrix from statistics. Parameters with high Fisher information (those critical to the pre-trained task) should change slowly. Empirically, lower layers have higher Fisher information for the pre-training objective, which is why LLRD applies lower learning rates to them. This connection is formalised in Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC), covered in the fine-tuning survey in Lesson 6.2.

Transfer learning as regularisation

Initialising from pre-trained weights is equivalent to an implicit prior: the model starts close to a good solution and the fine-tuning objective acts as a regulariser that keeps it close. Formally:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{fine-tune}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}} + \lambda \|\theta - \theta_{\text{pretrained}}\|^2 $$

This $L_2$ penalty toward the pre-trained weights is implicit when you use a small learning rate (the model does not move far from the initialisation). Explicit regularisation toward the pre-trained weights is used in more advanced methods like EWC.

NLP Transfer Learning: BERT Fine-Tuning

from transformers import (
    BertForSequenceClassification,
    BertTokenizer,
    Trainer,
    TrainingArguments
)

# Load pre-trained BERT
tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = BertForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(
    "bert-base-uncased",
    num_labels=6  # e.g., 6 complaint categories
)

# Tokenise dataset
def tokenize(examples):
    return tokenizer(
        examples["text"],
        padding="max_length",
        truncation=True,
        max_length=128
    )

train_ds = train_dataset.map(tokenize, batched=True)
eval_ds = eval_dataset.map(tokenize, batched=True)

# Training arguments — note the low learning rate
training_args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./results",
    num_train_epochs=3,
    per_device_train_batch_size=16,
    learning_rate=2e-5,        # 10-100x lower than training from scratch
    weight_decay=0.01,          # Regularisation
    warmup_steps=100,           # Gradual LR ramp-up
    evaluation_strategy="epoch"
)

# HuggingFace Trainer handles training loop, evaluation, checkpointing
trainer = Trainer(
    model=model,
    args=training_args,
    train_dataset=train_ds,
    eval_dataset=eval_ds
)
trainer.train()

# Typical result on 2000 examples:
# Fine-tuned BERT: 87% accuracy
# LSTM from scratch: 72% accuracy
# The 15-point gap is the transfer learning advantage
Theory

BERT's pre-training on 3.3 billion words of English text gives it a deep understanding of syntax, semantics, and world knowledge. When you fine-tune on 2,000 complaint examples, it does not need to learn "what English is" — it already knows. It only needs to learn "what makes a billing complaint different from a technical complaint". This is why 2,000 examples is enough for fine-tuning but not for training from scratch.

The Kailash Engine: OnnxBridge and InferenceServer

Kailash Engine

OnnxBridge exports any PyTorch model to ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) format — a portable representation that runs on any ONNX runtime (CPU, GPU, edge devices). InferenceServer wraps the ONNX model with predict(), predict_batch(), and warm_cache() methods. Together, they take a fine-tuned model from notebook to production in three lines of code. 

from kailash_ml import OnnxBridge, InferenceServer

# Export fine-tuned ResNet to ONNX
bridge = OnnxBridge()
bridge.export(
    model=model,
    dummy_input=torch.randn(1, 3, 224, 224),
    output_path="resnet_finetuned.onnx",
    input_names=["image"],
    output_names=["prediction"],
    dynamic_axes={"image": {0: "batch_size"}}
)

# Serve with InferenceServer
server = InferenceServer(model_path="resnet_finetuned.onnx")
server.warm_cache()  # Pre-load model into memory

# Single prediction
result = server.predict(image_tensor)
print(f"Class: {result.label}, Confidence: {result.confidence:.3f}")

# Batch prediction (faster for multiple images)
results = server.predict_batch(image_batch)
for r in results:
    print(f"  {r.label}: {r.confidence:.3f}")

Worked Example: End-to-End Transfer and Deployment

Goal

Fine-tune ResNet-18 on a Singapore mask detection dataset (1,000 images, 2 classes: mask / no mask), export to ONNX, and serve predictions with InferenceServer.

Step 1. Prepare data with augmentation. 
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader

transform = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.RandomResizedCrop(224),
    transforms.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    transforms.ColorJitter(brightness=0.3, contrast=0.3),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.485, 0.456, 0.406], [0.229, 0.224, 0.225])
])

train_ds = datasets.ImageFolder("data/mask_detection/train", transform)
test_ds = datasets.ImageFolder("data/mask_detection/test", transform)
train_loader = DataLoader(train_ds, batch_size=32, shuffle=True)
test_loader = DataLoader(test_ds, batch_size=32)
Step 2. Fine-tune ResNet-18. 
model = models.resnet18(weights=models.ResNet18_Weights.DEFAULT)
for param in model.parameters():
    param.requires_grad = False
model.fc = nn.Linear(512, 2)

optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.fc.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

for epoch in range(10):
    model.train()
    for images, labels in train_loader:
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()(model(images), labels)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

    # Evaluate
    model.eval()
    correct = sum(
        (model(imgs).argmax(1) == lbls).sum().item()
        for imgs, lbls in test_loader
    )
    print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}: {correct/len(test_ds):.3f}")
# Typical: 94% accuracy after 10 epochs on 1000 images

server.warm_cache()

# Predict on a new image

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Training all layers from the start

Unfreezing everything immediately with a normal learning rate destroys pre-trained features. The model "forgets" what it learned on ImageNet and overfits to your small dataset. Always start frozen, evaluate, then selectively unfreeze if needed.

Mistake 2: Too-high learning rate for fine-tuning

Pre-trained weights are in a good region of the loss landscape. A learning rate of $10^{-3}$ (normal for training from scratch) is too aggressive for fine-tuning — it pushes the weights out of the good region. Use $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ for fine-tuned layers. For the new head, $10^{-3}$ is fine because it has no pre-trained features to preserve.

Mistake 3: Skipping data augmentation

Small datasets benefit enormously from augmentation — random flips, rotations, colour jitter, cropping. Without augmentation, the model memorises the training images and generalises poorly. With augmentation on 1,000 images, the model effectively sees 10,000+ varied views.

Mistake 4: Forgetting ONNX export

A model that only runs in your Jupyter notebook is not deployed. It depends on PyTorch, your Python version, and your exact CUDA version. ONNX export produces a portable file that runs on any ONNX runtime — CPU server, GPU cluster, or edge device — without any Python dependency.

Mistake 5: Wrong normalisation

Pre-trained models expect inputs normalised with the same statistics used during pre-training. ResNet expects ImageNet normalisation: mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]. BERT expects its own tokeniser. Using wrong normalisation is a silent error — the model runs but produces degraded predictions.

Reflection

Transfer learning is the single most impactful technique in modern deep learning. Most production systems do not train from scratch — they fine-tune pre-trained models. Today you learned the why (hierarchical features), the how (freeze, fine-tune, new head), and the deployment path (OnnxBridge to InferenceServer).

The key numbers to remember: fine-tuning a ResNet on 1,000 images reaches 94% accuracy; training from scratch on the same data reaches 71%. Fine-tuning BERT on 2,000 examples reaches 87%; an LSTM from scratch reaches 72%. These gaps are the transfer learning dividend — pre-trained features are worth millions of training examples.
