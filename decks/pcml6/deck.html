6.1 — Supervised Fine-Tuning

Foundations

What is fine-tuning?

A pre-trained model already "knows" language. Fine-tuning is like giving a new employee on-the-job training — they already speak the language, but need to learn your company's way of doing things.

Pre-trained

Knows grammar, facts, reasoning

Fine-tuned

Knows your tone, your domain, your format

Full Fine-Tuning vs LoRA

Foundations

Full Fine-Tuning

  • Update ALL parameters
  • 7B model = 28 GB of gradients
  • Need multiple A100 GPUs
  • Catastrophic forgetting risk

LoRA

  • Freeze the original weights
  • Add tiny trainable matrices
  • 0.1–1% of parameters
  • Single GPU, laptop-viable
Analogy: Full fine-tuning rewrites the entire textbook. LoRA adds sticky notes to the pages that matter.

LoRA: The Core Idea

Theory

In a transformer, each attention layer has a weight matrix W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}. LoRA decomposes the update as a low-rank product:

W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + BA
where B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}, A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}, and r \ll d

Only B and A are trained. W_0 stays frozen.

Why Does Low Rank Work?

Theory

Aghajanyan et al. (2020) showed that pre-trained models have a low intrinsic dimensionality:

  • The weight updates during fine-tuning live in a low-dimensional subspace
  • Most of \Delta W's singular values are near zero
  • Rank r = 8 to r = 64 captures >95% of task-relevant information
Connection to M4.3 (PCA): Just as PCA finds that most variance lives in a few principal components, LoRA exploits the fact that weight updates are approximately low-rank.

LoRA: Forward Pass

Theory

During inference, the output for input x is:

h = W_0 x + BAx = (W_0 + BA)x
At deployment, merge: W_{\text{merged}} = W_0 + BA — zero inference overhead
Key insight
A is initialised with Kaiming normal; B is initialised to zero. So at the start of training, \Delta W = BA = 0 — the model begins as the original pre-trained model.

Scaling factor \alpha / r controls the magnitude of the update.

QLoRA: 4-Bit Training

Theory

QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) makes LoRA even more memory-efficient:

  1. NF4 quantization — 4-bit NormalFloat: quantization levels optimally spaced for normally distributed weights
  2. Double quantization — quantize the quantization constants themselves (saves ~0.37 bits/param)
  3. Paged optimizers — offload optimizer states to CPU when GPU memory spikes
Y = X \cdot \text{dequant}(W_{\text{NF4}}) + X \cdot B \cdot A
Base weights in 4-bit; LoRA adapters in 16-bit (BFloat16)

Result: fine-tune a 65B model on a single 48GB GPU.

Each adapter is a small file (~10–100 MB). You can:

  • Stack multiple adapters on the same base model
  • A/B test adapters in production

6.2 — Preference Alignment

Foundations

What is alignment?

A model that predicts the next word well is not necessarily helpful, harmless, or honest.

Alignment = making AI do what humans actually want, not just what they literally said.

Analogy: A perfectly obedient employee who follows every instruction literally — "delete all the old files" — is dangerous. You want someone who understands intent.

The RLHF Pipeline

Foundations

The original approach (InstructGPT, 2022):

1. SFT → 2. Reward Model → 3. PPO
  1. SFT — Fine-tune on demonstration data (lesson 6.1)
  2. Reward model — Train a separate model to score outputs from human preferences
  3. PPO — Use RL to maximise the reward model's score
Problem: Step 2 + 3 require training TWO extra models, are unstable, and sensitive to hyperparameters. Can we do better?

DPO: Skip the Reward Model

Foundations

Direct Preference Optimisation (Rafailov et al., 2023) collapses steps 2 and 3 into a single training step:

RLHF (3 steps)

SFT → Train reward model → PPO

3 models in memory, unstable

DPO (1 step)

SFT → DPO on preference pairs

1 model + reference, stable

All you need: pairs of (x, y_w, y_l) — a prompt, a preferred response, and a rejected response.

DPO Derivation: Step 1 — Bradley-Terry Model

Theory

Human preferences are modelled with the Bradley-Terry model. Given a prompt x and two responses y_1, y_2:

p^*(y_1 \succ y_2 \mid x) = \sigma(r^*(x, y_1) - r^*(x, y_2))
where \sigma is the sigmoid function and r^* is the "true" reward

The probability that a human prefers y_1 over y_2 depends on the difference in rewards.

DPO Derivation: Step 2 — The RLHF Objective

Theory

Standard RLHF optimises the policy \pi_\theta to maximise reward while staying close to a reference policy \pi_{\text{ref}}:

\max_{\pi_\theta} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \Big[ r(x, y) - \beta \, \text{KL}\big(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x)\big) \Big]
\beta controls how far the policy can drift from the reference

The KL penalty prevents the model from "gaming" the reward model by producing degenerate outputs.

DPO Derivation: Step 3 — Optimal Policy

Theory

The KL-constrained objective has a closed-form optimal policy:

\pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \, \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \, \exp\!\Big(\frac{1}{\beta}\, r(x, y)\Big)
where Z(x) = \sum_y \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)\exp(r(x,y)/\beta) is the partition function
How?
Take the functional derivative of the objective w.r.t. \pi(y|x), set to zero, and solve — this is a standard calculus of variations result for KL-regularised optimisation.

DPO Derivation: Step 4 — Solve for Reward

Theory

Rearrange the optimal policy to express the reward in terms of the policy:

r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)
Key move
Take log of both sides of the optimal policy equation:
\log \pi^*(y|x) = \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) + \frac{1}{\beta}r(x,y) - \log Z(x)
Then solve for r(x,y).

DPO Derivation: Step 5 — Substitute into Bradley-Terry

Theory

Substitute the reward expression into the Bradley-Terry preference model:

p^*(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)
The \beta \log Z(x) terms cancel!
Why this matters
The partition function Z(x) — which is intractable to compute — drops out. We now have preferences expressed entirely in terms of policy log-ratios.

DPO Derivation: Step 6 — The DPO Loss

Theory

Maximise the log-likelihood of observed preferences under the policy \pi_\theta:

\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta; \pi_{\text{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l)} \left[ \log \sigma\!\left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)} \right) \right]

This is a simple binary cross-entropy loss on log-probability ratios. No reward model. No RL loop. Just supervised learning on preference pairs.

DPO: What It Means

Foundations

The DPO loss says:

  • Increase the probability of the preferred response y_w
  • Decrease the probability of the rejected response y_l
  • Relative to how the reference model would score them
Plain English: "Make the model more likely to say things humans prefer, and less likely to say things humans dislike — but don't drift too far from what the model originally knew."

GRPO: Group Relative Policy Optimisation

Theory

DeepSeek-R1's approach — no reward model, no reference model:

\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}} = -\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G} \frac{1}{|O_i|}\sum_{t=1}^{|O_i|} \hat{A}_i \log \pi_\theta(o_t^i \mid q, o_{\lt t}^i)
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \text{mean}(\{r_j\})}{\text{std}(\{r_j\})} — advantage normalised within the group
  • Generate G outputs per prompt
  • Score each with a simple reward (e.g., correctness)
  • Normalise rewards within the group → no baseline model needed

LLM-as-Judge: Known Biases

Theory
  • Position bias — tends to prefer the first (or last) option presented
  • Verbosity bias — longer answers rated higher regardless of quality
  • Self-enhancement — rates its own outputs higher than other models'
  • Authority bias — confident-sounding wrong answers beat uncertain correct ones
Mitigation: Randomise position, use concise rubrics, employ diverse judge models, and always validate against human agreement rates.

6.3 — Reinforcement Learning

Foundations

What is RL?

An agent interacts with an environment. It takes actions, observes states, and receives rewards. It learns to maximise cumulative reward through trial and error.

Analogy: Teaching a dog new tricks. You don't tell the dog HOW to sit — you reward it when it does. Over many tries, it learns the action-to-reward connection.

RL Vocabulary

Foundations
TermSymbolPlain English
StatesWhat the world looks like right now
ActionaWhat the agent does
RewardrHow good that action was
Policy\pi(a|s)The agent's strategy: "in this state, do this"
ValueV(s)How good is it to BE in this state?
Q-valueQ(s,a)How good is it to DO this action in this state?
Discount\gammaHow much we value future vs immediate reward

Bellman Expectation Equation: Derivation

Theory

The value function is the expected cumulative discounted reward:

Definition
V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R_{t+1} \;\middle|\; S_0 = s\right]
Split first reward from rest
V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[R_1 + \gamma \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R_{t+2} \;\middle|\; S_0 = s\right]
Recognise recursion
The inner sum is just V^\pi(S_1) by the Markov property.

Bellman Expectation: Result

Theory
V^\pi(s) = \sum_{a} \pi(a|s) \sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\Big[r(s,a,s') + \gamma \, V^\pi(s')\Big]

Read it aloud:

  • For each action I might take (weighted by my policy)...
  • For each state I might land in (weighted by transition probability)...
  • Add the immediate reward PLUS the discounted value of where I end up
This is a system of linear equations — one per state. For small state spaces, solve directly. For large spaces, approximate.

Bellman Optimality Equation: Derivation

Theory

The optimal value function selects the best action at every step:

Definition
V^*(s) = \max_\pi V^\pi(s)
Optimality principle
The optimal policy at state s takes the action that maximises immediate reward + discounted future value:
V^*(s) = \max_{a} \sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\Big[r(s,a,s') + \gamma \, V^*(s')\Big]

Replace \sum_a \pi(a|s) with \max_a — the optimal policy is deterministic (in finite MDPs).

Bellman for Q-Values

Theory

Expectation

Q^\pi(s,a) = \sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\Big[r + \gamma \sum_{a'} \pi(a'|s') Q^\pi(s',a')\Big]

Optimality

Q^*(s,a) = \sum_{s'} p(s'|s,a)\Big[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s',a')\Big]
Relationship: V^*(s) = \max_a Q^*(s,a) and \pi^*(s) = \arg\max_a Q^*(s,a)

Policy Gradient: The Foundation of PPO

Theory

Instead of learning values, directly optimise the policy parameters \theta:

\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) \cdot A^{\pi_\theta}(s,a)\right]
The policy gradient theorem. A(s,a) is the advantage: how much better is action a than average?

Intuition: increase the probability of actions that are better than average; decrease those that are worse.

PPO: Clipped Objective — Step 1

Theory

Define the probability ratio between new and old policy:

r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t | s_t)}

The naive objective would be L^{CPI} = \mathbb{E}[r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t], but this allows unbounded policy updates — the policy can change drastically in a single step, causing instability.

Problem: TRPO solved this with constrained optimisation (expensive). PPO finds a simpler way.

PPO: Clipped Objective — Step 2

Theory

Clip the ratio to prevent large updates:

L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\min\!\Big(r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon)\hat{A}_t\Big)\right]
How clipping works
  • If \hat{A}_t > 0 (good action): ratio capped at 1+\epsilon — prevents over-exploitation
  • If \hat{A}_t \lt 0 (bad action): ratio floored at 1-\epsilon — prevents over-avoidance
  • Typical \epsilon = 0.2 — policy can change by at most 20% per step

GAE: Generalised Advantage Estimation

Theory

PPO needs advantage estimates \hat{A}_t. GAE (Schulman et al., 2015) balances bias and variance:

\hat{A}_t^{\text{GAE}} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}
where \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t) is the TD error
  • \lambda = 0: one-step TD (low variance, high bias)
  • \lambda = 1: Monte Carlo (high variance, low bias)
  • \lambda = 0.95: practical sweet spot

SAC: Maximum Entropy RL

Theory

Soft Actor-Critic adds an entropy bonus to the reward:

J(\pi) = \sum_{t} \mathbb{E}\!\left[r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_t))\right]
\alpha is the temperature — how much to value exploration
  • High \alpha: explore more (spread probability across actions)
  • Low \alpha: exploit more (concentrate on best action)
  • \alpha can be learned automatically to target a desired entropy level
Why entropy? It prevents premature convergence, improves robustness, and results in policies that can be fine-tuned more easily.

RLHF as RL Application

Advanced

RLHF connects the alignment block to the RL block:

RL ConceptRLHF Mapping
EnvironmentThe prompt distribution
StatePrompt + tokens generated so far
ActionNext token to generate
RewardReward model score (at end of sequence)
PolicyThe language model \pi_\theta
DPO bypasses this: DPO solves the RL objective in closed form, so you never need to run the RL loop. But understanding the RL formulation explains why DPO works.

Offline RL

Advanced

Learn a policy from a fixed dataset — no further environment interaction.

Conservative Q-Learning (CQL)

Penalises Q-values for unseen actions, preventing overestimation:

\hat{Q} \leq Q^{\pi_\beta} (conservative)

Decision Transformer

Frames RL as sequence modelling: condition on desired return, predict actions.

No Bellman equation needed — just a transformer.

Use case: Healthcare, finance — anywhere you cannot experiment in the real environment.

6.4 — Advanced Alignment: Model Merging

Advanced

What if you could combine adapters without retraining?

Train separate LoRA adapters for different skills, then merge them into a single model:

Adapter A (code) → Merge ← Adapter B (chat)

Merging Methods

Advanced
MethodMechanismStrength
Model SoupsWeighted average of parametersSimple, effective for similar models
TIESTrim low-magnitude, Elect sign, MergeHandles conflicting parameter signs
DARERandomly drop delta params, rescaleReduces interference between tasks
SLERPSpherical linear interpolationSmooth blending on the parameter hypersphere
Why merge? Training one adapter per task is cheaper than training one model for all tasks. Merging is a post-hoc combination strategy.

TIES Merging: Step by Step

Advanced
  1. Trim: Zero out parameters with small magnitude changes (below a threshold \lambda)
  2. Elect sign: For each parameter position, choose the sign that appears most frequently across models
  3. Merge: Average only the parameters that agree with the elected sign
\tau_m = \text{sign}\Big(\sum_k \tilde{\tau}_k^m\Big) \cdot \frac{\sum_k |\tilde{\tau}_k^m| \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{sign}(\tilde{\tau}_k^m) = \text{elected}]}{|\{k : \text{sign}(\tilde{\tau}_k^m) = \text{elected}\}|}
Conflicting updates cancel; agreeing updates reinforce

Differential Privacy in Alignment

Advanced

When fine-tuning on sensitive data, DP-SGD provides formal privacy guarantees:

\tilde{g}_t = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \text{clip}(g_i, C) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 C^2 I)
Clip gradients to norm C, add calibrated Gaussian noise
  • Privacy budget (\epsilon, \delta): lower \epsilon = stronger privacy
  • Trade-off: more noise → worse model quality
  • LoRA + DP-SGD: fewer parameters → less noise needed → better privacy-utility trade-off

6.5 — AI Governance with PACT

Foundations

What is governance?

Rules about who can do what, enforced automatically.

Analogy: A hotel key card. You don't ask the front desk every time you enter your room — the card encodes your access rights, and the lock enforces them. PACT is the key card system for AI agents.

Without governance, every AI system is a skeleton key.

EU AI Act: Risk Tiers

Theory

The EU AI Act classifies AI systems by risk level:

TierExamplesRequirements
Unacceptable Social scoring, real-time biometric mass surveillance Banned (Art. 5)
High Credit scoring, recruitment, medical devices Conformity assessment, human oversight (Art. 6, 9, 13)
Limited Chatbots, deepfakes, emotion recognition Transparency obligations (Art. 52)
Minimal Spam filters, game AI No requirements (voluntary codes of conduct)

Key EU AI Act Articles

Theory

  • Article 9 — Risk management system: identify, analyse, evaluate, mitigate. Continuous. Documented.
  • Article 13 — Transparency and provision of information: "sufficient for the user to interpret the system's output"
  • Article 52 — Disclosure obligations: users must know they are interacting with AI
Fines: Up to 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited practices. Up to 3% for other non-compliance.

Singapore: AI Verify & ISAGO 2.0

Theory

Singapore's approach: voluntary but market-expected

  • AI Verify — open-source testing toolkit for responsible AI
  • ISAGO 2.0 — International Standard for AI Governance and Operations
  • Covers: accountability, data management, performance, safety, fairness, transparency
  • Testing components: fairness metrics, explainability reports, robustness checks

PACT: The Governance Grammar

Foundations

PACT provides a formal grammar for organisational governance. Everything reduces to three letters:

D

Department

The organisational unit

T

Team

The functional group

R

Role

The individual capability

Every entity in the system — human or AI — has a D/T/R address.

D/T/R Addressing

Theory

Addresses are hierarchical paths:

org/ ├── engineering/ ← D: Department │ ├── ml-platform/ ← T: Team │ │ ├── senior-ml-engineer ← R: Role │ │ ├── junior-ml-engineer ← R: Role │ │ └── ml-agent-alpha ← R: Role (AI) │ └── backend/ │ ├── senior-backend-dev │ └── api-agent-beta ← R: Role (AI) └── compliance/ └── governance/ └── compliance-officer

Address format: org/engineering/ml-platform/senior-ml-engineer

Monotonic Tightening

Theory

Permissions can only get more restrictive as you go deeper:

\text{envelope}(R) \subseteq \text{envelope}(T) \subseteq \text{envelope}(D)
A role can never have MORE permissions than its team, which can never exceed its department
  • If Engineering can access datasets A, B, C — ML Platform can access at most A, B, C
  • If ML Platform can deploy to staging — junior-ml-engineer can deploy to at most staging
  • Tightening is monotonic: once restricted, cannot be un-restricted by a child
Fail-closed: If any level in the chain denies access, access is denied. No override. No exceptions.

Operating Envelopes

Theory

Every role has a role envelope. Every task has a task envelope. The agent can only act in the intersection:

\text{effective envelope} = \text{role envelope} \cap \text{task envelope}
What you CAN do ∩ what you are ASKED to do = what actually happens

Role Envelope

  • Max cost per request: $5
  • Allowed tools: DataExplorer, polars
  • Data access: public datasets only

Task Envelope

  • Budget: $2
  • Required tools: DataExplorer
  • Data: HDB prices dataset

5CriticalEncryption keys, PIIHuman-approved only

An agent with Level 3 clearance cannot access Level 4 data, even if its role envelope technically allows the operation.

Enforcement: TrustPosture & ConfidentialityLevel

Theory

TrustPosture

  • Untrusted — deny all by default
  • Cautious — allow reads, deny writes
  • Standard — normal operations
  • Elevated — expanded access for senior roles

ConfidentialityLevel

Maps to knowledge clearance 1–5. Attached to every piece of data, every tool, every endpoint.

The trust plane underneath everything: TrustPosture determines what you can do; ConfidentialityLevel determines what you can see.

6.6 — Governed Agents

Foundations

An agent without governance is a liability. A governed agent is an asset.

Ungoverned Agent

  • Can call any API
  • No spending limit
  • Accesses all data
  • No audit trail

PACT-Governed Agent

  • Tools restricted by role
  • Budget enforced per-task
  • Data filtered by clearance
  • Every action logged

GovernanceContext: Frozen by Design

Theory

When a governed agent starts a task, it receives a frozen GovernanceContext:

  • The context is computed at task start from the D/T/R address + task envelope
  • It is immutable — the agent cannot modify its own permissions mid-task
  • Every tool call checks the context before execution
  • If the context denies access, the operation fails immediately (fail-closed)
Why frozen? If an agent could modify its own context, a prompt injection could escalate privileges. Frozen contexts are immune to this attack.

6.7 — Agent Governance at Scale

Foundations

One governed agent is straightforward. What about fifty agents working together?

  • Budgets must cascade: team budget → individual agent budgets
  • Clearance must propagate: an agent cannot delegate to a higher-clearance agent
  • Failures must be handled: what happens when an agent exceeds its budget mid-task?

Budget Cascading

Theory
\sum_{i=1}^{n} b_i \leq B_{\text{team}} \leq B_{\text{department}}
Individual budgets must sum to at most the team budget, which is bounded by the department budget
  • A supervisor agent allocates budget to worker agents
  • Allocation is pre-committed: the budget is reserved before the task starts
  • Unspent budget returns to the pool after task completion
  • Over-budget triggers immediate halt (not a warning — a hard stop)

Clearance Propagation

Theory

When Agent A delegates to Agent B:

\text{clearance}(B_{\text{effective}}) = \min(\text{clearance}(B_{\text{role}}),\; \text{clearance}(A_{\text{task}}))
The delegated agent gets the lesser of its own clearance and the delegating task's clearance
Anti-pattern: Agent A (clearance 2) delegates to Agent B (clearance 4) to access restricted data. PACT prevents this: B's effective clearance becomes min(4, 2) = 2.

Dereliction: When Agents Fail

Theory
Failure ModeWhat HappensPACT Response
Budget exceededAgent tries to spend beyond limitHard stop, audit log, supervisor notified
Clearance violationAgent tries to access restricted dataImmediate denial, incident logged
TimeoutAgent runs beyond time envelopeGraceful shutdown, partial results returned
Tool misuseAgent calls a tool outside its allowed setCall blocked, agent receives error
Cascade failureWorker agent fails, supervisor must decideSupervisor can retry, reassign, or escalate

Algorithmic Impact Assessment

Advanced

Before deploying a governed agent system, conduct an AIA:

  1. Scope — What decisions does the system make or influence?
  2. Stakeholders — Who is affected? (customers, employees, public)
  3. Risk analysis — What could go wrong? (bias, privacy, safety, economic)
  4. Mitigation — What controls are in place? (PACT envelopes, audit, human review)
  5. Monitoring — How will you detect problems post-deployment? (DriftMonitor, audit alerts)
EU AI Act Art. 9 requires this for high-risk systems. PACT's audit chain provides the evidence base.

Key Takeaways: If You Followed the Math

Theory
  • LoRA works because weight updates are intrinsically low-rank: \Delta W = BA with r \ll d
  • DPO derives from Bradley-Terry + KL-constrained RLHF → closed-form loss on preference pairs
  • Bellman equations (expectation and optimality) are the foundation of all value-based RL
  • PPO clips the policy ratio to prevent destructive updates: [1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]
  • PACT envelopes are formally \text{role} \cap \text{task} with monotonic tightening
