Lesson 6.4 RAG Systems

Your aligned LLM is capable and safe but still has two fatal flaws: it does not know anything that post-dates its training cutoff, and it does not know anything that was private to your organisation. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) fixes both in one architectural move — fetch relevant facts from a search index and inject them into the prompt as context. In this lesson you will build a production-grade RAG pipeline on Singapore regulatory documents (MAS, PDPA, AI Verify), compare BM25, dense, and hybrid retrieval, evaluate quality with RAGAS, and try the HyDE trick for an easy accuracy boost.

Why RAG Beats Fine-Tuning for Knowledge

Fine-tuning teaches the model new skills and new styles. It is the wrong tool for teaching the model new facts. The reasons are practical:

  • Freshness: new regulations, product launches, policy changes happen daily. Retraining daily is absurd. Re-indexing daily is trivial.
  • Attribution: RAG can cite the source chunk that produced an answer. Fine-tuning blurs facts into weights with no traceability.
  • Cost: indexing a million documents costs pennies. Fine-tuning on a million documents costs thousands of dollars.
  • Privacy: the document stays in your index; only the retrieved snippets reach the model provider.

The trade-off: RAG pipelines are more complex to operate (indexing, retrieval tuning, evaluation) and retrieval errors cascade directly into generation errors.

The RAG Pipeline in Five Stages

Stage 1: Chunking

You cannot retrieve "the MAS TRM document" — it is 200 pages. You retrieve chunks. A chunk is a contiguous window of text small enough to fit comfortably in the prompt and focused enough that its meaning is self-contained.

Chunk size trade-offs

Chunk sizeProsCons
Small (~100 tokens)Precise retrieval, many chunks fit in contextOften lacks full context for a claim
Medium (~500 tokens)Good default — balance of context and precisionSometimes splits a single thought across chunks
Large (~1500 tokens)Self-contained — whole sectionsRetrieval is coarse, prompts get expensive

The overlap trick

A fixed-size chunker splits text at arbitrary token boundaries — which means a key sentence can end up cut in half. The fix is overlap: each chunk shares its last $k$ tokens with the start of the next chunk. Typical overlap is 10–20% of the chunk size (e.g., 50 tokens of overlap on a 500-token chunk). Every sentence that crosses a chunk boundary ends up fully inside at least one chunk. 

def chunk_text(text: str, chunk_size: int = 500, overlap: int = 50) -> list[str]:
    """Split text into overlapping chunks of approximately `chunk_size` tokens.

    Simple whitespace tokenisation — for production, use a proper tokenizer
    so your chunks match the embedder's view of length.
    """
    tokens = text.split()
    chunks: list[str] = []
    step = chunk_size - overlap
    for i in range(0, len(tokens), step):
        window = tokens[i : i + chunk_size]
        if len(window) < 20:     # skip tail fragments shorter than 20 tokens
            break
        chunks.append(" ".join(window))
    return chunks

Smarter chunking

The naive fixed-size chunker is good enough to get started. Two upgrades you should know about:

  • Sentence-boundary aware: split on sentence-ending punctuation and pack sentences into chunks until you hit the target size. Prevents sentence fragments.
  • Semantic chunking: use an embedding model to detect topic shifts and put chunk boundaries where topics change. Slow at indexing time, pays off in retrieval quality.

Stage 2: Sparse Retrieval with BM25

Before embeddings, retrieval was done with keyword scoring. BM25 is the classic, and it still works remarkably well — often better than embeddings on tasks with rare terms (names, numbers, acronyms) that embedding models fail to distinguish.

BM25 scores a document $d$ against a query $q$ as a sum over query terms of three factors: how rare the term is across the corpus, how frequent it is in the document, and a length correction.

$$\text{BM25}(q, d) \;=\; \sum_{t \in q} \text{IDF}(t) \cdot \frac{f(t, d) \cdot (k_1 + 1)}{f(t, d) + k_1 \cdot \left(1 - b + b \cdot \frac{|d|}{\bar{|d|}}\right)}$$
IDF(t) — inverse document frequency of $t$ $f(t, d)$ — term frequency in $d$ $|d|$ — document length $\bar{|d|}$ — average document length $k_1 \approx 1.5$ — term saturation $b \approx 0.75$ — length norm

Where:

$$\text{IDF}(t) \;=\; \log\!\left(\frac{N - n_t + 0.5}{n_t + 0.5} + 1\right)$$

$N$ is the total number of chunks in the corpus and $n_t$ is the number of chunks containing term $t$. Rare terms dominate; common terms like "the" contribute near zero.

🔵 Theory — why BM25 still wins on some queries

Dense embeddings compress meaning into a fixed-length vector (typically 768 or 1024 floats). They are magnificent at semantic similarity but they struggle with exact-match queries like "section 4.3.2 of MAS Notice 626" where the right answer depends on the precise term. BM25 retrieves by exact token match and handles this effortlessly. The standard production pattern is hybrid retrieval: run both, combine the scores, enjoy the best of both worlds.

Stage 3: Dense Retrieval with Embeddings

Dense retrieval replaces token matching with semantic similarity. You embed each chunk into a high-dimensional vector, embed the query into the same space, and retrieve the chunks whose vectors are closest to the query's.

The distance metric is almost always cosine similarity:

$$\text{cosine}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \, \|\mathbf{v}\|}$$
$\mathbf{u}$ — query embedding $\mathbf{v}$ — chunk embedding range: $[-1, 1]$ 1 = identical direction

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors — it is invariant to magnitude. Two embeddings that are pointing the same direction have cosine 1, regardless of length. This is the right metric for embeddings because the meaning is encoded in direction, not magnitude. 

import math

def cosine_similarity(u: list[float], v: list[float]) -> float:
    dot = sum(a * b for a, b in zip(u, v))
    nu = math.sqrt(sum(a * a for a in u))
    nv = math.sqrt(sum(b * b for b in v))
    return dot / (nu * nv + 1e-12)


class VectorStore:
    """A minimal in-memory vector store — good enough for teaching."""

    def __init__(self):
        self.chunks: list[str] = []
        self.embeddings: list[list[float]] = []
        self.metadata: list[dict] = []

    def add(self, chunk: str, embedding: list[float], meta: dict):
        self.chunks.append(chunk)
        self.embeddings.append(embedding)
        self.metadata.append(meta)

    def search(self, query_embedding: list[float], k: int = 5) -> list[tuple[float, str, dict]]:
        scored = [
            (cosine_similarity(query_embedding, emb), chunk, meta)
            for emb, chunk, meta in zip(self.embeddings, self.chunks, self.metadata)
        ]
        scored.sort(reverse=True, key=lambda x: x[0])
        return scored[:k]

For real systems use a dedicated vector database (pgvector, Qdrant, FAISS) that can handle millions of chunks with approximate nearest neighbour search. The in-memory version above is enough to teach the mechanics and small enough to debug.

Stage 4: Hybrid Retrieval

BM25 and dense retrieval have complementary failure modes. BM25 misses paraphrases ("credit exposure" vs "counterparty risk"). Dense retrieval misses exact identifiers ("MAS Notice 626"). A hybrid combines both:

$$\text{score}_\text{hybrid} \;=\; \alpha \cdot \text{score}_\text{dense} + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \text{score}_\text{BM25}$$

The scores are first normalised (both to [0, 1]) before combining, since BM25 scores are unbounded while cosine similarities live in $[-1, 1]$. Tune $\alpha$ on a held-out set; $\alpha = 0.6$ is a reasonable default for technical documents with rare terms.

Re-ranking with a cross-encoder

After retrieval, you often have ~20 candidates and need to pick the top 5 for the prompt. A cross-encoder re-ranker scores each (query, chunk) pair jointly — it takes both inputs through a transformer and outputs a single relevance score. This is more accurate than cosine similarity (because the model can attend between query and chunk tokens) but far too slow to use on an entire corpus. The standard pattern is retrieve 20 with dense + BM25, re-rank with a cross-encoder, keep top 5 for the prompt.

Stage 5: HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embeddings

Here is a clever trick. User queries are often short and sparse ("How is customer NRIC handled under PDPA?"), while the documents they want to retrieve are long and specific ("Personal data protection obligations include consent requirements before collecting NRIC numbers from individuals..."). The embeddings of short queries and long documents live in different parts of the vector space.

HyDE (Gao et al. 2022) fixes this by asking an LLM to generate a hypothetical answer to the query first, then embedding the hypothetical answer instead of the raw query.

  1. User asks: "How is customer NRIC handled under PDPA?"
  2. LLM writes a hypothetical answer (without retrieval): "Under PDPA, organisations must obtain consent before collecting NRIC numbers, and can only collect them when required by law..."
  3. Embed the hypothetical answer, not the query.
  4. Retrieve. The hypothetical answer is long and specific and lives in the same neighbourhood as the real PDPA text.
  5. Generate the final answer from the real retrieved chunks.
🟢 HyDE in one sentence

"Hallucinate the answer first so that the embedding of the hallucination can retrieve the real answer." The LLM does not need to hallucinate correctly — it just needs to hallucinate in the right neighbourhood.

Evaluating RAG with RAGAS

"Does the system work?" is a useless question. RAG quality has at least four independent axes, and a good pipeline should be measured on all of them. The RAGAS framework names and scores them:

MetricQuestionDiagnoses
Faithfulness Does the answer follow from the retrieved context? Hallucination — answer invents facts not in the sources
Answer relevance Does the answer actually address the question? Off-topic answers, verbose padding
Context relevance Is the retrieved context actually relevant to the question? Retrieval misses — wrong chunks fetched
Context recall Does the retrieved context contain all the needed information? Retrieval gaps — relevant chunks not fetched

Each metric is scored by an LLM judge in [0, 1]. Run RAGAS on a small gold-labelled set of questions and expected answers. When one metric drops, you know exactly where to look: low faithfulness means the generator is hallucinating; low context relevance means retrieval is fetching the wrong chunks; low context recall means retrieval is missing the right chunks.

🟣 Advanced — why separate context relevance from recall

Context relevance asks "are the chunks we fetched useful?". Context recall asks "did we fetch everything we needed?". You can have high relevance and low recall (fetched chunks are great, but we missed three others), or high recall and low relevance (we got everything, plus a pile of junk). Fixing these two failures requires different tools — relevance improves with re-ranking, recall improves with broader retrieval (more $k$, hybrid, query expansion).

Step 3.

Implement BM25 over the same chunks.

import math
from collections import Counter

class BM25Index:
    def __init__(self, chunks: list[str], k1: float = 1.5, b: float = 0.75):
        self.chunks = chunks
        self.k1, self.b = k1, b
        self.tokens = [c.lower().split() for c in chunks]
        self.lengths = [len(t) for t in self.tokens]
        self.avg_len = sum(self.lengths) / len(self.lengths)
        # document frequency
        self.df: Counter[str] = Counter()
        for toks in self.tokens:
            for t in set(toks):
                self.df[t] += 1
        self.N = len(chunks)

    def idf(self, term: str) -> float:
        n_t = self.df.get(term, 0)
        return math.log((self.N - n_t + 0.5) / (n_t + 0.5) + 1)

    def score(self, query: str, idx: int) -> float:
        q_terms = query.lower().split()
        doc_toks = self.tokens[idx]
        doc_len = self.lengths[idx]
        tf = Counter(doc_toks)
        score = 0.0
        for t in q_terms:
            f = tf.get(t, 0)
            if f == 0:
                continue
            num = f * (self.k1 + 1)
            den = f + self.k1 * (1 - self.b + self.b * doc_len / self.avg_len)
            score += self.idf(t) * num / den
        return score

    def top_k(self, query: str, k: int = 5) -> list[tuple[float, int]]:
        scored = [(self.score(query, i), i) for i in range(self.N)]
        scored.sort(reverse=True)
        return scored[:k]
Step 4.

Build a hybrid retriever that normalises and combines both.

def normalise(scores: list[float]) -> list[float]:
    lo, hi = min(scores), max(scores)
    if hi - lo < 1e-9:
        return [0.0] * len(scores)
    return [(s - lo) / (hi - lo) for s in scores]


async def hybrid_retrieve(query: str, k: int = 5, alpha: float = 0.6):
    q_emb = await embed(query)
    # dense scores
    dense_scores = [cosine_similarity(q_emb, e) for e in store.embeddings]
    # bm25 scores
    bm25_scores = [bm25.score(query, i) for i in range(len(store.chunks))]

    d_norm = normalise(dense_scores)
    b_norm = normalise(bm25_scores)

    hybrid = [alpha * d + (1 - alpha) * b for d, b in zip(d_norm, b_norm)]
    ranked = sorted(enumerate(hybrid), key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return [(store.chunks[i], s) for i, s in ranked[:k]]
Step 5.

Assemble the full RAG answer pipeline.

async def rag_answer(question: str, k: int = 5) -> dict:
    retrieved = await hybrid_retrieve(question, k=k)
    context = "\n\n---\n\n".join(f"[Source {i+1}]\n{chunk}" for i, (chunk, _) in enumerate(retrieved))

    prompt = f"""You are a Singapore regulatory compliance assistant.
Answer the question using only the sources below. Cite sources by number.
If the sources do not contain the answer, say so clearly.

SOURCES:
{context}

QUESTION: {question}

ANSWER:"""

    delegate = Delegate(model=model, max_llm_cost_usd=0.2)
    response = ""
    async for event in delegate.run(prompt):
        if hasattr(event, "text"):
            response += event.text

    return {
        "question": question,
        "answer": response.strip(),
        "sources": [chunk for chunk, _ in retrieved],
    }
Step 6.

Evaluate with RAGAS on a small gold set. Expect faithfulness near 0.9 when the retriever is working; watch for faithfulness drops that signal hallucination.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1: Chunks too big

A 2000-token chunk is easy to index but produces coarse retrieval — the user's question matches one sentence out of 2000 tokens and you pollute the prompt with 1999 irrelevant tokens. Start at 500, go smaller only if the system keeps retrieving almost-relevant chunks.

⚠ Mistake 2: Pure dense retrieval on rare-term queries

"What does MAS Notice 626 say about data governance?" The exact string "MAS Notice 626" is rare and specific. Dense embeddings compress it into a generic "regulatory document" signal and the retriever pulls the wrong notices. Always run hybrid on technical corpora.

⚠ Mistake 3: No RAGAS evaluation

Without faithfulness and context relevance scores you cannot diagnose which stage is failing. A full retrieval-augmented pipeline has 5+ failure modes, and "the answer is wrong" tells you nothing about which one.

⚠ Mistake 4: Stale index

You indexed the MAS documents a month ago. Last week MAS published a revision. Your RAG system confidently cites the old version. Set up an index refresh schedule and include a "last indexed" timestamp in your answer's footer.

Reflection

You now have a complete, production-grade RAG pipeline: chunks with overlap, BM25 for exact match, dense embeddings for semantics, a hybrid blender, a cross-encoder re-ranker, and RAGAS to tell you which stage is failing. That is the same architecture sitting behind enterprise copilots at scale, only with a bigger vector database.

More importantly, you now know the axis where RAG wins and where it loses. It wins on fresh knowledge, private corpora, and attribution — the three things fine-tuning cannot give you. It loses on style, tone, and task shape — which is exactly where Lessons 6.2 and 6.3 came in. A serious production stack uses both: fine-tune for behaviour, RAG for facts.
