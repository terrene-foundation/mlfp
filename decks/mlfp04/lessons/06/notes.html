Speaker Notes — Lesson 4.6 NLP — From Text to Topics

Total time: ~75 minutes  ·  17 slides  ·  ~4 min per slide

This lesson covers the most common NLP feature extraction pipeline: representing text as numbers, discovering topics, and evaluating topic quality. The conceptual load is moderate — TF-IDF is intuitive once you explain it as "surprise weighting". The real challenge is keeping three topic methods (NMF, LDA, BERTopic) distinct in the learners' minds. Use the comparison table on Slide 14 as the anchor. Emphasise the NMF-to-matrix-factorisation thread — it is the setup for 4.7's PIVOT.

Slide 1~2 minTitle

NLP — From Text to Topics

Frame: "Everything we have done so far used numbers as input — temperatures, prices, pixel values. Today we work with text. The question is: how do you turn words into numbers that a model can learn from? Three answers: TF-IDF (classical), word embeddings (neural preview), and topic models (discovery). By the end you will have run all three on Singapore news articles."

→ "Why text data matters."
Slide 2~3 minMotivation

Text: the most common unstructured data

Make it concrete: "Think of your own organisation. How many emails did your team send last month? How many support tickets? How many Slack messages? Nobody reads all of them. Topic modelling reads them for you and tells you: 40% are billing complaints, 25% are feature requests, 15% are onboarding questions. That is actionable intelligence from unstructured text."

For beginners: "Think of it as automatic labelling. You have thousands of unlabelled documents. Topic modelling gives each one a set of labels — without anyone reading them."
→ "The pipeline: text → vectors → topics → features."
Slide 3~3 minPipeline diagram

Text analysis pipeline

Walk the four-box pipeline left to right. Emphasise: "The biggest decision is at box 2 — do you use TF-IDF (sparse, fast, interpretable) or BERT embeddings (dense, semantic, heavier)? That choice cascades to box 3: NMF/LDA work on TF-IDF; BERTopic works on dense embeddings."

→ "Let's start with the classical approach: TF-IDF."
Slide 4~4 minTF-IDF

TF-IDF — weighting words by importance

Walk the formula piece by piece. "TF is just a count — how many times does this word appear in this document. IDF is the surprise factor: if the word appears in every document, idf is zero. Multiply them: frequent-in-this-doc AND rare-across-corpus = high TF-IDF. That is exactly the word that distinguishes this document from others."

The newspaper analogy: "In a newspaper, the word 'Singapore' appears in every article — it tells you nothing about the article's topic. The word 'CHAS' appears only in healthcare articles — it tells you everything. TF-IDF captures this mathematically."
→ "TF-IDF has two weaknesses. BM25 fixes them."
Slide 5~3 minBM25

BM25 — TF-IDF's smarter cousin

Do not dwell on the formula — point out the two fixes: "First, the numerator saturates: repeating a word 100 times does not give 100x weight, it asymptotes to about $k_1 + 1$. Second, longer documents are penalised via the length ratio in the denominator. These two fixes are why BM25 replaced TF-IDF in every production search engine."

For experts: mention the BM25+ variant and that the Okapi parameters ($k_1 = 1.2$, $b = 0.75$) are empirically robust — most practitioners never tune them.
→ "TF-IDF and BM25 are sparse. What about meaning?"
Slide 6~4 minWord embeddings

Word embeddings — dense semantic vectors

The diagram does the heavy lifting. Point at the sparse side: "happy and joyful are completely different dimensions — no similarity captured." Point at the dense side: "now they are nearby in vector space. This is the distributional hypothesis: words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings."

Name the three: Word2Vec (the original), GloVe (global statistics), FastText (subword, handles typos and OOV). Tell them: "Today we use these as tools — plug in and get vectors. In Lesson 4.8 we will understand how the neural network that produces them actually works. That understanding is the bridge to deep learning."

→ "Now: discovering topics."
Slide 7~4 minLDA story

LDA — the generative story

Walk the three-step story slowly. "Imagine an author writing a document. First they decide what topics to write about (60% transport, 30% policy, 10% economics). Then for each word they flip a topic coin, and given the topic, they pick a word from that topic's vocabulary. LDA says: if that is how documents are written, I can reverse-engineer the topics from the words."

If beginners struggle with the generative concept: "Don't worry about the math behind inference. The key idea is that each document is a mix of topics and each topic is a mix of words. LDA discovers both mixes simultaneously."
→ "The plate diagram shows the same story formally."
Slide 8~3 minPlate diagram

LDA plate notation

Walk the Greek letters. "Alpha controls how spread the topic mixture is per document. Beta controls how spread the word distribution is per topic. Theta is what we want to learn — the topic mix for each document. Phi is the other thing we want to learn — the word mix for each topic. Z is the latent variable — the topic assigned to each word position. W is the only thing we observe — the actual words."

For experts: briefly mention collapsed Gibbs sampling vs variational inference. Both integrate out theta and phi to sample z directly.
→ "A simpler approach: NMF."
Slide 9~3 minNMF

NMF — topics via matrix factorisation

Connect to PCA: "In 4.3 we factorised X into U Sigma V^T with PCA. NMF does the same but forces both factors to be non-negative. Why? Because word counts are never negative and topic weights should not be negative. The non-negativity constraint makes NMF topics sparser and more interpretable than PCA components."

Setup for the PIVOT: "Hold this thought: NMF factorises a document-word matrix. In 4.7 we factorise a user-item matrix. Same math, different data. That is the PIVOT."

→ "The modern approach: BERTopic."
Slide 10~4 minBERTopic

BERTopic — the 2020s upgrade

Walk the four-step pipeline diagram. "Step 1: embed every document with a pre-trained sentence transformer — now each document is a dense 384-dimensional vector. Step 2: UMAP reduces to 5 dimensions. Step 3: HDBSCAN clusters — no need to specify K. Step 4: c-TF-IDF extracts the most representative words per cluster. Done."

Emphasise the advantage: "BERTopic captures semantic similarity. If one article says 'MRT breakdown' and another says 'subway disruption', TF-IDF treats them as unrelated. BERTopic puts them in the same topic."

→ "Let's see the code."
Slide 11~3 minBERTopic code

BERTopic in practice

Run the code live if possible. Four lines: create the embedding model, create BERTopic, fit_transform, print topics. The output shows topic IDs, counts, and representative words. Mention the Kailash ModelVisualizer integration for heatmap visualisation.

→ "How do we know if the topics are good?"
Slide 12~4 minNPMI

NPMI — evaluating topic quality

Walk the formula: "NPMI measures whether the top words of a topic co-occur in a reference corpus more than chance. If they do, the topic is coherent — the words belong together. If they don't, the topic is noise — random words lumped together."

Hammer the warning: "LDA reports perplexity as a training metric. Do NOT use it to compare models. Perplexity measures how well the model predicts held-out words, not whether the topics make sense to a human. Use NPMI."

→ "Let's see TF-IDF + NMF on real data."
Slide 13~4 minWorked example

TF-IDF + NMF on Singapore news

Walk the code line by line. Emphasise the TfidfVectorizer parameters: max_features=5000 limits vocabulary, stop_words removes English stopwords, min_df=5 removes ultra-rare terms, max_df=0.7 removes terms appearing in more than 70% of docs. These four parameters handle 90% of preprocessing.

If beginners ask about stop_words: "These are words like 'the', 'is', 'and' — they appear everywhere and tell you nothing about the topic. Removing them is standard."
→ "Now compare the three methods."
Slide 14~4 minComparison

Three methods compared

This is the anchor table. Make them write it down or photograph it. "NMF: fast, interpretable, needs you to set K. LDA: soft assignments, generative model, also needs K. BERTopic: semantic, auto-K, heavier compute. For a quick exploration on a medium corpus, NMF. For probabilistic soft assignments, LDA. For the best quality on large corpora, BERTopic."

→ "Four things to avoid."
Slide 15~3 minMistakes

Four common mistakes

Walk each one. #3 (perplexity) is the most common in practice. Tell the anecdote: "I have seen data scientists spend weeks tuning LDA hyperparameters to minimise perplexity, then present topics that make zero sense to the business. NPMI would have caught that in five minutes."

→ "Where this connects."
Slide 16~3 minConnections

Connections

Walk the four links. The most important is the 4.7 PIVOT: "NMF factorises a document-word matrix. In 4.7, matrix factorisation factorises a user-item matrix. The learned factors are embeddings — features discovered by optimisation. That is the bridge between unsupervised methods and deep learning."

→ "Recap."
Slide 17~2 minRecap

What you can now do

Run through the checklist. Preview 4.7: "Next we take the matrix factorisation idea from NMF and apply it to users and items. The result: recommender systems. And the insight that comes with it — optimisation drives feature discovery — is the PIVOT that connects everything in M4 to deep learning."
