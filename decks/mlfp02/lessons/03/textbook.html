Lesson 2.3 Bootstrapping and Hypothesis Testing

Parametric formulas work when the model is right and $n$ is large. But what if your data is skewed, your sample is small, or you do not know the sampling distribution at all? The bootstrap lets the data tell you its own sampling distribution. This lesson pairs the bootstrap with the formal machinery of hypothesis testing — p-values, power, multiple testing corrections, and permutation tests — so you can answer the decision question "is this difference real?"

The Bootstrap

The core idea is simple to the point of feeling illegal. You have one sample of size $n$. You want to know the sampling distribution of some statistic $\hat\theta$ (a mean, a median, a correlation, a regression coefficient). You do not have more samples. So you pretend that your sample is the population and draw many "resamples" from it with replacement, computing $\hat\theta$ on each. The distribution of those resampled statistics is the bootstrap estimate of the sampling distribution.

The algorithm

  1. Let $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ be your sample.
  2. For $b = 1, \ldots, B$ (typically $B = 1{,}000$ to $10{,}000$):
    • Draw $n$ observations with replacement from the sample (this is $x^{*(b)}$).
    • Compute the statistic $\hat\theta^{*(b)}$ on the resample.
  3. The empirical distribution of $\{\hat\theta^{*(1)}, \ldots, \hat\theta^{*(B)}\}$ is the bootstrap sampling distribution.

Percentile bootstrap CI

The simplest bootstrap CI reads percentiles directly off the empirical distribution. A 95% CI is the interval from the 2.5th to the 97.5th percentile of the bootstrap replicates:

$$\text{CI}_{95\%} \;=\; \left[\,\hat\theta^*_{(0.025)},\; \hat\theta^*_{(0.975)}\,\right]$$
$\hat\theta^*_{(q)}$ — the $q$-quantile of the $B$ bootstrap replicates

BCa: bias-corrected and accelerated

The percentile method is biased when the sampling distribution is skewed. The BCa interval corrects for two things: bias (the median of the bootstrap distribution is not the original $\hat\theta$) and acceleration (skewness changes with the parameter). The correction uses two scalars $\hat{z}_0$ (bias) and $\hat{a}$ (acceleration) to re-map the percentile endpoints.

🔵 Theory: BCa in one screen

Let $\Phi$ be the standard Normal CDF and $\Phi^{-1}$ its inverse. Compute the bias correction as:

$$\hat z_0 \;=\; \Phi^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\#\{\hat\theta^{*(b)} < \hat\theta\}}{B}\right)$$

Compute the acceleration $\hat a$ from the jackknife values $\hat\theta_{(-i)}$ (leave-one-out statistics):

$$\hat a \;=\; \frac{\sum_i (\bar\theta_{(\cdot)} - \hat\theta_{(-i)})^3}{6\left[\sum_i (\bar\theta_{(\cdot)} - \hat\theta_{(-i)})^2\right]^{3/2}}$$

Then the BCa endpoints are not at $\alpha/2$ and $1 - \alpha/2$ but at:

$$\alpha_1 = \Phi\!\left(\hat z_0 + \frac{\hat z_0 + z_{\alpha/2}}{1 - \hat a(\hat z_0 + z_{\alpha/2})}\right), \quad \alpha_2 = \Phi\!\left(\hat z_0 + \frac{\hat z_0 + z_{1-\alpha/2}}{1 - \hat a(\hat z_0 + z_{1-\alpha/2})}\right)$$

If $\hat z_0 = 0$ and $\hat a = 0$, BCa reduces to the percentile method. In practice, use scipy.stats.bootstrap(..., method="BCa") and trust the library for the numerics.

Parametric vs non-parametric bootstrap

The resampling scheme above is the non-parametric bootstrap — it makes no assumption about the distribution, only about independence. A parametric bootstrap fits a model (e.g., Normal) to the sample and then draws new samples from that fitted model. The parametric version is more efficient when the model is right and worse when it is wrong.

Hypothesis Testing

A hypothesis test is a decision rule. You state a default position (the null hypothesis $H_0$) and an alternative ($H_1$). You compute a test statistic from the data. You ask: if the null were true, how likely would I be to see a test statistic at least as extreme as what I actually saw? That probability is the p-value.

The p-value, precisely

$$p \;=\; P\!\left(T \ge T_{\text{obs}} \;\middle|\; H_0\right)$$
$T$ is the test statistic treated as random under $H_0$ $T_{\text{obs}}$ is the one specific value you computed
⚠ What a p-value is NOT

It is not $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$. The p-value is the conditional probability of extreme data given the null, not the probability of the null given the data. To get the latter you need Bayes and a prior. A p-value of 0.03 does not mean "3% chance the null is true"; it means "if the null were true, I would see results at least this extreme 3% of the time by chance."

The $t$-statistic

For testing whether a sample mean differs from a hypothesised value $\mu_0$:

$$T \;=\; \frac{\bar x - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt n}$$

Under $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$, the statistic $T$ follows a Student's $t$-distribution with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom. The p-value is the tail probability beyond the observed $T$.

Significance levels and the decision

FieldTypical $\alpha$Interpretation
Marketing / AB tests0.10Fast decisions, tolerant of false positives
Social science0.05Default in most literature
Clinical trials0.01High cost of false positives
Particle physics$3 \times 10^{-7}$ (5σ)Discovering new phenomena

"Reject $H_0$" or "fail to reject $H_0$" — never "accept $H_0$". Failing to reject is not the same as confirming: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

Type I and Type II errors, power, MDE

For a two-sample $z$-test with effect size $\delta$, variance $\sigma^2$ per arm, and sample size $n$ per arm, the formula for power under a Normal approximation is:

$$\text{power} \;=\; \Phi\!\left(\frac{\delta\sqrt{n/2}}{\sigma} - z_{1 - \alpha/2}\right)$$

The minimum detectable effect (MDE) is the smallest $\delta$ such that power exceeds a target (usually 0.80). Solving the power formula for $\delta$ gives:

$$\delta_{\text{MDE}} \;=\; (z_{1 - \alpha/2} + z_{1 - \beta}) \cdot \sigma\sqrt{2/n}$$

Multiple testing

If you run 20 tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ on random data, you expect one of them to be "significant" by chance alone. The more tests you run, the more false positives you accumulate. Two corrections:

Bonferroni (controls FWER)

Divide your per-test $\alpha$ by the number of tests. For 20 tests at overall $\alpha = 0.05$, use $\alpha = 0.0025$ per test:

$$\alpha_{\text{Bonf}} = \frac{\alpha}{m}$$

Controls the family-wise error rate: the probability of any false positive across all tests. Very conservative when $m$ is large.

Benjamini–Hochberg (controls FDR)

Sort p-values $p_{(1)} \le p_{(2)} \le \cdots \le p_{(m)}$. Find the largest $k$ with $p_{(k)} \le \frac{k}{m}\alpha$. Reject $H_0$ for all tests with p-value $\le p_{(k)}$.

Controls the false discovery rate: the expected proportion of false positives among the rejected hypotheses. Much more powerful than Bonferroni when many nulls are false.

Permutation tests

A permutation test is the purest form of a distribution- free hypothesis test. To test whether two groups have the same mean:

  1. Compute the observed test statistic $T_{\text{obs}}$ (e.g., mean difference).
  2. Pool both groups into a single dataset.
  3. Randomly re-split the pool into two groups of the original sizes and compute the test statistic $T^{*(b)}$. Repeat $B$ times.
  4. The p-value is the fraction of $T^{*(b)}$ at least as extreme as $T_{\text{obs}}$.

The null hypothesis is that the labels are exchangeable — that the data came from a single distribution. If the observed $T$ sits in the tail of the permutation distribution, the labels actually matter.

Worked Example: A/B Test with Multiple Metrics

📝 Goal

An e-commerce A/B test has a control arm and a treatment arm. You want to test three metrics: conversion rate, average order value (AOV), and revenue per visitor (RPV). Apply both Bonferroni and BH-FDR corrections and compare.

Step 1 — Load. 
import polars as pl
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
from shared import MLFPDataLoader

df = MLFPDataLoader().load("mlfp02", "ab_test.parquet")
ctrl = df.filter(pl.col("arm") == "control")
treat = df.filter(pl.col("arm") == "treatment")
print(ctrl.shape, treat.shape)
Step 2 — Point estimates for three metrics. 
def metrics(d):
    return {
        "conv": d["converted"].mean(),
        "aov":  d.filter(pl.col("converted") == 1)["order_value"].mean(),
        "rpv":  d["order_value"].mean(),
    }
m_c, m_t = metrics(ctrl), metrics(treat)
for k in m_c: print(f"{k}: ctrl={m_c[k]:.3f}  treat={m_t[k]:.3f}")
Step 3 — Bootstrap CIs. 
B = 5000
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

def boot_mean_diff(a, b, key, B=5000):
    a_arr = a[key].to_numpy()
    b_arr = b[key].to_numpy()
    n_a, n_b = len(a_arr), len(b_arr)
    reps = np.empty(B)
    for i in range(B):
        ra = rng.choice(a_arr, n_a, replace=True)
        rb = rng.choice(b_arr, n_b, replace=True)
        reps[i] = rb.mean() - ra.mean()
    return reps

diff_conv = boot_mean_diff(ctrl, treat, "converted")
lo, hi = np.percentile(diff_conv, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"Conv rate lift 95% CI = [{lo:.4f}, {hi:.4f}]")
Step 4 — p-values via z-test for proportions. 
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest

n_c, k_c = ctrl.height, ctrl["converted"].sum()
n_t, k_t = treat.height, treat["converted"].sum()
z_conv, p_conv = proportions_ztest([k_t, k_c], [n_t, n_c])
t_aov,  p_aov  = stats.ttest_ind(
    treat.filter(pl.col("converted") == 1)["order_value"].to_numpy(),
    ctrl .filter(pl.col("converted") == 1)["order_value"].to_numpy(),
    equal_var=False,
)
t_rpv,  p_rpv  = stats.ttest_ind(
    treat["order_value"].to_numpy(),
    ctrl ["order_value"].to_numpy(),
    equal_var=False,
)
pvals = np.array([p_conv, p_aov, p_rpv])
print("Raw p-values:", pvals)
Step 5 — Apply corrections. 
from statsmodels.stats.multitest import multipletests

bonf_ok, bonf_p, _, _ = multipletests(pvals, alpha=0.05, method="bonferroni")
bh_ok,   bh_p,   _, _ = multipletests(pvals, alpha=0.05, method="fdr_bh")

print("Bonferroni rejects:", bonf_ok)
print("BH-FDR rejects:    ", bh_ok)

Typically BH-FDR rejects one or two metrics that Bonferroni misses — the price of Bonferroni's guarantee on the family-wise error rate is lower power.

Reflection

You now have two complementary tools for measuring uncertainty. The bootstrap resamples the data to estimate the sampling distribution directly. Hypothesis testing assumes a null distribution and asks how far your data lies from it. Both answer the same fundamental question — could chance explain what I see? — from different directions.
