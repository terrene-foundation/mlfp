Lesson 4.6 NLP — From Text to Topics

Text is the most common unstructured data type in every business: product reviews, support tickets, news articles, chat logs, legal contracts. Today you will turn raw text into numbers with TF-IDF and BM25, learn how LDA generates topics probabilistically, and watch BERTopic combine transformer embeddings with clustering to produce the most interpretable topic models available in 2026. By the end you will be able to extract and evaluate topics from Singapore news articles and explain what the numbers in a topic model actually mean.

Why This Matters

Every week your company generates thousands of text artefacts — tickets, emails, reviews, reports. Nobody has time to read them all. Topic modelling discovers the themes automatically: clusters of words that appear together in documents, each corresponding to a theme like "billing complaints", "feature requests", or "Q4 legal updates". The themes become features (how much of this document is about billing?) and the features drive downstream decisions (auto-route to the right team).

Core Concepts

TF-IDF derivation

The first step in any text pipeline is to turn documents into vectors. The simplest approach — bag of words — counts how many times each term appears in each document. This overweights common words (the, and, to). TF-IDF (term frequency times inverse document frequency) fixes this by downweighting terms that appear in many documents.

$$ \text{tf}(t, d) \;=\; \frac{\text{count}(t \text{ in } d)}{\text{len}(d)} $$ $$ \text{idf}(t) \;=\; \log \frac{N}{\text{df}(t)} $$ $$ \text{tfidf}(t, d) \;=\; \text{tf}(t, d) \cdot \text{idf}(t) $$

where $N$ is the number of documents and $\text{df}(t)$ is the number of documents containing term $t$. The intuition: a word that appears in every document (the) has $\text{idf} = \log(N/N) = 0$, contributing nothing. A word that appears in one document has $\text{idf} = \log N$, contributing a lot. TF-IDF vectors are the workhorse of classical NLP — sparse, interpretable, fast to compute.

BM25 — TF-IDF's smarter cousin

TF-IDF has two weaknesses: it is linear in term frequency (repeating a word 10 times contributes 10×, even though the 10th repetition tells you almost nothing new) and it does not account for document length (a term in a 10-word tweet should count more than the same term in a 10,000-word report). BM25 fixes both:

$$ \text{BM25}(t, d) \;=\; \text{idf}(t) \cdot \frac{(k_1 + 1)\,\text{tf}(t,d)}{\text{tf}(t,d) + k_1 \left(1 - b + b\,\frac{|d|}{\text{avg}|d|}\right)} $$

where $k_1 \approx 1.2$ and $b \approx 0.75$ are the standard parameters. The term-frequency contribution saturates (no matter how many times a word appears, it contributes at most $k_1 + 1$), and document length is normalised against the corpus average. BM25 is the ranking function behind Elasticsearch and Apache Lucene — if you have used Google's search, you have used a variant of BM25.

Word embeddings — a preview

TF-IDF represents each term as a one-hot dimension. "Bank" (financial) and "bank" (river) map to the same dimension. "Happy" and "joyful" map to different dimensions. This is the semantic limitation of sparse representations.

Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText solve this by learning dense vectors where similar words have similar coordinates. The principle: "words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings" (the distributional hypothesis). Skip-gram Word2Vec trains a small neural network to predict the context of a target word, and the hidden-layer weights become the embeddings.

🔵 Theory — preview of the pivot

How does Word2Vec learn these vectors? It uses gradient descent on a classification loss. The hidden layer activations are the word embeddings — features learned by optimisation. We will meet this idea explicitly in Lesson 4.8: hidden layers are automated feature engineering with error feedback.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)

LDA is a generative model for text. Its story:

  1. For each topic $k$, draw a word distribution $\boldsymbol{\phi}_k$ from a Dirichlet prior.
  2. For each document $d$, draw a topic distribution $\boldsymbol{\theta}_d$ from another Dirichlet.
  3. For each word position in document $d$: first draw a topic $z$ from $\boldsymbol{\theta}_d$, then draw a word from $\boldsymbol{\phi}_z$.

Inference reverses the story: given the words, find the most likely topic distributions. This is usually done with variational inference or collapsed Gibbs sampling. Each fitted document is represented as a distribution over topics — a soft assignment, just like GMM's responsibilities.

NMF — the matrix factorisation view

Non-negative Matrix Factorisation (NMF) decomposes the TF-IDF matrix into $W H$ where both $W$ and $H$ have non-negative entries. $W$ rows become topic weights for each document; $H$ rows become word weights for each topic. NMF is faster than LDA, gives sparser and often more interpretable topics, and is our go-to when the corpus is medium-sized and the task is exploratory.

BERTopic

BERTopic is the 2020s upgrade to topic modelling. It replaces TF-IDF with transformer embeddings (typically sentence-transformers), reduces them with UMAP, clusters with HDBSCAN, then computes a class-based TF-IDF (c-TF-IDF) to extract representative words for each cluster. The result: topics that capture semantic relationships (not just co-occurrence) and automatically determine their own number. 

from bertopic import BERTopic
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
topic_model = BERTopic(embedding_model=model, min_topic_size=15)
topics, probs = topic_model.fit_transform(sg_news_articles)
print(topic_model.get_topic_info().head(10))

Coherence metrics — how good is a topic?

Topic model quality is hard to evaluate objectively. The standard approach is coherence — how often do the top words of a topic co-occur in a reference corpus? A common variant is NPMI (normalised pointwise mutual information):

$$ \text{NPMI}(w_i, w_j) \;=\; \frac{\log\bigl(P(w_i, w_j) / (P(w_i)\,P(w_j))\bigr)}{-\log P(w_i, w_j)} $$

NPMI lives in $[-1, 1]$: positive for words that co-occur more than chance, negative otherwise. Average NPMI over the top 10 words of each topic and across topics — higher is better. NPMI is the metric reported in most modern topic modelling papers.

Worked Example: Singapore News Articles

import polars as pl
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.decomposition import NMF, LatentDirichletAllocation

from shared import MLFPDataLoader

loader = MLFPDataLoader()
news = loader.load("mlfp04", "sg_news.parquet")
corpus = news["article_body"].to_list()

# Method 1: TF-IDF + NMF
vec = TfidfVectorizer(max_features=5000, stop_words="english", min_df=5, max_df=0.7)
X_tfidf = vec.fit_transform(corpus)
nmf = NMF(n_components=10, random_state=42).fit(X_tfidf)

for k, topic in enumerate(nmf.components_):
    top_words = [vec.get_feature_names_out()[i] for i in topic.argsort()[-10:][::-1]]
    print(f"Topic {k}: {', '.join(top_words)}")

# Method 2: LDA
lda = LatentDirichletAllocation(n_components=10, random_state=42).fit(X_tfidf)
for k, topic in enumerate(lda.components_):
    top_words = [vec.get_feature_names_out()[i] for i in topic.argsort()[-10:][::-1]]
    print(f"LDA Topic {k}: {', '.join(top_words)}")

Typical outputs on Singapore news: topics emerge as "transport and MRT", "HDB and property", "Budget and policy", "food and hawker culture", "ASEAN diplomacy", etc. The LDA topics are usually softer (each topic has broad word distributions), while NMF topics are sparser and cleaner. Pick the method that matches your downstream need — NMF for visualisation-friendly single themes, LDA when you need soft topic assignments for mixture features.

Common Mistakes

  1. Skipping stopword removal. Without stopwords removed, your top words will be "the", "and", "to", "of". Always use a stopword list for the target language.
  2. Too few or too many topics. LDA and NMF both require you to set $K$. Start with 10 – 20 and tune with coherence.
  3. Evaluating on perplexity. LDA's likelihood-based metric does not correlate with human judgments of topic quality. Use NPMI instead.
  4. Running BERTopic on tiny datasets. BERTopic needs a few hundred documents minimum. For smaller corpora, fall back to TF-IDF + NMF.
