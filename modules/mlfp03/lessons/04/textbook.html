Lesson 3.4 Gradient Boosting Deep Dive

Gradient boosting is the single algorithmic family most responsible for winning tabular ML competitions in the last fifteen years. In the next 75 minutes you will see why. We derive XGBoost's split gain formula from a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss, contrast it with LightGBM's GOSS sampling and CatBoost's ordered boosting, and train all three on Singapore credit-scoring data. By the end you will be able to pick and tune the right booster for any tabular problem you meet.

Boosting vs Bagging — Two Ensembles, Two Strategies

The random forest from Lesson 3.3 uses bagging: train many high-variance trees in parallel on bootstrap samples and average them. Bagging reduces variance without changing bias.

Boosting does the opposite. It trains trees sequentially, each new tree correcting the errors of the previous ensemble. The individual trees are weak (often depth 3–6) but the ensemble becomes strong. Boosting reduces bias.

The boosting recipe

Let $F_m(x)$ be the ensemble after $m$ trees. We start with a constant $F_0(x) = \arg\min_c \sum_i L(y_i, c)$ — for squared loss, the mean of $y$; for log-loss, the log-odds of the base rate. At each subsequent step we fit a new weak learner $f_m$ to the gradient of the loss with respect to the current predictions, and add it:

$$F_m(x) \;=\; F_{m-1}(x) \;+\; \eta \cdot f_m(x)$$

$\eta$ is the learning rate (typically 0.01–0.3) that controls how much each tree contributes. Small $\eta$ with many trees generalises better than large $\eta$ with few trees — a version of the bias-variance dial from Lesson 3.2.

XGBoost — The Second-Order Taylor Expansion

The original gradient-boosting algorithm (Friedman 2001) fits each tree to the first-order gradient of the loss. XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) extends this to a second-order approximation — and the resulting algebra is where the celebrated split-gain formula comes from.

Setup

Let $\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}$ be the ensemble's prediction for row $i$ after $t-1$ trees. We want to add a new tree $f_t$ to minimise the objective:

$$\mathcal{L}^{(t)} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} L\bigl(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(x_i)\bigr) \;+\; \Omega(f_t)$$

The regulariser $\Omega(f_t) = \gamma T + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda\sum_{j=1}^T w_j^2$ penalises the number of leaves $T$ and the L2 norm of the leaf weights $w_j$. $\gamma$ controls tree complexity; $\lambda$ shrinks leaf values.

Second-order Taylor expansion

Expand $L$ around $\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}$ to second order:

$$L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(x_i)) \;\approx\; L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}) + g_i\,f_t(x_i) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,h_i\,f_t(x_i)^2$$

where $g_i$ and $h_i$ are the first and second derivatives of the loss:

$$g_i \;=\; \partial_{\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}}\, L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})\,,\qquad h_i \;=\; \partial^2_{\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}}\, L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})$$

Drop the constant $L(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})$ (it doesn't depend on $f_t$) and substitute into the objective:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(g_i f_t(x_i) + \tfrac{1}{2}h_i f_t(x_i)^2\bigr) + \gamma T + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda\sum_{j=1}^T w_j^2$$

Optimal leaf weight

A tree with $T$ leaves assigns every training point to exactly one leaf. Let $I_j$ be the set of training points landing in leaf $j$; then $f_t(x_i) = w_j$ for $i \in I_j$. Rewriting the sum:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{T}\Bigl[\bigl(\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i\bigr) w_j + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda\bigr) w_j^2\Bigr] + \gamma T$$

Let $G_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} g_i$ and $H_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} h_i$. The inner bracket is a quadratic in $w_j$ and its minimum is:

$$\boxed{\;w_j^* \;=\; -\frac{G_j}{H_j + \lambda}\;}$$

Substituting this back gives the optimal objective for a fixed tree structure:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)}_{\min} \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{T}\frac{G_j^2}{H_j + \lambda} + \gamma T$$

The split-gain formula

Now consider splitting a single leaf into left ($L$) and right ($R$) children. Before the split, the leaf contributed $-\tfrac{1}{2}(G_L + G_R)^2 / (H_L + H_R + \lambda)$ (plus the $\gamma$ for its own leaf). After the split we have two leaves each contributing their own term. The improvement from splitting is:

$$\boxed{\;\mathrm{Gain} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\Bigl[\frac{G_L^2}{H_L + \lambda} + \frac{G_R^2}{H_R + \lambda} - \frac{(G_L + G_R)^2}{H_L + H_R + \lambda}\Bigr] - \gamma\;}$$

XGBoost evaluates this formula for every candidate split and keeps the split with the largest gain. If no split has positive gain, the leaf is not split — the $\gamma$ penalty serves as a built-in pruning cost. This one formula is the mathematical core of XGBoost.

🔵 Theory — Why the second order matters

First-order boosting (Friedman) uses only $g_i$ and fits a tree to the pseudo-residuals $-g_i$. That works but requires a line search for each leaf weight. The second-order approach uses $g_i$ and $h_i$ together — the Newton step $-G_j / H_j$ is the optimal leaf weight in closed form. This avoids the line search, makes the gain formula loss-agnostic (works for any twice-differentiable loss), and lets XGBoost parallelise over features trivially. That is the combination that made it the default competition winner.

LightGBM — GOSS and Histogram Splits

LightGBM (Microsoft, 2017) optimises for speed on massive datasets with two big ideas.

Histogram-based split finding

XGBoost's exact split search evaluates every candidate threshold per feature: $O(n \log n)$ per feature per level. LightGBM bins each feature's values into ~256 buckets and evaluates splits only at bucket boundaries: $O(n + 256)$ per feature. That is orders of magnitude faster on large datasets with little loss in accuracy.

GOSS: Gradient-based One-Side Sampling

The insight: rows with small gradients are already well-predicted. Training further tree nodes on them adds little information. Rows with large gradients are under-fit and carry most of the signal. GOSS keeps all of the high-gradient rows and randomly subsamples the low-gradient rows:

  1. Sort training rows by $|g_i|$ descending.
  2. Keep the top $a\%$ (typically $a=20$) — the high-loss instances.
  3. Randomly sample $b\%$ (typically $b=10$) from the remaining 80%.
  4. Train the next tree only on these $a + b \approx 30\%$ of rows.
  5. Up-weight the sampled low-gradient rows by $(1 - a)/b$ so the gradient estimate remains unbiased.

The net effect: ~30% of the data per iteration, ~3× speedup, almost no accuracy loss. GOSS is why LightGBM scales to millions of rows where vanilla XGBoost starts to crawl.

Leaf-wise growth

XGBoost grows trees level-wise: every node at the current depth is split before going deeper. LightGBM grows leaf-wise: split the single leaf with the largest gain regardless of depth. Leaf-wise trees are unbalanced but each split is locally optimal. Faster convergence, but risk of overfitting — LightGBM exposes num_leaves as the main complexity dial.

CatBoost — Ordered Boosting for Categorical Features

CatBoost (Yandex, 2017) tackles a subtle problem that XGBoost and LightGBM brush under the rug: target leakage in categorical encoding. The standard trick for a high-cardinality categorical feature (postcode, city, user_id) is mean-target encoding: replace each category with the mean target over rows in that category. That is leakage — the encoding uses the target that the model is trying to predict.

Ordered target encoding

CatBoost fixes this by imposing an artificial ordering on the rows and, for each row, computing the target encoding from only the rows that precede it in that ordering:

$$\text{enc}(x_i^{\text{cat}}) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{j < i,\, x_j^{\text{cat}} = x_i^{\text{cat}}} y_j + a\cdot\bar{y}}{\sum_{j < i,\, x_j^{\text{cat}} = x_i^{\text{cat}}} 1 + a}$$

where $a$ is a smoothing prior. Because row $i$'s encoding never depends on its own target $y_i$, there is no leakage. This ordering is re-shuffled on each boosting iteration so different trees see different orderings, avoiding a bias toward the particular arrangement.

In practice: CatBoost is the booster to pick when your data has many high-cardinality categorical features (think Singapore postal codes or product IDs) and you do not want to hand-engineer the encoding.

Worked Example: Credit Scoring with All Three

📝 Goal

Load Singapore credit-scoring data (~5000 applications, 12% default rate — imbalanced). Train XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost with default and tuned parameters, compare AUC-PR (the appropriate metric for imbalanced classification), training time, and feature importance.

Step 1 — Load and split. 
import polars as pl, numpy as np
import xgboost as xgb, lightgbm as lgb, catboost as cb
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import average_precision_score, roc_auc_score
from kailash_ml.interop import to_sklearn_input

from shared import MLFPDataLoader
df = MLFPDataLoader().load("mlfp02", "credit_scoring.parquet")
X, y = to_sklearn_input(df, target="default", standardise=False)  # trees don't need scaling
X_tr, X_te, y_tr, y_te = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2,
                                          stratify=y, random_state=0)
Step 2 — Three boosters, sensible defaults. 
models = {
    "XGBoost":  xgb.XGBClassifier(n_estimators=400, learning_rate=0.05,
                                  max_depth=6, reg_lambda=1, gamma=0.1,
                                  eval_metric="aucpr", random_state=0),
    "LightGBM": lgb.LGBMClassifier(n_estimators=400, learning_rate=0.05,
                                   num_leaves=31, reg_lambda=1,
                                   random_state=0),
    "CatBoost": cb.CatBoostClassifier(iterations=400, learning_rate=0.05,
                                      depth=6, l2_leaf_reg=3,
                                      verbose=False, random_state=0),
}

for name, model in models.items():
    model.fit(X_tr, y_tr)
    p = model.predict_proba(X_te)[:, 1]
    print(f"{name:10s}  AUC-PR = {average_precision_score(y_te, p):.4f}  "
          f"AUC-ROC = {roc_auc_score(y_te, p):.4f}")
Step 3 — Learning curves and early stopping. 
xgb_model = xgb.XGBClassifier(n_estimators=1000, learning_rate=0.05,
                              early_stopping_rounds=25, eval_metric="aucpr")
xgb_model.fit(X_tr, y_tr, eval_set=[(X_te, y_te)], verbose=False)
print(f"Early stopping at round {xgb_model.best_iteration}")

Early stopping halts training when the validation metric has not improved for 25 rounds. It is the standard way to pick the number of trees without manual tuning.

Step 4 — Hyperparameter sensitivity. 
for lr in [0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3]:
    for depth in [3, 6, 10]:
        m = xgb.XGBClassifier(n_estimators=300, learning_rate=lr,
                              max_depth=depth, eval_metric="aucpr")
        m.fit(X_tr, y_tr)
        p = m.predict_proba(X_te)[:, 1]
        print(f"lr={lr:<5} depth={depth:<3} AUC-PR = "
              f"{average_precision_score(y_te, p):.4f}")

Classic pattern: high learning rate with deep trees overfits; low learning rate with shallow trees needs many more iterations but generalises best. Early stopping finds the right number of iterations automatically.

Common Mistakes

⚠ Mistake 1 — Reporting ROC-AUC on imbalanced data

With 12% default rate, ROC-AUC can stay high while the precision at useful recall is miserable. Use AUC-PR (average precision) or a business-weighted threshold instead. Lesson 3.5 develops this in detail.

⚠ Mistake 2 — No early stopping

Without a validation set and early stopping, you either underfit (picked too few trees) or overfit (picked too many). Always pass an eval_set and let the training loop find the right iteration.

⚠ Mistake 3 — Tuning learning_rate and n_estimators independently

They interact directly: halving the learning rate typically doubles the optimal number of trees. Fix a small learning rate (0.05 or 0.01), let early stopping pick n_estimators, and tune depth and regularisation separately.
